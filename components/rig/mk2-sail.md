# Mk2 sail

The revised Mk2 sail plan for the Paradox, including changes to its tack geometry.

## Measuring a sail against the plan

A sail bought or inherited second-hand does not necessarily match the plan it is assumed to be cut to. One example, checked directly against the original sail plan, came out with its luff 250 mm short, its foot 190 mm short, and its head 140 mm short — "a much smaller sail than the Mark 1" — yet its dimensions did not match the Mk2 plan either.[1] The only reliable way to identify which generation of sail is actually in hand is to measure it against the plan, not to go by its reputation or its seller's description.

## Tack geometry and the furling drum

The Mk2 sail's tack angle is greater than 90 degrees — wider than the [Mk1](/components/rig/sail.md)'s near-90-degree tack, which furls almost on top of itself — so the sail creeps forward along the boom, toward the furling drum, as it rolls up. See [furling system — the Mk1/Mk2 tack-angle difference, and why the sail can't be fenced onto the drum](/components/rig/furling.md#the-mk1mk2-tack-angle-difference-and-why-the-sail-cant-be-fenced-onto-the-drum) for the boom-taper fix this requires.

Run through Sailrite's own online sail calculator, the two plans' tack angles came out as **91.3 degrees** for the original sail against **95.65 degrees** for the newer one, with the calculator crediting the newer sail with only about **95.8 sq ft** of area — the calculator's own tack-angle output carried a caveat flagging it as approximate, and no reference to the climbing risk turned up in the archive's own posting history despite the wider angle.[7] Two further fixes for the same climbing tendency, beyond the boom taper above, were floated without being tried in practice: nudging the boom slightly further forward so the furled sail can overlap the drum a little as it rolls up, or lengthening the swivel rod's own tail so it reaches a spool of any diameter.[8] A simpler practical fix followed almost immediately: raise the boom's aft end on the topping lift until the furled sail rolls evenly, then mark the topping-lift line at that position — see [Furling system — a topping-lift fix for the sail's forward creep onto the drum](/components/rig/furling.md#a-topping-lift-fix-for-the-sails-forward-creep-onto-the-drum) for the full technique.[9]

## Locating the sail's forward top corner, and tuning balance through the sleeve gap

The sail's forward top corner sits about **800 mm** from the mast centerline, measured along the top edge — or about **900 mm** to the halyard attachment point along that same edge — a dimension not given directly on the plan and best confirmed by measuring a print of the drawing rather than assumed.[3]

A long gap left in the sleeve at the sail's head, where the sail meets the yard, makes this dimension easy to adjust after the fact: moving the halyard's attachment point along the yard, through the gap, retunes the boat's balance without altering the sail itself.[4] In practice a gap of roughly **12 in**, once the sail is tensioned along the yard, has no significant effect on performance — its only job is to keep sail material from being trapped between the yard and the mast — and a wear patch on the lower sleeve, where the boom presses the sail against the mast, is worth adding at the same time. The gap needs no particular shape (no half-round cut); the sleeve can simply stop and resume further along the yard.[5][6]

The boom's own taper, needed to keep a fuller sail's forward creep clear of the furling drum as it rolls up, is a related but separate fitting question — see [Furling system — the Mk1/Mk2 tack-angle difference](/components/rig/furling.md#the-mk1mk2-tack-angle-difference-and-why-the-sail-cant-be-fenced-onto-the-drum). The boom-to-mast reach at the tack, by contrast, is fixed by the tack strop's own pipe length and the curve bent into its swivel rod — see [Tack strop](/components/rig/tack-strop.md) for that fitting's geometry in full.[5]

## A peaked head, and what follows from it

The sail plan current in 2014 — the latest revision of the design's sail at that date — was **peaked**, its head not parallel to its foot, a shape shared with some Polynesian sails rather than with a Bermudan rig. Where a tall Bermudan mast exists to lengthen the luff, this rig keeps a deliberately short one.[2] Sails of this shape do not need the luff hauled down tight to perform on the wind, behaving more like a junk or lateen rig; see [Clew — trim: sheeting the clew](/components/rig/clew.md) for the sheeting rule of thumb that follows. (The later [Mk3](/components/rig/mk3-sail.md) sail was not drawn until 2023, so a 2014 reference to "the latest sail" cannot be to it.)
