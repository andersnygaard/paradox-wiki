# Sail

The Paradox's sail: making it, its shape, and its performance under the boat's simple lug rig.

## Buying a sail second-hand

Rather than commission a sail from a professional sailmaker, an older, "old-style" Paradox sail can be bought second-hand from another owner who has since moved on to a different sail — a materially cheaper route to a working sail than a new one cut to order.[1] Buying the sail before building the boom and yard lets those spars be sized to the sail actually in hand, rather than to a plan figure alone.[1] The older, lower-aspect-ratio sail rolls up more tidily around the boom when furled than the taller [Mk2 sail](/components/rig/mk2-sail.md), at some cost in windward efficiency.[1]

## Windward performance when reefed

A lugsail loses luff length as it is reefed, and the resulting loss of drive can be severe enough in Force 5 and above that the boat has to be freed off considerably to keep way on.[2] A reefing wing sail, cut more like a junk sail, was considered as a fix on the reasoning that it would heel the boat less than a Bermudan or gaff sail — but increasing luff length raises the sail's centre of effort and so reduces stability, and the sail's fore-and-aft centre of effort has to match the original unless a forward lifting plate is added to the hull; the idea was not carried further.[2]

## Wear and repair

A well-used sail chafes first at the head and foot, where it rubs against the mast, and at the luff opening through which the halyard passes to reach the yard.[3][4] A professional repair replaces worn leather at the head and re-stitches the opening, and adds sacrificial layers of cloth at the tack where the sail contacts the mast — wear that can recur after two or three further seasons' use.[4] A sail bought second-hand is also easily found to run shorter than the plan it was supposedly cut to: one, measured directly against the original sail plan, came out with its luff 250 mm short, its foot 190 mm short, and its head 140 mm short — "a much smaller sail than the Mark 1" — while not matching the [Mk2](/components/rig/mk2-sail.md) plan's dimensions either, a reminder that a used sail's actual generation is best confirmed by measuring it rather than by its reputation.[5]

## A higher-peaked replacement sail

Replacing a worn sail with a newer, higher-peaked design has been reported as the single biggest improvement made to a Paradox after her original build: it transformed windward performance and tacking, to the point of sharply reducing reliance on the outboard for progress to windward.[6] This was a one-off replacement sail fitted to an individual boat in 2013, not a change of sail plan — it should not be identified with the later [Mk3](/components/rig/mk3-sail.md), a larger higher-peaked sail not drawn until 2023, even though both were described as "higher-peaked."

## Sourcing a sail

A first Paradox sail (for the boat "[Zoë](/boats/zoe.md)") was sourced from **Sails etc.** in **St. Augustine, FL**, in **4.5 oz Dacron**, in **tan bark** — a premium, imported-from-England cloth color; plain **white** would be considerably cheaper if a sailmaker doesn't charge much of a premium for tan bark, whose main practical benefit is being **easier on the eyes** (less glare) than white.[7][8]

"[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)" later needed a replacement sail, prompting a much larger sail-sourcing discussion recorded below.

## Buying vs. building a sail: recommendations and firsthand accounts

Paradox owners have taken two practical paths to a sail: commissioning one from a small specialist sailmaker, or sewing it at home.[9]

**Recommended sailmakers:** three names recur — **Dabbler** in Virginia, **Douglas Fowler** in upstate New York, and **Todd Bradshaw** (author of the book *Canoe Rig*, believed to be somewhere in the midwest). Dabbler has a track record with sailors on sailing canoes and other small boats; **Douglas Fowler** is rated the favorite sailmaker among traditional sailing-canoe enthusiasts and is believed to have built at least one set of **Caledonia Yawl** sails. **Todd Bradshaw**, contacted by phone about sailmaking, proved knowledgeable and friendly.[11][14]

**Building a sail at home:** the fullest firsthand account of building a lug sail at home comes in the same message that also covers the [Little Cruiser rudder offsets](/components/rudder.md). There is not enough money in small, high-quality sails to attract much commercial interest, which is why good small-boat sailmakers can be hard to find.[10][12] A first attempt at buying a sail, from **Gambell and Hunter**, was expensive and **lasted only one season** due to a fundamental leech-design error — the leech was cut on the bias and went slack. Out of frustration came an old commercial zig-zag sewing machine and an exact copy of Matt Layden's own sail; the process was **"not easy, but nothing new is"** — that first home-made sail lasted three seasons and was still going strong, with a "new and improved" second version planned: heavier cloth (**4 to 5 oz**) and the top batten moved down to contact the sail's throat. The recommended resource for anyone serious about making their own lugsail is ***The Sailmaker's Apprentice***, with further reading at `http://www.microcruising.com/sailmaking.htm`. For builders who would rather commission the sail, it is simple enough for a **competent sailmaker** to build without needing the home-build route; contacting **Sailrite** and asking about a Sailrite sail's performance is one route in. The standing warning to anyone ordering rather than sewing: **specify exactly what you want**, since these are not "run of the mill 100 square foot sails" — they need **lots of draft** to pull a "heavy little boat" like Paradox along, and a sailmaker unfamiliar with that requirement could deliver a flat sail "fit to push a sunfish" rather than a proper cruising sail.[13]

This gives the two practical paths to a Paradox sail side by side — buying from a small specialist sailmaker versus building one at home — complementing [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md)'s own, considerably more complex sail already documented there.

Whether a plan drawing alone — such as one posted to the group's own Files section — gives a sailmaker or a Sailrite kit enough to work from, or whether more specification is needed, recurred as an open question in 2008 without an answer on record; the standing guidance above, to brief a sailmaker explicitly on the boat's draft requirements rather than assume a generic small-boat cut, is the best available answer.[33] A separate 2008 builder, still searching for a US sailmaker to quote him, considered a plain white tarpaulin sail as a stop-gap to get sailing quickly, having a suitably sized tarp already on hand — an option distinct from either commissioning a sail or sewing one from Dacron, though not recorded as ever being tried in practice.[34] A further sailmaker lead surfaced from a builder near one of the older, established sail lofts in his area, who offered to inquire there in person; interest in a Sailrite kit as an alternative continued alongside it.[35]

By September 2008, Sailrite's own website carried two separate Paradox sail patterns for builders to order directly from — one submitted to match the plans' Drawing 1 dimensions, the other, an earlier listing from a different builder, found to carry an incorrect clew-to-throat measurement, a discrepancy that could catch out anyone ordering from the wrong one without checking first.[36][37] The correctly-dimensioned listing matches the original sail plan rather than the taller, shorter-footed sail Matt Layden drew later, a distinction worth checking before ordering given the group's own "New Sail Plan" folder holds the newer drawing separately.[41] The earlier, mis-dimensioned listing had every other measurement right for the original plan; only its diagonal clew-to-throat figure was wrong, and oddly so, matching neither the original sail's dimension nor the newer one's.[44] Locating the correct listing on Sailrite's own site was not itself straightforward for at least one interested builder — answered with a suggestion to search "Sailrite" directly and, a few days later, the site's plain address.[38][39][40]

## Choosing between the two 2008 Sailrite listings

Faced with the two Sailrite listings, at least one builder chose the original sail plan over the newer one for his own order, having corresponded separately with its submitter: the original sail furls more easily on the boom, and since neither of them planned to race, the newer sail's extra performance did not seem to justify giving up that ease of handling — a newer sail can always be ordered later if wanted.[42]

A fuller comparison between the two sail plans set out the newer sail's trade-offs in more detail. Its shorter boom clears the self-steering vane and reduces the chance of the mainsheet catching on a raised outboard motor — a real consideration where light summer winds can make a motor necessary — and lessens the chance of the boom striking the water when running downwind in a broach with large waves. Higher-aspect-ratio lug sails are favored by sailmakers/designers Michael Storer and John Welsford, though not without trade-offs of their own; a lighter cloth was floated as a way to let the higher-aspect sail's loose upper section "fall" more smoothly when reefed, though the 5.4 oz cloth actually chosen was judged best suited to heavy air regardless.[43]

Once any loose extra cloth at the head is simply bundled up under the yard and left to drape over the side while stowed, it is no real handling problem; the practical limit instead comes in genuinely high winds, when a taller sail cannot be reefed down quite as far.[45] A direct comparison of the two plans, run through Sailrite's own online sail calculator, gave the original sail a **91.3-degree** tack angle against **95.65 degrees** for the newer sail, crediting the newer sail with only about **95.8 sq ft** of area — precise figures for the tack-angle difference already discussed above (see [Mk2 sail — tack geometry and the furling drum](/components/rig/mk2-sail.md#tack-geometry-and-the-furling-drum) for the fuller figures and the proposed fixes for the sail's tendency to climb the spool as it furls) — though no clear answer on record settled who, if anyone, was already sailing with the newer sail.[46] The calculator's own tack-angle reading was treated with some scepticism as approximate rather than settled, being flagged with an asterisk; asked what values belong in the calculator's own dimension boxes (labelled I, J, P, E, I2/P2, J2/E2), the answer from the builder who had actually submitted a listing was simply that he had ignored them.[47][48]

At least one order actually followed through on the original-plan choice: a **100 sq ft** kit in the old cut, priced at **$309 plus $28 shipping**, backed by direct phone support from Sailrite's own sail designer. A Sailrite kit's panels come **computer-cut**, with broadseaming and draft already built into the cut and alignment lines marked along each panel's edges; panels and reinforcement patches are individually labeled, and a narrow, repositionable double-sided tape holds everything together during stitching, with video tutorials available online for the whole process. See [Sailmaking — ordering a Sailrite kit](/construction/sailmaking.md#ordering-a-sailrite-kit-contents-and-process-2008) for the fuller kit-building account.[49] Even after nominally settling on the original plan, the comparison kept being turned over: closer study found the newer sail's shorter boom was not, in the end, that much shorter than the old one's, weakening the case for paying more for it, while the smaller original sail's lower price remained the more persuasive point.[50] Running other designs' (Michalak) dimensions through the same Sailrite calculator turned up a further reason for caution about the tool: the resulting sail area did not always match the figure stated on those plans, and the calculated tack angle sometimes ran high too. Weighing cost directly, a full custom-cut sail runs more than double a Sailrite kit's price — the standing recommendation for Paradox remains the smaller, original sail plan, fitted with a topping lift built exactly as already documented for "Zoë" (see [Furling system — full hardware and rigging recipe](/components/rig/furling.md#full-hardware-and-rigging-recipe)).[51] Once that cost breakdown was clarified, at least one builder who had been weighing a professionally finished sail decided to sew his own kit instead.[52]

## Sail pricing

Commercial Paradox sail prices held fairly steady across nearly a year and across the Atlantic: a first sail (2001, 4.5 oz Dacron, tanbark) was built by **Sails Ect.** in **St. Augustine, FL** (spelled "Sails etc." in earlier correspondence); **Dabbler** quoted for a **5 oz Dacron** sail with bag for "[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)," confirming they had built this exact sail before with success and had followed the "Building Paradox" articles "with great interest"; and a UK order came in at the same price whichever of white or tan was chosen (tan, in that case) and whichever weight (6 oz, in that case). Sewing a sail from materials at home runs well below any of the commercial quotes, and a **Sailrite** kit in white cloth costs less than one in a heavier weight — both comfortably below the commercial quotes, provided the builder already has a decent sewing machine and the time to use it.[15][16][17][18]

The original Sails Ect. sail, two years on, had performed well overall (an owner's estimate: **85-90 percent** satisfaction) but developed a **slight wrinkle** off the wind in light air on some points of sail — uncertain whether a trimming or a cutting issue — and Sails Ect. had taken liberties with the **tack fitting**, an irritant. The recommendation for future buyers: require the sailmaker to build **exactly as drawn and detailed in the plans**.[19][20]

Commercial sailmaking priced out considerably higher in Sweden, where a renowned Stockholm sailmaker's quote for a single Paradox sail ran several times the going US price. The standing answer for anyone weighing a professional quote against sewing their own is the yard-deflection sail-building advice at [construction/sailmaking.md](/construction/sailmaking.md); sewing a sail at home "is not really that complicated."[21][22]

Weighing sailmakers directly against each other, **Dabbler** draws consistently good reports — sailmaker **Hugh Horten** uses Dabbler-built sails on his own sailing canoes, enough of an endorsement that more than one builder has committed to having Dabbler build their next sail.[23][24] Beyond Dabbler, established **UK sailmakers** are also on record as options: **Banks Sails**, **Crusader Sails**, **Jeckells Sails**, **Kemp Sails**, and **Lucas Sails** — the last with a good reputation from a builder who had a junk sail made there some years earlier "at a competitive rate."[25]

A later quote direct from Dabbler, in 5 oz white or a color such as cream or tanbark, included the sailbag and a flat shipping charge, valid for 30 days given oil-driven price movement. Dabbler recalled having made two or three of these "challenging" sails, and separately recalled a photo of one "bellied out on a sand spit in the Bahamas."[26] The quote drew a direct, still-current endorsement from an owner whose own Dabbler-built Mark II sail (see [the Dabbler re-cut: Mark II sail, a decisive improvement](#the-dabbler-re-cut-mark-ii-sail-a-decisive-improvement) below) he remained very pleased with years on; that owner's earlier Mark I sail had by then passed to another builder.[27]

## The Dabbler re-cut: Mark II sail, a decisive improvement

Sending an unsatisfactory original sail to **Dabbler** for a re-cut produced a documented before/after case study. The resulting **Mark II** sail arrived beautifully made, with workmanship judged very impressive, and the fit was exact — including allowing about **6 in** to be trimmed off the end of the boom, which had been [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)'s intention when he drew up the new sail plan (see [construction/sailmaking.md](/construction/sailmaking.md) for his full dimensions and technique).[28]

The performance difference from the old sail was "no comparison": the new sail carries **a lot more draft** and powers up much more quickly, eliminated the tack-to-tack performance asymmetry the old sail had shown, and lets the boat be trimmed in much tighter without stalling — pointing noticeably higher as a result. Dabbler is highly recommended to anyone seeking a quality small-boat sail, especially a lug, spritsail, or Gunter rig, with the caveat that Dabbler is "a little pricey" — a case of getting what you pay for.[28]

Nearly four years after the re-cut, the Mark II sail was still giving full satisfaction — Dabbler's Stu Hopkins was again recommended as highly experienced with the lug sail.[32]

The Mark II sail is **5 oz cloth**, cut to Matt Layden's own sail-dimensions post with as much draft built in as could be carried while still furling neatly for reefing. The Dabbler loft, in Wicomico Church, Virginia, has built every sail for a competitive 17-boat fleet of Nutshell prams there, giving it a working feel for what small, performance-minded sailors actually want. Leech tell-tales, doubted at first, proved themselves in the sail trials — they read well in practice. **Matt Layden's eye for measurement** produced a striking anecdote: sailing together, Matt remarked that a sail looked like it needed about two inches of camber at the top; measuring the yard's bend afterward — hanging a fifty-pound weight at the center while supporting the ends — confirmed the gap was exactly two inches. The technique for checking this: pull a string tight across the yard's ends, then measure from string to yard at the quarter, half, and three-quarter points. Getting this camber measurement right at the yard, rather than guessing, is what the old sail had gotten wrong. The superseded original sail was later resold, still fairly new and only needing the camber and draft corrected.[29][30][31]
