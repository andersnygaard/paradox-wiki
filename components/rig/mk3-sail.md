# Mk3 sail

The later Mk3 sail revision for the Paradox, following on from the Mk2 plan: a **larger, higher-peaked sail** carrying about **15% more area than the original**, drawn to cure the design's sluggishness in light wind without moving its centre of effort.[1]

## Origin: a light-wind complaint, and a bigger sail to answer it

The sail was drawn in 2023 by the sailmakers Really Simple Sails, at the request of an owner who had sailed his Paradox for some time and found her "a bit 'sticky' in lighter wind." The case he made for more area rested on the rig's own reefing: because rolling the sail around the boom reefs it so effectively, the boat "can take a larger sail" — the extra area can simply be rolled away when the wind arrives.[1]

The result was reported to have changed the boat's light-air character outright: with the new sail she "feels more like a dinghy rather than being stuck in the water in light wind like a yacht."[1]

## What changed, and what deliberately did not

Two design decisions define the sail:

- **About 15% more area than the original**, with a **higher-peaked yard** — the peak raised specifically to improve windward performance.[1]
- **The sail's centre held exactly where it was.** The plan was "tweaked to keep the sail centre exactly where it was with the original sail," so the balance the hull was designed around is preserved and the boat's helm is not altered by the change.[1]

That second point is what makes the sail a retrofit rather than a re-rig: the larger sail "will retrofit to most existing Paradoxes."[1]

## Spar changes to carry it

No spar **diameters** change — the length increases are small enough that the existing sections carry the extra sail without restressing.[1] Three lengths change:

| Spar | Change |
|---|---|
| Yard (top spar) | **+100 mm (4″)** |
| Boom | **+50 mm (2″)** |
| Mast | **+150 mm (6″)**, added at the base |

## Reefed sail shape: an anticipated problem that did not appear

The sail's extra fullness raised a concern that rolling it down would leave a deep, uncontrolled "belly" just above the boom when reefed. The proposed remedy was a **lens-shaped piece of yoga-mat foam** — thin at the ends, fatter in the middle, cut to the length of the sail's foot — laid into the first roll, so more sail rolls up at the middle of the boom than at its ends. The technique is borrowed rather than novel: it is the same trick used to stop partly-rolled roller-reefing genoas from losing their shape.[1]

In practice the precaution proved unnecessary — trialling the sail ashore, the shape "seemed OK without it."[1] The foam lens therefore stands as a known fix held in reserve rather than a required part of the sail.
