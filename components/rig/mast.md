# Mast

Where the [Paradox](/paradox.md) mast passes through the deck, the structure is locally reinforced. This beefed-up area is called the **partners**: it distributes the loads that the sail induces around the mast into the deck and hull.

On the Paradox this reinforcement is built together with the **vent trunk** (the boxed structure carrying the mast down and the ventilation through the deck). The topping timber a builder sees on the vent trunk in construction photos is the partners; the **deck ply lays over that timber**, and the mast (and vent) holes are then cut to match.

## Build sequence: decking before or after the vent trunk

- **Plans method:** [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)'s plans call for waiting until **after the deck is on** before cutting the mast/vent holes.
- **Before-decking approach:** the vent trunk can instead be completed **before decking**, reasoning that there is little room to work with the deck off and even less with it on. To do this before decking, a **mock-up of the lower part of the mast** verifies that the control lines will clear the mast and **exit into the cabin at the correct angle** — confirming that the Paradox is sailed with its running rigging led into the enclosed cabin.
- **A second before-decking confirmation:** the same before-decking sequence can be followed by building the mast step and vent trunk (with one side left removable) onto **bulkhead 2** before assembling the hull, so the bulkhead can be set up horizontally on trestles to fit the mast to its step and partners — useful for working indoors without a 20 ft-high roof. The deck's mast hole is then simply cut to match once the deck goes on. See [rig and sail — reading the mast's plan views](/components/rig/index.md#reading-the-masts-plan-views) for more.

A sketch of the vent detail in the [building articles](/references/building-the-paradox-articles.md) shows the detail: `http://www.tomah.com/delliott/paradox3a.jpg`.

## Vent trunk top dimensions, and why exact measurements don't matter much

The plans leave several vent-trunk dimensions vague enough that a builder working strictly to spec can be left unsure of the design intent. The vent trunk's top measures **420 mm by 250 mm wide**, with a tolerance of about **±10 mm** since the dimension "isn't critical." A build may pencil in **72 mm** for the width of the tapered block at the trunk's narrow end (from the transverse section on plan sheet 9), but in practice the block can simply be built up from offcuts and ground to final shape freehand with an angle grinder and sanding disc — consistent with the broader view (see [hull assembly sequence](/construction/hull-assembly-sequence.md#transom-and-bulkhead-4-crown-and-fitting-bevels-at-trial-assembly)) that many Paradox joinery dimensions are better fitted by eye at assembly than pre-cut to a theoretically perfect figure. The vent trunk's side and rear panels can come entirely out of scrap plywood offcuts, needing no additional material.

The vent trunk and mast are best fitted **before assembling the bulkheads to the hull sides**, since there is no longer enough clearance to maneuver the mast once the hull is assembled and the builder must work with everything horizontal instead — consistent with the before-decking build sequence described above, and easing the mast-step/partners fitting work described there as well.

## Attaching the vent trunk top to bulkhead 2

The vent trunk's top simply **butts and glues** to bulkhead 2 at deck level, rather than needing a notch or dado cut into the bulkhead. The top of the box itself is fairly thick — about **1 in** — and can be built up from **several pieces of wood laminated together** to form the curve needed to line up with the top of bulkhead 2, which avoids any further shaping once the piece is made. A separate small cleat, curved to the deck crown and glued on to carry the trunk top, is an alternative worth avoiding: it makes drilling the fairleads for the roller-reefing line and the tack downhaul noticeably harder to get at afterward. The **port cleat** for the vent trunk can end up shifted outboard from the line originally drawn on the bulkhead without being a mistake — it reflects an intentional, if undocumented, adjustment rather than a drafting error; cleats built with roughly **250 mm between cleat sides at deck level**, bonded solid in epoxy, and less splayed than another builder's, are still within the design's tolerance.

On the **starboard side** of the vent box, leaving that side unfixed and instead screwed in place with a sealant allows it to be removed later for access to the box's interior; a permanent strip of wood added to the top of the box on that side both provides a fixing point for the screws and strengthens the box wall where the mast sits only a fraction of an inch away — an alternative to relying on bulkhead 2's own deck for that strength. Building the vent trunk this way, entirely from scrap plywood offcuts and other salvaged material — including offcuts left over from previous projects for the laminated frames, and a piece pinched from elsewhere to complete the rudder — can still finish within about 1–2 mm of the plans' stated dimensions.

The **drain pipe** through the vent trunk uses **1 in PVC pipe** per the plans; epoxy does not bond well to PVC mixing sticks and squeegees, but it does bond to the pipe itself once the surface is prepared: rough the pipe up with **40-grit sandpaper**, then cover both the pipe and the drilled hole with epoxy and a little filler powder. Drilling the hole through the hull needs a very powerful electric drill with a bit extension, taken slowly, letting the drill cool down periodically rather than run continuously. The pipe is installed **after the bottom is fitted**, so it can pass through the bottom sheet and be faired off underneath.

Building a **dummy short bottom section** to test-fit the mast-foot fitting before a mast exists is a workable way to make sense of the mast drawing at the same time as the vent trunk, for a builder working through both together.

## Vent trunk and mast step construction, confirmed again

A newcomer working through the plans for the first time raised the same vent-trunk/mast-step question this file already answers above: the base of the vent trunk, and how the mast sits into it, is confirmed once more to be built up from scrap offcuts and epoxy, leaving a square recess for the mast foot, then trimmed to final shape freehand with an **angle grinder and coarse sanding disc** — the same technique already on record. The recommended sequence is likewise confirmed: build the mast and fit the vent trunk to bulkhead 2 **before** assembling the hull, so the bulkhead can be set up horizontally on trestles for repeated trial fits of the mast step and partners — much easier than lifting the mast in and out of a part-finished hull.[20][21][22]

## No mast-lowering pin on Paradox, unlike Little Cruiser

Some of the [building articles](/references/building-the-paradox-articles.md) describe a pin that can be pulled to lower the mast, which construction photos of most built boats do not appear to show. **The Paradox mast in fact carries no such pin.** Its foot simply sits on a block fastened to the bottom of the hull, held upright by the partners described above; to lower it, the mast is lifted roughly 600 mm straight up and off, then laid down on top of the rolled-up sail and boom.

[Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md), by contrast, does use a mast pin: her mast is supported on the foredeck by two hinged struts, and pulling a pin at the mast's foot lets it fold backward until it rests on the cabin top — a genuine quick-lowering rig suited to ducking under bridges, distinct from Paradox's lift-off arrangement.

## Reading the mast drawing: a recurring point of confusion

The plans' spar drawing, sheet 12, recurs as the single hardest sheet in the whole plan set to interpret at first reading — its mixed-scale convention (full size fore-and-aft and sideways, but scaled along the spar's length) and stray dimension-arrow marks have tripped up more than one first-time builder working the plans page by page. See [building plans — reading the plans: a first-time builder's backlog of questions](/references/building-plans.md#reading-the-plans-a-first-time-builders-backlog-of-questions) for the fuller technical resolution.

The sheet actually carries a drafting error: a set of right-pointing arrow heads sits to the left of, and touching, the labelled centreline, and should simply be painted out with correcting fluid before the rest of the sheet is read. Once removed, the drawing splits cleanly into three views. The centreline and everything to its right is a **half section viewed from the bow** — the dimensions there apply to both the port and starboard sides of the mast. Everything to the left of the centreline (excluding the stray arrows) is a **section viewed from the port side**, showing the mast's taper along its length; the mast's **rear face is straight**, with all the taper cut into the front and sides. At the bottom of this side view sits a **half-section plan view at deck level**. Read this way, the rear (straight) section of the mast is built first, then one side is joined to it to establish the taper, then the front, with the masthead plugs and copper grounding strap fitted before the final side closes the box. The drawing is genuinely to scale, but the **vertical and horizontal scales differ** — so the rear stave's thickness can still be measured straight off the drawing even though it looks disproportionate at first glance; the reading "clicks" once a builder recognizes the mixed-scale convention, though it remains confusing on a first pass even for a builder already familiar with similar mixed-scale drawings from other designs.

The masthead detail on the same sheet shows a **halyard sheave**, but leaves the question of headroom for a masthead light open: the plans' dimensions leave very little room past the sheave and its axle for wiring or a fixture routed fully inside the mast at the top, a constraint that shapes how builders route masthead-light wiring in practice — see [running lights — routing masthead-light wiring outside the mast, below the sheave](/operations/running-lights.md#routing-masthead-light-wiring-outside-the-mast-below-the-sheave).

## Mast dimensions: slenderness as a deliberate weight-and-flex choice

The mast's upper section is noticeably slender compared with a naive expectation of a small boat's spar — a choice made deliberately for weight economy aloft and to let the spar flex and dissipate sudden gust loading rather than resist it rigidly.[23]

## Sourcing a sheave and rejecting a masthead Windex

Where a sheave of the exact size called for (12 mm x 30 mm) cannot be bought, a same-size sheave salvaged from spares has been substituted rather than special-ordering one.[24] A masthead Windex wind-direction indicator is not a natural fit for the mast: commercial models either lack a suitable mounting base or are designed to fit to a masthead VHF aerial, which the mast does not carry if the VHF is a handheld set rather than a fixed one.[24]

## The masthead-light circuit

A masthead light's wiring is commonly run outside the mast past the halyard sheave (see [running lights — routing masthead-light wiring outside the mast, below the sheave](/operations/running-lights.md#routing-masthead-light-wiring-outside-the-mast-below-the-sheave)) and down inside the hollow mast, held off the mast's sides with insulation pipe lagging so it doesn't bang about underway or at anchor; near the mast's foot the wire joins a socket set into the mast wall, so a flex from the battery can simply be plugged in rather than wired through permanently. Running the light from a light sensor rather than a manual switch removes the need for a switch altogether.[25]

## Rainwater ingress at the mast step, and the swelling it causes

The deck's ventilation hole is designed to drain rainwater down past the mast and away through a pipe under the mast's foot. Doing exactly what it is meant to do can still cause a problem: rainwater draining this way keeps the wood at the mast's base damp, and damp wood swells. On one boat the mast jammed solid at its step after several days of rain during a cruise, requiring another hand ashore to help free it the next morning once the wood had dried enough to shrink back; the same fault recurred on the same boat.[26][27] Because of this, a mast step built to fit the mast too snugly is a liability rather than a virtue: it can become almost impossible to extract once the wood has swollen wet, so a step built with a small deliberate clearance — taken up with thin removable wedges between the deck and the mast rather than relied on to be a perfect dry-fit — is the more forgiving choice.[28]

This lift-off simplicity (see [no mast-lowering pin on Paradox, unlike Little Cruiser](#no-mast-lowering-pin-on-paradox-unlike-little-cruiser), above) also makes removing the mast entirely a viable precaution ahead of an oncoming storm, rather than something to attempt only at haul-out.[29]

## Painting versus varnishing

Rainwater working into varnish over time causes slight wood staining and degradation, most noticeably at points that already take chafe wear, such as under the leather sheathing where the boom and sail rub against the mast.[30] Paint is lower-maintenance than varnish and, if a bright colour is chosen, adds real visibility for safety — considerations that favour paint over varnish for spars generally, even though a darker or more saturated paint colour absorbs more solar heat than a lighter one would.[31][32]
