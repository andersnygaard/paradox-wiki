# Yuloh

The **yuloh** is a curved, Chinese-style sculling oar carried aboard the [Paradox](/paradox.md) as standard auxiliary propulsion, diagrammed on page 12 of the plans. Underway or stowed, it rides in a brass-tube retaining loop on the starboard side of the coach roof — one of a pair of deck loops that also holds the lowered mast/rig to port. For that hardware — how each loop holds its load, installing the pipe stowage rails, loop material choices, and storing the rig ashore — see [Deck](/components/deck.md#stowage-loops-for-the-yuloh-and-lowered-rig).

## Where the yuloh is documented in the plans

**Page 12 of the Paradox plans has the yuloh diagram** — the yuloh is not an add-on but part of the design as sold, "an excellent way to propel a micro-cruiser when there is no wind." For more detail than the plans page alone gives, two external how-to sites cover construction and use (`members.tripod.com/simplicityboats/yulohpage.html`) and sculling technique (`geocities.com/Yosemite/Geyser/6383/yuloh2.htm`), complementing rather than duplicating the fuller sculling **technique** material at [sculling and yuloh technique](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md).

## The yuloh as standard auxiliary — and sculling technique (microcruising.com)

The **yuloh** (a curved Chinese-style sculling oar) is the standard engine-free auxiliary carried aboard Matt Layden's boats, retained on deck in the starboard brass tube loop (see [Deck — stowage loops for the yuloh and lowered rig](/components/deck.md#stowage-loops-for-the-yuloh-and-lowered-rig)). The yuloh's value is learned the hard way when an outboard fails during a calm far from shore: a **1,600-lb** [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) cannot be moved easily or very far with just a dinghy paddle, forcing a tow — "we couldn't move our 1600 pound boat easily or very far with just our dinghy paddle." That kind of experience is what convinces owners to carry a yuloh and learn to use it properly. The full sculling **technique** — stroke mechanics, training regimen, and speed/distance data from three Watertribe Challenges — is covered in depth at [sculling and yuloh technique](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md); that concept should be treated as the canonical home for the how-to material and is cross-linked here rather than duplicated.

One notable exception in the Layden lineage: [Elusion](/matt-layden/elusion.md)'s auxiliary is "not the usual sculling oar but instead a stand up paddle."

## Building the yuloh itself: blade curvature, and the pivot bolt/socket

Beyond page 12's diagram, the plans leave some of the yuloh's own construction to the builder's judgment — starting with the **30 mm longitudinal concavity** the plans specify along the blade's length. Read literally this figure is ambiguous (an offset from a straight line spanning the whole yuloh, or only across the blade portion), and the archive records no single authoritative resolution of the wording; builders have instead settled it by choosing a construction method that produces a workable curve without needing to hit an exact number. The clearer reading, reached on a second pass at the same question, is **deflection from the yuloh's own centerline**, not a fixed radius to be matched with a template: because the yuloh is laminated around a fairly arbitrary center and then shaped asymmetrically afterward, with the wood springing back somewhat as it's worked, the finished blade ends up looking quite different from the blank it started as — builders eyeballing noticeably more than 30 mm of deflection have had no problems.

Why curve the blade at all, rather than build it straight (as **Matt Layden**'s own newer yuloh is — see below): the curvature's purpose is to move the blade's **center of rotation** to the other side of its own longitudinal axis — a rationale for the shape, distinct from the various lamination methods builders have used to produce it.

Two lamination approaches are on record, both producing a workable curve: laminating **two planks bent together over a 30 mm-high block**, which simply "works"; and building to the plans' dimensions as drawn, which has likewise given no problems with the finished oar. A simple, repeatable lamination method for hitting a workable curve without a jig: lay the two planks on a flat floor over a **30 mm block of wood** at the point of maximum curvature, then load the free ends with weights until they touch the ground, holding the shape until the glue cures — one build used this method starting from **two Douglas fir 2x4s**, thinned on a radial arm saw, handle end narrowed with a saber saw, then shaped the finished blade with a portable grinder and sanding disc.

The **pivot bolt** — the fitting mounted atop the stern that the yuloh pivots against — has been built at least two ways:

- **Ball-and-socket:** a pivot bolt made from **10 mm stainless steel studding**, with the ball from a spherical bearing epoxied onto the end. The socket is a block of **tufnol**, recessed into the yuloh and drilled out to the ball's diameter, one diameter deep. Both parts "seem to work."
- **Brazed bronze bolt:** filler material **brazed onto the top of a bronze bolt** to build up a ball shape, the bolt itself hardware-bonded into the transom. Built to the plans' design, fitted with the **gas-line cap** specified there, this gave no problems once built — the only difficulty was the builder's own learning curve using it, documented separately in the sea-trials account at [sculling and yuloh technique](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md).

For the **socket end embedded in the yuloh itself**, a builder can also grind an **acorn nut** to a smooth concave surface as a simpler alternative to a machined tufnol block, though the archive does not record a first-hand report of that specific approach in use.

**Keeping the yuloh from jumping off the ball** — a real nuisance while learning the technique — has its own fix: a loop of **bungee cord** holding the yuloh down against the ball, confirmed to work well, with the tradeoff it shares with any positive fixing — it takes a moment to set up and remove, against the near-instant readiness of simply lifting the yuloh onto the ball to start sculling. **Matt Layden**'s own arrangement leaves the yuloh completely unfixed, tradeable in seconds for starting the outboard instead. A technique-only alternative needing no hardware at all: lowering the grip end whenever pausing for a break stops the yuloh jumping off the ball just as reliably, once the habit is learned.[81] Since the blade doubles as a push-pole in the shallows (see [sculling and yuloh technique](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md)), its tip is sometimes armored to withstand being used that way — pushing off a hard bottom or fending off an obstacle.[81]

Builders have not settled the underlying question of which pivot design is best; both the stainless-stud/tufnol-socket and brazed-bronze-ball approaches are reported as trouble-free once built, leaving the choice one of available materials and metalworking skill rather than a known performance difference.

**A standard-rowlock alternative to the plans' pivot bolt** has also been demonstrated, on a non-Paradox 14 ft lugger's own yuloh: the loom leathered where it fits the rowlock, with a leather "button" (three 3 mm layers) screwed to the underside to stop the loom sliding through. Contrary to the expectation that the button should sit against the rowlock's aft face to resist the loom riding up under load, it works best positioned against the **forward** face instead — the loom's own down-and-aft working angle is already enough to prevent riding up, making the button largely unnecessary in practice. This trades the plans' dedicated pivot fitting for hardware many small-boat builders already have on hand, at the cost of the wear and mainsheet-tangling risk any rowlock-mounted yuloh carries (see [sculling and yuloh technique — the rowlock/oar-socket question and caution](/boats/enuf.md#the-yard-revisited-a-strop-and-sheave-halyard-and-finishing-the-boom) for the fuller discussion of that trade-off).

**Matt Layden** has since built himself a **new yuloh** for his own Paradox, substantially different from the original: it is in fact **straight**, and works well but is noticeably harder on the arm and wrist than a curved blade — a tradeoff that also comes up in the fuller build-effort comparison of the two oars at [sculling and yuloh technique](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md#matt-laydens-own-yuloh-speed-and-the-straight-vs-curved-blade-trade-off).

## Blade camber, the "for'd" nomenclature, and pivot socket dimensions

A recurring point of confusion, distinct from the longitudinal curvature question above, is the yuloh's **cross-sectional camber** — which face of the blade is more curved, and which way that face should point in use. Plans sheet 12's "For'd" label (a designer abbreviation for "forward") has no genuinely forward part on a yuloh as such, since the whole assembly pivots and reverses; read against how the blade actually works, "forward" means the face that leads when the blade is driving the boat ahead. The **lower/forward face is the more cambered one**, working the same way an aeroplane wing produces lift: the fluid passing over the longer, more curved face must travel faster than over the shorter, flatter face, and by Bernoulli's principle this produces a pressure difference and a resulting force in that direction — though how much this lift effect actually matters in practice is genuinely uncertain, since some published photos of yulohs in use appear to show the more curved face uppermost/rearward rather than down/forward, suggesting that if the camber's lift contribution were large, such a reversal would likely have been noticed and corrected long since. In use the blade enters the water at roughly a 45-degree angle rather than vertically, so the lower face and the forward face are effectively the same surface.

Worcester's classic reference drawing of a yuloh in use establishes the fuller mechanical picture: the blade's plane sits **"parallel" to the transom**, not perpendicular to it; when working, the blade pushes downward and forward, so the lower/forward face is convex and the upper/after face is flat or concave, with the load resisted by the fulcrum and the handle rope; and the rope's attachment point on the handle must sit **below the blade's own projected axis** (in side view) to work at all — achieved either with a curved handle (as in Worcester's drawing) or an L-shaped one. Because that attachment point is offset from the blade's axis, pulling and pushing the rope twists the blade to drive the boat: a small offset gives a low gear (a low angle of attack, letting a larger blade area produce more drive for the same effort), while a large offset gives a high gear. Worcester's drawing also shows a distinct **S-shaped curve** along the yuloh's length, prompting a separate hypothesis: since the blade's thrust is always perpendicular to its own face, a more vertical blade should in principle be more efficient, making the S-curve possibly suited to very shallow water, where little of a fully vertical blade would actually be submerged.

On the **degree of longitudinal curvature** itself, opinion is split on how much it actually matters: one view holds it doesn't matter much, since a working yuloh can be laminated from two pieces bent over a block as small as **30 mm** high, with more curvature mainly helping the finished oar nest more compactly on deck rather than changing how it sculls; a different, more skeptical view (see [narrower dimensions, below](#building-the-yuloh-itself-blade-curvature-and-the-pivot-boltsocket)) doubts the blade's cross-sectional shape matters significantly either, given how inconsistently the "correct" camber orientation is actually depicted in circulating photographs.

## Ball-joint versus oarlock, and pivot-socket wear over time

The **ball-and-socket pivot** (see above) and a plain **oarlock/rowlock** are mechanically different and not interchangeable: an oarlock suits ordinary sculling, the technique of working a conventional oar back and forth with a twist at each end of the stroke, while a yuloh's action is different enough that a sculler's instinctive rowlock technique does not transfer — one UK builder who tried a fellow owner's yuloh at a boat show, already skilled at plain sculling, "made a hopeless attempt at it," since the ball joint helps drive the specific twisting action yulohing needs, a technique that has to be learned separately even by an experienced sculler.[31][32] A yuloh worked in a plain rowlock instead of a ball joint sees much more wear on the oar than the ball arrangement does, and should be removed from the rowlock when not in use, since it can otherwise be ripped out or, if the rowlock is plastic, broken off.[34]

Over several years of use, a **tufnol-block socket** — a blind hole bored into a tufnol block epoxied into the yuloh's shaft, sized to the ball's diameter — wears measurably: one such socket, in service about four years, had become noticeably worn and had also developed a squeak when not lubricated with water.[34] A **copper-pipe socket** is a tried alternative: a length of 22 mm copper water pipe, sized so the ball is a reasonable fit inside it, bonded into the yuloh's shaft at a second pivot position nearer the handle to test a different gearing; the change in gearing from moving the socket closer to the handle was not reported tested by sailing before the copper-pipe socket itself had seen any real use.[34] Plain copper run directly against a harder metal ball may not hold up as well as a plastic bearing surface; **stainless steel tubing** was floated as a longer-wearing substitute for the copper pipe, untested.[35] If a socket does wear out, the simplest repair is to fill the old hole with epoxy and re-drill it, rather than build a new socket from scratch.[34][36] A fuller version of the same repair, worked out over several seasons of use: bore the worn hole oversized, fill it with epoxy and chopped fibres for strength, then re-drill to fit the ball — good for another season or two before it needs doing again; a new pivot hole is likewise best bored shallow at first, about the depth of the ball's own radius, and deepened later only if needed, a caution drawn from a fellow builder's experience going in too deep from the start.[82] Beyond plain water, both molten candle wax (beeswax expected to do at least as well) and ordinary butter have each cured a squeaking socket and kept it from picking up grit — a no-tools alternative to a harder bearing material when nothing else is at hand.[82][81]

For the socket material itself, **graphite-loaded epoxy** was suggested as a slippery, tough alternative to a machined tufnol or plastic block — easy to repair in place by filling and re-drilling the hole exactly as with a plain-epoxy socket, should it ever wear or get damaged.[38][39][40]

A worked build using the **Delrin/Acetal** candidate raised in that same exchange gives concrete dimensions: two pockets machined directly into the yuloh's shaft with a 0.750 in dia. ball end mill, each 1 in in diameter and 0.625 in deep, each retained by a single #10 brass flathead screw — brass chosen so it would not scratch the steel ball — with about 0.125 in of plastic wear expected before the screw head is reached. The mating 0.750 in steel ball was drilled with a tungsten-carbide bit for a 0.375 in dia. stainless steel lag bolt, then brazed to it and polished, the high polish also intended to help retard rusting. Full construction detail: [boats/ironsides.md — yuloh pivot sockets](/boats/ironsides.md#yuloh-pivot-sockets-machined-delrin-and-the-hulls-fiberglass-finished).[85]

A snugly-fitted ball-and-socket pivot also has to be kept clear of the **mainsheet**: the sheet can drop into the gap between the rudder stock and the transom and catch on the pivot ball or fitting during ordinary sail adjustments, a problem shared with other transom-mounted hardware (see [design/rudder.md — rudder-stock height and mainsheet fouling at the transom notch](/components/mainsheet-outboard-fouling.md#rudder-stock-height-and-mainsheet-fouling-at-the-transom-notch)); a transom-mounted outboard sees the same snagging risk and is best mounted to one side (port, in practice) to keep the sheet clear of it.[37]

Further candidate socket materials were raised in the same exchange. **Lignum vitae** — used on an older cutter's yuloh for both the pin and a socket set into the yuloh's stalk — would work well if a supply can be found (a UK chandlery has been seen stocking rings of it that could be cut down for the purpose); one alternative pairing tried elsewhere is a **stainless steel ball working in a copper cup**, reasoning from the general bearing principle that dissimilar sliding materials (as in a car crankshaft's hard steel journal running in a soft tin/lead bearing) wear better than like-on-like — plain grease is an available but unwelcome lubricant compared to a self-lubricating socket material. **Acetal (Delrin)** is a further candidate, tough and very low-friction and already used as a bearing surface elsewhere, machinable with ordinary woodworking tools (sharper carbide gives a cleaner cut) though costly bought new in custom blocks — a plastics supply house's scrap bin is the recommended source. Where Acetal can't be found, **UHMW (Ultra High Molecular Weight) polyethylene** is nearly as good, if somewhat harder to machine cleanly without very sharp tools and light cutting pressure. A **teflon-coated ball running in a metal socket** was floated as a further low-friction option, along with the more tongue-in-cheek suggestion of sourcing a discarded artificial hip joint as a ready-made ball-and-socket bearing.[41][42][43][44][45][46]

The **rope customarily lashing a Chinese yuloh's handle to the deck**, standard on the full-sized junks and sampans the design descends from, does most of the propulsive work on those larger vessels — the sculler simply pushes the handle back and forth athwartships while the loom's curve and the rope combine to drive the boat. On a boat as small and light as Paradox, that rope is judged **unnecessary**: the boat is light enough that the sculler's own hand weight substitutes for it, a point corroborated independently by more than one Paradox owner. Traditional Chinese junks' characteristically high sterns may be partly explained by the same mechanics, steepening the entry angle of the loom into the water for better efficiency.

The plans' **pivot socket location** — center of the pivot socket **1,350 mm from the top of the shaft, 1,650 mm from the tip of the blade** — has been confirmed correct as drawn. One owner has since drilled a **second socket roughly 100 mm nearer the handle** than the plans' original position, on the theory that the increased gearing this produces (see the Worcester offset discussion above) might allow slightly faster sculling, at the cost of not yet being able to report whether the change actually delivered that benefit.

A later report fills in a further construction detail the plans leave unstated, and confirms a handle-ward relocation along these lines does pay off: the pivot-pin hole should be bored at an angle sufficient to twist the blade to roughly **50–60 degrees from horizontal** by the end of each stroke, and moving the hole a further **5.25 in (about 134 mm) toward the handle end** of the shaft — in the same direction as the untested relocation above — was found to give more power and let the yuloh be worked in a more upright stance; adopted as a modification, it made a real difference in practice.[75][76] Once fitted, the modified yuloh was reported working properly at last — quieter and more pleasant to use than running the outboard.[76][77] Whether that 50-60 degree figure is meant as viewed from aft of the boat (the blade's athwartships swing) or abeam (its fore-and-aft twist through the stroke) was asked directly but left unanswered on record.[79]

The same handle-ward relocation was independently confirmed a year later by the owner who had first tried it untested (see above): after finding the lower gearing gave too little purchase in practice, the pivot hole was moved a total of about 150 mm toward the handle — well past the 100 mm first tried — with no reason, in that owner's view, a yuloh couldn't carry several holes bored for different conditions to suit.[82]

A further Chinese yuloh design, turned up in an independent web search, offers a **range of several pin positions** along the shaft rather than a single fixed hole, raising an open question of whether that adjustability might suit headwinds, tailwinds, or shallow water better than a single fixed pivot point — left unanswered on record.[78]



A separate, non-Paradox Chinese-style yuloh built independently gives a further concrete comparison dataset: **150 mm of curve** end to end (matching the curve drawn on the plans), a **150 mm-wide blade**, flat on the underside/forward face and curved on the top/after edge — a design sourced from **The Mariner's Catalog, volume 3**, and reported to work well with no need for a handle rope.

A full rebuild years later reached the rowlock-versus-ball conclusion independently: an inherited yuloh already showing shaft damage from rowlock friction was rebuilt around a ball-and-socket pivot instead, moved to the boat's starboard side to match the design rather than left in a rowlock.[72][73] That rebuild, and the original pivot arrangement it replaced, are covered in full below.

## A swiveling pivot socket modification, matched to Little Jim's

Beyond the fixed ball-and-socket described above, one socket was modified to **swivel fully side-to-side as well as in the vertical plane**, with the ball's own height increased at the same time — a change made to match a modification already proven on Little Jim's yuloh, whose owner used it throughout a multi-day cruise with Faith and found it perfectly adequate for the whole trip, never once needing the boat's electric outboard, and comfortably outpaced a companion boat's owner who tried to keep pace by paddling instead. See [Faith — two days of force 5 off Bolt Head, and the tacking fix confirmed again](/boats/faith.md#two-days-of-force-5-off-bolt-head-and-the-tacking-fix-confirmed-again) for the cruise this comparison came from.[53]

## An alternative sculling-oar shape: the vertical "scimitar" blade

Distinct from the yuloh's curved, near-horizontal blade, some scullers have instead used oars with the blade held in the **vertical** position, shaped like a curved Arab scimitar. Their chief advantage is dual-purpose: the same blade shape works equally well as a **steering oar**, or can simply be left trailing to **tow obediently** behind the boat when let go — a versatility ordinary sculling oars don't offer. Despite this, the yuloh remains judged the better choice for the Paradox specifically, since its curve lets it stow more compactly on deck than a straight scimitar-bladed oar would.

## Wood for the yuloh

Yuloh blades on record have been built from **Douglas Fir** — the same species used for everything on a wooden hull that isn't marine plywood — and from **clear fir**, with a good piece of **pine** judged to work just as well: the yuloh comes out quite strong once its laminations are glued up, regardless of which softwood is chosen. A single piece of **5/4 Douglas Fir** also works, chosen for grain running straight enough along its length to fit both laminated pieces with little runout even when it runs slightly diagonal across the board's width; most species or grades likely work fine, and the yuloh overall is "a fun little project."

Comparing **both** Little Cruiser's original curved sculling oar and Matt Layden's new, straighter one aboard Matt's own Paradox, the settled comparison favors the original: the extra curve makes the blade scull more automatically, while the newer, straighter oar is more powerful — its blade pitch easier to control — at the cost of noticeably more wrist effort. See [sculling and yuloh technique](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md#matt-laydens-own-yuloh-speed-and-the-straight-vs-curved-blade-trade-off) for the fuller side-by-side comparison of the two oars.

## Steering by yuloh is easier with the rudder raised

Steering under yuloh power is noticeably easier with the **rudder raised** rather than left down — a lesson learned firsthand launching into a cross wind and glancing off every finger pontoon on the downwind side of a marina, leaving green paint along each one. Beyond that, yulohing well is mostly a matter of practice.[50]

## Faith's yuloh, recorded on video

A yuloh's first use on a newly launched Paradox, "Faith," was recorded on video and posted online — describing itself modestly as "a rather boring video," with a rough soundtrack for the first half before skylarks can be heard once the boat is nearly a dot in the distance. There is a lot to film-making, and a lot more practice needed to get the best out of a yuloh. See [sculling and yuloh technique](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md) for the fuller technique record.[51]

The video drew a comparison from a much more practised sculler: the trouble it showed was noticeably less than an early first attempt learning the technique, since using a yuloh eventually becomes automatic enough not to think about — relax, move it back and forth, and it settles into the "falling leaf" pattern by itself. Old habits from years of conventional sculling can still tempt an unneeded twist the yuloh works better without.[52]

## A full rebuild: gearing philosophy, a recovered original pivot, and a stronger stowage bracket

A five-week rebuild of an inherited (previously built) yuloh — Minnow's, dating to her original construction — began with measurement against the plan: she ran about a foot longer overall than specified, all the extra length in the blade, and the blade itself 55 mm wider besides.[54] A general sculling principle follows from blade width alone: the narrower a blade, the faster the stroking action must be to drive a boat at a given speed.[54] Matt Layden's own yulohs sit toward the low-geared end of that trade-off — narrow blades on long shafts — because a narrow blade takes less effort per stroke, at the cost of needing more strokes to cover a given distance; stroked at the same rate, a higher-geared, wider-bladed yuloh covers the distance faster.[54] The rebuild kept the existing, wider 148 mm blade rather than narrowing it to the plan's 94 mm maximum — deliberately higher-geared than Matt Layden's own preference — reasoning that a wide blade could always be narrowed later if it proved too much hard work; the tip was accordingly left uncapped with fibreglass, to leave that option open.[54] The same rebuild relocated the pivot cup 70 mm up the shaft from the plan's position, matching a balance improvement other builders of standard Paradox yulohs had independently reported from the same adjustment.[54]

Where the inherited shaft ran straight and its blade sat at a markedly greater angle to the shaft than the plan shows, curvature was restored by cutting and removing a thin wedge from the shaft and rejoining the parts with epoxy, continuing the curve from blade to handle and letting the finished yuloh nestle against the coachroof on the starboard side.[55] Fitting a pivot cup was judged the trickiest part of the whole job: mounting in a plain rowlock was ruled out from the start, on the strength of the friction-wear the shaft had already suffered that way, so the cap hole was instead drilled into the shaft at an angle to let the yuloh hook onto a pivot pin, with a bungee-tensioned restraining line anticipated to counter the blade's own buoyancy, which otherwise tends to thrust the yuloh upward off the pin.[55] A stainless steel pivot pin was custom-made to order; while waiting for delivery, the plan was to use the pin itself — its rounded end greased so the epoxy would not stick — to mould a matching cup directly in an epoxy base, suspending the pin so no more than half the ball sat submerged, since burying the whole ball would trap the pin in the cured epoxy.[56] Sanding the blade to finish it could not fully remove pitting left by an old fibreglass reinforcement, but still improved both appearance and hydrodynamic shape; the rule restated in the process — the curved, convex face belongs on the blade's forward side, the flat or slightly concave face aft and upper-facing — matches the camber discussion above.[57] A hardwood block for the pivot cap was built next, epoxied both to the shaft and to an old oar-retainer ring reused in the assembly.[58]

Deciding where to mount the pin took real thought. Matt Layden's plan places it on the starboard side, embedded in the aft deck, close enough to the deck's edge that the yuloh cannot foul it; an alternative seen elsewhere — a pin embedded in a wooden block on top of the transom, its upper surface angled away from horizontal to rake the pin aft — has the advantage of keeping the yuloh clear of the deck while holding the pin square to the shaft in its working position.[59] This particular yuloh, as originally built, had in fact been carried in a plain rowlock sliding into a hole in a wooden block on the starboard side of the transom, the hole 300 mm from the centre of the transom's top — an as-built detail otherwise undocumented for this boat.[60] The rebuild fixed a pivot pin permanently into that same original block and bonded it to the transom in the same position, on the reasoning that the pin would work better angled away from and clear of the transom than resting flush against it.[60] A new plywood backing piece was made to carry the pin as a single integral unit, epoxied together ahead of fitting.[61] Forming the cup in the shaft to receive the pinhead was reworked partway through: the first plan — lining a drilled hole with epoxy and setting the greased pinhead directly into it to cure in place — was abandoned out of concern the pin would become trapped, so the cup was instead shaped freehand with a spatula, using the same epoxy to fill shaft abrasions and the gaps around the pin's wooden mounting block.[62] Once the yuloh moved correctly on its support pin, the pin and its block were epoxied and screwed permanently to the transom, and the lower shaft and blade received a first coat of orange paint, ahead of further paint and several coats of varnish still to come on the upper shaft.[63][64]

Securing the finished yuloh on deck departed deliberately from the plan's own brass-tubing loops (see [Deck](/components/deck.md#stowage-loops-for-the-yuloh-and-lowered-rig)), on the judgment that a custom arrangement would be stronger.[65] Candidate fittings considered for the foredeck end included a shop-bought stainless steel curtain/hanger rail, a modified curtain ring, and a spare rowlock converted into a semicircular bracket; the requirement driving the choice was that such fittings must be robust enough to withstand waves washing over the side deck and, in the unlikely event of a full 360-degree capsize, strong enough to keep the yuloh in place.[66] The arrangement finally adopted combined a bought anodized alloy support bracket with a stainless steel u-bolt at the foredeck end, plus a cord or bungee at the blade-end bracket holding the yuloh to the side deck against both wave action and a capsize; the stowage rack was also padded with leather to protect the blade.[65][66][67][69] Whether the whole rebuilt yuloh would even function as intended was left an open question until it was actually tried — "I do not know how much I'll use the device. Indeed, I do not know if it will function as it should"[67] — "the yuloh may work. I shall only know when I give it a go."[69] The finished yuloh's blade and lower shaft were orange, its upper shaft varnished bright and natural, a colour scheme later matched by the boat's own bright-orange hull-name lettering.[68][74] Confirmed complete some months later as part of a wider six-month refit, the reshaped yuloh was fitted together with its new support pin as one of the last jobs on the list.[70]

A yuloh has also been reshaped by a later owner on a different boat: after buying Faith, her second owner reshaped her yuloh to give it more twist, alongside fitting a new higher-peaked sail and relocating the mainsheet lead — changes that together left him using the engine only rarely and gave him three working means of propulsion, sail, engine, and yuloh.[71]

[29] [Paradox builders mailing list](/references/mailing-list.md) message 4228 (william / "barnacleid" / Bill Serjeant, `paradoxbuilders`, 2007-03-11) — "Re: Yuloh - thank you": judges yulohs with more longitudinal curve easier to work, per Al's view that the blade's cross-section doesn't much matter; relays a detailed technical explanation from Simon Fishwick of the smallsailboats Yahoo group, citing Worcester's classic yuloh drawing (ayrs.org/yuloh.gif): the blade's plane is parallel to the transom, not perpendicular; when driving, the blade pushes down and forward so the lower/forward face is convex and the upper/after face flat or concave, resisted by the fulcrum and rope; the rope's attachment point on the handle must sit below the blade's projected axis, achieved by a curved or L-shaped handle; because that attachment is offset, working the rope twists the blade to drive the boat, with the offset amount setting the "gearing" (low offset/low gear = large blade area, low angle of attack, more force for the same effort; large offset = high gear); judges 50mm of offset fairly small unless an L-shaped handle is used, in which case the curve is mainly for stability. Signed "Bill."

[82] [Paradox builders mailing list](/references/mailing-list.md) message 5226 (openboat / "alopenboat" / Al, `paradoxbuilders`, 2008-06-25) — "Re: Alternative auxiliary power...": has moved his own pivot hole about 150mm nearer the handle after finding the lower gearing gave too little purchase, and sees no reason a yuloh couldn't carry several holes for different conditions; advises boring any new pivot hole shallow at first — about the ball's own radius deep — and deepening it later if needed, having seen a fellow builder go in too deep; every socket material he has tried eventually wears and needs repair, so he now bores the worn hole oversized, fills it with epoxy and fibres, then re-drills to fit the ball, good for another season or two before repeating; lubricates the socket with molten candle wax (expects beeswax would do at least as well), which stops the squeak and doesn't pick up grit; restates that the yuloh jumping off the ball is the biggest early problem, curable with a bit of bungee, and that with a Paradox the rudder must be kept up while sculling since steering means swinging the stern sideways — a lesson that cost him an hour and a lot of lost paint on his own first attempt (see [above](#steering-by-yuloh-is-easier-with-the-rudder-raised)); adds that half an hour of frustration is often cured by a rest and a fresh couple of hours' practice rather than persisting without a break.
