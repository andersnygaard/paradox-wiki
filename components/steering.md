# Internal steering — the cabin steering-line loop

The [Paradox](/paradox.md) is steered from inside the cabin by a rope loop rather than by hand on the tiller. The rudder is controlled by a line attached to the tiller that "runs in a continuous loop at the sides of the cabin," carried on "four small pulley blocks at each corner of that loop"; a sailor "can sit anywhere in the cabin and steer the boat," including "all the way forward."[1] This is part of a deliberate whole-boat pattern in which every control is brought inboard: "All control lines lead inside the cabin, including control of the rudder."[2] The result is a boat sailed with the steering line and the mainsheet both "readily to hand" from a single seated position,[3] with the halyard, topping lift and furling line locked off on cleats within the same reach — "all controls are inside."[4]

The system is distinct from, and prior to, the various [self-steering](/operations/self-steering.md) arrangements builders have added: the loop is the boat's primary helm, and the self-steering gear couples *to* it.

## How the loop is rigged

The line is made up as an endless circuit: attached to the tiller, led forward along one side of the cabin, around a block at each of the loop's four corners, and back down the other side.[1] Pulling the side of the loop nearest the hand swings the tiller that way, so the boat is steered exactly as a horse is reined — pull one side and the boat turns toward it.[4]

Two named parts appear on the labeled cutaway diagram published with the design's construction series, "STEERING LOCK" and "STEERING LINE," treated as separate components rather than as one fitting.[1] The lock is covered under [locking the helm off](#locking-the-helm-off) below.

## Where the corner blocks go: the plans against practice

The **forward** blocks are the one part of the loop builders have consistently had to work out for themselves. As drawn by Matt Layden, the forward blocks appear "suspended in mid air right in tight to bulkhead 2," a position at least one builder judged unworkable in the boat as built; mounting them instead **low down on the intermediate deck between bulkheads 1 and 2** gave a reasonable line-up.[7] The simpler recorded solution is to screw the forward blocks **directly to the intermediate deck beam**.[8] The **rear** blocks present no such difficulty and are not reported as a problem by anyone.[7]

No photographs showing other builders' forward-block placement were available at the time the question was asked, which is why the plans' drawn position had to be resolved by trial rather than by copying a known-good installation.[7]

## Knots, handles, and grip for a wet hand

Because the line is small in diameter and is held rather than cleated, grip is a real ergonomic concern. The design's own answer is knots: "There are knots in that line to give a wet hand a grip," and the gripping knots are drawn on the plans.[1] Which knot to tie is **not** specified — the plans show the knots but the construction manual makes no mention of them at all, and a direct request to the builders' list for "a nice looking, functional knot for use on the steering line" drew no recorded answer.[10] The knot is therefore left to the builder.

An alternative to knots is a molded **handle** seized to each side of the loop, one to port and one to starboard, positioned so that each falls comfortably to hand from the seat.[4] A third approach, a **PVC rope gripper** — devised for a different rope-steered boat whose steering system was explicitly copied from Paradox — addresses the same tiring-hand problem, though its own designer allowed it is "probably not any better than a couple strategically placed knots for a boat with a fixed helm position like Paradox."[9]

## Where the loop leaves the hull

The tiller arm passes through an opening in the transom vent baffle, whose function is deliberately combined: it is "the opening for the tiller arm... also for air to enter and for water to exit."[6] The opening's lowest point is set by a 165 mm dimension, and the plywood baffle piece runs 300 mm from the bottom of its curve to its top edge.[6]

The full treatment of that opening — why it exists, whether it is necessary, the drip-box alternative that was considered and rejected, the vinyl tiller "boot" fitted on [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md), bug-sealing with neoprene or roofing membrane, and the proposed second rubber baffle disc — lives at [transom deck plate — the vent baffle](/construction/transom-deck-plate.md#the-vent-baffle-why-the-tiller-rope-opening-exists-and-whether-its-necessary), with the permanent-boot debate at [a proposed permanent tiller boot](/construction/transom-deck-plate.md#a-proposed-permanent-tiller-boot-and-smiths-rebuttal-with-sea-anchor-vs-series-drogue-doctrine) and the simpler disc alternative at [a second rubber baffle disc](/construction/transom-deck-plate.md#a-simpler-alternative-for-the-tiller-opening-a-second-rubber-baffle-disc). It is not repeated here.

Where steering lines pierce a **bulkhead** rather than the transom baffle, individual **neoprene boots** have been made up per line to add water-tightness in the event of flooding — done on [Enigma](/matt-layden/enigma.md), alongside matching boots for the halyard and reef lines, and directly analogous to Little Cruiser's single tiller boot.[11]

## Locking the helm off

In open water the boat is not steered continuously. The technique is to cleat off both the sheet and the rudder line and simply watch the view: "Sailing in open water is a matter of getting it going in the right direction and locking off the sheet and rudder. Then admire the scenery or read a book."[3] A clamp fitted to the **underside of the deck** takes the rope, and once locked the boat "sounds like it's on rails, it doesn't need a lot of control" — a tweak of the steering every five minutes or so is enough to hold a course.[4] See also [first-boat suitability — steering feel](/operations/first-boat-suitability.md#steering-feel-bottom-care-tenders-and-the-head-a-newcomers-questions-answered).

The same lock earns its keep in heavy weather, where it becomes a survival tactic rather than a convenience: under roughly half sail or less in 2–3 m seas and about 25 knots, "By lashing the steering line Small Fry looks after herself allowing me to sleep, eat, take photos," the boat riding out the conditions comfortably provided her bow is kept as much as possible toward the waves.[12] See [heavy-weather tactics](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md) for the fuller doctrine.

The lock is a fitting builders have repeatedly found they need only **after** first sailing the boat: a post-shakedown-cruise task list records the realization that a lock-clamp for the steering line still had to be made.[13] Three worked tiller-lock designs — a two-disc bolt-and-nut clamp, a V-notched plywood clamp with a spring and wingnut, and a single-knob clamp — together with the reasons most builders do not bother with one, are documented at [self-steering — locking the tiller rope](/operations/self-steering.md#locking-the-tiller-rope-and-why-most-builders-dont-bother), and the friction-based light-air case at [light-air self-steering via tiller-rope friction](/operations/self-steering.md#light-air-self-steering-via-tiller-rope-friction).

## Tiller extensions: steering with the legs, or from the aft deck

The steering loop serves the sailor sitting *inside*. A short **tiller extension** covers the cases it does not: steering while sitting on the aft deck with the legs dangling, or while standing, "normally when motoring."[14] It is also useful on the rare occasions the rudder must be put down — and is sometimes preferred to the steering lines even from below.[18] One such extension **folds back under the tiller when not in use**, held by a stainless-steel pin.[16]

The extension exists partly because reaching the steering ropes is awkward in one specific situation: sculling with the yuloh while the rudder is down — needed in wind, in a narrow channel, where the yuloh alone will not hold a course — makes it "a bit of a pain to reach down to the steering ropes."[17] A dedicated tiller above the aft deck would solve this, but at the cost of the flat aft-deck surface used for filleting fish, washing dishes, drying the anchor, and showering.[17]

Opinion divides on whether a full **above-deck tiller** is worth having. Considered and rejected on one boat — "in retrospect, I can't remember any time I would have used it"[14] — it was nonetheless built on another as a **withdrawable** tiller, so the aft deck stays clear when it is stowed.[15] A third proposal reaches the same end differently: replacing the rear window with a single fold-down washboard with a port in it would give better access and "a nice place to sit when sailing during fine weather," where "a removable hinged tiller extension would be all you would need to steer the boat from that position."[19]

Steering lines are sometimes **colour-coded** to be found by eye and hand in a cluttered cabin — purple, on one boat, run down the port side within reach of the seat.[5]

For the tiller itself — laminated ash against bent galvanized conduit, the curve needed to clear the vent baffle and deck beams, and how to template it — see [rudder — tiller construction](/components/rudder.md#tiller-construction-laminated-wood-versus-bent-metal-tube).

## Diagnosing and fixing stiff steering

Stiff steering on a used boat traces most often to the pulleys, not the line: corroded, galvanized pulleys running roughly are enough by themselves to make the helm feel heavy, cured by replacing them with quality ball-bearing blocks — worth doing for more than smoothness alone, since it lets the helmsman actually "feel the helm" and judge whether the boat is balanced. Too much weather helm slows the boat; lee helm is no good for working to windward; a Paradox sails best with a neutral helm.[22] The forward blocks are also worth mounting on cord rather than bolting them rigidly to the bulkhead: cord lets them find their own angle and self-align, so the line runs freely through the bulkhead holes, where a rigidly bolted mount can bind the line and itself be part of the stiffness.[23]

## Tiller-line attachment: an alternative to Matt Layden's own system

Matt Layden's own tiller attachment routes the steering line through holes drilled in the tiller and over a slot at the tiller's end. An alternative — a clove hitch at one end of the line and a round turn with two half hitches at the other — has been adopted instead, judged to give two advantages over Matt's system: it is quicker to tension or slacken the line, and quicker to release it in an emergency.[23]

## A rubber or vinyl baffle for the tiller port in rough water

Where the tiller passes out through the transom's [vent baffle](/components/vent-baffle.md), the built-in baffle is not necessarily enough on its own for the worst conditions; fitting an added rubber or vinyl baffle over the tiller port specifically, before going to sea, is recommended as a rough-water precaution. See [vent baffle — a rubber or vinyl baffle for genuinely rough water](/components/vent-baffle.md#a-rubber-or-vinyl-baffle-for-genuinely-rough-water) for the fuller treatment.[24]

## Coupling an autopilot to the steering line

Because the helm is a rope rather than a bar, an electronic tillerpilot is coupled to the **line**, not to the tiller. One installation ties roughly 10-inch cords to the port steering line with **rolling hitches**, the unit mounted on the port upper storage rack beneath the tiller line with its steering rod facing the bow.[20] An independently developed system using the same rolling-hitch principle, with photographs, is documented at [self-steering — photographs of the autopilot coupling](/operations/self-steering.md#photographs-of-the-autopilot-coupling-and-further-tiller-lock-variants); the two are separate implementations of the same idea. The question of whether a tillerpilot should instead connect directly to the tiller is taken up in the same concept.

## What the loop is actually for

The system's point is not merely that the tiller is remote, but that the whole boat can be sailed from a bunk in shelter. Seen from alongside, it reads plainly enough: "There's a line circling the living area on blocks, pull on it and the rudder moves. Steering from bed. Sounds ideal to me!"[21]
