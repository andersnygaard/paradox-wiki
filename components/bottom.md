# Bottom

The Paradox's flat plywood bottom panel: fitting and fastening it to the hull, sealing it in epoxy, and painting it for antifouling protection.

## Material and the plywood cutting schedule

The bottom is cut from the heaviest plywood in the boat: two sheets of 18 mm marine ply supply the bottom, together with the rudder case and bulkhead #3, from the same cutting schedule.[1]

## Joining and fitting the bottom panel

The bottom is built up from two large, heavy pieces of plywood joined into one panel before it goes onto the hull. One build joined its two pieces indoors, on a cleared living-room floor protected with plastic sheeting, choosing a dry 48-hour weather window for the epoxy to cure undisturbed; once cured, the joined panel was lifted onto its side, moved out of the house on three rollers, and levered directly onto the upturned hull on its trolley — accomplished single-handed.[2]

## The flat bottom's effect on rolling and beaching

A flat bottom is heavy for the boat's length but "lessens her tendency to roll — especially when sailing downwind, unlike narrow yachts with bulbous keels."[3] Sailed downwind, the same flat bottom, together with the [chine runners](/components/chine-runners.md), is credited with hardly any roll at all.[4] The same construction that resists rolling underway is built specifically so the boat can dry out and sit upright on a beach without external support, rather than merely surviving an accidental grounding.[4][5]
