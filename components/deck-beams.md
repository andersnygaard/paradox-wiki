# Deck and cabin beams

The deck and cabin top are carried on beams laminated from thin plywood strips, bent and glued over a shop-built curved jig rather than sawn to a curve from solid stock. [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)'s plans call the standard deck beam a "typical" beam, "because all 7 deck beams are identical, except for the length of each, which is determined by its fore and aft station."[3]

## Building the jig

The jig's curvature is taken from the plan sheet detailing bulkhead #2: 12 measurement stations of 10 mm each, six either side of the vertical centreline, along a horizontal reference line.[3] The jig itself is built from two curved pieces of 16 mm building board bonded together for a combined 32 mm thickness, forming the underside curve of the beam; a third piece, bonded to the others and shaped to an identical curve overlapping the first by 45 mm, backs the beam's forward side, its edge finishing level with the beam's uppermost surface once clamped — a 7 mm overlap against the 25 mm laminated battens.[1][3]

## Lamination

Each beam is 25 mm in the fore-and-aft dimension, laminated from five strips of 5 mm plywood cut 25 mm x 1220 mm — with at least 2 mm allowed extra per strip for jigsaw kerf loss and finishing — for eight glued surfaces per beam across the full set of seven.[1] Cutting all seven beams' worth of battens with a jigsaw is straightforward; a bench saw is judged unnecessary, if possibly quicker, and any slight irregularities plane off easily afterward.[2]

Laminating one beam takes about 20 minutes in good working conditions: the jig is covered with a taped-down bin bag so the beam won't stick to it, slightly thickened epoxy is brushed onto each batten in turn with a cheap paintbrush (no separate gluing platform needed), each batten is bent around the jig and held temporarily by two large upright screws at each end while the jig lies flat on the bench, and once all the strips are in place, five clamps are tightened just enough to seat them — over-tightening starves the joint of epoxy.[4] A curing time of 24 hours on the jig, rather than the 48 hours first assumed, is enough for most builders — doubling the production rate to about three beams a week, finishing all seven "in just over a fortnight."[1][4] All seven deck beams were in fact completed within about three weeks of the jig going together.[5]

## Cabin and hatch beams

The cabin and hatch beams are trickier than the plain deck beams, because their batten widths vary from beam to beam and the forward and aft battens are angled differently to support the Perspex windows.[6] Worked dimensions from one build: the cabin-roof support beams are all 22 mm thick vertically; the forward beam, supporting the front window, uses battens 52 mm wide, while the beam at the rear of the cabin needs battens 74 mm wide; "beam number two," supporting the deck under the front of the hatch, uses battens 26 mm wide, with the beam above it (and the deck) at 20 mm but capped with a 26 mm piece giving a 6 mm forward overhang — a seat for the foam weather strip that stops water entering when the hatch is closed.[6]
