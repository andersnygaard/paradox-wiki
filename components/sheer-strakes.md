# Sheer strakes

The topmost side planks on the Paradox's hull, running up to the sheer.

## Where this is covered

- [Hull planking](/construction/hull-planking.md) — bending and fastening the plywood side panels, including the sheer strakes, onto the hull.

## Sheer clamp notch near the stern

The **sheer clamp is notched roughly 500 mm from the stern** on the plans (visible on sheet #3), rather than running the sheer's full length as built on some boats. One reading of the cutout is that it gives clearance for the **steering-line lead blocks**, which run at sheer height along that stretch of hull, though the plans are not conclusive on the point.[1] A second, competing reading of the same drawing takes the cutout as simply a **"removed for clarity" drafting convention**, matching how the deck itself is drawn cut away on sheet 3 so the structure underneath is visible — on this reading, the top view on sheet 3 and the hidden-detail view on sheet 4 both show the chine logs running continuous and unnotched. In practice the structure is sturdy enough either way that whether the sheer clamp is actually notched at that point makes little difference.[2]

## Bevel the sheer strake and chine log after fitting, not before

The plans' bevels on the sheer strakes and chine logs — where the top surface of the sheer and the bottom surface of the chine ultimately need to fair into the deck and bottom — are best cut **after** the strake or chine log is glued into the hull, not beforehand. Shaping and planing a long, unwieldy piece of lumber while it is still loose is harder to clamp securely and harder to get right than working it in place once it no longer needs holding for the cut. This confirms what the plans imply without stating outright: the bevel angle is not constant, it varies slightly along the strake or chine's whole length, so a bevel cut to a single fixed angle off the boat would need re-fairing anyway once installed. Not every idea on the plans will match a given builder's own experience or common sense, but the design's big underlying ideas are what count.[3][4]

Installed correctly to the plans, the chine and deck lines are already perfectly defined along their full length before any beveling starts — leaving only a small amount of timber to plane away from the strake or chine's external corners, working carefully inward from the corner without cutting past those control edges.[5][6]
