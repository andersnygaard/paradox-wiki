# Vent box

The ventilation box (vent trunk) that ventilates the Paradox's closed cabin while the boat is sailed hatch-shut. The cabin's normal ventilation is good enough to be comfortable even shut up tight in bad weather, with no separate stove-exhaust vent found necessary; on cold nights, stuffing a towel into the vent box is a simple way to cut the airflow down and stay warmer. See [Alastair](/people/alastair-law.md#construction-contributions) for the fuller cabin-ventilation answer this is drawn from.

## Solar-powered ventilation

A solar-powered vent fan — self-contained, needing no battery or wiring of its own — is a useful add-on for extra airflow, particularly with the cookstove running.[1] A Paradox-sized Enigma-style build mounts one on the foredeck in place of the stock vent trunk described above, joking that solar vents do little for a warm night once it's moonlight rather than sunlight doing the work.[2]

## Design rationale: ventilation for the Bahamas, and cold-climate adaptations

The vent box exists chiefly because [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md) designed the Paradox for the Bahamas, generally much warmer than a temperate cruising ground, and wanted a steady supply of fresh air through the cabin.[3] Air enters through a large circular opening in the foredeck and is drawn into the cabin from there; a second, inspection-cum-vent hatch in the transom encourages the flow, so that with its cover removed air passes from the front of the cabin, through the lazarette, and out the stern — a path chosen because the plans originally sited a gimballed cooker in the lazarette, needing somewhere for fumes and heat to go.[3] In a colder climate the aft vent is opened far less often than in the Bahamas, since keeping the cabin warm outweighs the wish for airflow; one cold-climate owner reports fitting a small charcoal stove for cabin heating instead.[3] Where the transom vent cover is made of Perspex rather than the stock design's opaque plastic inspection hatch, repeated tightening of its retaining nut can crack the Perspex around the pin hole; a plywood replacement solves it, at the cost of the light a clear cover otherwise lets into the lazarette.[3]

## Internal fittings: furling-line and topping-lift blocks

Inside the vent box, blocks for the furling line and the topping lift are best mounted one behind the other rather than side by side, since a side-by-side pair leaves too little room for the mast itself to pass through on the way to its step.[4] Small screws alone are not adequate to retain these blocks against the working loads they see: one such block pulled clean away from the bulkhead, left attached "only by the grace of God," and both blocks are better refitted on stainless bolts, washers, and nuts through the bulkhead instead.[4][5]

## A vent-box leak, diagnosed across a multi-post repair sequence

A leak reported, but not pinpointed, by a previous owner as being "somewhere in the region of vent box where the mast is stepped" set the priority for a subsequent refit: find where water is entering the boat, and stop it.[6] The mast itself can complicate the search — on at least one hull its cross-section did not match the moulded socket at the bottom of the vent box, and a wad of epoxied woven roving blocked full insertion; wedging the mast rather than fitting it perfectly is the recommended cure, since a too-perfect fit lets a wet, swollen mast become almost impossible to extract.[7]

Finding the actual leak took a sequence of exploratory repairs, described by their own author as a series of "operations." The first cleared a lump of epoxied woven roving obstructing the mast step's recess, then sealed the drainpipe there with epoxy and epoxy putty.[8] The second opened the bilge around the block the drainpipe passes through, where a tangled, spike-covered mass of resin-saturated woven roving — meant to keep water out of the forward locker but instead letting it in around the drainpipe itself — was chiselled out and replaced with epoxy and epoxy putty.[8] A third "operation," on the vent box's own throat, sewed together a split in the fibreglass with new cloth to stop water entering the vent from above deck reaching the cabin or bilge.[9] A fourth and final step probed the drain where it exits the vent box through the bottom of the hull: scraping the joint with a penknife blade — a self-described "biopsy" — found no fractures or open crevices between the epoxied roving, the plastic pipe, and the plywood, and the area was coated with epoxy anyway as a precaution.[10] The eventual verdict was necessarily provisional: "In theory, the boat should no longer leak. In practice we shall see."[10]
