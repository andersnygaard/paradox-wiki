# Hatch

The Paradox's cabin is closed by a single sliding hatch rather than the two-part hatch-and-door arrangement found on most small boats, and it is built to seal tightly enough that a shut boat has been compared to a submarine.

## No separate hatch door

Hatches on most boats are two-part affairs: a fixed hatch opening plus a removable hatch door, and the door is always the vulnerable part[1]. Paradox has no hatch door at all — it is eliminated from the design as undesirable, one of a deliberate list of features the design does without[1][3]. With the single sliding hatch shut, the boat can still be steered and all controls worked from inside; ducking below and slamming the watertight hatch closed can be done without moving more than a foot[2][3].

## Three-part sealing system

The hatch's sealing is handled in three places. Inside the top of the hatch, a thick strip of EVA (a spongy foam plastic) rides compressed against the side rails[1]. At the front of the hatch there are two separate seals: one sandwiched between the hatch edge and a beam on the hull, and a vinyl sweep-type seal to shed fast-moving water coming over the bow onto the cabin top[1]. At the rear, the cabin top carries a traversing beam, and the side rails allow for a little vertical movement there; when the hatch is pulled back it lifts slightly over the traversing beam and then drops down behind it, sealing the rear of the hatch[1]. The hatch seals tighter as pressure increases against it[1]. With all three seals engaged and the hatch shut, "Paradox looks like a submarine"[1].

## Scuppers in the side rails

Each side rail carries a scupper to drain water that might otherwise stand in the hatch's sliding slot[1].

## Sizing the hatch to your own hull

The plans cannot give a fixed width for the hatch, because that dimension is not controllable in advance — every hull will vary a little[1]. The width is instead taken off the boat as it is being built: first the outer rail is mounted temporarily and checked for parallel, then the inner rails are mounted with the correct slot left between the two, then the beams that will attach to the hatch are cut to clear the inner rails and positioned on the cabin top between the inner rails. A scale laid over the top curve of the hatch beam gives the true curved distance out to where the hatch edge should sit, next to the outer rail — that measured distance, taken in the flat, is the width to cut the plywood hatch top to[1].

## Keeping the top rails parallel

When the window side frames go in, the top rails that the hatch slides on must be checked for parallel so the hatch will run in its groove; they flex a little once fitted, but not much, and once every piece is in place the cabin structure becomes rigid[1]. If the side panels have already been glued up rigid and the rails have drifted out of parallel, a wedge or bar clamp is used against the structure to bring them back before the cabin roof beams go in[1].

## Building the hatch panel

The hatch is built by first gluing "L"-shaped legs, sized to ride in the rail slots, onto a flat plywood panel and letting the glue cure for a couple of days[1]. The panel is then clamped onto those legs and bent into a curve, and fiberglass cloth is applied inside and out while it is held in that shape[1]. The legs take real load during glassing — enough pressure that they did not break off — even though the epoxy bonding them to the panel holds "only by a 1/4-inch area," a demonstration of how strong an epoxy bond can be[1]. Beams are then attached to the hatch top panel so that it runs smoothly in the slot, and the remaining beams are added to the cabin top and glassed per the plans[1].

## Finishing: EVA, handle, and remaining seals

The EVA foam and the inside pull handle are installed before painting, but the remainder of the seals are deliberately left off until after the hull is painted[1].

## Access is a climb, not a step

Getting into Paradox's interior is not like stepping into an open day-sailer's accessible cockpit — access is a climb up and into the interior, "more like climbing into the cockpit of a jet fighter"[1]. Because of this, all interior work must be completed before the cabin structure goes on; once the cabin is closed in, that access is all there is[1].

## Steering from below, and the case for a port in the hatch

The hatch is what makes the boat's **below-decks helm station** habitable, and the earliest dated account of that station in use — the December 2001 [Cedar Key to Honeymoon Island sea trial](/boats/zoe.md#sea-trials-route-cedar-key-to-clearwater) — both endorses it and identifies the one thing it lacks. Running before a sea of about three feet that was rolling onto the quarter and sending spray over the hatch, the seat was folded to its stowed position, a cushion arranged to port, and the hatch closed; steering from inside at a steady 5.5 kt, completely protected from the elements, was judged "simply delightful."[4]

The single wanted addition was a **port in the hatch**, so the sail could be seen and trimmed properly while steering from below — and space had already been deliberately left between the end of the hatch and the solar panel to allow for it.[4] That port was subsequently built as a **skylight**, and proved itself on a later Cumberland Sound cruise, where it allowed the sail to be watched for trim without going on deck and, once the boat heeled, gave a view to leeward without stooping to the leeward windows; see ["Zoë" — the skylight and chart table in practical use](/boats/zoe.md#the-skylight-and-chart-table-in-practical-use-a-cumberland-sound-cruise). The requirement, in other words, was identified from the helm on the design's first extended cruise and answered by a modification that is now common on the type.

## Condition assessment of an eight-year-old hatch

Inspecting a secondhand Paradox's hatch after eight years of service is a useful check on how the sealing system in "Three-part sealing system" above holds up in the long run — and on how well a given hatch was built to begin with. One such hatch, removed for a full rebuild, "had not been built according to plan," with a rough finish throughout; the remedial list ran to replacing the roving on its forward part, redoing both the internal and external seals, and reseating the copper sliders top and bottom.[5] The verdict on the boat as bought was blunt: "When I first looked at the boat, I thought quite a bit of work would be required to make her hatch satisfactory, and I wasn't wrong."[6] Rebuilding to a standard judged "virtually watertight, even in the worst conditions when waves may break over the boat" added an external baffle at the front end (made from a household draught excluder), cramped the foam insulation under the lid to the lid itself with plastic edging strips to stop it snagging the hatch-gate baffle on every opening and closing, and relied on drain holes either side plus the small gaps at front and back between the outer retainer frames and the hatch side seals to shed whatever rain and spray still gets in.[6][7] A separate baffle glued to the forward end of the hatchway's arched beam, plus foam strips under the port and starboard hatch sides, stops water entering the cabin when the boat heels — built, on this account, "according to Matt Layden's Paradox drawings," since in heavy seas a wave can break over the cabin from forward.[8]

## A named sealant comparison

Refitting a hatch window, one owner weighed a well-known marine sealant, Geocel, against a cheaper hardware-store product, Ever Build's "General Purpose Sealant," and chose the latter at roughly half the cost after checking its technical data sheet confirmed it adequate for the job — sealing only the window's outer and inner perimeters plus the nut and bolt holes, leaving the cavity between the two seals unsealed but protected from ingress, and using the same product for the cabin windows too.[9]

## A slot-top cabin: the self-righting trade-off

An open **slot-top cabin** — cutting the cabin roof back to a slot, with a lift-out or removable rear window, for easier access to the mast and bow and more standing room — has been floated more than once as a departure from the stock closed cabin, but it carries a real structural trade-off rather than being a simple upgrade.

The case against: an open slot-top and washboard opening meaningfully reduces the boat's **self-righting characteristic**, inviting water to rush in on a knockdown and potentially leaving her floating low even after righting herself, buoyant foam notwithstanding — illustrated by precedent from other small craft rather than Paradox herself, including a 22 ft catamaran knocked down by a sudden, freak gust on an otherwise calm afternoon, and a small sailing cruiser and, separately, a racing yacht each sinking within minutes of a similar knockdown after taking on water through an open cockpit and cabin.[10]

A counterpoint drawn from an actual Paradox knockdown complicates the case for keeping the cabin fully closed at all costs: water did not start coming inside until the mast was nearly horizontal in the water, and wind alone was judged unable to cause a full knockdown without wave action assisting it; once the wind eased, the boat popped back upright almost immediately, limiting water ingress to little more than a wet sleeve (see [heavy-weather tactics — securing the crew during a knockdown](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#securing-the-crew-during-a-knockdown) for the incident in full). A slot-top could work if a soft cover sealed reasonably well — the RNLI's own closely fitting plasticised-fabric deck-locker covers, held on by hooks and bungee, are cited as a precedent — but weighed against that is a bigger reservation: an open, fabric-covered slot sacrifices the stock cabin's theft security, which as designed keeps the boat's contents safe from all but the most determined intruder.[11]

A practical way to test a slot-top or any other cabin-roof modification without committing to it irreversibly: **bolt the cabin roof on rather than glue and nail it**, so the change can be tried and, if it doesn't work out, reversed back to the stock design.[12]

## Fabric roll-top versus the stock hard hatch: security, headroom, and knockdown risk

A **fabric roll-top** — a strong, waterproof fabric held over a lip by bungee cord and clips, closing well enough for ordinary rain and folding back easily in fine weather, backed for genuinely bad weather by a ply or plastic board bolted in under or over the fabric — is a recurring alternative to the stock sliding hatch, valued mainly for its simplicity rather than for any advantage in security.

**Headroom.** Whether standing headroom and a closed, weatherproof top have to be treated as mutually exclusive was raised directly: could the top simply be built permanently a little higher than the plans specify, clearing the boom, so the seat position always has headroom regardless of whether the top is open or shut?[13][14] That question fed into a broader conversation drawing on precedents from trailer-sailer pop-tops (a hinged, scissor-propped roof raised at anchor) and tent design. Ideas floated included hinging a rigid top at the forward end with fabric sides, removable as a unit — dropped once it became clear that lifting it far enough to stand would put it at roughly 45 degrees and foul the boom;[15] and a **hooped fabric cover** built like a dome tent, using permanently-sleeved bendy rods stuck into angled sockets at each end so the hoops alone would hold it braced, extending a little beyond the hatch opening at both ends to keep a seated sailor dry even with the ends left open — potentially usable underway as well as at anchor, and not needing to be very tall if kept to the minimum headroom required.[16] A separate suggestion wondered whether a plexiglass dome insert could be fitted into the existing hatch opening instead, and whether there would be enough clearance for one under the boom;[17] one such dome, salvaged from a charity money box, would solve the see-through problem but not the underlying need for some form of sliding roof beneath it.[18] Measured against a Paradox hatch's own dimensions, a dome clears the boom without difficulty, though a sailor's head sits close enough to the aft edge of the opening that a dome needs to be somewhat truncated there, with getting in or out meaning sliding forward and down before ducking out; after three full seasons sailed with the stock hatch instead, its perceived shortcomings had not actually shown up in practice, leaving only one modification judged genuinely worth the effort — fitting **proper hatch runners**, which in practice proved hard to source ready-made, with ordinary U-section curtain rail floated as an adaptable fallback.[19][20][21]

**Security.** An already-practiced improvised solution to the sun/rain problem, without building anything new: an ordinary umbrella clamped to the back of the sliding hatch, adjustable for height and for fore/aft and lateral position by sliding the hatch itself, serving at anchor for shelter while still sitting in the open air and doing double duty going ashore in the rain — several builders have similarly used ordinary golfing umbrellas as an improvised cuddy when stopping for a brew-up aboard open boats.[22][23] Weighed against any soft-top scheme, though, the stock **hard hatch** is preferred by builders who value the security it gives locked shut and unattended — a preference sharpened whenever a soft top is compared directly against how securely a stock hatch can be left unattended, for instance by a canal towpath with a padlock through the slider.[24]

**Knockdown risk.** The sharpest test of the debate is what happens if the boat is knocked down with a soft top rather than the stock hatch's own strength between the crew and the sea: a sliding hatch's real advantage over a fabric or hinged one is being **slammable shut in an instant** if a knockdown is developing.[25] Firsthand experience complicates even that argument, though — in one Paradox knockdown, the boat went from about 25 degrees of heel to 90 degrees in roughly three seconds and was back upright and sailing again within ten, too fast for a slam-shut reaction to matter either way; the harness strap's own gap in the closed hatch let in enough water to soak a sleeve as a hand was put under the flow to brace, but not enough depth reached the bilge to be worth pumping, and the whole event was over in a few seconds — water that would likely have stayed in the "nuisance" rather than "danger" range even had the hatch been open throughout, given how briefly the boat was actually knocked down.[26][27] The greater worry with a fabric or dome top, on this account, is not reaction time but the hatch **lifting under the wave itself** as the boat goes over, admitting a significant volume of water unless somehow fastened down — a concern that, the more it was weighed, left the stock sliding hatch looking like the safer of the two designs, whose own chief weakness is simply that its runners eventually wear smooth.[26][28]

Matt Layden's own report of being tipped out of an open hatch by a wave, the boat still righting itself regardless, is remembered as happening aboard one of his early boats — "Swamp Thing" or "Gjac," accounts differ — and, elsewhere, recalled not as a Paradox incident at all but as the trigger for a design progression.[29][30][31][32]

## A folding hatch, and a combined sliding-and-folding design

**A folding hatch.** An independently drawn folding-hatch concept, posted as a photograph rather than built,[33] uses steel hinges on the underside of each panel for a rigid connection, a watertight flexible seal running along the top of the folding lines, and simple elastic bands sealing the hatch both inside (while sailing) and outside (combined with a lock, when the boat is left) — opening or closing fully in a matter of seconds, offered as an alternative to the sliding hatch rather than a replacement for it.[34] Its chief appeal over a plain fabric or dome top is being able to leave it propped half-open — a position used often enough (sailing with just the head sticking out) that a hatch unable to hold it is a real objection to the alternatives above; the number of folding stages can be varied to give a half-open as well as a fully open or closed position, with the dome itself built from narrow welded steel sections glazed with Lexan or plexiglass to preserve forward visibility.[35][28] Weighed against the stock sliding hatch, a folding hatch was judged easier to build but possibly less watertight in a rollover, against a sliding hatch that is proven but prone to trouble once its own mechanism wears smooth.[28]

**A sliding/folding hybrid: the "Griffiths hatch" adaptation.** A third concept adapts the "Griffiths hatch" (a sliding-hatch design published in *Practical Sailor*) by replacing its front hinge with a block of wood on each side of the top cover, riding in loose guide-tracks along the coach-roof. With the hatch closed, the blocks sit at the rear of their tracks and the track geometry locks the hatch's front edge down; lifting the hatch frees the blocks to slide forward, letting the whole hatch travel like an ordinary sliding hatch. Leaving the tracks' front ends open lets the hatch be lifted off entirely and remounted on vertical bars above the opening at anchor; with the hatch partly raised and a washboard in place, a sailor can sit on the rear deck with the raised hatch acting as a windscreen.[36] The idea was judged to have "cracked it" — some combination of the sliding-track hatch and the folding hatch above was thought likely to deliver both good headroom and a secure fit, though a full-size mock-up and a check against mast and boom clearance is the only way to confirm any of these alternatives actually work before committing plywood to one.[37]

With the folding-versus-sliding debate otherwise fully aired, the closing view was that the choice needn't be either/or: nothing rules out building both designs, and a builder undecided between them should simply build whichever is preferred and try the other later if not satisfied.[38]

## A removable roof and hatch, proposed but not built

A speculative modification for opening up the cabin to sun and fresh air for two occupants at once, without abandoning the enclosed design for longer passages: lift the whole roof and hatch off together as one unit and stow it inside the boat, tilted to fit through the cabin opening and then secured upright against the side. The new roof would be fastened down with bolts and wing nuts against a compressible rubber seal, keeping the rest of the roof and hatch to the original plan; no detailed sketches or scaled drawings were produced. With the roof removed, the boat would suit fairweather day sailing or motoring/yulohing in a calm rather than longer cruising, and might be left at the dock for such outings; at anchor, the roof could instead be raised above the cabin on four posts (as already done for a sunshade on "[Zero](/boats/aussie-paradox.md)"), giving a little extra headroom for eating under cover. For longer single-handed cruises the roof would go back on and be secured down, restoring the boat to its designed watertight enclosure. The idea remains unbuilt and untested; the closest built precedent for a two-part opening cabin is "[Ladikan](/boats/ladikan.md)"'s sliding aft cabin section, though that boat's open back window was noted as vulnerable to a rogue wave in a way the lift-off roof proposal, sealed shut for passages, would not be.[39]

A separate, more modest alternative to the lift-off roof would rebuild the cabin front as a flat plywood panel fitted with a couple of commercial opening ports, judged worth the occasional splash and reduced forward visibility for the ventilation gained over the stock vent trunk, especially at anchor; the same builder would also reduce the side windows to keep the cabin cooler.[40] A canvas top in place of the plywood roof and hatch was independently considered and rejected on the same safety reasoning: it would considerably reduce survivability in a knockdown compared with a solid roof, even though it would still outperform most other boats of similar size; a hard top kept for serious cruising and swapped for a soft top on social, sheltered-water outings was floated as a middle course, since where the boat is actually used matters more than an across-the-board answer.[41] Two-up cruising was judged fundamentally a matter of how much comfort both people are prepared to trade for excitement, with the choice of crew mattering more than the choice of boat; a Paradox is well suited to taking a small child or grandchild sailing, once old enough.[40] A further, unbuilt idea floated flipping the cabin top over to serve as the footwell, but drew no engineering answer beyond a pointer to a separate small-sailboat modification forum for general ideas.[42][43]
