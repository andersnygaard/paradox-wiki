# Hatch

The Paradox's cabin is closed by a single sliding hatch rather than the two-part hatch-and-door arrangement found on most small boats, and it is built to seal tightly enough that a shut boat has been compared to a submarine.

## No separate hatch door

Hatches on most boats are two-part affairs: a fixed hatch opening plus a removable hatch door, and the door is always the vulnerable part[1]. Paradox has no hatch door at all — it is eliminated from the design as undesirable, one of a deliberate list of features the design does without[1][3]. With the single sliding hatch shut, the boat can still be steered and all controls worked from inside; ducking below and slamming the watertight hatch closed can be done without moving more than a foot[2][3].

## Three-part sealing system

The hatch's sealing is handled in three places. Inside the top of the hatch, a thick strip of EVA (a spongy foam plastic) rides compressed against the side rails[1]. At the front of the hatch there are two separate seals: one sandwiched between the hatch edge and a beam on the hull, and a vinyl sweep-type seal to shed fast-moving water coming over the bow onto the cabin top[1]. At the rear, the cabin top carries a traversing beam, and the side rails allow for a little vertical movement there; when the hatch is pulled back it lifts slightly over the traversing beam and then drops down behind it, sealing the rear of the hatch[1]. The hatch seals tighter as pressure increases against it[1]. With all three seals engaged and the hatch shut, "Paradox looks like a submarine"[1].

## Scuppers in the side rails

Each side rail carries a scupper to drain water that might otherwise stand in the hatch's sliding slot[1].

## Sizing the hatch to your own hull

The plans cannot give a fixed width for the hatch, because that dimension is not controllable in advance — every hull will vary a little[1]. The width is instead taken off the boat as it is being built: first the outer rail is mounted temporarily and checked for parallel, then the inner rails are mounted with the correct slot left between the two, then the beams that will attach to the hatch are cut to clear the inner rails and positioned on the cabin top between the inner rails. A scale laid over the top curve of the hatch beam gives the true curved distance out to where the hatch edge should sit, next to the outer rail — that measured distance, taken in the flat, is the width to cut the plywood hatch top to[1].

## Keeping the top rails parallel

When the window side frames go in, the top rails that the hatch slides on must be checked for parallel so the hatch will run in its groove; they flex a little once fitted, but not much, and once every piece is in place the cabin structure becomes rigid[1]. If the side panels have already been glued up rigid and the rails have drifted out of parallel, a wedge or bar clamp is used against the structure to bring them back before the cabin roof beams go in[1].

## Building the hatch panel

The hatch is built by first gluing "L"-shaped legs, sized to ride in the rail slots, onto a flat plywood panel and letting the glue cure for a couple of days[1]. The panel is then clamped onto those legs and bent into a curve, and fiberglass cloth is applied inside and out while it is held in that shape[1]. The legs take real load during glassing — enough pressure that they did not break off — even though the epoxy bonding them to the panel holds "only by a 1/4-inch area," a demonstration of how strong an epoxy bond can be[1]. Beams are then attached to the hatch top panel so that it runs smoothly in the slot, and the remaining beams are added to the cabin top and glassed per the plans[1].

## Finishing: EVA, handle, and remaining seals

The EVA foam and the inside pull handle are installed before painting, but the remainder of the seals are deliberately left off until after the hull is painted[1].

## Access is a climb, not a step

Getting into Paradox's interior is not like stepping into an open day-sailer's accessible cockpit — access is a climb up and into the interior, "more like climbing into the cockpit of a jet fighter"[1]. Because of this, all interior work must be completed before the cabin structure goes on; once the cabin is closed in, that access is all there is[1].

## Steering from below, and the case for a port in the hatch

The hatch is what makes the boat's **below-decks helm station** habitable, and the earliest dated account of that station in use — the December 2001 [Cedar Key to Honeymoon Island sea trial](/boats/zoe.md#sea-trials-route-cedar-key-to-clearwater) — both endorses it and identifies the one thing it lacks. Running before a sea of about three feet that was rolling onto the quarter and sending spray over the hatch, the seat was folded to its stowed position, a cushion arranged to port, and the hatch closed; steering from inside at a steady 5.5 kt, completely protected from the elements, was judged "simply delightful."[4]

The single wanted addition was a **port in the hatch**, so the sail could be seen and trimmed properly while steering from below — and space had already been deliberately left between the end of the hatch and the solar panel to allow for it.[4] That port was subsequently built as a **skylight**, and proved itself on a later Cumberland Sound cruise, where it allowed the sail to be watched for trim without going on deck and, once the boat heeled, gave a view to leeward without stooping to the leeward windows; see ["Zoë" — the skylight and chart table in practical use](/boats/zoe.md#the-skylight-and-chart-table-in-practical-use-a-cumberland-sound-cruise). The requirement, in other words, was identified from the helm on the design's first extended cruise and answered by a modification that is now common on the type.

## Condition assessment of an eight-year-old hatch

Inspecting a secondhand Paradox's hatch after eight years of service is a useful check on how the sealing system in "Three-part sealing system" above holds up in the long run — and on how well a given hatch was built to begin with. One such hatch, removed for a full rebuild, "had not been built according to plan," with a rough finish throughout; the remedial list ran to replacing the roving on its forward part, redoing both the internal and external seals, and reseating the copper sliders top and bottom.[5] The verdict on the boat as bought was blunt: "When I first looked at the boat, I thought quite a bit of work would be required to make her hatch satisfactory, and I wasn't wrong."[6] Rebuilding to a standard judged "virtually watertight, even in the worst conditions when waves may break over the boat" added an external baffle at the front end (made from a household draught excluder), cramped the foam insulation under the lid to the lid itself with plastic edging strips to stop it snagging the hatch-gate baffle on every opening and closing, and relied on drain holes either side plus the small gaps at front and back between the outer retainer frames and the hatch side seals to shed whatever rain and spray still gets in.[6][7] A separate baffle glued to the forward end of the hatchway's arched beam, plus foam strips under the port and starboard hatch sides, stops water entering the cabin when the boat heels — built, on this account, "according to Matt Layden's Paradox drawings," since in heavy seas a wave can break over the cabin from forward.[8]

## A named sealant comparison

Refitting a hatch window, one owner weighed a well-known marine sealant, Geocel, against a cheaper hardware-store product, Ever Build's "General Purpose Sealant," and chose the latter at roughly half the cost after checking its technical data sheet confirmed it adequate for the job — sealing only the window's outer and inner perimeters plus the nut and bolt holes, leaving the cavity between the two seals unsealed but protected from ingress, and using the same product for the cabin windows too.[9]
