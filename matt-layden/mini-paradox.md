# Mini Paradox

**"Mini Paradox"** is an experimental variant of the [Paradox](/paradox.md)
built and sailed by [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md) himself,
distinguished by a narrower, taller sail plan and by
[chine runners](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md) repositioned from their
stock location — features easy to miss in photographs of the boat.

## Chine runners, present but easy to miss

Mini Paradox is fitted with [chine runners](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md),
though they are hard to make out in photographs of the boat — easily
mistaken for absent, especially given the impression that the hull might not
sit deep enough in the water for them to work. [Glen C.
Maxwell](/people/glen-maxwell/matt-layden-eyewitness.md), who saw the boat in
person, confirmed they are present. Their presence on an experimental,
narrower-hulled variant confirms chine runners as a constant across Matt's
small-boat lineage, consistent with their role elsewhere in the family (see
[GJAC](/matt-layden/gjac.md) and [chine runners — how they
work](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md)).

On Mini Paradox the runners **start farther aft and end a little farther
forward** than on a stock Paradox — a real placement difference, not an
optical illusion of the photographs.

## A higher-aspect-ratio sail experiment

Mini Paradox's sail is **shorter but wider** than the stock Paradox rig, used
by **Matt Layden** to explore the effect of a **higher aspect ratio** on
performance. No other particulars of the sail's dimensions are on record.

## Sailing performance and accommodation

Sailed by Matt Layden and his wife **Karen**, Mini Paradox "sails like a
witch" two-up and is **very roomy for her size** below (no interior
photographs are on record). No further dimensions, construction details, or
plans are on record for this variant, which remains, as far as the archive
shows, a one-off built and sailed only by Matt Layden himself.

## Runner placement, and doubt cast on the chine-curvature theory

The mechanism behind Mini Paradox's chine runners is attributed to
**end-plate effect** — the same principle by which a plate fixed at the tip
of a foil or wing reduces tip-vortex losses and improves the foil's
efficiency — rather than to the curvature of the chine itself. Curvature is
a weak candidate mechanism in any case: a boat's continual pitching would
constantly change the curvature's orientation relative to the direction of
travel, undercutting curvature as a stable explanation. The Bolducs'
`microcruising.com` site carries a dedicated page, under "Latest News," on
**building the Mini Paradox**, with clear photographs of the chine-runner
details.

## An outboard-only answer to a catamaran hypothetical

Extending the chine-runner concept to a catamaran, the runners belong only
on the **outboard side** of each hull, not both sides of both hulls. Fitting
runners to a catamaran's **inner** hull surfaces is a poor use of the
concept regardless of side count: a sailing catamaran rarely sails level, so
one hull typically rides noticeably deeper than the other, and any
inner-hull runner would sit mostly in the turbulence of the other hull's own
bow wake, undermining its usefulness. It is more productive to size the
**outboard** runners larger instead, to compensate for whatever leeway
resistance the inner runners would otherwise have contributed, with the
runners' precise vertical placement set by calculating the boat's average
heel angle so as to sit within the widest practical portion of the
operational heel curve. A parallel exists in the fixed keel-form appendages
molded into the underside of some beach catamaran hulls, which serve the
same leeway-resistance role as a chine runner while avoiding the
vulnerability of a moving daggerboard or centerboard; canting the hulls to
present that surface at an optimal angle across a wide range of conditions
would let a catamaran keep chine-runner-style resistance without needing a
board or trunk at all, at no real cost in interior volume and no greater
design complexity than drawing a flat hull in the first place.

## A construction detail from the Bolducs' Mini Paradox build page

Mini Paradox's construction and chine-runner fitting is documented in
photographs on the Bolducs' `microcruising.com` site. The runners appear to
**follow the curve of the hull side and hull bottom** — a "sea of peas"
visual effect — and a second page on the same site shows the finished boat
being **car-topped**, confirming Mini Paradox is light and small enough to
be carried on a vehicle roof rather than trailered.

There is "some" curvature visible in the runners, though not a lot, leaving
open whether that curvature is functionally essential, whether it instead
follows automatically from keeping the runner even with the hull bottom
along the chine, or whether it serves the secondary purpose of locally
strengthening the hull — the same open question already recorded for stock
Paradox runners (see [chine runners — how they
work](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md#the-chine-runner-curve-cosmetic-fabrication-choice-or-hydrodynamic-feature)).
Whether any of Mini Paradox's speed comes from the slight **V-shape tortured
into the bottom at the bow** is unresolved; Matt Layden has expressed being
unimpressed with stitch-and-glue construction generally, while allowing that
forming a slight V this way seemed comparatively easy to achieve. The likelier
explanation offered for Mini Paradox's speed is not the hull's slight V at all,
but simply that she is sailed by someone who **knows how to sail a small
sharpie well**, combined with her being **very light** — an estimated **130
lb** hull that, with a 250 lb crew aboard, totals under a third of stock
Paradox's own displacement. Her sail area to displacement ratio is thought
likely better than Paradox's, though no figures were recorded to confirm it.

## Runner placement compared to a low-aspect-ratio keel, and a center-of-lateral-plane question

Mini Paradox's chine runners, in photographs, appear placed along the chine
much as a **low-aspect-ratio keel** might be fitted to a boat's centerline —
ending abruptly at the aft edge while fairing smoothly into the chine at the
forward end, the same shape convention low-aspect-ratio keels commonly use.
This raises a question for calculating a chine-runner boat's center of
lateral plane: whether the runners' own area should be treated as a **keel
extension** for that purpose, alongside the hull and rudder. The runner area
should indeed count as a keel extension — adding the runners' profile to the
bottom of the chine curve and computing lateral resistance from the
resulting combined profile should come out close to the true figure.[14]

## The 3D modeling software behind the published renderings

The Mini Paradox renderings on Matt Layden's brother's website were produced
in **Rhino 3D**, a NURBS-based CAD package that handles both chine-hulled
and smooth-hulled boats and accepts plugins for full hydrodynamic analysis
via **RhinoMarine** (from Proteus Engineering, also makers of the dedicated
NURBS-based marine design package **FastShip**). The specific computer that
produced the published screen grabs was not running RhinoMarine at the
time.

## Whether Matt Layden will release plans for Mini Paradox

Matt Layden has not committed to drawing plans for Mini Paradox, and by his
own stated practice will not release plans for any boat he has not personally
built and sailed enough to confirm it performs as intended. Interest in the
design has been strong, and he is pleased with her performance so far, which
keeps the possibility open. In the meantime, Mini Paradox's build process is
documented in full on his brother's website, `physics.bgsu.edu/~layden`,
maintained specifically to show her complete construction.
