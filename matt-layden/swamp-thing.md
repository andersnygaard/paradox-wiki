# Swamp Thing

**Swamp Thing** is one of [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)'s small
cruising boats, part of the Layden design family alongside
[Little Cruiser](little-cruiser.md) and the [Paradox](/paradox.md). Built in
**1985**, she was **Matt Layden's first micro-cruiser to use
[chine runners](/design/chine-runners-and-windward-performance.md)** — the
tapered external chine log he first tried and named on this boat, replacing the
fouling leeboards of his earlier [Terrapin](terrapin.md). Matt sailed her
**engineless** from Connecticut down the Intracoastal Waterway and out to the
Bahamas. The Bolducs call her "one of his prettiest designs," though her
accommodations for one are "quite spartan," and she appears — with a photo of
Matt aboard in the Abacos — in Ida Little and Michael Walsh's book
*Beachcruising and Coastal Camping*.

## Design particulars

| Particular | Value |
|---|---|
| LOA | 13' 2" |
| Beam | 3' 8" (44") |
| Draft | 7" |
| Sail area | 73 sq ft |
| Displacement | 900 lb |

Her notable details: **fresh-water ballasted**; **roller-furling main**; **all
lines lead back to the cabin**; and **oars that stick out the sides for quick
use** — the "oar holes on the sides" that remain her most-remembered
identifying feature.

[Dave Bolduc](/people/bolduc/dave.md) **spent considerable time helping to
restore Swamp Thing**, part of the hands-on experience with Matt's boats that
informs his guidance to the builders' community.

## The Gulf Stream rollover — and self-righting proof

The most dramatic episode in Swamp Thing's record is a **complete rollover in
the Gulf Stream**, which the Bolducs cite as a real-world demonstration of the
design's self-righting. One rough night mid-Gulf Stream, Matt was on deck
signalling a nearby freighter with a flashlight; the ship passed too close and
its wake upset already-confused 10-foot seas, knocking the boat onto her side.
Matt grabbed a **chine runner** for support, which pulled her over on top of
him; she was "soon righted in the tepid waters off Florida," Matt climbed back
aboard the flooded boat, bailed her out, and continued on to the Bahamas. Dave
Bolduc's inference: had Matt been **inside** the cabin — as the boats are
designed to be sailed — he "most likely would not have had any trouble." This
is one of two documented Layden-boat knockdowns (the other being
[Terrapin](terrapin.md)'s off Maine); see
[blue-water suitability](/design/blue-water-suitability.md) and
[heavy-weather tactics](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md).

## Sale history and the best-sailing of Matt's designs

Swamp Thing sold for **$600** a few years before late 2001, to **Dave Gatan**
— David Bolduc had urged him to buy her, ready to buy her himself otherwise,
and made clear that if Gatan ever sold her on, "she's coming with me!"

Swamp Thing is rated **"really the best sailing boat of all of Matt's
designs,"** compared to an **Albert Strange canoe yawl** for her **very
narrow transom**, sailing to windward "like a witch" and tacking "like a
dream." This is the most enthusiastic comparative performance endorsement of
any single Layden design on record — rating Swamp Thing above Paradox and
Little Cruiser on sailing quality, even though Paradox remains the
best-documented design in this bundle.

## From Dave Gatan to the Bolducs: the 2004 rescue and 2005–2008 rebuild

**Dave Gatan** owned Swamp Thing and day-sailed her around his home in the
Florida Keys, but let her fall into disrepair. By **2004** the Bolducs rescued
her, bringing her north from Key Largo (per their site, ahead of that season's
Hurricanes Frances and Ivan); Gatan gave the boat to them. Gatan, a longtime
Keys small-boat liveaboard (including a 23' Glander Cay) who never used an
outboard and was an accomplished Flamenco guitarist, died around 2009.

With the boat newly arrived in his back yard by mid-September 2004, the
question of what on her was original versus later-added — against a
high-resolution scan of the Swamp Thing photo from Ida Little's book — was
resolved with help from other builders' copies of the image; two further
photos of Swamp Thing at the Bolducs' own house are hosted on
`microcruising.com`. Swamp Thing's condition at that point was rot in both
the bottom and the deck, "going to take a bit of work," though restoration
was initially expected to wait until the following summer, since
[Little Cruiser](little-cruiser.md) was mid-way through being covered in
fiberglass.[13]

In fact the rebuild began **months earlier** than that "next summer"
timeline — the boat's sad state ruled out leaving her sitting idle.
Probing with a screwdriver turned up rot in both the plywood hull and the
deck, plus problems at the stem and stern; the bad wood was cut away and new
pieces spliced in, with rotten areas filled using epoxy, fiberglass, and
microballoons. The **skeg** was removed, its original galvanized bolts —
some twenty years old — rusted away almost entirely, and **new blades for
the rudder and for one of her custom oars** were made, both of which had
also seen better days. This work proceeded alongside finishing
[Little Cruiser](little-cruiser.md)'s fiberglassing, the two refits
continuing in parallel rather than fully sequentially (see [Little
Cruiser — a mid-October 2004 progress
update](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md#a-mid-october-2004-progress-update)).[14][15]

The Bolducs then undertook a **multi-year rebuild (2005–2008)**, running in
parallel with [Little Cruiser](little-cruiser.md)'s own 2005 refit. Swamp Thing
had arrived with **serious rot** in her transom, rear deck, coaming, and stem;
they ground out and saturated the rotten areas with unthickened epoxy, rebuilt
with new wood and glass, and eventually **replaced the entire bottom with new
½" A/C-grade plywood** (glued with epoxy/cabosil and fastened with bronze ring
nails), fitted **five new floor timbers**, made a new rudder blade, and
re-glassed the deck. In **2008**, at Matt Layden's suggestion, they rebuilt her
**chine runners about ¼" wider and ⅛" thicker — roughly 25% wider than the
originals — to try to improve her windward performance**. See
[construction](/construction/index.md) for the epoxy/rot-repair techniques used
and [chine runners and windward performance](/design/chine-runners-and-windward-performance.md)
for the runner iteration.

## Rudder rebuild technique: beveled lead versus screwed lead, and a lead-melting caution

Ahead of the fuller 2005–2008 structural rebuild, [Dave Bolduc](/people/bolduc/dave.md)
made a **new rudder blade for Swamp Thing** as an early, standalone repair — pulling
133 old ring nails and grinding the rotten bottom away down to the
plywood/mahogany chine-log glue line first. For the new blade's **lead
weighting**, he departed from the method shown on the Paradox plans (wood
screws holding the lead in place) and instead **beveled the mounting hole on
both sides to lock the lead in mechanically**. [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)
used this same beveled-lead method on both Swamp Thing and
[Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) (rudder blade and
centerboard alike), and it works well specifically **when the lead weight
sits some distance from the trailing edge**, as on these designs' blades.

Melting the lead for the casting itself carries its own technique and hazard.
Dave's practical tip: start the pot with only a **small amount of lead**, then
gradually add more as the existing metal melts and the total quantity grows,
rather than loading the pot heavily from the start. [Alastair](/people/alastair.md)
added a serious safety warning from his own experience **melting down old lead
pipe**: residual moisture trapped in the pipe can flash to steam and make the
pipe fire a slug of molten lead like a gun barrel — in his case, a 6 in
length of pipe threw molten lead about **10 ft**. His caution: always point
any pipe being melted down away from anyone standing nearby.

## Why she sails so well: the chine runners

What makes Swamp Thing a better sailing boat than Paradox or Little Cruiser was
long an open question — candidate explanations included a **keel**, a
**narrower beam**, or **lighter displacement**. The answer credited by Dave
Bolduc is her **[chine runners](/design/chine-runners-and-windward-performance.md)**
(rather than keel, beam, or displacement), which make her **"the most
manueverable of Matt's designs,"** tacking "on a dime." See
[sailing characteristics and rig balance — "Trim is very important"](/operations/sailing-characteristics.md)
for the full cross-design comparison, which also contrasts Swamp Thing's easy
tacking with Paradox's deliberately more directionally-stable, harder-to-tack
hull form. This accounts for the maneuverability of her sailing; the separate
windward-speed quality ("sails to windward like a witch") is attributed to the
same chine-runner foils.
