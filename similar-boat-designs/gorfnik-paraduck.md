# Gorfnik

**"Gorfnik"** is a **"Paraduck"** — a modified **Puddle Duck Racer** hull built with a fully enclosed, sitting-headroom cabin — built by **André-François Bourbeau** of **Saguenay, Québec**, who is himself an owner and builder of a [Paradox](/paradox.md). Her cabin resembles a smaller version of Matt Layden's Paradox design, though the hull underneath is a Puddle Duck Racer's, not a Paradox's.[1] She is registered with the Puddle Duck Racer class as **PDR #952**.[2]

## A seventh of a Paradox's weight, and where that lets her go

"Gorfnik" weighs about **200 lb**, against roughly **1,410 lb** for a Paradox — light enough to be launched from an ordinary dinghy trolley rather than a boat trailer, letting her reach put-in spots a Paradox cannot, since a Paradox needs a slipway with vehicle access.[3] Free building plans for "Gorfnik," a 6.3 MB PDF, were made available for download, alongside a set of photographs.[4]

## The designer's own account

Writing on the Paradox Sailboat Facebook group, **[André-François Bourbeau](/people/andre-francois-bourbeau.md)** describes "Gorfnik" as his own design: a minimalist, sail-from-inside variant of the **Puddle Duck Racer** hull, carrying over ideas from the Paradox, and he travels with two or sometimes three Gorfniks loaded on the same trailer so friends can come along.[5] His longest trip aboard a Gorfnik has run four weeks, and he finds the type sees more use than his Paradoxes simply because it is smaller and easier to launch.[5] Comparing the two designs after further seasons of sailing, he sums the Gorfnik up as, in effect, a stable, square-shaped live-aboard canoe — roomy enough inside, and stable enough, that he can lean out and wash his hands while cooking — but one with **nil secondary stability**: leaned too far, or caught by a hard puff without releasing the sheet, she can flip instantly and without warning, unlike a boat that heels progressively and gives a sailor time to react.[6]
