# Paradox compared to other small-boat designs

Paradox has repeatedly come up for comparison against other small cruising and beach-camping designs, most often Jim Michalak's and Phil Bolger's. The comparisons converge on a consistent shape: Paradox trades interior room and build cost for strength, weight, and a closed-cabin, self-righting approach to genuine offshore conditions, while the alternative designs below generally offer more space and an easier build at the cost of some combination of stability, seaworthiness, or beachability. For Paradox's own place in the historical sharpie lineage — a separate question from these cross-design comparisons — see [Sharpie lineage: is Paradox a sharpie?](/design/sharpie-lineage.md), not repeated here.

## Jim Michalak's AF3

Michalak's **AF3** was raised as a similarly beach-landable design offering more interior space, including room enough for a dog in the cockpit.[1] Weighed against Paradox, the AF3 was judged less stable, though possibly a better boat for lying out in the sun; a capsize-recovery account for the AF3 was separately pointed to for context.[2][3] Set against that comparison, Paradox was judged the right boat on the strength of others' experience, if still a little too small for some tastes — prompting, in one case, a suggestion to consider a small catamaran instead.[4]

## Jim Michalak's Robbsboat, and Phil Bolger redesigns

Michalak's **Robbsboat**, published on Duckworks, was separately raised as a possible Paradox alternative — wider for her length than Paradox, and reflective of Michalak's broader habit of reworking other designers' hulls, among them numerous Phil Bolger redesigns.[5] One of those, the **Cormorant** — a 31 ft light expansion of Michalak's own **Caprice** design — was framed as an answer to perceived shortcomings in Bolger's **AS29**: the AS29, though probably more seaworthy, slaps badly at anchor, carries a lot of weight, does not beach well, and steers poorly under power because its motor sits aft of the rudder post. The Cormorant, by contrast, is light, dries out on a beach without crushing its bottom, and locates its motor well forward of the rudder post for more authoritative steering; a long slotted cabin top similar to the Birdwatcher's, with a removable forward panel, gives through-ventilation and standing headroom without a Bolger box's high windage. Michalak's simple, iterative approach to solving these problems — light weight, beachability, and modest seaworthiness for coastal and Bahamas-style cruising — was judged increasingly persuasive on its own terms, without displacing Paradox itself in the comparison.[6] The pointer drew only a brief acknowledgment before that thread moved on to unrelated build progress.[7]

A further, chine-runner-specific critique of Michalak's own view of chine runners is covered separately at [chine runners: how they work — Jim Michalak's "Robbsboat," and a critique of his view of chine runners](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md#jim-michalaks-robbsboat-and-a-critique-of-his-view-of-chine-runners).

## The "lake boats, not sea boats" verdict, and a Frolic2 counterexample

The Robbsboat pointer resurfaced in mid-2006 as a cheap, simple alternative buildable in 3/8 in plywood and possibly roomier inside than Paradox, though none of the handful then under construction had yet been finished; Michalak's own building book for the design was again recommended as highly regarded.[8] Michalak himself, asked directly about his own boats, characterized his designs as built for lake sailing rather than genuine sea conditions.[9] That verdict was answered with a counterexample rather than accepted outright: a Michalak **Frolic2** had been entered in the WaterTribe Everglades Challenge — the same ocean-and-mangrove race Paradox and [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) have both completed — and finished in good shape, suggesting the lake/sea distinction is not absolute.[10] Pressed on whether Michalak's designs are proven for long-distance, single-handed coastal cruising, on the reasoning that a boat suited to its purpose is surely the better starting point, the point was conceded "somewhat," while maintaining that a sailor can do anything in anything, given enough skill.[11] Pressed further on whether the Frolic2's Everglades finish undercut the "lake boat" verdict, the fuller answer was that her two entrants never really left sheltered water or faced serious seas — Frolic2 remains a comfortable, capable design on her own terms — and that Michalak, like Bolger, tends to describe his own small boats conservatively as a matter of course, plausibly for liability reasons, even though several are self-righting from a 90-degree-plus knockdown and unsinkable unless holed or caught with the hatches open; both designers hold that a boat's seaworthiness is inseparable from her captain's skill and judgment, not a property of the hull alone.[12]

## Matt Layden's own offshore record

Matt Layden's own record of offshore work has been raised directly against that same designer caution. Michalak has said he would not take a Paradox to sea, but by the same reasoning he is not the offshore sailor Matt Layden has proven himself to be: Matt is credited with having sailed, rowed, sculled, and paddled over 25,000 miles in small boats, including one winter spent aboard a boat in ice and snow, and is understood to remain reluctant to publish plans himself for liability reasons — the same caution attributed to Michalak and Bolger above.[13] The general lesson drawn from the comparison is to take capability claims one step at a time, matched against an honest evaluation of the sailor's own experience rather than either bravado or a designer's disclaimer.

## Adapting a Robbsboat toward Paradox's offshore formula

A further technical exchange considered what it would take to adapt Michalak's cheap, simple Robbsboat hull toward Paradox's own offshore-capable formula: adding [chine runners](/chine-runners/index.md) (with or without a leeboard) and the Paradox sail rig was judged a reasonable starting point, along with substituting a steel bottom plate for internal ballast. Matt's own [reefing system](/components/rig/index.md), which closes the boat up as securely as Bolger's own storm-tactic comparison to a corked, sealed bottle once the hatches are fully closed, was judged the piece still missing from a scratch-built alternative, and not something to trust without first learning a new hull's own capabilities incrementally. Set against Paradox, a Robbsboat would be faster and cheaper to build and offers more interior sleeping room, but is neither as strong nor as heavy — a real disadvantage for offshore jaunts, where a collision is more likely to end badly for the boat.[13]

## Phil Bolger's Black Skimmer and Martha Jane

A Robbsboat build was separately weighed against a Bolger-designed **Black Skimmer**, which looked promising but was judged hard to right after a capsize.[14] Bolger's own upgrades to the Black Skimmer since the original plans were flagged as a reason to check with him directly before building — the original design (still the version sold by Dynamite Payson) is comparatively complex to build, has low cabin space, and carries a tall, heavy mast that is not easy to step. Bolger's **Martha Jane**, which supersedes the Black Skimmer, was recommended as more practical, more trailer-friendly, and roomier, if less handsome to look at — fitted with tabernacled masts and water ballast, and, in a later-upgraded form, a pilothouse with standing headroom down the central slot plus sponsons on either side of the cockpit for self-righting from a 90-degree-plus knockdown, along with an improved leeboard design. A Robbsboat, though, would still get a builder out on the water sooner than either Bolger design.[15]

## Where the plans are sold: Duckworks Magazine

Duckworks Magazine (`duckworksmagazine.com`) sells Michalak's plans alongside John Welsford's and others', and was the outlet that published the Robbsboat design discussed above; its publisher himself competed in a Frolic2 in the WaterTribe Everglades Challenge.[16]
