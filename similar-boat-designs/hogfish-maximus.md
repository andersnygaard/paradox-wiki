# "Hogfish Maximus"

**"Hogfish Maximus"** is a roughly 38 ft flat-bottomed, shoal-draft cruising sharpie designed and built by **Chris Morejohn**, described in the Paradox archive as "basically a 38 foot version of Paradox"[1] and best known for crossing the Atlantic in 2004 with Paradox-style [chine runners](/chine-runners/index.md) doing the work of lateral resistance. It is the largest and most-travelled of the boats treated here as kin to Paradox — a full liveaboard hull carrying the same runner-on-a-flat-bottom idea to ocean-crossing scale. Its designer holds that, despite the resemblance, it is a separate design of his own rather than a scaled-up Paradox (see ["Modified Sharpie," not a scaled-up Paradox](#modified-sharpie-not-a-scaled-up-paradox) below).

## The Hogfish family

Chris Morejohn, a Florida Keys boatbuilder and liveaboard cruiser, built a family of large flat-bottomed sharpies and is remembered as "the creator of several large liveaboard sized sharpies as well as some revolutionary flats boats"[2]:

- **"Hogfish Lips"** — a 32 ft sharpie, the boat the Morejohns were living aboard when they were met cruising the Bahamas in 1994 (see [Connection to the Paradox circle](#connection-to-the-paradox-circle) below)[2].
- **"Hogfish Maximus"** — the larger, roughly 38 ft hull, "basically a 38 foot version of Paradox," and the boat that crossed the Atlantic in 2004[1][2].
- **"Jubilee"** — a 40 ft version of the same "Hogfish" design, advertised for sale in 2005 and later photographed on a travelift with its narrow chine runners clearly visible; like "Hogfish Maximus," it combines chine runners with a centerboard[3][4][5].

All three carry **"Paradox"-style chine runners**, the feature that most visibly links the family to Matt Layden's boat[3][4].

## Chine runners at ocean scale — the 2004 Atlantic crossing

In May–June 2004 "Hogfish Maximus" crossed the Atlantic from Florida to **Portugal** by way of **Bermuda and the Azores**, three months allowed for the passage[1]. A mid-crossing report from **Horta, in the Azores** gave the first firsthand account of a chine-runner hull at ocean-crossing scale: the passage never rolled or sank, the leg to Bermuda ran nearly 14 days for 1,070 miles and the leg on to the Azores almost 18 days for 1,890 miles, and — the point of interest for Paradox — **the chine runners worked well even at this larger size, with the daggerboard needed for under 48 hours of the whole passage** (only when hard on the wind), the boat otherwise going to weather fine without it[6]. Loaded to her waterline with a family's tools, spares, and nearly a year's food, she was judged to pound no more than some fin-keeled boats and to roll markedly less than a Hans Christian 38 sailed on the same points of sail[6][7]. The crossing left a settled view that a **21 to 25 ft version of Paradox** would suit ocean voyaging well[6].

For the full passage account, the fuller return verdict, and the list's replies, see [Glen C. ## Chine runners plus a centerboard

The one clear design difference from Paradox — which relies on chine runners alone — is that both "Hogfish Maximus" and "Jubilee" **combine chine runners with a centerboard**[5]. The narrow "wing" runners proved more effective than expected and did not create excessive turbulence even running with the board fully raised; a centerboard is reckoned to become worth adding alongside the runners at around a 25 ft waterline, depending partly on how much offshore sailing is planned[5]. Morejohn's runners are also **not simply scaled-up Paradox runners**: they work from the flow at the chine/hull junction rather than [Matt Layden's lifting-body approach](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md), ride wetter because they do not extend as far aft, and are less aerodynamically shaped and narrower than a runner scaled directly from a Paradox would be[8]. The full runner-sizing discussion is at [how chine runners work — narrow "wing" runners on a Chris Morejohn sharpie, and a centerboard-plus-runner combination](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md#narrow-wing-runners-on-a-chris-morejohn-sharpie-and-a-centerboard-plus-runner-combination).

## "Modified Sharpie," not a scaled-up Paradox

One design point is on the record directly from the boat's designer: although "Hogfish Maximus" resembles what an enlarged Paradox would look like, it is **not a scaled-up Paradox but a separate design, arrived at through a separate design process**[7]. A firm line is also drawn between these hulls and **traditional sharpies** — which have low sheer and a low center of gravity and sail differently — with the term **"Modified Sharpie"** coined to mark the distinction[7]. For where that distinction sits within the wider question of Paradox's own sharpie ancestry, see [sharpie lineage — whether the scaled-up family still counts as sharpies](/design/sharpie-lineage.md#whether-the-scaled-up-family-still-counts-as-sharpies).

## Connection to the Paradox circle

The link between the Hogfish sharpies and Paradox is not only one of hull shape. Chris Morejohn belongs to the wider circle of small-boat builders around [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md) and the [Bolducs](/people/bolduc/dave.md): the Morejohns were met living aboard "Hogfish Lips" during a **1994 joint cruise to the Bahamas made in tandem with Matt Layden**[2]. For the designer's own biography, his other boats and stories (including the well-known photograph of him at the helm of a Paradox on Exuma Sound), and his place in that circle, see [Chris Morejohn](/people/chris-morejohn.md).
