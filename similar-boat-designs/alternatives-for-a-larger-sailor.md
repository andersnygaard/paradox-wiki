# Alternatives for a larger sailor

Designs *other than* stock [Paradox](/paradox.md), considered by sailors who do not fit her, and the case for choosing a different boat rather than modifying this one. Where [Building Paradox larger](/design/larger-paradox.md) covers the designer-sanctioned scale-up of Paradox herself, the pages below are genuine rival designs and counterpoints — a different boat, not a bigger Paradox.

## Paramour: a ground-up redesign for a bigger sailor

Rather than scaling stock Paradox further, a builder in the UK drew a **new design, "Paramour,"** as a bigger boat for a bigger sailor — a from-scratch 16 ft microcruiser sharing Paradox's rig and rudder but with a flatter, faster hull run computed via the PLYBOATS design program. See [Paramour](/similar-boat-designs/paramour-design.md) for the full specification, buyer interest, and its later, scaled-back reappearance.[1]

## A recommended alternative for a larger sailor: Michalak's Caroline, and the case against modifying Paradox to fit

Rather than reworking Paradox's cabin, the standing recommendation for a sailor who simply does not fit the stock boat — considerably taller, heavier, or less agile than the design's own reference build — is to choose a different, more spacious existing design instead of modifying this one. Jim Michalak's **"Caroline,"** his first design aimed at off-shore coastal cruising, has been suggested for exactly this case, and already has at least one Paradox-list member's plans on order.[2]

The physical case for treating fit as a hard limit rather than something to design around: the stock boat suits a sailor of the designer's own build well, fitting "like a comfortable slipper," while a taller, fatter, or less agile sailor will find her "like a bunion in an iron shoe" — such a sailor is better served looking for a boat with Paradox's own strengths but more habitable cabin space, with Michalak's Caroline offered as one candidate, rather than by modifying Paradox itself.[3] The same reasoning extends to auxiliary propulsion: Paradox's minimal, largely engine-free approach to close-quarters maneuvering (see [auxiliary outboard power](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-port-quarter-honda-23-mounting-and-a-port-side-electric-outboards-setup-time) and [sculling and yuloh technique](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md)) suits the sailor she was drawn for but is a real practical shortcoming for anyone who needs reliable powered maneuvering in a mooring or narrow waterway.[3]

A different critique of the same recommendation held that Caroline was not in Paradox's class for seaworthiness and handiness in windy conditions, and pressed the underlying question harder: does any design at all offer Paradox's specific combination of sail-from-inside steering, self-righting stability, and shoal-draft cruising in a small package — a want sharpened by an anticipated need for a small auxiliary motor, given limited available cruising time (see [auxiliary outboard power — whether fitting a motor is worth it at all](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#whether-fitting-a-motor-is-worth-it-at-all)).[4] The closest match on record turned out not to be a rival design at all but Matt Layden's own other small cruisers: [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md), built in 1988 and still sailed by her owners two decades on, and the newer, lighter [Enigma](/matt-layden/enigma.md) family, all sharing the same chine-runner, self-righting approach in what was described as a developmental progression — alongside a UK builder's own Paradox-sized Enigma derivative then under construction (see [Noli](/boats/noli.md#a-removable-hardtop-concept-and-noli-restated-as-a-paradox-sized-enigma)).[5]

## Bolger's Micro, Birdwatcher, and the "Adelie": alternatives for a larger sailor

A parallel comparison — a builder weighing an enlarged Paradox against small-cruiser designs by Phil Bolger, around the same time as a separate Paradox-vs-Sven-Yrvind's-"Sofia" comparison (see [Blue-water suitability — a 2004 revisiting](/design/blue-water-suitability.md#a-2004-revisiting-paradox-against-sven-yrvinds-sofia)) — sharpens what Paradox actually offers at this size. The Bolger **"Birdwatcher"** is roomy but lacks Paradox's self-righting capsize recovery (center-slot hatches, as fitted on the "BWII" variant, have been proposed to address this); the Bolger **"Micro"** is often ruled out on beachability grounds given her 18 in draft. Chine runners have been considered for a Micro hull and abandoned: a Micro's flatter, less-rockered bottom would need chine runners **long enough to cause unacceptable drag**, more so than Paradox's own runners, with no reliable way to predict where the resulting center of lateral resistance would fall, risking an unbalanced rig. One Micro was instead built with a **centerboard case off-center against the port bunk** — 2.25 in wide, 4 ft long, flush with the bunk top and barely noticeable in use except when beached or trailered — ballasted with **425 lb of lead shot mixed with epoxy and poured like concrete** under the sole and bunks and capped with screwed-down plywood; the rudder, mounted normally but hung off a skeg where the keel had been, carries a large end-plate to compensate for losing six inches of depth, reasoned adequate by comparison to a smaller rudder on the AS19.

On the beachability question, Micros in practice **beach better than their 18 in draft suggests**, since the first foot or so of the bow rises clear of the water; a photograph of a Bolger Long Micro, a regular Micro, and an Oldshoe beached side by side bears this out, and owners more often anchor a little offshore to avoid bugs and other pests than from any real difficulty getting a Micro onto a beach.

A related design comparison weighs a "near-cousin" of Paradox: a small hard-chine cruiser sold by bateau.com as **"Adelie,"** built along similar lines to Paradox but without chine runners. Her cockpit has **no backrests**, only straps, risking a wet backside, and she exists only as **computer-generated renderings** rather than a real, built boat — a real mark against her next to Paradox and against a **Bolger Micro**, which is judged superior in several respects. The recurring verdict across this whole comparison: "I love Paradox — but it is just too small for what I wanted," with a standing call — **"We need someone to come forward and enlarge the Paradox by 20%!"**

## A counterpoint: Paradox as arguably too seaworthy for two-up sailing, and a further design pointer

A further design pointed to as possibly meeting the shoal-draft requirement, though not evaluated against Paradox's other criteria, was a small beachable dinghy able to be left leaning on its own edge as the tide goes out without damage, and anchored with the crew able to wade ashore.[6]

A different answer to the same question held that Paradox's real problem may be being too seaworthy rather than not seaworthy enough for two-up cruising: a hull that stays safe and comfortable in wind and rough water can tempt a sailor into conditions that would otherwise be given a wide berth, a risk that changes character once a companion who did not choose to take it is also aboard. Twenty years running a small motorboat as a family day-boat — tolerant of rough water but prone to rolling badly past about a force 4 — produced the opposite habit, since discomfort rather than danger kept the boat off open water in poor conditions; sailing solo, several outings have instead gone ahead specifically because the boat was trusted to handle worse than it in fact met, trips that would not have been risked sailing two-up. A sailor choosing a design for two should weigh the weather actually expected as a criterion of its own — for ordinary coastal cruising with the forecast properly read, Paradox's blue-water capability can be overkill, and being genuinely caught out by a sudden change in conditions after five years of coastal sailing is rare enough to suggest inadequate homework rather than bad luck.[7]

A concrete illustration recalled from a Solent crossing: two 40 ft yachts that had crossed from Lymington in storm-force winds and lumpy seas — one of three such yachts having turned back partway, judging it too rough — arrived at Cowes only about a quarter of an hour ahead of a Paradox that had come from Poole the same day, to the visible surprise of their crews. Sailing that particular crossing solo made it an acceptable risk; sailing it with someone else aboard, it would not have been attempted.[8][9]
