# Sailing characteristics and rig balance

See also [heavy-weather tactics](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md) for
deliberate storm-handling questions (sea-anchor rudder position, lying
a-hull, maximum heel) distinct from the day-to-day tacking/trim issues
covered here.

How the [Paradox](/paradox.md)'s [lug rig](/design/rig.md) balances on
different points of sail — whether beating to weather generates significant
weather helm, whether that can be counteracted through sail trim rather than
by holding the tiller over, and how balance shifts as the boat heels — remains
an open question in the archive, alongside a related, also-open
[ballast-placement question](/design/weight-and-ballast.md).
It sits next to the [self-steering](/operations/self-steering.md) finding that
in **light air** the Paradox holds a course well enough, by tiller-rope
friction alone, to leave the helm briefly — a light-air data point that does
not by itself settle how she balances **to windward** or **fully powered up**.

## A firsthand tacking/grounding lesson

A Paradox can fail to complete a tack under real conditions, with real
consequences: during one solo winter river cruise, trying to claw off a lee
shore, a boat came about too late and would not complete the tack, driving
aground on a rock ledge. The lesson: "be careful of a lee shore if you have to
claw off into the wind." See
[group cruise planning and firsthand trip reports](/operations/group-cruise-reports.md)
for the full trip report — a data point on real-world handling limits that the
weather-helm/heeling question above still leaves open.

The same failure-to-complete-a-tack symptom has shown up on another boat,
stuck head-to-wind in irons repeatedly one weekend in the Salcombe estuary,
recoverable only with the yuloh — see
[rig and sail — a speculative small jib](/design/rig.md#a-speculative-small-jib)
for the fuller account and the small-jib idea it prompted.

## Real difficulty completing tacks, and successful (if inconsistent) jibing

The most detailed real-world account on record of Paradox tacking behavior
comes from days of hard sailing aboard "[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)". Her
**pointing ability** was not a concern — she kept up with bigger boats, if not
pointing quite as high — but her **bow would not reliably come around into
the wind to start a new tack**, to the point of never completing a tack
without using the motor to help bring the bow through. By contrast, jibing
worked, though not reliably ("very few" successful attempts) — a marked
contrast with a sloop rig, which gave no such trouble.

This is a second, independent data point (after the lee-shore grounding
above) of a Paradox failing to come about under a given set of conditions —
attributed here to some sailing-technique or trim issue rather than a
lee-shore/timing problem, but not reconciled with the generally
positive tacking-angle figures in
[pointing and tacking-angle expectations](/operations/pointing-and-tacking-angles.md)
(90-110°, from the same "Whisper," two months earlier). Whether the cause is
technique, ballast trim, sea state, or rig set-up is not resolved.

## Technique suggestions, a weight-distribution theory, and a full helm-balance debate

Tacking trouble like "Whisper"'s has drawn a range of practical technique
tips and the fullest theoretical debate on record on hull trim and helm
balance.

**Light-air technique.** Tacking in **light air** is sometimes frustrating
even on a well-sorted boat, and can require **backwinding the main**. The
standing pointing advice is that the [Paradox](/paradox.md) dislikes being
sailed too close to the wind, and falling off a little to build speed before
tacking usually lets her come around fine — a problem mainly in **chop or a
running sea**, which slows her markedly. If caught **in irons**, the
recommended recovery is to backwind the main and **motor/paddle in reverse**
until back on the desired tack — slow, but potentially saves a boat from
going ashore. The same two standard suggestions recur elsewhere: try
**falling off** to gain speed before tacking, and check whether the boat is
trimmed **down by the head** (too much weight in the bow), which "can cause a
boat to miss stays."

**A full loaded-weight breakdown** for "Whisper" ran to **400 lb lead
ballast** (most of it where the water tanks were meant to go), **65 lb
outboard**, **190 lb** of the sailor's own body weight, **6.5 gal fuel**, **5
gal water**, and roughly **75 lb** of gear/food/ground tackle — an estimated
**750 lb total**. See
[weight, ballast, and displacement](/design/weight-and-ballast.md) for the
boat's other ballast/trim data points this adds to.

**The weight-forward/weight-aft diagnosis.** A boat that won't come about is
"almost always a victim of too much weight forward or too much rig aft" — a
lesson drawn from a catamaran that would not tack until crew weight moved
aft. The broader theoretical point: a sailboat should be **"astable"** — not
a weathervane that points itself into the wind unassisted — and while a
**neutral-to-weather helm** is correct once under way and gaining speed, the
same is not true when the boat is stalled and pointed close to the wind. A
Paradox is otherwise "known to function properly when trimmed and sailed by
others," pointing to a trim/loading issue specific to a given boat rather
than a design flaw. The suggested diagnostic experiment: temporarily load 500
lb first in the bow, then in the stern, and compare tacking behavior in each
condition.

**The counter-view, on lee helm and safety.** A boat's helm "should be
neutral towards weather," so that letting go of the tiller lets her head up
(slowly) into the wind rather than risk a **knock-down** — a boat needing
much rudder deflection just to hold off the wind suffers "excess drag and
accelerated wear on rudder fittings."

**The fuller defense of the weight-aft diagnosis** holds that a boat trimmed
head-to-wind-stable with the tiller free simply **will not tack** — it will
only drift backward downwind (the image offered: a well-designed **badminton
shuttlecock**, whose favorable weight-to-lateral-plane distribution is
exactly why it "comes about really well"). **Gavin Atkin's rules for
designers** are cited as never recommending a sail plan set aft of the boat's
combined center of displacement and lateral plane. Two regimes are
distinguished: at speed and under way, a neutral-to-weather helm is correct,
but getting a stalled or anchored boat to **fall off onto a new tack** is the
opposite problem, needing the reverse weight bias. A **lee helm is not
necessarily less safe** than a neutral one in a knockdown ("you're still
going over" either way) — citing **Francis Chichester**'s storm tactic of
letting go the tiller and running off downwind under bare poles in a gale, a
maneuver that depends on mass positioned well **upwind of windage**, i.e.
functionally a strong lee helm. A secondary mechanism is also on record:
**weather helm is partly produced by hull-shape asymmetry under heel** (the
bow driving down / stern lifting), an effect judged **less pronounced on the
Paradox** given its modest beam.

The theoretical disagreement between the neutral-helm and weight-aft/lee-helm
positions was aired but not settled, and the practical question of why
"Whisper" specifically won't complete a tack remained unanswered beyond the
general weight-forward/weight-aft diagnosis.

## A second round: weathercocking, ballast, and rudder stall

The same weight-forward/weight-aft question resurfaced when "Whisper," sailed
in light air, again stopped dead, nose into the wind, on a tack. From
experience aboard "[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)": too much weight
forward makes a Paradox hang nose-to-wind on a tack, while too much weight aft
keeps her from coming up into the wind at all. Moving ballast aft after
launch fixed the same problem on that boat, apparently settling it for a
season, but the trouble recurred after adding more gear and a second anchor
over a winter layup — pointing to too much ballast overall, correctable by
experimenting with adding and removing ballast to isolate the cause.

The same weight-aft diagnosis recurs elsewhere under the term
"weathervaning," with two standard fixes: trim more weight into the boat's
stern, or move the sail forward (raking the mast, or relocating the sail's
attachment point).

A complementary theory, drawn from Phil Bolger's writing on sharpies: the
lighter a boat's displacement, the less pronounced its weathercocking
tendency, since low momentum lets the hull stop dead when tacking. A related
**rudder-stall risk** is also on record — a large-rudder-area boat tempts the
helmsman into applying too much rudder to force the bow through the wind
before way is lost, turning the rudder into a brake; the recommended
technique is to use as little rudder as possible to carve the turn while
retaining way, falling off to regather speed if the boat slows too much, or
kicking the stern over with an oar stroke or two.

A counter-view holds the sharpie comparison overstated: at roughly **1,400
lb** and 14 ft, a Paradox is "hardly a lightweight Sharpie," and the behavior
is more likely a balance issue — a sailing canoe shows the same
weathercocking if trim is neglected. The rudder is not a foil suited to high
angles of attack and stalls easily. Taken together the record airs competing
theories (weight trim, sail position, hull momentum, rudder stall) without a
single resolution specific to "Whisper," and no follow-up report survives of
an on-the-water experiment to isolate the cause.

## Glen's CE/CLR diagnosis for lee helm, and a motor-weight tangent

The **opposite** symptom from the tacking trouble above is also on record:
"Whisper" at one point needed constant **negative (lee) rudder** just to hold
her on course, the amount varying by point of sail, with no room on the spars
to shift the sail fore-or-aft to compensate.

The most direct textbook-style explanation on record of helm balance: **lee
helm** (needing negative rudder) means the boat's **center of effort (CE)**
sits too far **forward** of its **center of lateral resistance (CLR)**,
causing the bow to fall away from the wind. **Four possible fixes** apply,
usable alone or in combination: **rake the mast aft**, if the rig allows it;
**tie the halyard to the forward-most point on the yard**; **move weight
aft**; or some blend of the three. This is a more theoretical,
textbook-grounded diagnosis than the practical weight-distribution/trim
debate above — and notably diagnoses the **opposite** helm condition (lee
helm, too little weather helm) from what that earlier debate mostly
concerned.

A motor-weight theory runs alongside the sail-balance one: a big motor
mounted on the stern is "especially bad" without enough load to sink the boat
to her lines, echoing trouble on another micro caused by a heavy outboard
hung too far aft. "Whisper" carried a **55 lb motor** on the stern, called
"part of my problem," with a search for a suitably light replacement turning
up a **Yamaha 2.5** (about 37 lb) of uncertain adequacy and **Honda** as the
only other option under consideration. Neither weight is "that light
either" — a **27 lb, 2.5 hp short-shaft Nissan** (used on a dinghy and a Sea
Pearl trimaran, mounted low to the water) is a one-person-liftable motor that
starts easily and pushes the trimaran to hull speed (5.5 kt); two-stroke
motors run lighter than four-strokes, "though dirtier." Whether the lee-helm
symptom was actually a sail-balance, ballast, or motor-weight issue was not
settled. See [Whisper](/boats/whisper.md) for the boat-level record, and
[weight, ballast, and displacement](/design/weight-and-ballast.md) for
"Whisper"'s other loaded-weight and outboard data points this adds to.

A further diagnostic **experiment**, distinct from a direct fix: furl the
sail and go under **bare poles** in a breeze — if the boat **rounds up into
the wind**, that indicates **too much ballast forward**; then try again with
the sail raised but not sheeted in tight, rudder neutral, and see whether she
still rounds up (she should not, if trimmed correctly). The suggested remedy,
if the bare-poles test rounds up, is simply to **move ballast aft**.

### Glen's method for finding CE and CLR by hand

A halyard-position change is on record as having fixed helm balance aboard
"Zoë" (see
["Building encouragement" — a Cumberland Sound trip report](/people/glen-maxwell/build-and-zoe.md)),
raising the question of how far in front of the CLR the CE actually sits, if
at all.

No numeric CLR has been worked out for "Zoë," but a **low-tech method** for
finding both figures by hand is on record in full:

- **CLR**: cut a scale drawing of the underwater profile (hull plus any
  appendages — rudder, keel, etc. — with prop openings or similar cut away),
  fold it so it becomes stiff, and balance it on a knife edge; the balance
  point is the CLR.
- **CE**: cut a scale drawing of the sail, pin a weighted string (a
  ballpoint pen works) through each corner in turn and let it hang freely,
  tracing a line down the string each time; on Paradox's four-sided sail this
  yields a four-sided bounded area, and the CE lies at its center.

The exercise has not actually been run for Paradox with the **rudder in the
down position**, and the simple method has one known gap: it does **not**
account for the [chine runners](/design/chine-runners-and-windward-performance.md),
which add roughly **2 sq ft** of lateral-resistance area of their own.

The sail plan can be shifted fore-and-aft by repositioning the **halyard
attach point** on the yard and by changing the **tack-fitting length**, to
balance the helm or deliberately add weather helm. A safety point not
recorded elsewhere in the archive: **lee helm is dangerous for a
single-hander**, because if the helmsman falls overboard, the resulting loss
of weight aft throws the boat into lee helm and she will **jibe off downwind
away from him** — a reason some single-handers, per advice attributed to a
Coast Guardsman on the theme of drowned men being recovered with their fly
open, make a habit of **urinating into a jug** rather than leaning over the
side. See [Glen C. Maxwell](/people/glen-maxwell/build-and-zoe.md) for the
full exchange.

No reader is recorded actually measuring "Whisper"'s or "Zoë"'s CE/CLR
against each other, but this stands as the fullest general **method** the
archive records for a builder to find both figures for their own boat.

## Dave Bolduc: directional stability as a deliberate trade-off, not a flaw

[Dave Bolduc](/people/bolduc/dave.md), drawing on his own long firsthand
experience sailing **[Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md)**, and
his familiarity (short of ownership) with **Paradox** and **[Swamp
Thing](/matt-layden/swamp-thing.md)**, generalized about trim and tacking
across all three of Matt Layden's small-cruiser designs — the fullest
cross-design comparison of tacking/maneuverability behavior in the archive.

**Trim practice on Little Cruiser.** Dave and Mindy Bolduc are, in his words,
"careful" about how they ballast the boat: most heavy gear is kept
**amidships**, with lighter items toward the ends — except when sailing to
windward all day, when they shift weight slightly **forward** (mainly by
having Mindy "sit farther up"), and when running downwind in heavy air, when
they both shift **farther aft**. This is a concrete example of active,
point-of-sail-dependent crew trim on a Layden boat, complementing the
theoretical bow/stern-weight debate above for Paradox specifically.

**Motor weight hurts tacking/performance.** A stern-mounted outboard
**"definitely affects the boat's performance adversely."** Dave's own **3.3
hp Mercury (28 lb)** was, in his judgment, probably too heavy for Little
Cruiser's **narrow transom**; for shorter trips he would use a lighter 2 hp
motor, or leave the motor off entirely and use the
[yuloh](/design/yuloh-and-stowed-rig-retention.md) instead. This is an
independent, cross-design echo of the outboard-weight/tacking concerns David
Beard raised for Paradox specifically (his own 65 lb four-stroke outboard) in
the debate above and in
[weight, ballast, and displacement](/design/weight-and-ballast.md).

**Paradox is "very stiff tracking" — a deliberate design trade-off.** Dave
gave the clearest design-level explanation on record for the tacking
difficulty debate above: Paradox **"is indeed very stiff tracking and at
times hard to tack at slow speeds,"** a consequence of her **wide transom**
and **large rudder** — slamming the rudder hard over "acts like a big brake"
rather than turning the boat. The recommended technique is to **nudge the
tiller over gradually**, a little at a time, rather than putting the helm
hard over, to complete a tack. This is not a flaw but a direct consequence of
[Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)'s design priority for Paradox
specifically: **"Matt's emphasis in Paradox was to get as much directional
stability as possible."**

**Contrast with Little Cruiser and Swamp Thing.** Layden's other two designs,
**Swamp Thing** and **Little Cruiser**, "tack more easily," but as a
trade-off "tend to wander more" (i.e. sacrifice some of Paradox's
straight-line directional stability for easier maneuvering). **Swamp
Thing** — Dave's favorite-sailing Layden design (see
[Swamp Thing](/matt-layden/swamp-thing.md)) — is, "believe it or not," the
**most maneuverable of Matt's designs** thanks to her
[chine runners](/design/chine-runners-and-windward-performance.md): she
"tacks on a dime at almost" [the remainder of this message was not recovered
from the archive].

Taken together, this reframes the tacking debate above: "Whisper"'s reported
difficulty completing tacks is not necessarily a sign of a boat mistrimmed or
an aberrant hull, but at least partly an inherent, designed-in property of
Paradox's hull form (wide transom, large rudder) traded deliberately for
directional stability — a property shared to a lesser degree by all Layden
boats but most pronounced on Paradox, and least pronounced on Swamp Thing.
This does not resolve the specific tacking trouble reported aboard "Whisper,"
but supplies an independent, credible explanation (design intent, not
necessarily poor trim) alongside the individual weight-distribution/helm-balance
theories above.

## How a standing lug rig tacks

A Bermudan or Gunter rig has a pivot at the gooseneck and the foot of the yard
that lets the sail swing around the mast when tacking. Paradox's
[lugsail rig](/design/rig.md) works differently: the rig is **designed to
stay on one side of the mast permanently** — the boom and yard do **not**
swing around to the opposite side on a tack at all. This confirms, from the
design's actual behavior, the standing-lug configuration described in
[rig and sail](/design/rig.md).

## Center of effort, rudder stall, and telltale placement as the real tacking variables

Whether the lug rig's own geometry — rather than trim or hull form —
explains reported slow tacking turns on whether the forward part of the sail
(the portion ahead of the mast, standing in for a jib) is simply too small,
and whether moving the halyard attachment further aft (see
[rig](/design/rig.md#quantifying-the-new-sailplans-effect-on-boom-furling-geometry))
would help.

The more useful mental model, though, is the sail's overall **center of
effort/center of sail area**, not a fore/aft split around the mast — proper
balance is found empirically by experimenting with halyard placement once
the boat is correctly trimmed to her lines (see the [hand method for finding
CE](#glens-method-for-finding-ce-and-clr-by-hand) above). As with most
catboats, coming about struggles if the **rudder stalls** from being pushed
to leeward too quickly — a particular risk on Paradox given how large a
fraction of the boat's total lateral area her rudder represents — and light
air makes the problem worse. Over-sheeting causes the same struggle. The
recommended technique is to sail a catboat rig **through the tack more
slowly** than a marconi fin-keeler, tending the sheets throughout so the luff
is just curling or fluttering, and to add **telltales** at the luff, leech,
and draft, plus a second set just aft of the mast to read flow where the sail
bears against the spar. The balanced lug is capable of "fantastic shape" with
less active control than other rigs, being naturally low-aspect with both CE
and center of lateral resistance sitting low — noting also that a sail cut by
a loft needs noticeably more draft than an equivalent sail on a lighter boat,
per Matt Layden's own original "new sail" specification.

A complementary view puts the tacking-speed question in perspective from the
hull-form side: compared with a centerboard dinghy, Paradox is **sluggish
coming about**, a consequence of being a **heavy-displacement craft** (14 ft,
over half a ton) with the functional equivalent of a **full-length keel** —
likely tacking just the way the numbers would predict for that hull type, an
explanation that sits alongside, rather than replaces, the
weight-distribution and rudder-stall diagnoses given elsewhere in this
article, and echoes the "stiff tracking, hard to tack at slow speeds" verdict
[above](#dave-bolduc-directional-stability-as-a-deliberate-trade-off-not-a-flaw).

Separately, "Whisper" **will tack without the outboard motor** aboard (68 lb —
a different, heavier motor than the 65 lb one referenced elsewhere in the
weight breakdown above; the archive does not reconcile the two figures).
Tacking **with** the outboard fitted proved harder to work out, trim again
being the key variable, echoing the same underlying motor-weight-vs-trim
problem generalized about for Little Cruiser above.

A related pair of handling questions remains open: how a Paradox handles
**large motorboat wakes in shallow water** — whether a change of course is
needed to deal with them or a steady course can be held — and how she
behaves running in a **following sea**. See [heavy-weather tactics — an open
question on wakes and following
seas](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#an-open-question-on-motorboat-wakes-and-following-seas)
for the related storm-handling material this sits alongside.

## A jet-ski collision at anchor, and praise for the hull

At anchor around 11:30 one night, a **drunk jet-skier collided with the side
of "Whisper."** The jet-ski's speed could not be judged directly, but based
on how much the boat rolled and the scratches left behind, the rider "wasn't
idling." The jet-skier, sensing the reaction aboard, "took off." **Nobody was
hurt**, and Whisper suffered only **cosmetic damage** — a positive data point
on the boat's having weathered the impact without structural harm: "I can
verify the sound design of the hull!!!!" This is a rare, incidental
real-world data point on hull toughness under an unplanned impact, distinct
from the deliberate rock-ledge grounding recorded above.

## Handling and feel from microcruising.com

David and Mindy Bolduc's own site adds a first-person "what it feels like to
sail her" layer that complements the tacking/trim debate above. The
pilothouse arrangement is central to their account: sailing from inside
"gives one the feeling that they are driving a 'convertible'" — when it gets
rough, they simply close the hatch and keep going, carrying no foul-weather
gear as a result. They credit the boat's short waterline with a genuine
windward advantage in a seaway: "because we are so short, we can often fit in
between the wave troughs that larger boats aren't quite able to bridge." Once
heeled, the narrow beam and hard chine "present a sharp V to the oncoming
waves," which along with low windage and a heavy loaded weight lets the boat
"maintain momentum while still sailing nicely upwind."

On surfing: in 8-ft seas with 25 knots blowing from astern, "Paradox and
Little Cruiser can probably surf at 8 or 9 knots in short bursts," applying
corrective rudder as the stern lifts to counter the boat's tendency to yaw.
Groundings are routine rather than dramatic, given the roughly 9-inch draft —
"a simple push" is all that is usually needed to get going again — and the
mast lowers on a tabernacle to duck under low bridges without having to wait
for an opening. See also
[pointing and tacking angles](/operations/pointing-and-tacking-angles.md) and
[hull speed](/operations/hull-speed.md).

## Why the seat's position damps the felt motion

A striking feature of the pilothouse layout is how little of the boat's
motion the helmsman actually feels. A film of "[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)"
beating to windward in a force 5 shows her pitching quite violently on
screen, yet none of that motion was felt aboard, because the single helm seat
sits close to the boat's **centre of pitch rotation**. The corollary is that
a second person, sitting further forward in the cabin, feels far more of the
motion: on a two-up Helford-to-Falmouth passage, whoever sat forward kept
getting seasick, prompting the crew to repeatedly swap places. This
complements the wider two-up finding that the forward berth is the
uncomfortable one (see
[crew size and cabin fit](/operations/crew-size-and-fit.md)) and the
"convertible" pilothouse feel documented above (see
[AYRS meeting talk](/references/ayrs-meeting-video.md)).

## Sailing standing up in the hatch

Because the cabin *is* the cockpit, the sailor can work the boat **standing
in the hatchway**: "The sailor can also stand while sailing, with his arms
and torso extending through the cabin hatch. Some sailors actually read a
book while standing in the cabin hatchway." A Paradox owner elaborates: "in
all but extreme conditions it is quite possible to sail standing up. I quite
often stand leaning with my elbows on the hatch cover with a book in front of
me… I have even been known to climb onto the aft deck and dangle my feet in
the water over the transom, during a quiet downwind run." This complements
the "convertible"/pilothouse feel documented above from the Bolducs.

## A one-tack-fluttering puzzle, and a contrary experience

"Whisper"'s sail set well on every point of sail except one: on a
**starboard tack**, or any point where the boom and yard lie **against the
mast**, getting the tell-tales to fly correctly left a slight **flutter on
the leading edge**; tightening the sheet enough to stop the flutter then
threw the tell-tales off, with no detectable difference in speed either way.

Flutter is more usually seen on the **leech** rather than the luff, possibly
from a stretched luff or a halyard needing tightening — though it need not be
a cause for concern. A broader observation from years sailing both [Little
Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) and Matt Layden's own Paradox: **one
tack is always better than the other**, because the mast disrupts airflow
over the sail on whichever side it sits against — "just the tradeoff you
have to live with" on a balanced lug rig with no parrel. This matches, from a
different angle, a **full-knot tacking-speed asymmetry** found aboard "Zoë"
(see [rig and sail — a tacking speed asymmetry](/design/rig.md)) — between
the two, independent builders confirm the lug rig sails measurably or
noticeably differently depending on which side the sail lies against the
mast.

A contrary data point runs the **opposite** way to that general rule: on one
boat, the **best sailing performance comes on the port tack** (sail against
the mast), with no trouble tacking from that position, whereas on the
**starboard tack** (sail in free air) she still occasionally gets "stuck"
partway through a tack and has to try again. The same whole-sail flutter
described above shows up there too, and actually gives the **best
performance**, sailing with the luff "just occasionally fluttering" — though
the sail may simply need attention. This is the first report on record of a
builder finding **better**, not worse, performance and easier tacking with
the sail held against the mast, complicating the simple "one tack is always
better" rule the other two data points had suggested.
