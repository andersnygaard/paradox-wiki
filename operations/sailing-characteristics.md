# Sailing characteristics and rig balance

See also [heavy-weather tactics](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md) for deliberate storm-handling questions (sea-anchor rudder position, lying a-hull, maximum heel) distinct from the day-to-day tacking/trim issues covered here.

How the [Paradox](/paradox.md)'s [lug rig](/components/rig/index.md) balances on different points of sail — whether beating to weather generates significant weather helm, whether that can be counteracted through sail trim rather than by holding the tiller over, and how balance shifts as the boat heels — remains an open question in the archive, alongside a related, also-open [ballast-placement question](/design/weight-and-ballast.md). It sits next to the [self-steering](/operations/self-steering.md) finding that in **light air** the Paradox holds a course well enough, by tiller-rope friction alone, to leave the helm briefly — a light-air data point that does not by itself settle how she balances **to windward** or **fully powered up**.

## A firsthand tacking/grounding lesson

A Paradox can fail to complete a tack under real conditions, with real consequences: during one solo winter river cruise, trying to claw off a lee shore, a boat came about too late and would not complete the tack, driving aground on a rock ledge. The lesson: "be careful of a lee shore if you have to claw off into the wind." See [group cruise planning and firsthand trip reports](/operations/group-cruise-reports.md) for the full trip report — a data point on real-world handling limits that the weather-helm/heeling question above still leaves open.

The same failure-to-complete-a-tack symptom has shown up on another boat, stuck head-to-wind in irons repeatedly one weekend in the Salcombe estuary, recoverable only with the yuloh — see [rig and sail — a speculative small jib](/components/rig/index.md#a-speculative-small-jib) for the fuller account and the small-jib idea it prompted.

## Real difficulty completing tacks, and successful (if inconsistent) jibing

The most detailed real-world account on record of Paradox tacking behavior comes from days of hard sailing aboard "[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)". Her **pointing ability** was not a concern — she kept up with bigger boats, if not pointing quite as high — but her **bow would not reliably come around into the wind to start a new tack**, to the point of never completing a tack without using the motor to help bring the bow through. By contrast, jibing worked, though not reliably ("very few" successful attempts) — a marked contrast with a sloop rig, which gave no such trouble.

This is a second, independent data point (after the lee-shore grounding above) of a Paradox failing to come about under a given set of conditions — attributed here to some sailing-technique or trim issue rather than a lee-shore/timing problem, but not reconciled with the generally positive tacking-angle figures in [pointing and tacking-angle expectations](/operations/pointing-and-tacking-angles.md) (90-110°, from the same "Whisper," two months earlier). Whether the cause is technique, ballast trim, sea state, or rig set-up is not resolved.

## Technique suggestions, a weight-distribution theory, and a full helm-balance debate

Tacking trouble like "Whisper"'s has drawn a range of practical technique tips and the fullest theoretical debate on record on hull trim and helm balance.

**Light-air technique.** Tacking in **light air** is sometimes frustrating even on a well-sorted boat, and can require **backwinding the main**. The standing pointing advice is that the [Paradox](/paradox.md) dislikes being sailed too close to the wind, and falling off a little to build speed before tacking usually lets her come around fine — a problem mainly in **chop or a running sea**, which slows her markedly. If caught **in irons**, the recommended recovery is to backwind the main and **motor/paddle in reverse** until back on the desired tack — slow, but potentially saves a boat from going ashore. The same two standard suggestions recur elsewhere: try **falling off** to gain speed before tacking, and check whether the boat is trimmed **down by the head** (too much weight in the bow), which "can cause a boat to miss stays."

**A full loaded-weight breakdown** for "Whisper" ran to **400 lb lead ballast** (most of it where the water tanks were meant to go), **65 lb outboard**, **190 lb** of the sailor's own body weight, **6.5 gal fuel**, **5 gal water**, and roughly **75 lb** of gear/food/ground tackle — an estimated **750 lb total**. See [weight, ballast, and displacement](/design/weight-and-ballast.md) for the boat's other ballast/trim data points this adds to.

**The weight-forward/weight-aft diagnosis.** A boat that won't come about is "almost always a victim of too much weight forward or too much rig aft" — a lesson drawn from a catamaran that would not tack until crew weight moved aft. The broader theoretical point: a sailboat should be **"astable"** — not a weathervane that points itself into the wind unassisted — and while a **neutral-to-weather helm** is correct once under way and gaining speed, the same is not true when the boat is stalled and pointed close to the wind. A Paradox is otherwise "known to function properly when trimmed and sailed by others," pointing to a trim/loading issue specific to a given boat rather than a design flaw. The suggested diagnostic experiment: temporarily load 500 lb first in the bow, then in the stern, and compare tacking behavior in each condition.

**The counter-view, on lee helm and safety.** A boat's helm "should be neutral towards weather," so that letting go of the tiller lets her head up (slowly) into the wind rather than risk a **knock-down** — a boat needing much rudder deflection just to hold off the wind suffers "excess drag and accelerated wear on rudder fittings."

**The fuller defense of the weight-aft diagnosis** holds that a boat trimmed head-to-wind-stable with the tiller free simply **will not tack** — it will only drift backward downwind (the image offered: a well-designed **badminton shuttlecock**, whose favorable weight-to-lateral-plane distribution is exactly why it "comes about really well"). Established rules for designers are cited as never recommending a sail plan set aft of the boat's combined center of displacement and lateral plane. Two regimes are distinguished: at speed and under way, a neutral-to-weather helm is correct, but getting a stalled or anchored boat to **fall off onto a new tack** is the opposite problem, needing the reverse weight bias. A **lee helm is not necessarily less safe** than a neutral one in a knockdown ("you're still going over" either way) — as in the well-known storm tactic of letting go the tiller and running off downwind under bare poles in a gale, a maneuver that depends on mass positioned well **upwind of windage**, i.e. functionally a strong lee helm. A secondary mechanism is also on record: **weather helm is partly produced by hull-shape asymmetry under heel** (the bow driving down / stern lifting), an effect judged **less pronounced on the Paradox** given its modest beam.

The theoretical disagreement between the neutral-helm and weight-aft/lee-helm positions was aired but not settled, and the practical question of why "Whisper" specifically won't complete a tack remained unanswered beyond the general weight-forward/weight-aft diagnosis.

## A second round: weathercocking, ballast, and rudder stall

The same weight-forward/weight-aft question resurfaced when "Whisper," sailed in light air, again stopped dead, nose into the wind, on a tack. From experience aboard "[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)": too much weight forward makes a Paradox hang nose-to-wind on a tack, while too much weight aft keeps her from coming up into the wind at all. Moving ballast aft after launch fixed the same problem on that boat, apparently settling it for a season, but the trouble recurred after adding more gear and a second anchor over a winter layup — pointing to too much ballast overall, correctable by experimenting with adding and removing ballast to isolate the cause.

The same weight-aft diagnosis recurs elsewhere under the term "weathervaning," with two standard fixes: trim more weight into the boat's stern, or move the sail forward (raking the mast, or relocating the sail's attachment point).

A complementary theory, drawn from published writing on sharpies: the lighter a boat's displacement, the less pronounced its weathercocking tendency, since low momentum lets the hull stop dead when tacking. A related **rudder-stall risk** is also on record — a large-rudder-area boat tempts the helmsman into applying too much rudder to force the bow through the wind before way is lost, turning the rudder into a brake; the recommended technique is to use as little rudder as possible to carve the turn while retaining way, falling off to regather speed if the boat slows too much, or kicking the stern over with an oar stroke or two.

A counter-view holds the sharpie comparison overstated: at roughly **1,400 lb** and 14 ft, a Paradox is "hardly a lightweight Sharpie," and the behavior is more likely a balance issue — a sailing canoe shows the same weathercocking if trim is neglected. See [sharpie lineage](/design/sharpie-lineage.md#whether-sharpie-theory-explains-her-handling) for the fuller question of how far sharpie theory actually applies to Paradox. The rudder is not a foil suited to high angles of attack and stalls easily. Taken together the record airs competing theories (weight trim, sail position, hull momentum, rudder stall) without a single resolution specific to "Whisper," and no follow-up report survives of an on-the-water experiment to isolate the cause.

## Glen's CE/CLR diagnosis for lee helm, and a motor-weight tangent

The **opposite** symptom from the tacking trouble above is also on record: "Whisper" at one point needed constant **negative (lee) rudder** just to hold her on course, the amount varying by point of sail, with no room on the spars to shift the sail fore-or-aft to compensate.

The most direct textbook-style explanation on record of helm balance: **lee helm** (needing negative rudder) means the boat's **center of effort (CE)** sits too far **forward** of its **center of lateral resistance (CLR)**, causing the bow to fall away from the wind. **Four possible fixes** apply, usable alone or in combination: **rake the mast aft**, if the rig allows it; **tie the halyard to the forward-most point on the yard**; **move weight aft**; or some blend of the three. This is a more theoretical, textbook-grounded diagnosis than the practical weight-distribution/trim debate above — and notably diagnoses the **opposite** helm condition (lee helm, too little weather helm) from what that earlier debate mostly concerned.

A motor-weight theory runs alongside the sail-balance one: a big motor mounted on the stern is "especially bad" without enough load to sink the boat to her lines, echoing trouble on another micro caused by a heavy outboard hung too far aft. "Whisper" carried a **55 lb motor** on the stern, called "part of my problem," with a search for a suitably light replacement turning up a **Yamaha 2.5** (about 37 lb) of uncertain adequacy and **Honda** as the only other option under consideration. Neither weight is "that light either" — a **27 lb, 2.5 hp short-shaft Nissan** (used on a dinghy and a Sea Pearl trimaran, mounted low to the water) is a one-person-liftable motor that starts easily and pushes the trimaran to hull speed (5.5 kt); two-stroke motors run lighter than four-strokes, "though dirtier." Whether the lee-helm symptom was actually a sail-balance, ballast, or motor-weight issue was not settled. See [Whisper](/boats/whisper.md) for the boat-level record, and [weight, ballast, and displacement](/design/weight-and-ballast.md) for "Whisper"'s other loaded-weight and outboard data points this adds to.

A further diagnostic **experiment**, distinct from a direct fix: furl the sail and go under **bare poles** in a breeze — if the boat **rounds up into the wind**, that indicates **too much ballast forward**; then try again with the sail raised but not sheeted in tight, rudder neutral, and see whether she still rounds up (she should not, if trimmed correctly). The suggested remedy, if the bare-poles test rounds up, is simply to **move ballast aft**.

### Glen's method for finding CE and CLR by hand

A halyard-position change is on record as having fixed helm balance aboard "Zoë" (see ["Building encouragement" — a Cumberland Sound trip report](/people/glen-maxwell/build-and-zoe.md)), raising the question of how far in front of the CLR the CE actually sits, if at all.

No numeric CLR has been worked out for "Zoë," but a **low-tech method** for finding both figures by hand is on record in full:

- **CLR**: cut a scale drawing of the underwater profile (hull plus any appendages — rudder, keel, etc. — with prop openings or similar cut away), fold it so it becomes stiff, and balance it on a knife edge; the balance point is the CLR.
- **CE**: cut a scale drawing of the sail, pin a weighted string (a ballpoint pen works) through each corner in turn and let it hang freely, tracing a line down the string each time; on Paradox's four-sided sail this yields a four-sided bounded area, and the CE lies at its center.

The exercise has not actually been run for Paradox with the **rudder in the down position**, and the simple method has one known gap: it does **not** account for the [chine runners](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md), which add roughly **2 sq ft** of lateral-resistance area of their own.

The sail plan can be shifted fore-and-aft by repositioning the **halyard attach point** on the yard and by changing the **tack-fitting length**, to balance the helm or deliberately add weather helm. A safety point not recorded elsewhere in the archive: **lee helm is dangerous for a single-hander**, because if the helmsman falls overboard, the resulting loss of weight aft throws the boat into lee helm and she will **jibe off downwind away from him** — a reason some single-handers, following a Coast Guardsman's aside on the theme of drowned men being recovered with their fly open, make a habit of **urinating into a jug** rather than leaning over the side. See [the full exchange](/people/glen-maxwell/build-and-zoe.md).

No reader is recorded actually measuring "Whisper"'s or "Zoë"'s CE/CLR against each other, but this stands as the fullest general **method** the archive records for a builder to find both figures for their own boat.

## Dave Bolduc: directional stability as a deliberate trade-off, not a flaw

Long firsthand experience sailing **[Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md)**, together with familiarity (short of ownership) with **Paradox** and **[Swamp Thing](/matt-layden/swamp-thing.md)**, generalized about trim and tacking across all three of Matt Layden's small-cruiser designs — the fullest cross-design comparison of tacking/maneuverability behavior in the archive.

**Trim practice on Little Cruiser.** The boat is ballasted "carefully": most heavy gear is kept **amidships**, with lighter items toward the ends — except when sailing to windward all day, when weight shifts slightly **forward** (mainly by having the second crew member "sit farther up"), and when running downwind in heavy air, when both crew shift **farther aft**. This is a concrete example of active, point-of-sail-dependent crew trim on a Layden boat, complementing the theoretical bow/stern-weight debate above for Paradox specifically.

**Motor weight hurts tacking/performance.** A stern-mounted outboard **"definitely affects the boat's performance adversely."** A **3.3 hp Mercury (28 lb)** was, in this judgment, probably too heavy for Little Cruiser's **narrow transom**; for shorter trips a lighter 2 hp motor, or leaving the motor off entirely and using the [yuloh](/components/yuloh.md) instead, worked better. This is an independent, cross-design echo of the outboard-weight/tacking concerns raised for Paradox specifically (a 65 lb four-stroke outboard) in the debate above and in [weight, ballast, and displacement](/design/weight-and-ballast.md).

**Paradox is "very stiff tracking" — a deliberate design trade-off.** The clearest design-level explanation on record for the tacking difficulty debate above: Paradox **"is indeed very stiff tracking and at times hard to tack at slow speeds,"** a consequence of her **wide transom** and **large rudder** — slamming the rudder hard over "acts like a big brake" rather than turning the boat. The recommended technique is to **nudge the tiller over gradually**, a little at a time, rather than putting the helm hard over, to complete a tack. This is not a flaw but a direct consequence of [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)'s design priority for Paradox specifically: **"Matt's emphasis in Paradox was to get as much directional stability as possible."**

**Contrast with Little Cruiser and Swamp Thing.** Layden's other two designs, **Swamp Thing** and **Little Cruiser**, "tack more easily," but as a trade-off "tend to wander more" (i.e. sacrifice some of Paradox's straight-line directional stability for easier maneuvering). **Swamp Thing** — a favorite-sailing Layden design (see [Swamp Thing](/matt-layden/swamp-thing.md)) — is, "believe it or not," the **most maneuverable of Matt's designs** thanks to her [chine runners](/chine-runners/windward-performance.md): she "tacks on a dime at almost" [the remainder of this message was not recovered from the archive].

Taken together, this reframes the tacking debate above: "Whisper"'s reported difficulty completing tacks is not necessarily a sign of a boat mistrimmed or an aberrant hull, but at least partly an inherent, designed-in property of Paradox's hull form (wide transom, large rudder) traded deliberately for directional stability — a property shared to a lesser degree by all Layden boats but most pronounced on Paradox, and least pronounced on Swamp Thing. This does not resolve the specific tacking trouble reported aboard "Whisper," but supplies an independent, credible explanation (design intent, not necessarily poor trim) alongside the individual weight-distribution/helm-balance theories above.

## How a standing lug rig tacks

A Bermudan or Gunter rig has a pivot at the gooseneck and the foot of the yard that lets the sail swing around the mast when tacking. Paradox's [lugsail rig](/components/rig/index.md) works differently: the rig is **designed to stay on one side of the mast permanently** — the boom and yard do **not** swing around to the opposite side on a tack at all. This confirms, from the design's actual behavior, the standing-lug configuration described in [rig and sail](/components/rig/index.md).

## Center of effort, rudder stall, and telltale placement as the real tacking variables

Whether the lug rig's own geometry — rather than trim or hull form — explains reported slow tacking turns on whether the forward part of the sail (the portion ahead of the mast, standing in for a jib) is simply too small, and whether moving the halyard attachment further aft (see [rig](/components/rig/index.md#quantifying-the-new-sailplans-effect-on-boom-furling-geometry)) would help.

The more useful mental model, though, is the sail's overall **center of effort/center of sail area**, not a fore/aft split around the mast — proper balance is found empirically by experimenting with halyard placement once the boat is correctly trimmed to her lines (see the [hand method for finding CE](#glens-method-for-finding-ce-and-clr-by-hand) above). As with most catboats, coming about struggles if the **rudder stalls** from being pushed to leeward too quickly — a particular risk on Paradox given how large a fraction of the boat's total lateral area her rudder represents — and light air makes the problem worse. Over-sheeting causes the same struggle. The recommended technique is to sail a catboat rig **through the tack more slowly** than a marconi fin-keeler, tending the sheets throughout so the luff is just curling or fluttering, and to add **telltales** at the luff, leech, and draft, plus a second set just aft of the mast to read flow where the sail bears against the spar. The balanced lug is capable of "fantastic shape" with less active control than other rigs, being naturally low-aspect with both CE and center of lateral resistance sitting low — noting also that a sail cut by a loft needs noticeably more draft than an equivalent sail on a lighter boat, per Matt Layden's own original "new sail" specification.

A complementary view puts the tacking-speed question in perspective from the hull-form side: compared with a centerboard dinghy, Paradox is **sluggish coming about**, a consequence of being a **heavy-displacement craft** (14 ft, over half a ton) with the functional equivalent of a **full-length keel** — likely tacking just the way the numbers would predict for that hull type, an explanation that sits alongside, rather than replaces, the weight-distribution and rudder-stall diagnoses given elsewhere in this article, and echoes the "stiff tracking, hard to tack at slow speeds" verdict [above](#dave-bolduc-directional-stability-as-a-deliberate-trade-off-not-a-flaw).

Windward passage speed lags a dinghy's in the same way, distinct from the tacking maneuver itself: sailing in company with other boats on a course to windward, the boat is usually the last of the fleet to arrive — "but when I do I am usually on my second or third mug of coffee."[55]

Separately, "Whisper" **will tack without the outboard motor** aboard (68 lb — a different, heavier motor than the 65 lb one referenced elsewhere in the weight breakdown above; the archive does not reconcile the two figures). Tacking **with** the outboard fitted proved harder to work out, trim again being the key variable, echoing the same underlying motor-weight-vs-trim problem generalized about for Little Cruiser above.

A related pair of handling questions remains open: how a Paradox handles **large motorboat wakes in shallow water** — whether a change of course is needed to deal with them or a steady course can be held — and how she behaves running in a **following sea**. See [heavy-weather tactics — an open question on wakes and following seas](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#an-open-question-on-motorboat-wakes-and-following-seas) for the related storm-handling material this sits alongside.

## A jet-ski collision at anchor, and praise for the hull

At anchor around 11:30 one night, a **drunk jet-skier collided with the side of "Whisper."** The jet-ski's speed could not be judged directly, but based on how much the boat rolled and the scratches left behind, the rider "wasn't idling." The jet-skier, sensing the reaction aboard, "took off." **Nobody was hurt**, and Whisper suffered only **cosmetic damage** — a positive data point on the boat's having weathered the impact without structural harm: "I can verify the sound design of the hull!!!!" This is a rare, incidental real-world data point on hull toughness under an unplanned impact, distinct from the deliberate rock-ledge grounding recorded above.

## Pounding: rare at anchor, occasional underway

Asked directly whether Paradox pounds, either at anchor or under sail — the question prompted by [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)'s newer [Enigma](/matt-layden/enigma.md) design, which departs from Paradox's flat sharpie bottom with a **"V" bottom at the bow** — three separate owners reported the same experience: pounding at anchor is essentially unknown, and underway it is rare, at most occasional spray tossed rather than a hull slamming into waves. One owner speculated the V-bow on Enigma was Matt's way of cutting down that spray and picking up a little extra speed, rather than a response to any real pounding problem on the flat-bottomed original.[42][43][44][45] Sailing experience with both [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) and [Swamp Thing](/matt-layden/swamp-thing.md) as well as Paradox confirmed the same experience across all three heavy-displacement, wet-forefoot hulls, with at most an occasional slap at anchor in real rough conditions — but noted the one exception found: motoring straight into a short head sea could set up pounding in Little Cruiser, a problem corrected simply by having both crew sit on one side of the boat, heeling her enough to restore a "V" to the hull's presentation to the waves.[46]

The comparison to Matt Layden's newer, lighter 12 ft design was worked out in more detail: Paradox's greater displacement and lesser rocker keep its forefoot submerged more of the time than the newer design's, which is one reason a pounding problem is more likely on the lighter boat and why its bow needed the "V" that stock Paradox does not. Phil Bolger's 15 ft "Micro" was cited as the cautionary case for the same failure mode — enough rocker to pound badly, prompting Bolger to retrofit his own V-bottomed bow to several of his flat-bottomed designs in later years. A further, more speculative case was raised for the newer 12 ft design specifically: pressed into service towed as a ship's lifeboat or tender astern of a larger vessel above hull speed, its V bow was thought likely to help with tracking as well as reducing pounding.[47][48] The original question was considered settled by these replies.[49]

## Handling and feel from microcruising.com

A first-person "what it feels like to sail her" layer complements the tacking/trim debate above. The pilothouse arrangement is central to this account: sailing from inside "gives one the feeling that they are driving a 'convertible'" — when it gets rough, the hatch simply closes and the boat keeps going, carrying no foul-weather gear as a result. The boat's short waterline gives a genuine windward advantage in a seaway: "because we are so short, we can often fit in between the wave troughs that larger boats aren't quite able to bridge." Once heeled, the narrow beam and hard chine "present a sharp V to the oncoming waves," which along with low windage and a heavy loaded weight lets the boat "maintain momentum while still sailing nicely upwind."

On surfing: in 8-ft seas with 25 knots blowing from astern, "Paradox and Little Cruiser can probably surf at 8 or 9 knots in short bursts," applying corrective rudder as the stern lifts to counter the boat's tendency to yaw. Groundings are routine rather than dramatic, given the roughly 9-inch draft — "a simple push" is all that is usually needed to get going again — and the mast lowers on a tabernacle to duck under low bridges without having to wait for an opening. See also [pointing and tacking angles](/operations/pointing-and-tacking-angles.md) and [hull speed](/operations/hull-speed.md).

## Why the seat's position damps the felt motion

A striking feature of the pilothouse layout is how little of the boat's motion the helmsman actually feels. A film of "[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)" beating to windward in a force 5 shows her pitching quite violently on screen, yet none of that motion was felt aboard, because the single helm seat sits close to the boat's **centre of pitch rotation**. The corollary is that a second person, sitting further forward in the cabin, feels far more of the motion: on a two-up Helford-to-Falmouth passage, whoever sat forward kept getting seasick, prompting the crew to repeatedly swap places. This complements the wider two-up finding that the forward berth is the uncomfortable one (see [crew size and cabin fit](/operations/crew-size-and-fit.md)) and the "convertible" pilothouse feel documented above (see [AYRS meeting talk](/references/ayrs-meeting-video.md)).

## An independent trial: hove-to by default, and self-steering by sail balance

An independent sail trial aboard "[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)" adds an outside data point to the [self-steering](/operations/self-steering.md) finding noted in the introduction above — evidence gathered on a first acquaintance with the boat rather than built up over years of an owner's own accumulated habit. With no sail set and the rudder cleated up, she was found to lie **hove-to by default, side-on to the wind** — not a deliberate manoeuvre, but simply her resting state. Under way, pointed where the sailor wants to go and trimmed to best advantage, she **holds a straight line unattended** and keeps holding it until the wind itself shifts, the sail's own balance acting **"like windvane steering"** with no constant tiller correction needed.

Tacking needed almost no helm: she picked up speed on the new tack while the helmsman did little beyond a gentle adjustment of the steering line — no **"Lee-Ho!,"** no ducking, no risk of damaged shins or cracked skulls, and no risk of capsize. Off the wind she proved **very stable**. The **99.75 sq ft lug sail** hoists from inside the cabin so easily it looks like sleight of hand, and both steering line and sheet fall readily to hand from a relaxed seat, lounging equally easy to windward or to leeward; the helmsman sits **facing forward** — no trouble for the young or supple, but a real boon to anyone with an arthritic neck.

The evidential weight sits in who is reporting it: this is not an owner's accumulated trim skill, refined over seasons, but a sailor new to the boat getting the same hands-off, self-correcting behaviour immediately, on a single day's acquaintance — independent confirmation that the trait belongs to the design itself rather than to any particular owner's practice. See ["Paradox" — DCA Bulletin 193](/references/dca-paradox-gilchrist.md) for the fuller trial, including its windward-performance verdict.[56]

## Sailing standing up in the hatch

Because the cabin *is* the cockpit, the sailor can work the boat **standing in the hatchway**: "The sailor can also stand while sailing, with his arms and torso extending through the cabin hatch. Some sailors actually read a book while standing in the cabin hatchway." A Paradox owner elaborates: "in all but extreme conditions it is quite possible to sail standing up. I quite often stand leaning with my elbows on the hatch cover with a book in front of me… I have even been known to climb onto the aft deck and dangle my feet in the water over the transom, during a quiet downwind run." This complements the "convertible"/pilothouse feel documented above.

## A one-tack-fluttering puzzle, and a contrary experience

"Whisper"'s sail set well on every point of sail except one: on a **starboard tack**, or any point where the boom and yard lie **against the mast**, getting the tell-tales to fly correctly left a slight **flutter on the leading edge**; tightening the sheet enough to stop the flutter then threw the tell-tales off, with no detectable difference in speed either way.

Flutter is more usually seen on the **leech** rather than the luff, possibly from a stretched luff or a halyard needing tightening — though it need not be a cause for concern. A broader observation from years sailing both [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) and Matt Layden's own Paradox: **one tack is always better than the other**, because the mast disrupts airflow over the sail on whichever side it sits against — "just the tradeoff you have to live with" on a balanced lug rig with no parrel. This matches, from a different angle, a **full-knot tacking-speed asymmetry** found aboard "Zoë" (see [rig and sail — a tacking speed asymmetry](/components/rig/index.md)) — between the two, independent builders confirm the lug rig sails measurably or noticeably differently depending on which side the sail lies against the mast.

A contrary data point runs the **opposite** way to that general rule: on one boat, the **best sailing performance comes on the port tack** (sail against the mast), with no trouble tacking from that position, whereas on the **starboard tack** (sail in free air) she still occasionally gets "stuck" partway through a tack and has to try again. The same whole-sail flutter described above shows up there too, and actually gives the **best performance**, sailing with the luff "just occasionally fluttering" — though the sail may simply need attention. This is the first report on record of a builder finding **better**, not worse, performance and easier tacking with the sail held against the mast, complicating the simple "one tack is always better" rule the other two data points had suggested.

## A new owner's lee-helm and immersion questions

A newly relocated owner sailing "[Enuf](/boats/enuf.md)" — bought secondhand and shipped to Norway, see [people/robert-biegler.md](/people/robert-biegler.md) — reported the same lee-helm symptom on her first two short outings: compensating with the rudder produced a lot of leeway, roughly **25 degrees** on the first sail. On the second outing, with a bit more wind (5-8 kt) and sitting to leeward, the combined effect of immersing the [chine runner](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md) more deeply and shifting the sail's center of effort further to leeward eliminated the lee helm and cut the leeway to roughly **10 degrees**. At the time the boat carried only **140 kg** of ballast and no cruising stores, well short of a full loaded cruising trim; the open question left was whether the deeper immersion a full cruising load brings would finish the job of curing the lee helm and improve pointing further.[52]

Two independent replies confirmed deeper immersion and correct trim are indeed the fix, converging with the CE/CLR diagnosis already on record above. Sailing "[Faith](/boats/faith.md)" to windward needs only a very tiny amount of weather helm, on any point of sail; the same trim that gives that light weather helm also gives less leeway, and the key is not to sheet the sail in too tightly — treating it, especially close-hauled, as if it were a junk sail with the wind striking the cloth at barely 2-3 degrees of incidence rather than hauled hard in. Lee helm signals a bow riding too high: trimming with more ballast to sink the stem further is the fix, and a correctly trimmed boat both carries less leeway and lets the designed waterline exit cleanly at the transom without drag, with the stem sitting some 3-4 inches under water. Built to plan, a Paradox carries **180 kg/397 lb of lead ballast and 70 kg/154 lb of water ballast** — a specific target figure against which the 140 kg reported above is markedly short.[53]

A second reply gave a concrete figure for how much ballast a Paradox the size of "[Zero](/boats/aussie-paradox.md)" needs to sail well on all points with stores aboard: **200 kg**, an amount that still demands some agility to work the foredeck when setting the anchor.[54]

## A 14-degree heel target, and near-neutral helm running

A design-level heel-angle figure, distinct from the maximum-safe-heel data recorded on [heavy-weather tactics](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#rudder-position-under-a-sea-anchor), sets the boat's optimum working angle for windward performance: chine runners are most effective at about **14 degrees** of heel, ballasted so the windward chine runner just breaks the surface — at that angle the transom, apart from a "smidgen," clears the water entirely.[57] A closely matching, independently measured figure gives **14.5 degrees**, at a wind speed of 10 kt (Force 3) and a displacement of 640 kg (1,410 lb), as the point where "her windward chine runner will skim the surface of the water. She is at her optimum."[58] For the underlying lateral-resistance mechanism, see [chine runners: how they work](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md).

A related tenderness comparison, made against a different small boat trimmed the same day: "by comparison with 'Faith' I found she was more tender, but like the former she hardened up when heeling at about **15 degrees**" — a close, corroborating figure alongside the 14/14.5-degree chine-runner target above, taken from a stiffness rather than a lateral-resistance perspective.[59]

Running before the wind produces a further, counter-intuitive balance finding, echoing the general self-steering-while-running record already documented (see [self-steering — an Autohelm 800 fitted to two boats, and dated firsthand course-holding instances](/operations/self-steering.md#an-autohelm-800-fitted-to-two-boats-and-dated-firsthand-course-holding-instances)): although the sail's centre of effort sits far off the centreline running, which would suggest weather helm, a Paradox in fact carries "virtually neutral helm" and can hold a course unattended for minutes at a time in a smooth sea and steady wind, "even if the crew changes position."[58] The same neutral balance is stated as the design's own working target on the wind too, independent of point of sail: **"She will sail best with a neutral helm."**[60]

## No Portsmouth Yardstick rating on record

No Paradox has a registered **Portsmouth Yardstick** rating — the handicap system used to let dissimilar one-design boats race against each other on a level footing — and no attempt to obtain one for the hull and standard sail plan is recorded.[50] The idea drew a considered decline rather than an answer: competitive sailing runs against the whole point of owning a Paradox, since arriving quicker or slower, or not at all, is beside the point of a boat built as a comfortable home rather than a racer.[51]

[56] "Paradox," *DCA Bulletin* 193: 45-47 (Dinghy Cruising Association), Duncan Gilchrist (`source-articles/gilchrist/paradox-by-duncan-gilchrist/source.pdf`) — an independent sail trial aboard "Little Jim" at Chew Valley Lake SC, October 2006: with no sail up and the rudder cleated up she lies hove-to side-on to the wind; under way she holds a straight line unattended until the wind shifts, the sail acting effectively like windvane steering, with no constant tiller adjustment; she picks up speed through a tack on no more than a gentle adjustment of the steering line, with no "Lee-Ho!", no risk to shins or skulls and no capsize risk; off-wind she is very stable; the 99.75 sq ft lug sail hoists from inside the cabin so easily it looks like sleight of hand; steering line and sheet fall readily to hand and the helmsman sits facing forward, a real boon to anyone with an arthritic neck.

With a wind speed of 10 knots (Force 3) and a displacement of 640 kilos (1,410 lbs) her windward chine runner will skim the surface of the water. She is at her optimum"; running before the wind, although the sail's centre of effort sits far from the centreline, the boat carries "virtually neutral helm...
