# Self-steering

The [Paradox](/paradox.md)'s enclosed, sailed-from-inside layout and simple
[balanced-lug rig](/design/rig.md) make unattended self-steering a practical
question: a boat that holds a course on her own for periods lets a solo
sailor leave the tiller to handle ground tackle, sails, or navigation from
below, complementing the cockpit-worked
[anchoring technique](/operations/ground-tackle.md) already documented for
solo, hands-free operation near anchorages.

## Light-air self-steering via tiller-rope friction

**In light air** a Paradox will sail herself with a little tweaking of the
tiller ropes: the helm seems content to stay where it is put, an effect
attributed to **friction in the tiller-rope setup** — the boat is steered by
ropes led to the tiller from the helm station rather than a direct tiller arm
reachable from outside, consistent with the sailed-from-inside layout on the
[trunk](/paradox.md). On one overnighter aboard "Zoë" the crew was able to
**move around inside and make a sandwich and a drink** without the boat's
course changing much, if at all — a concrete demonstration of unattended
light-air steadiness.

## Locking the tiller rope, and why most builders don't bother

A dedicated tiller-rope lock is not standard equipment, and the fullest
practical exchange on the question ends up arguing against needing one at
all. Two working lock designs have nonetheless been built: a pair of roughly
3-inch discs, one fixed under the side deck and the other on a bolt passed
through it, clamp the tiller rope between them when the bolt's disc is
screwed up tight; and a two-piece plywood clamp, one piece V-notched to hold
the rope and screwed to bulkhead 3, the other piece bolted over it with a
wingnut backed by a spring so the rope can be pulled free without undoing the
nut, achieves the same result. Both designs work by simple friction, and
either must resist a pull that can come from either direction as the rudder
loads up.

Against building one at all, the case runs: **ordinary tiller-rope friction
already holds a course** without any dedicated lock in most conditions,
consistent with the light-air self-steering described above, so a lock is
rarely necessary during normal sailing — a sailor can be seen standing up in
the cockpit with only the mainsheet cleated off and the tiller rope
un-locked, relying on friction alone. The one situation where a lock, or at
least a firmly held rope, genuinely matters is **sailing in shallow water
with the rudder partly raised**, when tiller loads can become surprisingly
large. Set against that convenience, at least one builder actively dislikes
steering by tiller rope at all, never feeling in full control of the rudder
regardless of how the rope's tension was set, and preferring to steer by
tiller directly (holding it in the small of the back while motoring, freeing
both hands) — a dissatisfaction serious enough to prompt sketching out a
wheel-and-pulley or Bowden-cable conversion mounted to one side of the
cockpit, to shorten the rope run and improve feel, though no such conversion
has been built. On balance, a builder weighing whether to add a lock at all
is best served waiting to judge the tiller rope's tension and friction under
actual sailing and motoring conditions before building anything, since the
friction-only approach has repeatedly proven adequate.

## Hands-free capability matters more in strong wind

Light-air steadiness doesn't extend automatically to a breeze: being able to
**free oneself from the tiller in stronger winds** is important, for example
to go **forward and reef**. On boats without the Paradox's
reef-from-the-helm-station arrangement, an unattended tiller can move "mighty
fast" under a **weather helm** and jibe the boat before the sailor can reach
the mast to act — a safety-margin concern rather than a mere convenience (see
[sailing characteristics and rig balance](/operations/sailing-characteristics.md)
for how weather helm behaves specifically on a Paradox under the lug rig).

## A purpose-built option: wind-vane self-steering

The book **_Wind Vane Self-Steering_ by Bill Belcher** contains plans for a
"very simple, very cheap" plywood wind vane judged to be a good fit for the
Paradox — see
[Wind Vane Self-Steering (book)](/references/wind-vane-self-steering-book.md).
No build of this vane on a Paradox is recorded.

A separate, easy-and-cheap self-steering rudder/vane design published on the
**Wharram** catamaran-building website
(`www.wharram.com/building_image1.shtml` and `_image2.shtml`) was flagged as a
possible adaptation; no discussion, build, or outcome is recorded.

## Auto-pilot versus wind-vane self-steering: a practical comparison

The twin-rudder/centerline-mount idea raised in "[Double
Rudders](/design/rudder.md#a-twin-rudder-proposal-for-a-centerline-outboard-judged-not-worth-the-complexity)"
also raises the question of what type of self-steering gear suits a Paradox
and where it could be mounted without fouling the mainsheet or the rudder.
The fullest practical comparison on record of the two main options runs as
follows:

- **Electric auto-pilot** suits simple **one- or two-day cruises** well: a
  single deep-cycle battery gives an auto-pilot plenty of power for several
  days aboard a well-balanced boat, and Paradox's layout suits it especially
  well since the actuator can be mounted inside the hull, connected to the
  tiller, and kept entirely out of the weather. An auto-pilot has proved
  itself on a 17 ft cruiser, even finding it worthwhile for crossing a
  single lake.
- **Wind-vane self-steering** becomes more practical for **longer voyages**,
  for the obvious reasons that it needs no electrical power and has nothing
  to fail electrically — but simple vane designs of the kind used by
  Marin-Marie or John Riding are notoriously unreliable, and the smaller the
  boat, the less practical they tend to be: builders keep adding features to
  compensate until the mechanism is no longer simple, reliable, or even
  feasible on a small boat. A **vane/paddle gear** is the best of the
  wind-powered designs, but would have to be built from scratch for a boat
  this small — the vane and paddle themselves are no problem, but the
  gearing and linkage between them are the real design challenge. John
  Letcher's book *Self-Steering for Sailing Craft* is the best available
  reference for designing one that would actually work on a small boat.
- The overall recommendation, even for long-distance cruising, leans toward
  the **auto-pilot**: it is simpler and cheaper than other self-steering
  gear, easy to use, and — while it may fail, as any gear may — a spare
  auto-pilot takes up no more stowage room than a spare paddle or vane would
  for a wind-vane rig. Power need not be a limiting factor either: very
  small generators taking up under a cubic foot of space could recharge a
  deep-cycle battery, or a wind generator could be mounted on the transom.

## A trim-tab wind vane and Paradox's folding rudder

Self-steering gear has performed well on other small boats by way of
comparison: a **windvane self-steering system on a Folksong**, using a tab
mounted on the rudder, proved "very successful"; a **Hasler SP 90** wind
vane on a Round Britain boat was rated excellent; and a Buccaneer's
**Autohelm** electric auto-pilot, powered by a battery kept charged with a
**Wasp wind generator**, "worked perfectly well" too. For UK coastal
cruising specifically, wind-vane steering is preferable to an Autohelm
mainly because it avoids the continuous whirring sound the electric unit
makes underway.

A **Hiscock-style vane** driving a trim tab bolted directly to the rudder
runs into a design obstacle specific to Paradox: there is no obvious way to
attach a trim tab to a rudder that **folds upwards**. The lightest and most
suitable wind-vane unit available, the **Navik**, has the same problem — it
needs to mount outside the rudder, but Paradox's folding rudder blade makes
that mounting arrangement unclear. Vane-operated designs that mount to one
side of the hull instead are all too large and heavy for a boat Paradox's
size.

The resolution: to keep the rudder's fold-up feature while still using a
trim tab, operate the tab through a **cable running in a flexible housing
(Bowden-type)**, of the kind used for a bicycle brake or a car's throttle
linkage — giving a fully flexible joint between the tab and its control line
that would move with the rudder as it folds, rather than requiring a rigid
linkage that would bind or break.

## Weighing a tillerpilot against a genuine wind vane

A small electric **tillerpilot** is the most practical choice short of an
ocean voyage, best mounted **longitudinally along the tiller line** rather
than athwartships across the tiller — the same orientation used for Glen
Maxwell's own tillerpilot aboard "Zoë." For a genuine wind vane, two
Paradox-specific obstacles stand out: **mainsheet fouling** (worst mounted on
the transom, reducible but not eliminated by a cantilevered mount set as far
aft as possible) and the need to **beach the boat normally**, which means any
water paddle or trim tab must be raisable and must not block the rudder's own
kick-up action. One sketch for resolving both: a small horizontally-pivoting
vane (mounted either right-side up or upside down) coupled by a push/pull
cable to a trim tab built into the lower rudder's trailing edge, with a small
tiller off the tab's starboard side so the cable's own length could still let
the rudder (and tab) kick up — the cable could be routed to avoid binding
through most of the rudder's travel, but fully inverting the rudder blade
might still defeat it. On balance, the smallest available tillerpilot mounted
longitudinally right next to the helm is judged "darn near foolproof" given
the boat's already-demonstrated ability to hold a course, and a wind
generator is unlikely to fit the stern to power anything larger.

Anything mounted on the transom risks fouling the mainsheet, especially
during an unintentional gybe in a crowded anchorage, while a **tiller pilot
fitted inside the hull** (as aboard "Zoë") sidesteps the problem entirely.
One alternative untried on any Paradox: a **powerful wind vane not needing a
trim tab or paddle**, mounted forward of the mast, where the tiller rope
already passes anyway. Cruising experience aboard "[little
Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)" suggests purpose-built self-steering gear would
be overkill for Paradox's typical use: in rough weather the boat needs little
adjustment once the steering line is locked off, and in calm conditions (such
as returning from the Isle of Wight) a sailor can read a book, checking
course every couple of pages. [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)
getting some sleep while still under way during his Watertribe Challenge win
is the clearest existing evidence that Paradox does not demand elaborate
self-steering gear to sail unattended for a stretch — though heavy motoring,
rather than sailing, might change that calculus.

## A VectaVane director's skeptical outside view

Group consensus settled on a **tillerpilot** as the practical choice. A
pointed outside dissent on record comes from a director of **VectaVane
Ltd.**, a UK manufacturer of servo-pendulum wind-vane self-steering gear
described as the lightest of its kind: despite an obvious commercial stake in
selling more units, fitting a wind vane to Paradox is judged "a waste of
time," since the boat sails at only 10-20 degrees of heel, where double
rudders and vane gear alike are of use mainly at extreme angles; storing
spars inside the hull would foul a centerline-mounted rudder's linkage, and
Paradox's small transom leaves little room for it regardless; and Matt
Layden's own Everglades Challenge win, achieved by letting Paradox largely
sail and look after herself offshore, is offered as the clearest proof the
design already self-steers well enough without added gear.

The counter-case runs point by point. The original twin-rudder proposal was
about mounting a **windvane centrally between the rudders**, with an
outboard motor able to take the same central position when not sailing —
not a claim that Paradox needs double rudders for their own sake. Most
owners don't actually stow their spars inside the hull at all ("Petite
Lady" carries hers on the cabin top instead while trailering); a canted-out
twin-rudder mount on the transom's own edge need not be as cramped as
suggested; and VectaVane's lighter weight and lower cost, if anything, argue
for it over rival vane gears rather than against fitting one at all. On the
"she steers herself" claim specifically, that holds only for short periods in
stable wind and sea — and Matt Layden's Everglades Challenge run included
time **hove to**, not only self-steered underway. A suitable windvane system
could still benefit Paradox as a coastal cruiser, on this view, pointing to
**Sven Yrvind's** own rudder-integrated self-steering arrangement (see
[rudder and chine experiments — Sven's new rudder
system](/design/rudder.md)) as a possible model worth adapting.

## A forward-of-mast wind vane, and a case for self-steering's real value

One further design surfaced from the **James Wharram** catamaran-building
website: a simple wind vane needing no paddle at all, steered purely by wind
pressure on the vane itself, potentially mountable forward of the mast with
the steering lines led aft to the tiller — though it would look unsightly,
would not steer a downwind course, and would sit in the helmsman's line of
sight. The whole idea of a Paradox wind vane is arguably "purely academic":
for a genuine multi-day offshore passage, a more suitable boat than a
"coastal cruiser that thinks it's an offshore one" would serve better — a bar
that explicitly does not apply to **Matt Layden, Dave, and Mindy**
themselves, since in their hands Paradox already **is** an offshore boat.

Against that, the fullest case on record for wanting self-steering gear at
all comes from decades of single-handed sailing around the UK coast: the
value is chiefly the **time it frees up** for watchkeeping, navigation, and
cooking a proper meal underway (in a pressure cooker), and simply relieving
the tedium of holding a tiller for the 12-14 hours a 60-mile day's passage,
such as Dover to Brighton, can demand — a stretch expected to take even
longer aboard Paradox. Paradox may match **Dave and Mindy Bolduc**'s own
"coastal cruiser" self-description, yet in the right hands still be capable
of crossing the Atlantic — rubber inflatables, folding canoes, and "nutter
record breakers" having already done so in far less likely craft.

## Glen Maxwell's horizontal-axis wind-vane concept for "Zoë"

[Glen C. Maxwell](/people/glen-maxwell.md) worked out, still on paper, his
own design for a purpose-built wind vane for "Zoë" — the fullest concrete
vane concept on record aimed specifically at Paradox's own boom and rudder
geometry. The boom hangs so far over the rudder that it rules out a
conventional **vertical-axis vane**. His answer was a **horizontal-axis
vane** instead, arranged so the boom would simply deflect the vane out of
the way during tacking and jibing rather than fouling it, driving a **servo
tab** mounted on standoffs on the rudder's left-hand side. Mounting the tab
on standoffs, rather than bolting it directly to the rudder blade, was
chosen specifically to let the **rudder blade still kick up and retract as
designed** — the same fold-up-rudder constraint that rules out a
directly-bolted trim tab. The design was worked out mentally but not yet
built, pending a transom-and-rudder mockup to finalize the idea before
building and reporting results.

Building a wind vane at all, given a working electric autopilot already
aboard "Zoë," reflects **Glen Maxwell**'s own distrust of electronics as a
**retired electronics technician**, sharpened by a concrete failure in the
field: a brand-new **Autohelm** autopilot aboard "Hogfish Maximus" lasted
only **three days** into an Atlantic crossing made with [Chris
Morejohn](/people/chris-morejohn.md) (see [Glen Maxwell's Matt Layden
eyewitness record — an Atlantic crossing aboard "Hogfish
Maximus"](/people/glen-maxwell/matt-layden-eyewitness.md#an-atlantic-crossing-aboard-hogfish-maximus)
for the fuller account of that autopilot's failure alongside the
longitudinal-stability trouble that compounded it). A further data point
comes from multihull experience: a trimaran normally carries at least two
autopilots and would carry a wind vane too, but **multihulls accelerate too
fast for a wind vane to keep up**, since rising boat speed moves the
apparent wind forward faster than a vane can track. What draws a sailor to
a vane regardless is its simplicity and the fact that **it draws no
electrical current** at all. (By way of comparison, with **Mindy** along on
"[Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md)" there is less need of
self-steering; a long solo passage on "[Swamp
Thing](/matt-layden/swamp-thing.md)" is the scenario most likely to make
**Dave Bolduc** consider an autopilot of his own.)

## Heaving-to without deploying a sea anchor

Beyond [Glen Maxwell's own sea-anchor
technique](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#a-sea-anchor-technique-for-heaving-to-lying-a-few-degrees-off-the-wind),
a sail-carrying alternative has been proposed on principle rather than direct
experience with a single standing lugsail: reduce sail to the minimum, sheet
the boom hard to one quarter, and offset the tiller slightly toward the side
opposite the boom. In that configuration the boat should make only slight
way while falling off to leeward at perhaps half a knot, varying with wind
strength, with the remaining sail area helping stabilize her motion for a
more comfortable ride than lying bare-poled. The simplest **certain** method,
if the lugsail approach fails in practice, is simply to strike all sail and
raise the rudder, letting the hull find her own natural lie to wind and sea —
the same rudder-up/sea-anchor-optional tactic also recorded at [heavy-weather
tactics — rudder position under a sea
anchor](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#rudder-position-under-a-sea-anchor).
The lugsail method remains conjecture, untested as of this record.
