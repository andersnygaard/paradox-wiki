# Self-steering

The [Paradox](/paradox.md)'s enclosed, sailed-from-inside layout and simple [balanced-lug rig](/components/rig/index.md) make unattended self-steering a practical question: a boat that holds a course on her own for periods lets a solo sailor leave the tiller to handle ground tackle, sails, or navigation from below, complementing the cockpit-worked [anchoring technique](/operations/ground-tackle.md) already documented for solo, hands-free operation near anchorages.

## Light-air self-steering via tiller-rope friction

**In light air** a Paradox will sail herself with a little tweaking of the tiller ropes: the helm seems content to stay where it is put, an effect attributed to **friction in the tiller-rope setup** — the boat is steered by ropes led to the tiller from the helm station rather than a direct tiller arm reachable from outside, consistent with the sailed-from-inside layout on the [trunk](/paradox.md). On one overnighter aboard "Zoë" it was possible to **move around inside and make a sandwich and a drink** without the boat's course changing much, if at all — a concrete demonstration of unattended light-air steadiness.

The earliest dated account of the technique, from the December 2001 [Cedar Key to Honeymoon Island sea trial](/boats/zoe.md#sea-trials-route-cedar-key-to-clearwater), also establishes its **limit — and that the limit depends on point of sail**. The helm was light but carried enough weather helm to be worth exploiting, so a short line was made fast to the aft bulkhead through the tiller-line exit hole and attached to the tiller line with a **rolling hitch**, letting tension be added or subtracted by sliding the hitch fore and aft. On a **broad reach** the result was judged unsatisfactory: any gust sent the boat up to windward, and while a quick pull to weather would often bring her back, as often as not the adjustment had to be started over from the beginning. Even so, the arrangement bought enough freedom from the helm to read the chart, fix a position, or pour a drink. **Beating to windward**, the same setup self-steered very satisfactorily — as, it was noted, most sailboats do. The broad-reach failure was considered serious enough to warrant fitting an **Autohelm 800** from the spares locker for the following trip, specifically so that reading underway would be possible — an intention that anticipates by several years the electronic-versus-vane comparisons below.[88]

## Locking the tiller rope, and why most builders don't bother

A dedicated tiller-rope lock is not standard equipment, and the fullest practical exchange on the question ends up arguing against needing one at all. Two working lock designs have nonetheless been built: a pair of roughly 3-inch discs, one fixed under the side deck and the other on a bolt passed through it, clamp the tiller rope between them when the bolt's disc is screwed up tight; and a two-piece plywood clamp, one piece V-notched to hold the rope and screwed to bulkhead 3, the other piece bolted over it with a wingnut backed by a spring so the rope can be pulled free without undoing the nut, achieves the same result. Both designs work by simple friction, and either must resist a pull that can come from either direction as the rudder loads up.

Against building one at all, the case runs: **ordinary tiller-rope friction already holds a course** without any dedicated lock in most conditions, consistent with the light-air self-steering described above, so a lock is rarely necessary during normal sailing — a sailor can be seen standing up in the cockpit with only the mainsheet cleated off and the tiller rope un-locked, relying on friction alone. The one situation where a lock, or at least a firmly held rope, genuinely matters is **sailing in shallow water with the rudder partly raised**, when tiller loads can become surprisingly large. Set against that convenience, at least one builder actively dislikes steering by tiller rope at all, never feeling in full control of the rudder regardless of how the rope's tension was set, and preferring to steer by tiller directly (holding it in the small of the back while motoring, freeing both hands) — a dissatisfaction serious enough to prompt sketching out a wheel-and-pulley or Bowden-cable conversion mounted to one side of the cockpit, to shorten the rope run and improve feel, though no such conversion has been built. On balance, a builder weighing whether to add a lock at all is best served waiting to judge the tiller rope's tension and friction under actual sailing and motoring conditions before building anything, since the friction-only approach has repeatedly proven adequate.

## Hands-free capability matters more in strong wind

Light-air steadiness doesn't extend automatically to a breeze: being able to **free oneself from the tiller in stronger winds** is important, for example to go **forward and reef**. On boats without the Paradox's reef-from-the-helm-station arrangement, an unattended tiller can move "mighty fast" under a **weather helm** and jibe the boat before the sailor can reach the mast to act — a safety-margin concern rather than a mere convenience (see [sailing characteristics and rig balance](/operations/sailing-characteristics.md) for how weather helm behaves specifically on a Paradox under the lug rig).

## A purpose-built option: wind-vane self-steering

The book **_Wind Vane Self-Steering_ by Bill Belcher** contains plans for a "very simple, very cheap" plywood wind vane judged to be a good fit for the Paradox. No build of this vane on a Paradox is recorded. As of December 2001 the book was out of print — a check of Amazon.com and BarnesAndNoble.com turned up no copies — though still available via armchairsailor.com for $36.95.[33]

A separate, easy-and-cheap self-steering rudder/vane design published on the **Wharram** catamaran-building website (`www.wharram.com/building_image1.shtml` and `_image2.shtml`) was flagged as a possible adaptation; no discussion, build, or outcome is recorded.

## Auto-pilot versus wind-vane self-steering: a practical comparison

The twin-rudder/centerline-mount idea raised in "[Double Rudders](/components/rudder.md#a-twin-rudder-proposal-for-a-centerline-outboard-judged-not-worth-the-complexity)" also raises the question of what type of self-steering gear suits a Paradox and where it could be mounted without fouling the mainsheet or the rudder. The fullest practical comparison on record of the two main options runs as follows:

- **Electric auto-pilot** suits simple **one- or two-day cruises** well: a single deep-cycle battery gives an auto-pilot plenty of power for several days aboard a well-balanced boat, and Paradox's layout suits it especially well since the actuator can be mounted inside the hull, connected to the tiller, and kept entirely out of the weather. An auto-pilot has proved itself on a 17 ft cruiser, even finding it worthwhile for crossing a single lake.
- **Wind-vane self-steering** becomes more practical for **longer voyages**, for the obvious reasons that it needs no electrical power and has nothing to fail electrically — but simple vane designs of the kind used by Marin-Marie or John Riding are notoriously unreliable, and the smaller the boat, the less practical they tend to be: builders keep adding features to compensate until the mechanism is no longer simple, reliable, or even feasible on a small boat. A **vane/paddle gear** is the best of the wind-powered designs, but would have to be built from scratch for a boat this small — the vane and paddle themselves are no problem, but the gearing and linkage between them are the real design challenge. John Letcher's book *Self-Steering for Sailing Craft* is the best available reference for designing one that would actually work on a small boat.
- The overall recommendation, even for long-distance cruising, leans toward the **auto-pilot**: it is simpler and cheaper than other self-steering gear, easy to use, and — while it may fail, as any gear may — a spare auto-pilot takes up no more stowage room than a spare paddle or vane would for a wind-vane rig. Power need not be a limiting factor either: very small generators taking up under a cubic foot of space could recharge a deep-cycle battery, or a wind generator could be mounted on the transom.

## A trim-tab wind vane and Paradox's folding rudder

Self-steering gear has performed well on other small boats by way of comparison: a **windvane self-steering system on a Folksong**, using a tab mounted on the rudder, proved "very successful"; a **Hasler SP 90** wind vane on a Round Britain boat was rated excellent; and a Buccaneer's **Autohelm** electric auto-pilot, powered by a battery kept charged with a **Wasp wind generator**, "worked perfectly well" too. For UK coastal cruising specifically, wind-vane steering is preferable to an Autohelm mainly because it avoids the continuous whirring sound the electric unit makes underway.

A **Hiscock-style vane** driving a trim tab bolted directly to the rudder runs into a design obstacle specific to Paradox: there is no obvious way to attach a trim tab to a rudder that **folds upwards**. The lightest and most suitable wind-vane unit available, the **Navik**, has the same problem — it needs to mount outside the rudder, but Paradox's folding rudder blade makes that mounting arrangement unclear. Vane-operated designs that mount to one side of the hull instead are all too large and heavy for a boat Paradox's size.

The resolution: to keep the rudder's fold-up feature while still using a trim tab, operate the tab through a **cable running in a flexible housing (Bowden-type)**, of the kind used for a bicycle brake or a car's throttle linkage — giving a fully flexible joint between the tab and its control line that would move with the rudder as it folds, rather than requiring a rigid linkage that would bind or break.

## Weighing a tillerpilot against a genuine wind vane

A small electric **tillerpilot** is the most practical choice short of an ocean voyage, best mounted **longitudinally along the tiller line** rather than athwartships across the tiller — the same orientation used for a tillerpilot fitted aboard "Zoë." For a genuine wind vane, two Paradox-specific obstacles stand out: **mainsheet fouling** (worst mounted on the transom, reducible but not eliminated by a cantilevered mount set as far aft as possible) and the need to **beach the boat normally**, which means any water paddle or trim tab must be raisable and must not block the rudder's own kick-up action. One sketch for resolving both: a small horizontally-pivoting vane (mounted either right-side up or upside down) coupled by a push/pull cable to a trim tab built into the lower rudder's trailing edge, with a small tiller off the tab's starboard side so the cable's own length could still let the rudder (and tab) kick up — the cable could be routed to avoid binding through most of the rudder's travel, but fully inverting the rudder blade might still defeat it. On balance, the smallest available tillerpilot mounted longitudinally right next to the helm is judged "darn near foolproof" given the boat's already-demonstrated ability to hold a course, and a wind generator is unlikely to fit the stern to power anything larger.

Anything mounted on the transom risks fouling the mainsheet, especially during an unintentional gybe in a crowded anchorage, while a **tiller pilot fitted inside the hull** (as aboard "Zoë") sidesteps the problem entirely. One alternative untried on any Paradox: a **powerful wind vane not needing a trim tab or paddle**, mounted forward of the mast, where the tiller rope already passes anyway. Cruising experience aboard "[little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)" suggests purpose-built self-steering gear would be overkill for Paradox's typical use: in rough weather the boat needs little adjustment once the steering line is locked off, and in calm conditions (such as returning from the Isle of Wight) a sailor can read a book, checking course every couple of pages. [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md) getting some sleep while still under way during his Watertribe Challenge win is the clearest existing evidence that Paradox does not demand elaborate self-steering gear to sail unattended for a stretch — though heavy motoring, rather than sailing, might change that calculus.

## A VectaVane director's skeptical outside view

Group consensus settled on a **tillerpilot** as the practical choice. A pointed outside dissent on record comes from a director of **VectaVane Ltd.**, a UK manufacturer of servo-pendulum wind-vane self-steering gear described as the lightest of its kind: despite an obvious commercial stake in selling more units, fitting a wind vane to Paradox is judged "a waste of time," since the boat sails at only 10-20 degrees of heel, where double rudders and vane gear alike are of use mainly at extreme angles; storing spars inside the hull would foul a centerline-mounted rudder's linkage, and Paradox's small transom leaves little room for it regardless; and Matt Layden's own Everglades Challenge win, achieved by letting Paradox largely sail and look after herself offshore, is offered as the clearest proof the design already self-steers well enough without added gear.

The counter-case runs point by point. The original twin-rudder proposal was about mounting a **windvane centrally between the rudders**, with an outboard motor able to take the same central position when not sailing — not a claim that Paradox needs double rudders for their own sake. Most owners don't actually stow their spars inside the hull at all ("Petite Lady" carries hers on the cabin top instead while trailering); a canted-out twin-rudder mount on the transom's own edge need not be as cramped as suggested; and VectaVane's lighter weight and lower cost, if anything, argue for it over rival vane gears rather than against fitting one at all. On the "she steers herself" claim specifically, that holds only for short periods in stable wind and sea — and Matt Layden's Everglades Challenge run included time **hove to**, not only self-steered underway. A suitable windvane system could still benefit Paradox as a coastal cruiser, on this view, pointing to a rudder-integrated self-steering arrangement developed for another small boat (see [rudder and chine experiments — a new rudder system](/components/rudder.md)) as a possible model worth adapting.

## A forward-of-mast wind vane, and a case for self-steering's real value

One further design surfaced from the **James Wharram** catamaran-building website: a simple wind vane needing no paddle at all, steered purely by wind pressure on the vane itself, potentially mountable forward of the mast with the steering lines led aft to the tiller — though it would look unsightly, would not steer a downwind course, and would sit in the helmsman's line of sight. The whole idea of a Paradox wind vane is arguably "purely academic": for a genuine multi-day offshore passage, a more suitable boat than a "coastal cruiser that thinks it's an offshore one" would serve better — a bar that explicitly does not apply to owners who have taken Paradox offshore themselves, since in their hands Paradox already **is** an offshore boat.

Against that, the fullest case on record for wanting self-steering gear at all comes from decades of single-handed sailing around the UK coast: the value is chiefly the **time it frees up** for watchkeeping, navigation, and cooking a proper meal underway (in a pressure cooker), and simply relieving the tedium of holding a tiller for the 12-14 hours a 60-mile day's passage, such as Dover to Brighton, can demand — a stretch expected to take even longer aboard Paradox. Paradox may match some owners' own "coastal cruiser" self-description, yet in the right hands still be capable of crossing the Atlantic — rubber inflatables, folding canoes, and "nutter record breakers" having already done so in far less likely craft.

## Glen Maxwell's horizontal-axis wind-vane concept for "Zoë"

A design for a purpose-built wind vane for "Zoë" was worked out, still on paper — the fullest concrete vane concept on record aimed specifically at Paradox's own boom and rudder geometry. The boom hangs so far over the rudder that it rules out a conventional **vertical-axis vane**. The answer was a **horizontal-axis vane** instead, arranged so the boom would simply deflect the vane out of the way during tacking and jibing rather than fouling it, driving a **servo tab** mounted on standoffs on the rudder's left-hand side. Mounting the tab on standoffs, rather than bolting it directly to the rudder blade, was chosen specifically to let the **rudder blade still kick up and retract as designed** — the same fold-up-rudder constraint that rules out a directly-bolted trim tab. The design was worked out mentally but not yet built, pending a transom-and-rudder mockup to finalize the idea before building and reporting results.

## Sea trial and damage at the 2007 Cedar Key gathering

Two years later, the vane gear was actually built and sea-tried on "Zoë" at the **2007 Cedar Key small-boat gathering** (see [group cruise planning and firsthand trip reports — the 2006 Cedar Key gathering, and notable past attendees](/operations/group-cruise-reports.md#the-2006-cedar-key-gathering-and-notable-past-attendees) for the gathering itself), the first time the concept was tested on the water. Short-tacking down Crystal River, the mainsheet broke the gear's **oar carrier** off before a full trial could be made — the failure exposing a genuine weak spot in the design, though judged an easy fix. Despite the damage, the underlying **wind-vane mechanism** itself was checked out and found to work fine, and correcting the main-sheet-fouling problem was likewise judged straightforward.

The built unit is an **upside-down vane, rudder-head-mounted type**: the real design challenge was building something that would let the main rudder blade still kick up as designed while also clearing the end of the boom. The solution mounted the oar carrier on top of the rudder and shaped the vane carrier's own mount so the main rudder blade could still swing up into its normal stowed configuration, with the mount set far enough aft to clear the boom. A **mock-up transom**, built and mounted to a workshop wall, let the gear be developed and tuned on the bench before the move to the boat itself went smoothly; relocating the mainsheet was also needed, and the new position worked well. Had the oar carrier not been damaged by the mainsheet at Cedar Key, it likely would not have held up long-term to the loads against the rudder regardless — meaning the failure, though it cut the sea trial short, usefully exposed a problem the mock-up alone had not revealed. All the design's goals were judged met, with only tuning left before drawings would be posted for other owners to build their own.

The gear's core difficulty — keeping a windvane self-steering system clear of the mainsheet at all — is general to Paradox: several lightweight vane gears exist that could suit the boat, but working out a mounting that does not interfere with the mainsheet remains the sticking point for any of them, a difficulty distinct from (though related to) the mainsheet-fouling problem already tracked for a fixed transom-mounted vane at [a trim-tab wind vane and Paradox's folding rudder](#a-trim-tab-wind-vane-and-paradoxs-folding-rudder) above.

Building a wind vane at all, given a working electric autopilot already aboard "Zoë," reflects a distrust of electronics rooted in experience as a **retired electronics technician**, sharpened by a concrete failure in the field: a brand-new **Autohelm** autopilot aboard "Hogfish Maximus" lasted only **three days** into an Atlantic crossing made with [Chris Morejohn](/people/chris-morejohn.md) (see [Glen Maxwell's Matt Layden eyewitness record — an Atlantic crossing aboard "Hogfish Maximus"](/people/glen-maxwell/matt-layden-eyewitness.md#an-atlantic-crossing-aboard-hogfish-maximus) for the fuller account of that autopilot's failure alongside the longitudinal-stability trouble that compounded it). A further data point comes from multihull experience: a trimaran normally carries at least two autopilots and would carry a wind vane too, but **multihulls accelerate too fast for a wind vane to keep up**, since rising boat speed moves the apparent wind forward faster than a vane can track. What draws a sailor to a vane regardless is its simplicity and the fact that **it draws no electrical current** at all. (By way of comparison, with a partner along on "[Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md)" there is less need of self-steering; a long solo passage on "[Swamp Thing](/matt-layden/swamp-thing.md)" is the scenario most likely to make an owner consider an autopilot of their own.)

A **tillermaster-style electric autohelm**, tried on "Zoë" alongside the wind-vane project, worked fine over a short cruise of a few days but drained its battery faster than it could be replaced, making it a poor fit for longer time away from shore power — the same power-budget tradeoff already recorded above for an electric auto-pilot versus a wind vane. A couple of weeks after the Cedar Key sea trial, the vane gear itself was still under repair, waiting on epoxy to cure, alongside a separate fix — a pair of bow fairleads fitted to stop the mainsheet fouling the gear, the same problem the Cedar Key trial had exposed.[48][49]

The underlying motivation for a Paradox-specific vane is the boat's own **longitudinal stability**: on a Zoë passage that included at least one overnighter, very little steering was needed at all, which is what suggested a wind vane could hold a course for hours on end without the five- or ten-minute tweaking a tiller otherwise demands, and would have an easy time of it given how little the hull needs correcting.[39] A UK builder who had given up on fitting a windvane to his own boat, "Faith," unable to overcome the mounting problems himself, welcomed the Cedar Key result as confirmation that a workable design exists, having previously only had secondhand reports of Paradox's course-keeping to go on and having imagined she would hold a course "like Slocum's 'Spray.'"[40]

## A pendulum-weight self-steering idea, and its limits

A separate self-steering concept was floated independent of any wind vane: fit a horizontal shaft to the aft side of the rudderhead, above the waterline, with a weight free to slide along it and a lockscrew to fix its position. As a Paradox heels, the sail's center of effort moves to leeward of the hull's center of lateral resistance, tending to round the bow up into the wind; the theory holds that the same heel swings the slider weight down to leeward too, applying a falling-off correction at the rudderhead that offsets the round-up tendency and holds a balance. The idea traces to a technique used on model sailboats roughly a century ago, said to have influenced Joshua Slocum's own thinking on self-steering.[41] The same lead weight cast into the trailing edge of a Paradox's own rudder blade — fitted to sink the buoyant wood blade — was suggested as already doing double duty toward this same falling-off effect, on top of its ballasting job.[41]

Two working precedents for the underlying idea were reported from model sailboats: a shaft-and-slider weight fitted to a roughly 2 ft Tahiti Ketch model held a heading with only slight variation even in gusty conditions once the rig itself was well balanced, since the model already had a lot of inherent course stability; a second, shorter-keeled cruising model instead used a dynamic sheet-to-tiller arrangement — the mainsheet led to an aft-pointing tiller extension, damped with an elastic band — judged more reliable and better suited to a hull with less course stability of its own, though still somewhat less able to handle gusty conditions than the weighted-shaft method.[42] A further assessment judged the weight idea workable in principle, though needing trial-and-error tuning, and likely limited to beating and reaching rather than all points of sail — the same reasoning behind why self-steering vanes generally carry auxiliary weight for the same effect. Leading the mainsheet to an aft extension on the rudderhead itself, worked from a traveller inside the cabin, was floated as a cleaner way to get the same sheet-to-tiller effect used in full-size gear, without giving up the enclosed-cabin layout.[43] Against the pendulum-weight idea specifically: it could still matter to windward, but any builder who locks the steering line while sailing negates the weight's effect entirely, since a locked line overrides whatever small helm correction the weight would otherwise apply.[44]

Two supporting book references for a genuine self-steering build, beyond the weight/pendulum idea: Bill Belcher's *Yacht Wind-vane Steering* and John S. Letcher Jr's *Self-Steering for Sailing Craft* — both already cited above at [a purpose-built option: wind-vane self-steering](#a-purpose-built-option-wind-vane-self-steering) — were reaffirmed as containing tested designs plus the underlying principles and requirements for a self-steering system to actually work.[43] A **Hasler SP90** wind vane, the first fitted by a UK sailor to an early-1970s boat and already noted above as rated excellent on a Round Britain boat (see [a trim-tab wind vane and Paradox's folding rudder](#a-trim-tab-wind-vane-and-paradoxs-folding-rudder)), worked well running downwind too, apart from a slight meandering — a point offered as a caution that running is generally the weak point for any wind-vane gear, since a following wind lessens the apparent wind driving the vane, a problem every modern vane design still has to work around.[45]

Separately, big iron rudder straps seen on some traditional full-size sailboats were raised as a possible parallel to the weighted-shaft idea; the straps' actual purpose was structural rather than self-steering-related — reinforcement for older rudders built up from several vertical planks pinned and bolted together, holding the planks to each other rather than adding any pendulum weight.[46]

In practice, locking the steering line down and making only minimal adjustments every five or ten minutes is the normal way a Paradox is sailed, without the boat ever being left to steer herself unattended for long — the same locked-line practice that already negates the pendulum-weight idea's effect, above.[47] A rudder built with the lead weight the plans already call for, cast into the trailing edge to sink the buoyant wood blade, had not yet had that lead fitted as of this exchange, leaving the pendulum-weight question untested on the water.[50]

The self-steering thread ran on long enough, under its original subject line, that a follow-up question about adapting an existing self-steering gear to a Paradox was posted as a reply to it rather than as a new message — an accidental subject-line drift common enough on the list that keeping the subject line current when switching topics was flagged as good practice, since it makes the archive easier to search later.[51][52][53]

## The 8 July 2007 sea trial: a full course-holding test

Two months after the Cedar Key gathering, once the vane gear was repaired, a dedicated sea trial on "Zoë" put the mechanism through a full test on its own, rather than the short, damage-cut-short trial at Cedar Key. Once clear of the ramp in open air and deeper water, the vane gear was deployed and put through a round of minor teething problems: the oar tiller kept jumping out of the vane's out-put crank, wind stronger than the crank's range could handle blew the vane blade out of alignment, and several screws had vibrated loose in transit. With those faults corrected, if only temporarily, the gear held a course better than expected — the tiller's own end moved only about an inch at the most and typically half an inch or less, its biggest excursions coming in gusts or when the crew shifted weight aboard. Wind was measured directly at 16 mph gusting to 19 mph, matching a VHF forecast of 16 kt gusting to 25.

After tacking onto a close reach heading due south at 5.3 kt by GPS, the tiller was tied down to hold an approximate course and the vane gear engaged; after about a minute to settle, the compass read roughly 165°, and a short period of tuning the course-setting lines corrected that to a steady 180° plus or minus a couple of degrees — steadier than achievable by hand short of staying planted at the helm continuously. The exercise underscored the real value of a self-steering gear over a simple tiller tamer: a tiller tamer can hold a course only so long as the sailor doesn't move, whereas true self-steering frees a solo sailor to leave the helm entirely — crawling into lockers, checking the chart, or fixing a meal — without giving up course-keeping.

A three-minute mpeg video of the gear working was shot for the group's files, though the upload attempt over a dial-up connection was abandoned after several hours at only 16% complete. Remaining work identified from this trial: a wind-blade stop to stop strong gusts blowing the vane out of alignment, a spring on the oar-tiller/oar-carrier joint, larger engagement lines once new ones arrived, and a means of adjusting the wind-blade carrier tube's friction from inside the hatch — plus further testing before finalizing a design to publish with build instructions.

The built gear is an **upside-down-type vane assembly**, chosen because that type is affected less by heel angle than a vertical-axis vane. The oar assembly mounts on the rudder head; to engage the gear, the oar carrier is locked from swinging by a set of lines led to interior cam cleats, so a wind-deflected oar transmits its load to the rudder head directly rather than through tiller lines — avoiding tiller-line friction entirely. The controls are a down-haul and an up-haul line to flip the oar blade in and out of the water, a course-setting line, a friction adjuster, and two engagement lines set together. During development, `www.mindspring.com/~waltmur/Self-Steering/advanced.htm` was consulted more than any other reference; while this particular gear was purpose-built for Paradox, its individual components can be adapted and mixed to fit other small-boat self-steering needs once that site's underlying principles are understood.

## The vane's drive mechanism, explained

The wind vane's motion reaches the oar blade through a linkage rather than a direct connection: the vane's movement is transferred to a bell crank on its own mounting shaft, which drives a push rod down inside the mount's tall tube; the push rod exits the bottom of the mount and pushes or pulls a second bell crank, the out-put crank, converting the vane's rotary motion to an up-and-down motion and back to rotary again. At the far end of the out-put crank a long, tall U-shaped slot carries the oar tiller, so as the crank rotates it sweeps the oar tiller left and right, turning the oar blade — the same motion as sticking a hand out of a moving car's window and twisting the wrist. In a conventional servo-pendulum gear this motion would drive the yacht's own tiller via control lines; here, because the oar carrier is locked against swinging by lines led to interior cam cleats, the oar instead rotates about the knot securing it to the carrier and pushes the boat's own rudder left or right, since the carrier mounts to the rudder head directly.

The steering-rope friction that holds the tiller in normal sailing (see [light-air self-steering via tiller-rope friction](#light-air-self-steering-via-tiller-rope-friction) above) is fully adjustable and must be released for the vane gear to drive the rudder: tightening the steering rope increases friction, and the plans' own U-shaped notch at the end of the tiller exists to let the steering ropes be tied off with enough slack to hook over the notch and remove slack when sailing normally — popping the lines back out of the U frees enough slack to let the vane gear drive the rudder without fighting steering-rope friction.

## Why a trim tab, not a wind vane, suits a small cruising catamaran

The advice to keep a small cruising catamaran's self-steering to a simple trim-tab system, rather than a wind vane, on the grounds that multihulls accelerate too fast for a vane to track the fast-shifting apparent wind, drew a numbers-based objection in reply: at 120 sq ft of sail and 1,100 lb maximum displacement (an SA/D ratio of 18), the boat would need a hurricane to reach 5 kt, and even in daysailing trim (800 lb, a ratio of 22) only a tropical storm — offered half in jest against a fully loaded Paradox's own SA/D ratio of 12.8, to no avail.[69]

## A windvane built for the Mark II sailplan, and whether it suits the original rig

A further question followed once the wind-vane repair news reached the list: whether the gear, built and proven on "Zoë" 's **Mark II sailplan** with its shorter boom, would also suit a boat carrying the plans' **original sailplan** and its longer boom.[54] The answer: the mount would fit a boat on the original sailplan too, provided the mount itself were extended back farther to compensate for the longer boom.[55]

## Photos posted, and a fuller public writeup of the vane gear

Photographs of the vane gear went up in the files section, in a "Zoë's Vane Gear" folder, though the upload was slow going over a dial-up connection.[55] A fuller public writeup followed as its own thread: as of posting, the vane unit was still untested, with the oar blade needing fiberglassing and the up-haul and down-haul lines still to be installed, and a trial sail and a quick video of the gear in action were planned for that week; if the trial went well, plans would be drawn up and posted. The vane assembly itself was noted to be adaptable beyond a plain vane — the same mechanism could drive any output device, such as a trim tab or a servo-pendulum oar, with only the mount needing to be redeveloped for a different application.[56] The photos drew thanks for their usefulness to anyone wanting to build a similar gear, and further thanks for the level of detail shown.[57][58]

## An independent, vane-driven auxiliary rudder, and a bow-mounted alternative

A parallel refinement question, given how easily a Paradox balances on her main rudder, asked whether a wind vane could instead drive a small, entirely independent second rudder — applying only the correcting adjustments while the main rudder holds the general course — and whether such a vane could be mounted right forward on the foredeck instead of near the stern, since there is ample open space there clear of any sail interference.[70] The reply confirmed the boat's easy balance is real, needing only minor rudder adjustments to hold a course, making a separate vane-driven rudder a logical pairing in principle — the existing horizontal-axis vane concept above already solves the related problem of placing a vane far enough behind the sail. A bow-mounted vane, though, risks the sail itself interfering with, or even blocking, the wind reaching it: Robin Knox-Johnston's practice of mounting a windvane on each side of *Suhaili* for his solo world circumnavigation was cited as a precedent for a bow- or forward-mounted vane, one judged to complicate the arrangement rather than simplify it.[71][72]

## Renewed testing: an undersized vane blade, and a rolling-hitch tillerpilot adapter

Renewed light-air testing of the vane gear produced disappointing results, attributed to an undersized vane blade — enlarging it, and correcting a run of minor assembly troubles as they surfaced, was the planned next step, with no drawings to follow until the gear was working correctly and dependably.[73] For an electric alternative, an Autohelm 800 connects to the tiller lines through a rolling-hitch adapter: a hole drilled through a large washer sized to fit under the autopilot's own rod-end fitting takes a line tied off to the tiller line with a rolling hitch — needed running both fore and aft, since a line can only pull, not push. The pilot itself mounts on a cradle at the edge of the aft locker, supporting its body and rod end; making the connecting lines long enough lets the two rolling hitches be slid together to uncouple the autopilot, then pulled apart to take up slack in the tiller lines and re-couple it. Such a setup draws more power than ideal, suiting trips of no more than about three days — consistent with the power-budget tradeoff already recorded above (see [weighing a tillerpilot against a genuine wind vane](#weighing-a-tillerpilot-against-a-genuine-wind-vane)).[73]

## Boom interference, vane-blade materials, and a commercial precedent: the QME

A lightweight, simple horizontal-axis vane design documented on an outside blog was passed along as worth a look — judged to work well on most points of sail but not effective running downwind.[74] Windvane gear generally has to share space with the boom, creating an interference zone whose severity depends on the vane's own axis: a **vertical-axis vane** is always unforgiving in a boom collision there, while a **horizontal-axis vane** can be knocked around by the boom and mostly recovers, breaking only in the rare moment it sits at right angles to the boom — a risk removable by building the vane itself from a flexible material. Indestructible fiberglass rods and tubes made for stunt-kite construction, available in a range of sizes and stiffnesses from kite shops, were suggested as a ready source of such a material, drawing on the intersection of two published references consulted side by side — a self-steering design manual and a kite-building manual.[75] A commercially built vane once fitted to a Hunter 19 offers a cautionary precedent for the same simple-vane idea applied to Paradox: the **QME**, made by Quantock Marine Enterprises of Highbridge in England's West Country, sold in large numbers for being cheap, but only worked well on an exceptionally well-balanced boat, and carried an enormous lead counterweight that made the whole unit heavy; on Paradox specifically, the sail and its sheet would foul such a vane unless the entire assembly could be mounted well astern of the sail.[76][77]

## Weighing an electronic system's simplicity against a vane, revisited

For an owner not planning long ocean passages, an electronic autopilot's simplicity and light weight remains an attractive tradeoff over a wind vane, since periodically topping up batteries poses little problem on coastal cruises — consistent with the auto-pilot-versus-wind-vane comparison already recorded above (see [auto-pilot versus wind-vane self-steering](#auto-pilot-versus-wind-vane-self-steering-a-practical-comparison)). Running in light winds has always been a weak point for windvane systems generally, made worse on Paradox specifically by the noticeable friction the tiller lines contribute once properly tensioned. A working, perfected windvane gear nonetheless remains tempting enough to copy.[78]

## Photographs of the autopilot coupling, and further tiller-lock variants

Photographs of the Autohelm-to-tiller-line coupling described above went up in a new "Zoë's Autopilot Setup" files folder.[79][81] The setup drew praise as a clever, simple system worth duplicating on any steering-line-based rudder, including a small cruising catamaran's; weighing a spare autopilot's battery against the convenience of running a fishfinder off the same power was raised as the main tradeoff, alongside the option of a lighter motorcycle battery over a full car battery for occasional, brief use. The same rolling hitches used to couple and uncouple the autopilot were suggested as a workable tiller lock in their own right.[82] The photographs also showed a third working lock design, simpler than either the disc-clamp or the plywood-clamp method already recorded above (see [locking the tiller rope, and why most builders don't bother](#locking-the-tiller-rope-and-why-most-builders-dont-bother)): a single knob that clamps the tiller rope once it is dropped into a slot by screwing the knob down, releasing quickly by simply yanking the rope back up.[83] Where the autopilot mounts relative to an installed compass matters too: mounting on the side opposite the compass avoids the two fouling each other, and may if anything prove the easier side to use regardless.[84][85] Connecting a Tillermaster-style autopilot directly to the tiller, rather than to the steering lines, was considered and declined for the same boat: there is not enough athwartship room there, and the position would be inconvenient to reach.[86][87]

## Heaving-to without deploying a sea anchor

Beyond [a sea-anchor technique for heaving to](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#a-sea-anchor-technique-for-heaving-to-lying-a-few-degrees-off-the-wind), a sail-carrying alternative has been proposed on principle rather than direct experience with a single standing lugsail: reduce sail to the minimum, sheet the boom hard to one quarter, and offset the tiller slightly toward the side opposite the boom. In that configuration the boat should make only slight way while falling off to leeward at perhaps half a knot, varying with wind strength, with the remaining sail area helping stabilize her motion for a more comfortable ride than lying bare-poled. The simplest **certain** method, if the lugsail approach fails in practice, is simply to strike all sail and raise the rudder, letting the hull find her own natural lie to wind and sea — the same rudder-up/sea-anchor-optional tactic also recorded at [heavy-weather tactics — rudder position under a sea anchor](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#rudder-position-under-a-sea-anchor). The lugsail method remains conjecture, untested as of this record.
