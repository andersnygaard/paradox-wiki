# Onboard entertainment and diversions

What is worth carrying aboard a Paradox for music, reading, and other leisure turns on the same weight-and-power tradeoffs as any other onboard system, sharpened by how small and power-limited the boat is.

## Consumer electronics versus reused legacy gear

A single small laptop has been proposed as a consolidated stand-in for a whole stack of individual entertainment devices, though it is no substitute for navigation lights or a VHF radio, which are safety equipment rather than entertainment.[1][2][3] A more conservative, weight-conscious approach favors reusing whatever 12-volt gear is already on hand rather than buying new: a bright searchlight doubles as a way to find buoys and markers at night and to get the attention of careless towboat pilots; a full DVD library beats a small player's cramped screen, since a single disc in a compressed format can hold 10-12 hours of video or days of music, with some portable players reading MP3s as well as movies; even an old handheld VHF with a long-dead battery pack still works plugged straight into a 12-volt supply.[2] A portable digital picture frame with up to a 10-inch screen, built-in stereo speakers, and slots for assorted flash-media formats has also been floated as an all-in-one photo/video/music player, fed from an SD card recorded directly by camera — closed out, the next day, with the joking correction that there is no "black magic" behind it, only the "magic smoke" every electronic device runs on until it escapes.[4][5]

## A minimalist counterpoint: safety gear only, plus a book and a harmonica

Set against the case for more onboard electronics is a minimalist philosophy that keeps only the safety-relevant items — navigation lights and VHF — and dumps the rest, substituting a book and a harmonica for everything else. In practice the harmonica goes mostly unplayed: there is simply too much going on at anchor, from the wildlife to the surroundings themselves, to want any more diversion than that. Films and music are escapism, and escaping from the boat defeats the point of being aboard it in the first place.[6]

## Acoustic instruments as a compact, power-free alternative

Where cabin space allows for an instrument at all, a compact one wins out: a harmonica ("blues harp") takes almost no room, plays one-handed, or hands-free with a rack, leaving the other hand free for sailing, with online lesson sites available to learn or practice from — downloaded straight to an MP3 player.[7][8] A full guitar has been ruled out for the same reason, judged too space-hungry to play underway, with a ukulele considered instead as a smaller substitute.[7] A wider onboard-leisure kit built on the same logic runs to an MP3 player, a handheld mini-TV, and possibly a small laptop-class computer — with the expectation that, most of the time, simply being on the boat is diversion enough.[7]

The idea drew good-natured exaggeration too: a trombone paired with a wind-up 78 rpm gramophone needing no batteries at all was floated as one builder's own answer to the question, with the trombone's mouthpiece proposed as a stand-in foghorn for when the fog comes down.[9][10]
