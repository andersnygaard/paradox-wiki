# Secondhand Paradox market

A secondhand Paradox is a rare find. Owners have consistently reported, across more than a decade of the mailing list's history, that built Paradoxes seldom come up for resale at all, and prospective buyers who go looking for one — in the United States or the United Kingdom alike — routinely come away empty-handed. Asked to sell or trade a Paradox outright, an owner's usual answer is no: a boat built by hand tends to be kept for the long haul, or passed quietly to another builder or list member, rather than advertised on the open market. The practical consequence, repeated to newcomer after newcomer who asks about buying rather than building, is consistent: anyone who wants a Paradox should expect to build one rather than buy one used.

## Owners rarely sell

Only one sale of a built Paradox was ever recalled when a newcomer asked, in 2005, whether a used boat might be available.[1] A UK owner who went looking, in 2012, for a second boat "similar to" his own Paradox found none at all for sale in the country and bought a conventional boat instead.[2] Demand for a ready-built boat has also come from the opposite direction: at least one prospective builder, already committed to building his own, separately wanted to buy a second, already-built Paradox — and never found one.[3] At least one owner asked directly whether he would sell or trade his finished Paradox declined outright, preferring to keep her rather than part with a boat already suited to his own use.[4]

When a Paradox has changed hands, it has typically been through direct, informal contact between list members rather than public advertising. The earliest recorded sale, in 2001, went to a member who spent several weeks trying to reach the seller through the mailing list before a deal was struck.[5][6][7] Before committing to restore the boat, he checked her wood for hidden rot using nothing more than a simple ice-pick probe — a still-useful way to judge any secondhand hull's soundness beyond her cosmetic condition (see [checking a used Paradox for rot](/construction/rot-inspection-ice-pick.md)).[8] [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)'s older, related design [GJAC](/matt-layden/gjac.md) — a sibling design rather than a Paradox hull herself — surfaced for resale through the same kind of informal, list-based contact around the same period; no record survives of her actually selling.[9] When an unfinished hull's builder could no longer continue, other list members have sometimes stepped in to help find a buyer informally — for instance by adding a stalled builder's contact details to a UK builders' registry — though such help has not always led to a completed sale (see "RHXII," below).

## A want-to-buy inquiry from Texas

A newcomer asked, in December 2005, whether a finished or in-progress Paradox — or a similar boat — might be for sale, and how to order study plans. The reply was direct: Paradox owners rarely part with their boats, with only one sale ever heard of, and pointed instead to [study plans](/references/study-plans.md) at microcruising.com and to the full [building plans](/references/building-plans.md) as the realistic way in.[1] Asked where he was located, the correspondent answered Texas.[10]

## Build vs. buy secondhand: a UK newcomer's cost-benefit hesitation

A recurring question behind every listing on this page is really its reverse: is building a Paradox from scratch even worth it, against simply buying something ready-made? One UK correspondent weighing exactly that tradeoff gave the archive's fullest first-person statement of the hesitation. He was drawn to the Paradox specifically because she can be sailed entirely from inside her cabin, avoiding sitting exposed to wet and cold in an open cockpit — but no secondhand boat on the conventional market answered to that preference, since none of them can be sailed from inside. Buying secondhand, in other words, would still mean accepting an open-cockpit "open air life," regardless of which boat was chosen. Against that, a from-scratch Paradox build represents a substantial commitment of time and money, with an uncertain resale market by the time it eventually comes to be sold.[11]

The same exchange also touched on why an unconventional, high-performing small-boat design like Paradox is hard to sell either new or used — a question of marketing and boat-buying culture more than of actual sailing performance, addressed at length in [chine runners and windward performance](/chine-runners/windward-performance.md#a-recurring-skeptics-question-does-british-water-sail-differently) — and drew a separate build-cost comparison against a bigger boat, covered on [Faith](/boats/faith.md#build-cost-comparison-and-a-hull-assembly-milestone) and in [build effort and workspace](/construction/build-effort.md).

## An unfinished hull for sale: "Swash" (July 2004)

A part-built Paradox, **"Swash,"** was offered for sale in 2004 when her builder's marriage ended. The hull was largely complete — foam installed, ready to be skinned in plywood, her yuloh finished and her rudder started — but not yet glassed, with her chine runners still only unblocked bottom extensions. The asking price was set to recover roughly what had been spent on materials, rather than to profit from the labor already invested.[12] A third party expressed interest and asked for more photos and a completion estimate, offering a trailer and cash if a deal could be struck,[13] but the sale never went through: the builder grew increasingly reluctant to let the boat go, and ultimately finished her himself.[14] See [boats/swash.md](/boats/swash.md) for her fuller build and ownership history.

## An abandoned build for sale: "RHXII" (April 2006)

An Amsterdam builder's part-built hull, known to the list only by her working name "RHXII," could not be finished when family circumstances forced him to abandon the build and move on; he offered the unfinished boat for sale.[15] Other list members offered to help find a buyer by adding his contact details to a UK builders' registry.[16] No sale or further progress is recorded in the archive after that point.

## The "Minnow" campaign: an eBay listing, a tiered offer structure, and a full inventory (2014)

The fullest single secondhand-Paradox sale on record involved an already cruising-equipped boat, listed on eBay in the UK. The listing itemized her full inventory — sail, rudder, running rigging led into the cabin, navigation instruments, solar power, ground tackle, an auxiliary outboard, a yuloh, and a road trailer — and structured the sale in tiers, with gear removable from the package to suit a buyer's budget.[17] The listing drew genuine interest over several months, including prospective buyers who travelled a considerable distance to view her in person, before she finally sold.[18][19] One visit fell through for a reason unrelated to price or condition: a disabled prospective buyer found he could not safely get in and out of the forecabin from a dinghy given his disability.[20]

## Valuing a used Paradox

Asked directly what a used Paradox is worth, the consistent answer on the list has been to weigh her against what it would cost to have an equivalent boat built professionally from scratch — adjusted for her actual condition and the quality of her workmanship, and for the fact that Paradox, never having been a production boat, is rare to begin with. The final word, though, is the oldest rule of any secondhand market: a boat is worth whatever a buyer is willing to pay, or an owner is willing to accept.[21] In the one fully documented case of a complete, cruising-equipped Paradox reaching the open market (the "Minnow" sale above), the asking price for the whole package — boat, trailer, and gear together — worked out to well under half of what building an equivalent boat from scratch would cost over a comparable span of time, even before counting the builder's own labor at all.[22]

Buying a used Paradox from overseas adds a further, often decisive complication: importing a boat internationally brings freight, customs, duty, and handling costs on top of the purchase price itself — expensive and involved enough that at least one interested overseas buyer chose to build a Paradox derivative from scratch rather than import a used one,[23][24] and a later would-be importer found that shipping a part-built hull across the Atlantic added roughly as much again to the purchase price.[25]
