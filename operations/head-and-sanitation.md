# Head and sanitation arrangements

The Paradox's layout has no obvious dedicated place for a portable head, short of stowing it aft near the tiller — a gap in the "Building the Paradox" article sketches (see [building-the-paradox-articles](/references/building-the-paradox-articles.md)). No single design-level answer (a designated stowage location) has been settled; head arrangements on a Paradox remain individual and improvised rather than a solved design detail.

## Informal practice: "bucket and chuck it," not a fitted head

**"Bucket and chuck it"** is the practice Paradox and [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) cruisers actually use, including Matt Layden himself, who waits for shore facilities. A **chemical/marine head with a proper holding tank** is impractical on a boat this size — the regulatory requirement is that any *installed* marine head must have a holding tank — so most crews instead prefer to wait until reaching shore and use rest-room facilities wherever they happen to sail. If a bucket is used, used paper should go in a zip-lock bag for shoreside disposal rather than overboard. The general rule of thumb: prefer shore facilities near civilization, and accept bucket-and-chuck-it only when cruising remote areas. See [Motorsailer Paradox — a sanitation aside](/design/variants/motorsailer-idea.md) for a fuller exchange, including portapotti practice on a separate motor boat.

## Legality and enforcement of "bucket and chuck it"

An external boat-law reference (boatsafe.com's "nautical know-how" page on marine sanitation) confirms that "bucket and chuck it" is in fact legal under U.S. federal rules — though laws are only as real as their enforcement, which varies enormously by jurisdiction.

- In **Florida**, the Coast Guard does not seriously enforce sanitation rules — chasing drug smugglers is a higher priority.
- In **California**, by contrast, the law has been tightened to the point of making it economically prohibitive to live aboard or make an extended stay in state waters.
- In **Turkey**, authorities reportedly share fine proceeds with citizens who report direct overboard dumping from a yacht, with especially steep fines for American offenders.

Sailors and liveaboards, including Paradox cruisers, generally lack the resources to lobby effectively for changes to sanitation law, so it is worth finding out how the law is actually enforced in a given cruising area before arriving, rather than relying on its letter.

## Placement options for a portable head

A detailed brainstorm of where a marine sanitation device (MSD) — i.e. a portable head — could go, worked through against the study plans and the "Building the Paradox" sketches, narrows to two broad approaches.

**Under the helm seat:**

- Build a small hollow under the center of the helm seat (stealing space from both food-storage compartments), with a **Lexan/plexiglass panel** in its floor and a removable cover to close over it at night once bedding is rolled out; the head would sit there underway and be stowed aft at night unless the crew slept head-forward. This requires widening the opening aft.
- Alternatively, mount the head under the **port side** of the helm seat and slide it aft into the compartment currently used for anchor stowage (see [ground tackle and anchoring](/operations/ground-tackle.md)) — which would allow **bulkhead #1 to be sealed completely**, freeing the space forward of it as a chain locker (deck access only), at the cost of a new large opening in bulkhead #4, decking over that quarter compartment to keep water out, and a more complex port/starboard weight balance — with the benefit that one crew member could use the head underway while the other holds the helm.
- Or, give the helm a **permanent seat** with the head built into its center (or port side) flanked by small lockers, pushing the berths forward by a couple of feet — which requires rethinking the ventilator and forces the crew to sleep with the mast step between their feet (as on Matt Layden's "long cabin" 23-footer), and also opens up bulkhead #2, forcing a rethink of the second compartment's layout.

**In the second (forward) compartment:**

- Install a **foredeck hatch** directly above the head's location, improving ventilation but adding cost, awkward to use, and unusable in a storm.
- Or extend the bulkhead-#2 opening down to the floor (see [interior bins and hinged floor](/design/interior-bins-and-floor.md) for how tight that opening already is) and keep the head just forward of it, pulled into the cabin for use — though it would be free to slide around in heavy seas unless secured.

Stretching the design (see [stretched Paradox](/design/variants/stretched-paradox.md)) would sidestep the problem entirely, at the cost of exceeding the 14 ft trailering-rule limit that many builders want to stay within.

Of the placement ideas, the **helm-seat location** is generally regarded as the best option, built as a **complete watertight box** around the head rather than a simple hollow; locating the head in the middle of the food-storage/preparation area (the forward-compartment variants) is problematic for hygiene and practicality. A watertight box under the helm seat is the closest thing to a consensus recommendation, though no single design has been adopted as standard.

A separate, experience-based view holds that dedicated compartment layout matters less than judgment: anywhere overboard disposal would be a bad idea is usually also shallow enough to **wade ashore** and use a rest room or dig a hole, since nobody wants waste floating in a shallow anchorage or a dried-out tidal creek. A small **portable "Passport Potty" (8 L, 11.5 in tall x 14 in deep x 12 in wide)** — sized for car-camping use — would fit in the Paradox's **forward compartment** if a particular trip warranted carrying one. The underlying tradeoff is how much toilet time/convenience a sailor is willing to give up versus the inconvenience of doing without — canoeists and sea kayakers have long cruised and camped without one — and enjoying a microcruiser means accepting the compromises of "a camper."

## Disposable-bagging alternatives

A related idea, circulated via the companion [Micro Cruising](/references/micro-cruising-group.md) list: a commercial **X-shaped folding stand**, like a camp stool, with a toilet seat on top and a bag placed underneath/attached, tied off with a twist-wrap after use — though probably unsuitable for use in a genuine seaway.

A further alternative to a fitted MSD for boats too small to fit one is a **continuous-tube waste-bagging method** modeled on disposable-diaper "diaper genie"-style bins: depositing waste into a length of plastic tubing, twisting or knotting it off after each use to form a sealed "link-sausage" chain that can be dropped in a shoreside trash can when reached, on the reasoning that if diapers can go in household trash, this method should be similarly acceptable. Heavy-duty freezer zip-lock bags serve the same purpose. This is one of several independent examples — along with the zip-lock-bag disposal of used paper, above — of Paradox/microcruiser sailors converging on **disposable bagging** as a practical alternative to a fitted holding-tank head.

A **bucket with a sealed lid**, carried for emergencies while relying on going ashore before it's needed, is a further restatement of the "bucket and chuck it, prefer shore facilities" consensus recorded above (see also [self-righting range, and bailing without opening the hatch](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#how-the-no-open-hatch-bailing-actually-works) for the related bailing discussion this came up alongside).

A wider round of suggestions — prompted by a prospective cruiser new to extended small-boat travel and based in Puget Sound, where no discharge of any fluids is permitted locally[12] — restates and adds to the consensus above:

- Most people simply use a small **porta-potti**, stowed forward.[13][16]
- White-water rafters' practice of a small **ammunition box** lined with a plastic bag and filled with **cat litter**, and a "green" composting-toilet design (via a Dutch eco-technology site) judged reducible to a size that would fit a Paradox.[14]
- A commercial single-use **WAG Bag** kit, required in some parks for backcountry camping and hiking and available from outdoor retailers, as a further ready-made disposable option.[15]
- A **sawdust-toilet** method, field-tested at home for several days during a plumbing repair: a plastic-bag-lined 5-gallon bucket with a seat on top, table-saw sawdust dusted over the waste after each use, the filled bag simply going in the household trash at day's end. The sawdust controlled odor better than expected, and the method should work fine aboard a boat given a ready sawdust supply and a place to dispose of the waste ashore.[17]

Between the ammunition-box/cat-litter idea, the WAG Bag, and the sawdust method, this exchange adds three further disposable/absorbent options to the "bucket and chuck it" and diaper-genie-style bagging approaches recorded above — all converging on the same underlying practice: absorb or bag the waste, and dispose of it ashore, rather than fit a true holding-tank head.

## Relationship to the forward-compartment design

Head/sanitation placement is closely tied to the design of the forward cabin stowage (see [interior bins and hinged floor](/design/interior-bins-and-floor.md)): the tight forward compartment is clearly not where emergency flares (or, by extension, a head) would be kept, reinforcing that the Paradox's small hull leaves sanitation as an improvised, rather than a designed-in, arrangement.
