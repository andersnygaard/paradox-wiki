# Gulf Stream crossing (Florida–Bahamas)

For [Dave and Mindy Bolduc](/people/bolduc/dave.md), the passage across the
Gulf Stream between Florida and the Bahamas aboard
[Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) — and, on at least one
occasion, sailed alongside [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md) himself
in the original [Paradox](/paradox.md) — is the signature open-water passage
of the whole [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) design family.
It is roughly **65 nautical miles** of open ocean, made **five times** (later
"now seven") by the time of a May/June 2000 interview, and repeated many
times since. This concept records the specific, repeatable method they
developed for it: timing, routing, and — the part that surprises most
newcomers — collision avoidance with ship traffic, which they treat as the
real danger of the crossing, not weather. It is a companion to
[heavy-weather tactics](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md), which covers
storm-survival rigging (sea anchor, rudder position, lying a-hull) rather
than passage planning.

## The crossing itself

The shortest Miami–Bimini route is not a straight line: the Gulf Stream sets
**north** along its axis at up to **3.5 knots**, which nets out to roughly
**2.5 knots of northward drift per hour** over a **12-hour crossing** — enough
that a boat aiming straight for Bimini would be swept well north of it. To
compensate, the Bolducs start from well south of the direct line, departing
from **Key Largo** or, a little farther north, **Angelfish Creek** (they stock
up in Key Largo first, since "there really isn't anything at Angelfish
Creek"). They get up and depart around **6 a.m.**, steer **due east** if
motoring, and hold that course without much adjustment until the **last five
miles** to Bimini, where the Stream's effect lessens and the true bearing to
the island can finally be steered. The return trip typically makes landfall
near **Fort Lauderdale**, from which they run down the Intracoastal Waterway
— "the ditch" — back to Key Largo.

## Weather doctrine: never cross with a north wind

The one hard rule is directional, not a wind-speed threshold: **never cross
with the wind out of the north**, because a north wind blows directly against
the north-setting Gulf Stream current and produces dangerously steep,
breaking seas. East winds are merely slow going (on the nose); the seas only
turn genuinely nasty with north wind against the current.

Their timing exploits the normal wind rotation ahead of a cold front, which
**clocks SE → S → W → NW → N**. The ideal is to leave while the wind is
**still out of the south**, so that it swings favorably onto the beam and
then the stern as the crossing proceeds, ideally reaching port before it
backs around to the north. A secondary strategy is to leave right during a
**weak** cold front itself, since frontal north winds, even though from the
"wrong" direction, are at least steady rather than shifting — acceptable if
under about 10 knots. Dead calm or very light days are good too.

Good weather windows are scarce: they typically get only about **one good
Gulf Stream crossing day per week** in January–March, and have sometimes
waited as long as **two weeks** for the right conditions. A large, capable
boat (their example: a 60-footer) could cross almost any time, but would
still get "bounced around" in 10-14 ft seas when it's blowing 35-40 knots out
of the north — the small-boat weather discipline is about avoiding that
scenario entirely, not surviving it.

The doctrine holds even after departure: if conditions look **foreboding**,
turn back — "don't be afraid to turn around even if you have already
started." They report having done exactly this "several times in the first
ten miles of a crossing."

## The real danger is ship traffic, not weather

The Bolducs are explicit that the greater hazard crossing the Gulf Stream is
**man-made**: large ships, many running on autopilot, may not see a small
boat even with radar, and cannot maneuver or stop in time regardless. Their
collision-avoidance method:

- **Cross in daylight** whenever possible.
- Take a **hand-bearing-compass** bearing on any approaching ship, then
  repeatedly recheck it. A **steady, unchanging bearing means a collision
  course**.
- **Never try to cross ahead of a ship's bow** — ships travel "upwards of 14
  knots," faster than it looks from a small boat's low vantage point.
- When several ships are visible at once — their example is a 1998 return
  trip to Fort Lauderdale with roughly a dozen ships in sight in the last
  hours of the crossing — prioritize bearing checks on only the most
  threatening ones.

The stakes are not abstract. They cite a couple **run down at night in the
middle of the Gulf Stream** by another sailboat: its skipper had switched the
boat to autopilot to make coffee and had shut off his navigation lights to
save electricity, and a lookout posted on deck aboard the striking boat never
saw the smaller boat coming. Dave and Mindy's own scariest moment on the
crossing was being caught at night between two large freighters, unable to
judge either ship's distance or closing speed from their lights alone —
which is why they now prefer to make the passage in daylight.

## Real crossings as evidence

- **1993** — their first Bahamas passage, departing after several days of
  35-knot northerlies had finally eased. They left in light air and met
  large Atlantic swells "as tall as our mast" that blanketed the wind in the
  troughs; they sheeted in tight and **motor-sailed 14 hours**, making
  landfall at **Cat Cay** (at the time still storm-damaged from Hurricane
  Andrew).
- **1995** — sailing in company with Matt Layden, newly arrived overland in
  the original Paradox. Both boats crossed in "sloppy" 5-7 ft seas; the
  crossing became **becalmed 25 miles out** (about halfway) at daybreak when
  an unpredicted easterly headwind replaced the forecast beam wind. The
  Bolducs motored the rest of the way and made Bimini that afternoon after
  **20 hours**; Matt — "always the sailing purist" — refused a tow and
  instead sailed or sculled, arriving a day later.
- **2001** — a much quicker crossing, completed in **9.5 hours**.
- **2013** — Dave's solo **night** crossing, made "amongst dozens of fast
  moving ships," a direct test in practice of the bearing-check
  ship-avoidance method described above.

Weather can turn dangerous even on the far side of the Stream, once inside
the Bahama Bank: on the 1993 trip, the **75 nm** run from Chub Cay (Berry
Islands) to Bimini began in 15-20 kt wind that built overnight, with a cold
front, to at least 40 kt. Little Cruiser held hull speed downwind at **5
knots** with her last reef tied in, surfing regularly in breaking seas; the
skipper became too seasick to continue steering, and Mindy took the helm for
the remainder of the **17-hour** passage.

## Resting mid-passage: the sea anchor option

On a long or rough crossing, a **parachute (sea) anchor** is the Bolducs'
option for stopping to rest rather than pressing on exhausted — carried "in
case we want to rest or we get caught out in something really ugly," though
they report never having actually needed one in a genuine emergency. The
full treatment of sea-anchor deployment, rudder handling while riding to
one, and the alternative (not recommended) of lying a-hull is covered in
[heavy-weather tactics](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md); ground tackle
and rode specifications are covered in
[ground tackle](/operations/ground-tackle.md).
