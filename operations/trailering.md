# Trailering — tow weight and vehicle capacity

See also [car-topping conversion ideas](/design/ideas/car-topping-conversion-idea.md) for car-topping considered as an alternative to trailering altogether, on Enigma's lighter hulls.

A Paradox's **fully loaded, ready-to-sail weight** — not its bare hull weight — is what determines whether a given tow vehicle is adequate. That loaded figure is around **410 kg**, comfortably within, for example, a 2000 Volkswagen Golf TDI's 900 kg tow rating (a bare-hull figure of a little over 650 kg is sometimes quoted separately, and refers to the unloaded boat).

Reported loaded weights vary somewhat depending on the individual boat's build and exactly what is counted as "loaded": alongside the ~410 kg figure, a heavier-built boat's loaded weight has been recorded at roughly 620 kg (~1,365 lb — see [weight, ballast, and displacement](/design/weight-and-ballast.md)), and the design displacement at the design waterline is **1,410 lb** — twice a familiar 20-footer's weight at first glance, until the figure is understood as loaded and crewed rather than bare hull.

## Converting a boat's own displacement into an actual trailer load

The trailer load a Paradox actually presents is well below her full sailing displacement, because most of that displacement leaves the boat before she goes on the road. Starting from a boat ballasted to about 1,400 lb displacement, taking out the skipper, the water ballast, and a few other items just as easily carried in the tow vehicle brings the real trailer load down to around **1,000 lb**; removing still more — anchors, lead ballast, tinned food — trims it further, at the cost of reloading everything at the launch site. A heavier anchor or a heavier skipper taken on afterward can simply be offset by removing a little lead.

Rigged and loaded exactly to the design waterline, the 1,410 lb design displacement breaks down as follows: towed fully loaded except for a roughly 200 lb skipper, trailer load comes to about **1,210 lb**; with the water ballast tanks also empty, about **1,060 lb**. Built boats rarely hit the exact design displacement, and a Paradox shows no noticeable handling difference from 100 lb more or less than the design figure, possibly considerably more — sailing without the water tanks topped up (a 150 lb difference) or with an extra passenger aboard both go largely unnoticed, and checking actual displacement is rarely necessary once a boat is sailing well. **Matt Layden**'s own practice on a long cruise was to remove some ballast and replace it with stores, then replace depleted stores with sand or gravel in bags as they were used up.

Of a Paradox's roughly 1,400 lb sailing displacement, on the order of **400 lb is typically lead ballast**, with the balance made up of gear, stores, water, and the skipper — a boat carrying several anchors and a lot of stowed food and gear correspondingly needs less lead to reach the same total.

## A trailer-failure data point, and the case for overbuilding

A stock or lightly-modified trailer built for a small, lightweight boat is **not adequate** for a Paradox. On "[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)," driving home from a sail, a loose strap led to the discovery that the trailer's **winch was bent and nearly pulled out**, its mounting support also bent, with **4-5 teeth broken off the winch gear** — a close call underlining the governing lesson that a Paradox needs a well-built trailer, and that the general advice is to **overbuild**.

The safest approach is to go well over the top on sturdiness rather than adapt something marginal: a trailer built for an inflatable boat, expected to be just within the Paradox's weight limit, is a case worth checking against professional judgment by shopping the adaptation around to several trailer manufacturers before trusting it.

## Glen Maxwell's trailer specifications, and a loaded-height target

The most detailed trailer-build specification on record comes from "[Zoë](/boats/zoe.md)." The root cause of trailer failures like the one above: a trailer built for a boat of only about **500 lb**, where a Paradox loaded and ready for a week's cruising comes in close to **900 lb**. The recommended rebuild sequence:

- Remove the fenders.
- Fit a **longer axle** and **heavier three-leaf springs** (2,000 lb rated).
- Mount the axle **on top of the springs** (rather than underneath) to lower the trailer.
- Have the **spindles welded to the top of the axle** instead of coming out the ends — a further 1.5 in of height reduction.
- Build **fender extensions** afterward to bring the fenders back out over the tires, clear of the narrower frame.

This sequence gave Zoë's trailer a **loaded height to the bottom of the boat of 14 in**; adding the Paradox's roughly **9 in waterline draft** comes out to just under 2 ft overall — the target worth aiming for. Even this setup would not suit every launch site: at **Apalachicola** (see [Apalachicola Bay — a proposed meeting ground, scouted solo](/merits/gatherings.md#apalachicola-bay-a-proposed-meeting-ground-scouted-solo-2002)), the drop-off is shallow enough that launch or recovery at low tide is in doubt, and a **magic-tilt-type trailer** may help in such cases. Trailer parts of the kind described above are available from **Northern Tool & Equipment** (catalog by phone, 800-556-7885).

This ~900 lb loaded-for-cruising figure converts to almost exactly **410 kg** — the same fully-loaded weight used above for tow-vehicle sizing, just expressed in different units.

## Ground clearance for Gulf Coast ramps

Ground clearance from the bottom of the boat to the ground is a key rebuild variable for shallow ramps. A trailer rebuilt to leave **24 3/4 in** of clearance was judged workable for **Cedar Key**, whose ramp is comparatively steep, by comparison with "Zoë," whose hull bottom sits **15 in** off the ground — a height at which retrieval at low tide was still in doubt at **Apalachicola** (see [Apalachicola Bay — a proposed meeting ground, scouted solo](/merits/gatherings.md#apalachicola-bay-a-proposed-meeting-ground-scouted-solo-2002)), though other ramps in that area reportedly allow launch/retrieval at low water. Switching to **pontoon (low-profile) tires** can gain roughly another **3 in** of clearance reduction where needed; a **tilt trailer** is a workable base design for this kind of rebuild.

## Low-profile and retracting-trailer concepts

For launching off a **slip** without wetting the wheels or brakes, and also loading/unloading onto a **flat surface such as a beach** at low tide, several approaches have been discussed:

- **Drift-boat-type trailers** (used to launch drift boats into very shallow rivers from the banks) are a useful reference design.
- **Torsional axles** — each side sprung/mounted independently of the other — allow the bottom of the boat to sit lower, below the axle itself, and are available from **Northern Tool & Equipment**. In the UK, almost all trailers use **independent rubber-in-compression suspension units** rather than leaf springs, which are rare to see there.
- A **motorcycle trailer** design using a torsional axle plus a **winch** to lower its bed flat onto the ground suggests a possible scaled-up approach for a boat trailer, though likely expensive to build as a one-off.

A more radical concept, considered but never built: mounting a pair of **torsion axles inside the boat structure itself**, at bulkhead #3, so that once the boat was launched the axles (with wheels attached) could be **unbolted or unpinned from inside and pulled away** from the hull while it floated — with the **towbar** similarly detachable from the stem. The wheels, axles, and towbar would then stow in the tow vehicle until needed again, with the mounting ports in the hull sides and stem covered over when not in use. The idea is judged to have merit but was never developed further.

A related concept seen on a dinghy at a boat show: a towbar slotted through a hole in the bow, with wheels that **lowered like an aircraft undercarriage** by winding a handle. That particular implementation is judged flawed — it used standard car suspension parts, likely a maintenance nightmare, with doubts about how a boat's structure would hold up to road-pounding with wheels mounted so directly to it — but the **basic principle is sound**. A simpler variant: a **suspension-free launching undercarriage** used together with a car-transporter-type road trailer, retracting once on the trailer to lower the boat onto conventional supports for the road. A third variant: **leaving the tow ball on the car** and simply **dropping the trailer's back end onto the ground** to lower the boat for launch/recovery, rather than retracting the axle or undercarriage itself. As with the other concepts in this group, any such actuating mechanism would need to be simple and inexpensive to be worthwhile — none of these retracting-axle or retracting-undercarriage concepts is recorded as ever having been built or tested.

## A small, affordable trailer option, and its real-world weight limit

The **"T-2000"** trailer sold by **Portage Pal** (`portagepal.com`) is a small, relatively affordable commercial option — but inadequate for a Paradox: it would **not even carry an empty Paradox**, let alone one loaded with stores and food for a couple of weeks' cruising, which brings her weight up to around **1,000 lb**. A **custom-built trailer** rated to a proper **2,500 lb capacity** costs only a little more than the undersized commercial option — a concrete counter-example reinforcing the same don't-under-build-the-trailer lesson documented above.

## Adapting a used heavy-duty trailer (the Bolduc/Layden approach)

Long experience trailering [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) alongside Paradox points to a **used trailer that is fairly heavy duty** for either boat: because both designs have **flat bottoms**, they adapt readily to almost anything from a flatbed to a custom trailer with simple modifications — a looser, more forgiving approach than the detailed built-to-spec rebuild worked out for "Zoë" trailer (above).

Even a repurposed trailer must not be under-built: a lightweight-to-medium-duty trailer rated for only **1,400 lb**, used for Little Cruiser, has needed **welding gussets and reinforcement plates onto it ever since** — bouncing down the road and across railroad tracks with a full load cracks the welds. It also went through **several blow-outs** with the trailer's original **skinny 4 in x 12 in** tires before switching to **full-size trailer tires** matching those of the tow vehicle. As set up, Little Cruiser rests on the trailer on **two treated 2x6 boards amidships** and a **2x4 board forward**, with the axle mounted **atop the leaf springs** — the same height-lowering technique used for Zoë's trailer above — allowing launch at most ramps.

**Matt Layden**'s own approach illustrates the alternative end of the spectrum: rather than build or heavily modify a trailer, he simply bought a good used heavy-duty powerboat trailer with full-sized wheels, cheaply. It is a characteristically pragmatic, minimal-effort approach to boat-support infrastructure, consistent with his prioritizing simplicity and low cost elsewhere (see, e.g., his original ~4-month, bicycle-trailer-hauled build in [original Paradox](/boats/original-paradox.md)) — here applied to trailer selection rather than the boat itself.

## Trailer equipment: brakes and bearing buddies

A well-specified trailer need not have brakes: the custom-built trailer for "[Zoë](/boats/zoe.md)" has **no brakes**, but does use **bearing buddies** (grease-fitting hub caps that keep wheel bearings packed and protected during repeated launch/retrieval) — a short, concrete data point rounding out the trailer specification given above.

## Always carry a spare tire

Even a **brand new** trailer can suffer a **tire blowout** — one occurred before even reaching the boat ramp, with no spare aboard, on a long Cumberland Sound trip aboard "[Zoë](/boats/zoe.md)." The failure happened close enough to a tire store to walk to for a replacement — a matter of luck, since the same problem far from any town would have been far worse. The lesson, distinct from the build-quality/overbuilding lessons above since the trailer itself was new and presumably sound: **always carry a spare**.

## UK trailer weight limits, and whether brakes are necessary

The governing **UK legal limit** for an unbraked trailer: it may weigh up to **750 kg gross**, or **half the towing vehicle's kerb weight**, whichever is lower — so adapting a candidate dinghy trailer (large transverse leaf-spring suspension, unbraked wheels) for a laden "Enuf" may well be legal without brakes. In practice, though, a loaded trailer of that kind would be very difficult for one person (or even two) to manhandle with no brakes at all; keeping the wheels from getting wet during launch and recovery makes the lack of trailer brakes much less of a liability. For comparison, "[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)" and "[Zoë](/boats/zoe.md)" both ride on fairly ordinary trailers.

A choice of tow vehicle between an aging short-wheelbase Land Rover (poor brakes, low speed) and a modern saloon (higher speed, better brakes) need not weigh unbraked-trailer risk too heavily: private trailers are stopped and checked mainly when flagrantly breaking the law (such as speeding), since enforcement effort concentrates on commercial trailers, which are more often overloaded or in poor condition.

## UK combi-trailer prices, and buying a fellow builder's used trailer

UK prices for a suitable trailer vary widely depending on weight rating and whether it is bought new, built custom, or found used. A new combi trailer commands enough of a premium that buying secondhand from a fellow Paradox owner, on the strength of that owner's own experience with it, is a worthwhile way to beat the price of new. By contrast, a lighter-built boat can get away with a lighter, cheaper trailer, and a new stock trailer bought at a discount off list price costs distinctly less than a heavier combi trailer would. A custom-built trailer, sized to a boat's own requirements for simple single-handed launch and recovery, sits in between — in line with the fuller custom-trailer cost accounting given for "[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md#weigh-in-and-a-full-build-cost-accounting-januarymarch-2003)."

## A stock power-boat trailer, adapted for tongue weight

A **regular power-boat trailer** works fine for towing both Little Cruiser and Swamp Thing, needing only custom **2x6 treated-lumber bunks** shaped to follow each hull's rocker. Because that kind of trailer is designed around a boat with a heavy engine hanging off the transom, a Paradox has to sit centered **slightly ahead of the axle**, leaving the stern looking as if it hangs off the trailer a bit — the tradeoff that gets the **tongue weight** into the right range, roughly **100-150 lb** for a Honda Accord tow vehicle and a fully-loaded, expedition-ready boat. The exact figure is not critical, but too little tongue weight lets the boat bounce and pitch sharply over bumps; on periodic 900-mile trips to the Florida Keys, Little Cruiser's trim on the trailer has been adjusted using trip stores and loose lead ballast to keep the tongue weight comfortable.

Building from a similar stock power-boat trailer, one builder modified his to **sit lower** for launching at low tides, ahead of attending the group's Cedar Key gathering.

## Trailering the Bolducs' way (microcruising.com)

An account of trailering [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) gives an independent, long-running real-world counterpart to the specifications and lessons above. Little Cruiser's shallow **9-inch draft** makes her easy to launch at any ramp, and she tows well behind a small four-cylinder car — a notably lighter tow vehicle than generally discussed above.

Little Cruiser was routinely trailered from **Greensboro, North Carolina**, to **Key Largo, Florida** — a one- to two-day drive — before launching for a Bahamas crossing. Underway, the mast **lowers** on its struts/tabernacle to clear low bridges, allowing passage underneath without waiting for a bridge opening. Cross-link [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md).

A distinct practical figure covers the other end of the trailering process — not towing capacity, but time to get sailing once at the ramp: rigging a Paradox has been timed at about **one hour, car to boat**.[38]

## An oversized general-purpose utility trailer, doing double duty

Most of the record above works one way — starting from a boat trailer and adapting it to carry a Paradox. The choice can be made from the other end instead: buy an **oversized general-purpose utility trailer** big enough to take the boat, so that it doubles as a boat trailer and is put to many other uses when not launching the boat.[39] One such rig in long-term use is a galvanised, mesh-sided box trailer carrying the hull directly on its bed, with a spare wheel stowed beneath the bow — a boat trailer only on the days it is needed as one, and an ordinary load-hauling trailer the rest of the year.[39]

The trade is straightforward. A flat-bedded utility trailer gives up the launching convenience a purpose-built boat trailer is designed around — the rollers, the tilt bed, and the low loaded height that keeps a hull's bottom close to the ramp (see [ground clearance for Gulf Coast ramps](#ground-clearance-for-gulf-coast-ramps) and [low-profile and retracting-trailer concepts](#low-profile-and-retracting-trailer-concepts)). What it buys back is that the trailer earns its keep year-round instead of standing idle between cruises, which suits an owner trailering a few times a season to distant grounds rather than launching weekly at a local ramp.[39]

## A 600-mile tow on an unbraked road trailer

A Paradox has been towed 600 miles round trip — from the East Coast of England to Falmouth and back — on an **unbraked** road trailer behind an ordinary Ford Mondeo family saloon, without difficulty.[40] The same tow vehicle towed four different trailer-sailers in total over twelve years, including through the hills of Devon and Cornwall, without ever feeling inadequate to the job.[43]

## Reversing without docking arms, and two launch/recovery procedures

A boat that sits well forward on her trailer can completely block the tow car's rear view while reversing, unlike a smaller, lighter boat that sits further back on hers — making it essential to line trailer and car up centrally at the top of a slipway before reversing, and to hold that line throughout, which is easier said than done.[50] Launching and retrieving single-handed is harder without **docking arms** fitted to the trailer: without them the job becomes a considerable handful, particularly if the slipway is steep and exposed to waves, more so with a transverse current running.[42] Guide posts fitted to a trailer let the whole retrieval be done single-handed instead: lower the trailer by hand nearly to the water's edge and chock it, then run a rope from the trailer's front support to the tow car's ball hitch to lower the trailer fully into the water so the boat floats free above it while being winched forward.[41]

With a short tow bar, the trailer has to be lowered into the water on a rope run from the car's tow ball, taking a single turn round the ball to control the descent as the car eases forward. An alternative procedure: chock the trailer's wheels near the waterline, unhook the trailer from the car, drive the car up the slipway, link car and trailer with a rope, drive forward a little to pull the chocks free, then reverse slowly until the boat floats clear, securing her by the painter before retrieving the empty trailer on the rope. A long tow bar simplifies the whole sequence down to reversing boat and trailer straight into the water until the boat floats off, then towing the trailer straight back out, still attached to the car.[50]

A pre-launch checklist from one changeover of ownership: check periodically that the trailer's lighting board is held only by turn screws that can vibrate loose underway (taping is worth doing); keep the boat trimmed bow-heavy for road travel and don't forget the bow lashing and the aft strop; remove the lighting board before launching; run a thick warp from the ball hitch for the extra depth needed to float the boat off; and chock the boat on the slipway to separate trailer from car only once there is enough bow weight aboard that she will not fall back onto her rudder.[51]

## Rebuilding and specifying a De Graaff trailer

A **De Graaff SB 730/2 ADJ** trailer, adapted with flat rubber rollers to spread the load and guide arms to keep the boat straight on retrieval in a side wind or current, is one specific rebuild on record for a Paradox — the tow bar had to be shortened to let the rig fit into a domestic garage, and the trailer's winch is rated to handle up to **1,102 lb**.[46] The same manufacturer's advice on coupling load: downward thrust on the coupling should be no more than **73 kg** and preferably not less than **50 kg** (30 kg judged possibly sufficient but not recommended), measured in practice with a bathroom scale; correct trim on the trailer depends on the boat's forward ballast and battery being aboard.[45] A trailer's axle, originally mounted too far forward for adequate tow-ball thrust, was moved **17 cm** rearward by loosening the four u-bolts securing the axle carriage; the rule of thumb given for correct hitch loading generally is about **10% of the combined weight** of trailer and boat.[44] A virtually brand-new De Graaff trailer of this kind, used only twice, cost markedly more than a comparable secondhand road trailer bought years earlier, consistent with the general escalation in trailer and build costs recorded over the intervening years (see [build effort and workspace](/construction/build-effort.md)).[47][48]

## Single-handed launching as the real reason not to skimp

Beyond load capacity, a trailer's practical ease of use matters in its own right: a trailer built well enough to make **single-handed launching easy** can be the difference between actually going sailing and staying home — a restatement of the overbuilding lesson above framed around motivation rather than just structural margin, and consistent with the single-handed launch/recovery difficulties already recorded (see [reversing without docking arms, and two launch/recovery procedures](#reversing-without-docking-arms-and-two-launchrecovery-procedures)).[52]

## Why trailering with the mast through the vent proved impractical

Matt Layden's plans offer stowing the mast fully inside the hull for trailering, passed through the aft vent port, as an alternative to leaving the assembled rig secured to the side-deck stowage rails. In practice this proved far from easy, and the stowed mast got in the way if the crew wanted to sleep aboard while the boat sat on the trailer — the reason the whole rig, mast included, is more often kept assembled and secured to the port side-deck stowage rails instead, trading a bulkier road load for a much faster rig set-up at the water.[49] By contrast, a different builder's practice stows the whole rig inside the hull specifically for the road, loading and retrieving it through a separate transom inspection hatch rather than the aft vent, and rigs the boat fully only on arrival at the launch site — see [components/deck.md — rigging at the launch site, and stowing the spars for the road](/components/deck.md#rigging-at-the-launch-site-and-stowing-the-spars-for-the-road) for the contrasting account.

[50] [Bill's Log](/references/bills-log.md) — "Trial Road Run with 'Minnow'", Bill Serjeant, 2014-03-22 — a boat that sat well forward on her trailer completely blocked the tow car's rear view when reversing, versus an earlier, smaller boat that had sat further back; the practical fix is to line up trailer and car centrally at the top of the slipway before reversing and to keep them in line throughout, "easier said than done"; with a short tow bar, lowering the trailer into the water for launch needs a rope run from the car's tow ball with a single turn round the ball to control the descent; an alternative method chocks the trailer wheels near the water, unhooks the trailer, drives the car up the slipway, links car and trailer by rope, drives forward a little to remove the chocks, then reverses slowly until the boat floats off, securing her by the painter before retrieving the trailer on the rope; a long tow bar simplifies the whole process to reversing boat and trailer straight into the water until the boat floats clear, then towing the trailer straight back out still attached to the car.
[51] [Bill's Log](/references/bills-log.md) — "'Minnow' Away," Bill Serjeant, 2015-04-19 — a handover note on trailering practice: check periodically that the trailer's lighting board is held by its turn screws, since they can vibrate loose underway (taping is recommended); keep the boat bow-heavy for road travel and do not forget the bow lashing and the aft strop; before launching, remove the lighting board, use a thick warp from the ball hitch to get enough depth to float the boat off, and chock the boat on the slipway to separate trailer from car only once there is enough bow weight to stop her falling back onto her rudder.
