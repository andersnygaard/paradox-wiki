# Auxiliary outboard power

Outboard motors are an option for auxiliary power on a Paradox, though the boat's default handling is sail-only (see [rig and sail](/components/rig/index.md) and [self-steering](/operations/self-steering.md)). A small outboard, such as a 2 hp Evinrude, is normally limited to its own small built-in fuel tank; a generic conversion lets it draw instead from a larger external tank.

## Converting a small outboard to an external fuel tank

To feed a small outboard from an external tank rather than its built-in one, run a longer fuel line from the external tank to a **12-volt electric fuel pump** (available from an auto-parts store or J.C. Whitney, about $30), with a **fuel-pressure regulator** (of the type suited to "foreign cars") plumbed between the pump and the carburetor:

```
external fuel tank -> 12V electric pump -> pressure regulator -> carburetor
```

Start the regulator at **3 psi** and adjust from there: with the pump running and the engine off, no fuel should be visible flowing into the carburetor — if it is, lower the regulator setting; if the engine stalls, raise it. Keep all connections tight while leaving enough slack in the lines for vibration and movement.

## A heavier outboard, and rebalancing ballast

A larger, heavier outboard — for example a **4 hp four-stroke weighing 65 lb** — is noticeably heavier on the transom than a small 2 hp motor and can call for rearranging ballast to compensate; see [weight, ballast, and displacement](/design/weight-and-ballast.md) for the full ballast discussion.

## The Bolducs' outboard experience, and the move to sail-and-oar

A multi-year outboard history aboard [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) gives a real-world field report on auxiliary power for a Layden-family hull. The working setup there was a **4-hp two-cycle Evinrude**, with a stated intention to someday switch to a Honda four-stroke. A lighter **2.5-hp four-stroke** outboard was separately judged too weak to push a heavy boat like Little Cruiser in difficult conditions.

The clearest reliability lesson came when a roughly 10-year-old outboard **sheared its drive shaft** mid-crossing on the Great Bahama Bank, leaving the boat becalmed and unable to make way — forcing a tow the following morning. That failure prompted carrying a **yuloh** as backup propulsion (see [sculling and yuloh technique](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md)), after the discovery that a 1,600-lb boat could not otherwise be moved far with just a dinghy paddle.

Matt Layden himself sailed engine-free — both Swamp Thing and Paradox were sailed and sculled without an outboard at all. That example was eventually followed aboard Enigma: by around **2008** the outboard was left behind entirely for Watertribe events, Enigma being sculled instead, and by 2013 a six-week solo Bahamas cruise was completed in Enigma with **no engine aboard at all** — sail and oar only. Cross-link [sculling and yuloh technique](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md).

## A hand-crank generator idea, and why a lever beats it

A speculative alternative to a conventional outboard was floated: a hand-crank 12V generator (built DIY from an old car alternator hooked to a battery) driving a small electric trolling motor, avoiding both a gas motor and a bank of batteries.[61] The idea drew a physics-based objection: converting mechanical energy to electrical and back again, then from rotation to thrust through a screw, stacks up losses at every stage, leaving only a fraction of the original effort to drive the boat forward — the simpler the system, the fewer the losses, and nothing is simpler than a lever, meaning an oar (or yuloh) beats a hand-crank generator on efficiency grounds alone.[62] Weighed against that, the intended use was narrower than a full crossing: quiet, controlled propulsion for tight spaces such as docks and boat ramps, with the generator meant only as backup to a solar trickle charger, and a proper motor still preferred for a genuine offshore crossing near a shipping lane at night.[63]

Run against real numbers, the idea does not hold up even for its intended tight-quarters use: the smallest Minn Kota electric trolling motors draw roughly **30 amps**, so an hour's use would need a 100 mA trickle charger running flat out for some **300 hours** to recharge the battery, ignoring losses. The yuloh remains the better tool for close-quarters maneuvering; a builder wanting a motor at all is better served by a proper one — several builders report good results from the **Honda 2 hp** — and simply accepting its drawbacks.[64]

A concrete solar trickle-charger product was named in the same exchange: a **1.26-watt, weatherproof panel** that clips straight to the battery for about **$30**, working out to **105 mA** at 12 volts. Confirmed against the same Minn Kota draw figures above, that panel's charging time in full bright sunshine runs roughly **300 times** its discharging time, before even accounting for further losses — the same order-of-magnitude mismatch driving the skepticism toward a hand-crank/trickle-charger combination as primary propulsion. One owner who already carries an **electric outboard** on a Paradox would still not consider using it around a dock, judging the yuloh both more convenient and more versatile for that close-quarters work.[65][66]

Confronted with the real numbers, the human-powered-generator idea was conceded outright: a "trickle" charger of this kind is only intended to keep an already-charged battery topped up over long idle periods, not to power a load such as a trolling motor, and the original battery-for-lights-and-occasional-motor-use plan was acknowledged as impractical once the actual figures were understood.[68]

Weighing a generator against a small outboard for backup power at all, the fuel-versus-battery tradeoff cuts both ways: a back-up generator itself needs a fuel supply and somewhere to store it, on top of the generator's own stowage — which can make a small outboard motor the simpler starting point instead.[67]

## A DIY pedal generator: converting an exercise bike

A separate, independent hand-crank/pedal-generator project converted a personal exerciser bought secondhand on eBay for about **$40** into a roughly **60-watt pedal generator** (about 5 amps at 12 volts), using a home-built **permanent magnet generator (PMG)** adapted from a reduced-size wind-power PMG design, with the extra magnets needed to avoid gearing given the pedal-driven input. The conversion parts cost a further **$20-30** depending on what components were already on hand. A daily cycling habit for exercise and transport made this a realistic output level: 60 watts is judged more power than the electronic gear aboard would need, even before accounting for a solar panel working alongside it, for a few hours a day of combined battery charging and exercise.[69]

Progress was slow going, limited mainly by having "too many things to work on at once" — the winding of the generator's coils had only just begun by the time of the last report, with the project treated as longer-term rather than urgent.[71]

## A watermaker as an alternative use for pedal power

Rather than a trolling motor, connecting a pedal- or hand-crank generator to a **watermaker/desalinator** was suggested as a potentially more valuable use of the same power source — reasoning that having enough water is the single biggest challenge to survival at sea, more so than food, and that sponsorship from a watermaker manufacturer might be worth pursuing for a build like this. A practical caution: try the unit before relying on it, since some watermakers produce water that tastes "yucky," and building or provisioning around dehydrated food would only increase the water demand a pedal-powered watermaker would need to meet.[70]

## Operating an outboard from inside the cabin

Running and controlling an outboard entirely from inside the cabin with the hatch closed — a workaround for a bad back that rules out an open cockpit — remains an open question, including whether a **2.3 hp four-stroke Honda** could be run under cover from inside the boat in the rain, and how far its integral one-litre tank would last at half throttle. [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) has been fitted with an outboard, but whether that installation handled inside-cabin operation is not recorded.

On motor and hull clearance: **the rudder does not clear the propeller**, though this is only a problem when maneuvering in tight quarters. As a historical aside on mounting, **British Seagull** motors used to be supplied with a fitting for mounting on the gunwale, in addition to the usual transom bracket — a possible option for a builder wanting to keep the transom clear.

A **2 hp Honda** on "[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)" works well, pushing her at 4-5 knots at a little over half throttle, and confirms the outboard-weight lesson above: a heavier **58 lb 4 hp Yamaha** could not be balanced properly for sailing on the same boat, while the lighter Honda gave no such trouble. On shaft length, both were tried and the **short shaft** preferred, reconfirming the same reaching-hull-speed-at-a-little-under-half-throttle figure — a piece of build-specific advice that also came up separately in another builder's own outboard-choice question, see [Enuf](/boats/enuf.md#the-yard-revisited-a-strop-and-sheave-halyard-and-finishing-the-boom).[33][34]

## Weighing a 2 hp four-stroke against a bigger motor, and whether Paradox can plane

A **2 hp Yamaha four-stroke** is a viable Paradox auxiliary, and the case for four-strokes generally: they tolerate the "little work" a heavy-displacement hull like Paradox's asks of them, and burn noticeably less fuel than an equivalent two-stroke, at the cost of added mechanical complexity. Between the two leading four-stroke brands, **Yamaha** has the wider worldwide parts-and-service network, favoring it over Honda where that availability matters. The weight-and-practicality question for a given hull is best settled by builders with hands-on experience running an outboard on a Matt Layden design — see the field record above. One reserve-power plan pairs a yuloh as primary auxiliary with a small, removable outboard (side-mounted from deck chocks, or through the hull) kept in reserve; the chief risk with any such setup is an oversized mount adding needless weight, not the engine itself.

Whether a larger engine can get a Paradox up onto a plane has a definite answer: **no** — by her hull shape alone, Paradox will not plane, though she will surf. Bartender-type hulls, a design comparison sometimes drawn against Paradox's chine runners, are able to plane because, unlike Paradox, they are double-enders with a **flat run aft and deliberate deadrise**, whose "planing rails" (also called spray rails in the UK) provide lift for a drier, softer ride at speed — a purpose-designed powerboat feature, not something that can be added to a sailing hull's underlying shape. Achieving genuine planing performance under sail-and-power requires designing a power boat from the start and fitting a sailing rig to it after the fact, as some US manufacturers have done — such craft are typically built light, water-ballasted, fitted with a centreboard, and given a flat run aft, none of which describes Paradox. Given that a human can generate only about a quarter horsepower, any outboard is a real gain in absolute terms even though it cannot change the hull's fundamental performance; if fitting one at all, a **2 to 2.3 hp Honda** is judged an ideal choice, and light US two-stroke outboards are also a good option. Experience with the **Honda 2 hp** confirms the small-and-light principle directly: it works well and reaches hull speed at about half throttle, whereas outboards in the **50 lb range** gave no luck getting the sailing trim right — the lighter the motor, the better a Paradox handles it.

A field test bears this out directly: a **4 hp four-stroke Yamaha** on "Whisper" reached 6.5 kn at most while "pushing way too much water" to lift onto a plane. The hull-shape reason is too much **rocker in the stern section** to climb over its own bow wave — a flatter run aft would be needed to plane, but that would drag its own tail when the boat heels under sail, a trade-off familiar from Bolger's sharpie designs, some of which are drawn to plane and sail well but never both equally. A short, flat-bottomed, rockered hull of this type moves easily through the water on its low wetted surface but not quickly, and even rows poorly for lack of "carry" between strokes — the same reason a yuloh, which applies continuous low-effort power, suits it well, and low-hp motors do too. **Phil Bolger**'s own summary of the type: a boat like this needs only about 2 hp at half throttle to reach hull speed; give it 10 hp and it will tolerate the power but gain only about half a knot more, "while dragging half the lake behind her."

On oar power more generally: heavier boats "carry" farther between strokes of an oar than light ones, though how Paradox in particular would perform under oars, or how a pair of oars would even be stowed aboard her, is untested. **John Letcher** wrote extensively on the viability of oar power on displacement sailboats and used oars as his sole auxiliary power on several boats. **Sculling** is generally preferred over rowing for a hull of this type: rowing feels like a treadmill, with the work broken into discrete strokes, while sculling with a yuloh applies a steady, rhythmic, continuous effort — more like an elliptical trainer.

## A side-mounted outboard, to keep the cabin layout unchanged

A separate proposal considered mounting a small outboard **outside the hull**, clipped to the port side near bulkhead #4, rather than on the transom or through a removable cabin panel — the motive being to keep the stock cabin arrangement intact while still allowing a 2 hp four-stroke to be started, refueled, and throttled from inside the hatchway without reaching over the stern. Small clips would let the motor and its mount be unshipped and stowed inside when not needed, along with the engine itself (about 27 lb). The trade-off of a side-mounted, off-center thrust line is a compensating turn of the motor toward the opposite side to hold a straight course, in exchange for handling convenience over full propulsion efficiency — proposed by a builder whose hands could no longer manage a yuloh comfortably.

## Further field experience with a side-mounted outboard

The side-mounted-outboard proposal above drew direct field confirmation and caution alike. On "[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)," an **electric** motor was fitted to the hull's side rather than the transom, precisely to avoid keeping petrol inside the cabin; the bracket needed to be genuinely sturdy, since a first attempt distorted at full throttle, and the working mounting settled on used **brass hinges with a removable pin**, braced by a forward strut to hold it square to the hull. A side-mounted motor cannot be left in place while sailing, since it would spend much of its time underwater, but needs surprisingly little angling to hold a straight course — the boat turns to starboard quicker than to port with the motor fixed and steering left to the rudder, since swinging the motor itself the other way would foul the propeller on the chine.[36] The motor is kept strictly as an emergency backup rather than routine auxiliary power, given its limited range of about 6 miles.[73] The motor itself is a 28 lb-thrust, 12-volt Minn Kota trolling motor, pushing the boat at about 3 knots on roughly 30 amps or 2 knots on roughly 15 amps — concrete consumption figures confirming the generic 30-amp draw estimated above for the smallest Minn Kota motors.[144] Choosing that thrust rating came down to a practical trial rather than a rule of thumb: advice going in called for at least 54 lb of thrust for a boat this heavy, but a dealer's own demonstrator at 28 lb thrust proved entirely adequate, topping out at 3 knots — chasing the higher figure and outright hull speed would only have bought a modest gain at a much steeper power-consumption cost.[174] The battery behind it is an 80Ah AGM deep-cycle unit, charged at home before setting off and again on return; underway it also runs the echosounder and GPS, charges a VHF radio and phone, and can drive an electric bilge pump, though that has never actually been needed, plus interior and navigation LEDs. A mains charger is carried as backup but has never been used afloat; after a three-week cruise it was still about 80% charged — a concrete measure of just how rarely the motor itself gets called on, since the boat is sculled by yuloh for preference, with the motor reserved for the rare case sculling alone won't do, such as one launch site reached only up a narrow river against a current not worth fighting by oar.[175][176]

The scheme is not unprecedented: side-mounted motors have appeared in more than one published design for **double-ended** hulls, which have no clear transom to mount a motor on in the first place, and the same logic underlies the older practice of lashing a dinghy-and-outboard alongside a larger cruiser to move her. Whichever form it takes, a side-mounted or lashed-on propeller needs to be kept well clear of the chine runners, chines, and sides, which it can otherwise damage.[37] A related but distinct case — mounting a small outboard directly on a double-ended **canoe's** side rather than its stern — was tried and found only a compromise at best, since the canoe crabbed sideways under power; the canoe's lack of a keel was judged a contributing factor, suggesting a keeled, chine-runner-equipped Paradox hull should track straighter under the same off-center thrust than a keel-less canoe would.[38][39]

## A worked side-mount installation, and the risk of shipping water while fitting it afloat

A side-mounted bracket for a **Honda 2 hp** outboard on "Ironsides" worked out as a wooden pad sized to the motor, small enough to add little windage or weight, with a hitch pin to lock the motor mount shaft's inner end (its hole not yet drilled at the time of reporting) and a plastic ball valve to keep water out of the hull when the mount is unshipped; a stiffening shelf braces up the area, and the vent-box cutout was left roughed in for the time being. Rather than drilling a separate lightning-ground conductor through the same area, it was run as a **copper pipe** doing double duty as a groove for another fitting, in place of Matt Layden's plans-specified plastic pipe. At bulkhead/frame 3 the resulting laydown width inside the hull measures **44 inches side to side**.[44]

Fitting a side-mounted motor while already afloat carries a real risk of shipping water, since the fitting process itself can heel the boat as the builder leans over the side to align the motor shaft with its mount — a risk not obvious in advance, since the concern with a side mount is usually assumed to be the motor's own submersion rather than the boat's trim while installing it. A practical mitigation: start the motor shaft into its hole only as far as the ball valve, then open the valve, rather than trying to align and seat the shaft in one motion; a lanyard on the motor itself is also worth adding, since an outboard dropped over the side while fitting it will not float.[45][46]

## An outboard bracket published for reference

Photographs of a **removable, side-mounted outboard bracket** — mounting the motor so it does not tangle in the sheet and so the boat can be motor-sailed, though the heel angle sailing must not be great enough to submerge the outboard's head — were separately published on a builder's own website as a reference for other builders, alongside a change-of-address notice after the previous site was overwhelmed by spam.[47]

## A Honda 2 hp's spare-parts and reliability record

Separately from the mounting question, the **Honda 2 hp**'s own reliability and spare-parts needs have a direct field record. Researching spares ahead of an extended cruise, one owner had already gathered the routine items — shear pins, propellers, spark plugs — and asked what else long-term Honda 2 hp users typically carry.[48] The answers were reassuring rather than alarming: two years of use with nothing going wrong at all, and no reports of trouble from other Honda 2 hp owners either.[49] One practical maintenance tip for topping up the motor's oil sump: keep a plastic squeeze bottle of the kind used for ketchup or mustard in a restaurant, decanting motor oil from it as needed rather than pouring from a full bottle or can.[52] On whether a spare throttle cable is worth carrying,[53] the consensus from owners who had never worn one out was that it sees so little stress or resistance in normal use that it should last a very long time, though anyone who does wear one out was asked to report back.[56] The cable's likely failure mode, by analogy with motorcycle throttle cables of essentially the same construction, is breakage or a nipple pulling off rather than gradual wear; a broken cable can still be worked around afloat by tying string directly to the exposed piano-wire part of the throttle control, though simply carrying a spare and replacing the cable outright is easier.[57][58]

## A winter Tenn-Tom Waterway delivery cruise, and Whisper's outboard as primary power

The spare-parts research above was prompted by a planned roughly 300-mile, single-season delivery cruise down the **Tennessee-Tombigbee Waterway** ("the Tenn-Tom") from the Tennessee River to the Gulf of Mexico, using the Honda 2 hp as main propulsion rather than as backup or auxiliary power — a rare case of a Paradox owner relying on the outboard, not the sailing rig, as primary power for an entire passage. The planned route ran from **Bay Springs Lake** (itself about 30 miles down the Tenn-Tom from the Tennessee River's Pickwick Lake) to **Pirates Cove, Alabama**, across the bay from Pensacola — a lock system carrying the boat down from mountain country to coastal flat land.[48][51][54][55] This is the same "[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)" whose 2 hp Honda is already the reference data point above for reaching hull speed at half throttle;[10][34] the cruise plan itself, and the well-wishes it drew from other list members, are recorded in full at [Whisper — a winter Tenn-Tom Waterway delivery cruise](/boats/whisper.md#a-winter-tenn-tom-waterway-delivery-cruise).

## A port-quarter Honda 2.3 mounting, and a port-side electric outboard's setup time

A further recollection describes Honda 2.3 hp outboards mounted on the **port quarter** rather than the transom, cautioning that snagging of the mainsheet on the engine in that position "can and does happen" — a caution worth weighing against the transom- and side-mounting options detailed above.[72] The same recollection describes a port-side electric outboard as a working setup, but not an instantaneous one to bring into use.[72]

## A side-mounted motor-and-fuel sponson considered and rejected; a three-position transom bracket

A further side-mounting idea combined the motor mount with fuel stowage in one fitting: a sponson-like extension of an existing side motor-mount bracket, long enough to also hold a plastic jerrycan of fuel, doubling as some shelter for the motor from big swells.[135] Weighed against it: the same location works well for clearing fouling problems (confirmed by an electric outboard already mounted there), but it cannot stay there for any serious sailing unless made properly waterproof — on a fast reach the lee rail runs close to submerged, and even to windward in a moderate Force 3 the boat heels around **20 degrees** by her own stability curve. Fuel itself is better kept on the aft deck against the cabin window, clear of sailing gear and any water coming aboard.[136]

A firsthand account of weighing the same side-mount idea for "[Faith](/boats/faith.md)" settled it in favor of the transom instead: mounting a Honda 2.3 to starboard was considered and rejected as impracticable, since the engine would be vulnerable to submersion whenever the boat heeled or was motor-sailing; a sponson-style extension raised its own problem of where to stow it when not in use, since a permanent fitting there would hurt sailing performance. The transom proved the only practical spot, protected from swamping even in boisterous conditions, with the mainsheet fouling the motor only rarely — when the motor is raised to its highest position with the shaft near horizontal. A three-position mounting bracket (high, medium, low) lets the motor run at any of the three; in the high position, with the engine vertical, only the tip of the skeg touches the water, so there is no appreciable drag and the sheet runs clear. Petrol storage was no problem either, keeping two 5-litre cans at the forward end of the cabin, with the cooking stove never lit while the engine is being refuelled.[137]

The idea's originator accepted the heeling objection as decisive against his own side-mount sketch, and asked whether a transom-mounted motor's throttle and refuelling are both reachable from inside the cabin, or whether managing the engine still means going out onto the deck.[138]

The throttle can be reached standing inside the cabin, though it takes a bit of a stretch; refuelling can likewise be managed from inside, but not blind, since judging how much fuel remains in the tank still means stepping onto the rear deck to look inside it, pouring through a large funnel tied to the boat with a cord. A rubber bulb pump fitted with a tube at each end, to transfer fuel between cans without fully opening either, and a fishing float rigged inside the tank as a level gauge, were both proposed as refinements — solutions that would have suited a sustained delivery-style passage far better than repeatedly clambering onto the stern deck, but judged unnecessary for occasional use: a 2 hp Honda at a third throttle already runs about two hours at 3 knots, more motoring endurance than a Paradox's short bursts of engine use typically call for, so the bulb-pump refinement was left for whenever another long cruise comes along.[139] The exchange was illustrated afterward with photographs — captioned as taken "in the middle of the Atlantic" — posted to the group's photo section, showing the refuelling process candidly in practice: engine still running and in gear, with no safety harness worn.[140]

The same photographs drew a two-word verdict from an outside reader — the boat, name and all, "serves well."[146] Pressed further on the same routine, reaching the tank from inside the cabin by stretching over the aft deck is possible, though not while judging the fuel level, and a bulb pump would ease the pumping itself without solving that same blind spot. On the missing harness: going without one was defended as the safer choice in practice, since a harness gets in the way and can cause its own problems, with free movement preferred except in the very worst conditions — precisely when refuelling would not be attempted regardless. A further trick found by experience: leaving the engine ticking over in gear, rather than out of it, makes the boat noticeably more stable and the whole job easier. Every choice here — engine, refuelling method, harness or none — comes down to the individual owner's own judgment and accepted risk.[147]

## A transom-mounted petrol outboard, fuel stowage, and trim

Separately from the side-mount approach, a transom-mounted petrol outboard remains a live option, with its own fuel-stowage and trim tradeoffs. One proposal for keeping the fuel and controls manageable at the stern: build an **open-topped outboard bracket box** on the transom, sized to hold a 2-gallon fuel jug, with the outboard itself clamped to the box's aft edge and its throttle, kill switch, and pull-start cord all extended forward to the cockpit.[40]

Loading roughly 50 lb of fuel and motor that far aft of the transom calls for a deliberate trim correction, shifting crew and ballast to compensate. Because Paradox's cabin forward sole sits level with the boat's designed waterline, a simple built-in level check is available for getting that trim right: either a cheap caravan spirit-level gauge, or a closed loop of 1/4-3/8 in clear fuel line (joined with a short piece of copper tube and the water inside tinted with ink) run between marks of equal height on the bins — either method reads trim directly off the boat herself rather than requiring any separate calculation.[41]

A full working example of a transom-mounted petrol installation: a **2.3 hp Honda**, chosen over an electric motor specifically to avoid depending on recharging, mounted on the transom with its throttle extended by a length of PVC pipe and its kill switch worked by a cord, both reachable through a removable rear window — a fitting substantial enough that its temporary loss overboard was, briefly, mistaken as plausible aboard a boat as small as a Paradox. In practice this motor returned about **7 miles per litre at 4 mph**, carried as one litre in the motor's own integral tank plus a four-litre reserve in fuel bottles stowed in a bucket behind bulkhead four — a storage compromise considered too close to the cooking area for comfort, given the ventilation otherwise maintained while cooking further forward near bulkhead two. A longer-term fix under consideration was an external tank feeding the motor's integral tank by bulb and pipe (the approach used aboard Little Cruiser), with the outboard's own throttle brought inside via a Bowden cable. Both a side-mounted electric motor and a transom-mounted petrol motor are confirmed working setups in practice, the choice coming down to the recharging-versus-carrying-fuel tradeoff described above.[42]

A separate builder, weighing the same tradeoff for his own boat, settled on keeping the fuel supply entirely outside the hull rather than adopting the bucket-behind-bulkhead-four arrangement above: a plastic **jerrycan** stowed on the transom itself, in the same spot the motor normally occupies, fed to the motor via the same bulb-and-pipe approach. Separately, mounting the outboard's **anticavitation plate flush with the bottom of the hull** — rather than at the manufacturer's default depth — keeps the propeller from running any deeper than the chine runners themselves, protecting them from contact.[43]

Weighing an outboard against the alternative of no motor at all, the **transom-mounted mainsheet** is itself a hazard to anything mounted there: it is almost guaranteed to tangle with a transom-mounted fitting, and in the worst case a violent gybe could tear the motor off and throw it into the sea; the deeper problem with a petrol motor is simply where to put the fuel, since carrying it inside the cabin means keeping it in the same space used for sleeping and cooking.[73]

A parallel account frames the same choice explicitly as inside-with-the-cooker versus on-deck, and records a foredeck-strapped can as one documented on-deck practice — though "with some of the seas I have been in I could not guarantee it would stay there."[86]

## Petrol safety practice, and two aft-deck fuel-compartment proposals

A **prior microcruiser's** experience offers one data point on the risk in practice: aboard a 13 ft 4 in Polycell Voyager, a few gallons of petrol were kept below decks for a season in old steel oil cans without incident.[74] Building on that, a mockup tested whether a Paradox's **stern quarters** could each take a small sealed, fiberglassed compartment — glassed inside and out, vented, holding a standard 5-litre polypropylene fuel container, and accessed from the deck only — confirming plenty of room remained for the tiller's full arc; the real complication was that deck access to the compartment would be blocked by the stowed yuloh, yard, and boom, and cutting the vent baffle to fit it took hours to get right.[74] The idea was judged sound if an engine is wanted at all, in keeping with a settled build philosophy of finishing to the stock plans first and retrofitting ideas only once their real consequences are clear — in practice, none of one builder's own pre-build ideas for "[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)" were ever implemented, each turning out to conflict with some other aspect not appreciated at the time.[75]

A professional safety background sharpened the caution around carrying petrol at all: work surveying for a sign company supplying **BP** petrol stations required a three-day BP safety course, which drove home that a petrol station forecourt is statistically among the most dangerous places a person routinely enters; petrol vapour, heavier than air, sinks to the lowest points available — a hazard on foggy or misty days ashore, and directly analogous to a boat's bilge — making specialist advice worth seeking before fitting a petrol motor on a boat as small as Paradox.[76] A practical field precaution for a petrol can carried in the cockpit: keep a full-size towel soaked in water draped over the can, re-wetting it as it dries.[77] A second, independent aft-deck fuel-compartment proposal reached a similar conclusion to the stern-quarter mockup above: a small area under the aft deck, sized for a 1/2- to 1-gallon can, enclosed in fibreglass inside and out with two vents and a small gasketed deck hatch for access, judged a safe way to carry petrol aboard.[77]

A further precedent pointed to for safe petrol stowage was a purpose-built small powerboat design (mokai.com), which carries its fuel in an enclosed engine compartment vented to the outside, fed to the motor by a line, rather than in an open can — the same enclosed-and-vented principle behind both aft-deck fuel-compartment proposals above.[85]

## A 2008 recurrence: a published account, quarter-tank proposals, and a pivot to electric power

The fuel-storage question recurred again in 2008, prompted partly by a well-known solo trans-Atlantic sailor's own published cruising account of fuel odor in his boat's cabin despite fuel cans that were not themselves leaking, and by a nearby Paradox owner's fuel filler cap having recently gone missing overboard — raising, at the pre-build stage, whether sealed fuel "bins" could be built into the port and starboard quarters on the same principle as the built-in water-ballast tanks, with filler caps and vents on the quarterdeck sealed off from the cabin space.[96]

The recurrence pivoted toward avoiding petrol storage altogether: an electric auxiliary was argued as the better option specifically because a cook stove otherwise sits too close to the outboard's own fuel supply, with a further proposal to replace a meaningful share of the lead ballast with deep-cycle batteries, charged by a small wind generator, and put to use beyond just the motor — a small, light modern radar set, an amateur radio for calls home, and the GPS/navigation computer, plus a small inverter for limited AC aboard; docked in a side yard between trips, the same wind generator and battery bank could double as useful backup power.[97] The idea drew interest in a dedicated electric-boats discussion group as further reading, alongside an open, unresolved question of whether solar panels alone could keep up with the battery bank's charging demand.[98]

The same electric-power pivot prompted a galley-side idea from the pedal-generator project's own correspondent, above: running a miniature microwave oven — a compact 10 x 10 x 12 in, roughly 10 lb unit drawing 600 W and outputting 350 W — off the same kind of battery bank, judged practical once the actual time cost is weighed rather than assumed prohibitive: a few minutes to boil water for a hot drink, about ten minutes for a full meal, leaving the rest of the day to put the energy back via solar or wind charging; a conventional sealed-gas-cartridge stove was suggested to keep in reserve for whenever the electric outboard sees heavier use than planned.[99]

A firsthand fuel-storage practice came from an owner already partway through a live cruise at the time: two five-litre petrol cans stowed in the forepeak, inspected often for leaks, the cabin kept clear of fuel fumes, and the stove never lit while fuel is being handled — paired with a considered judgment that a petrol engine is more reliable than an electric motor over real distances. Full first-person account at [Faith — gale-bound at Brighton: a petrol-storage routine, and a case for reliability over electric power](/boats/faith.md#gale-bound-at-brighton-a-petrol-storage-routine-and-a-case-for-reliability-over-electric-power).[100]

The battery-ballast proposal drew a safety caution in reply: charging batteries gives off flammable, explosive hydrogen gas, illustrated by a workshop accident in which a battery charger left running blew the top off a car battery and showered the mechanic handling it with acid — a hazard judged more serious in a confined space such as a Paradox cabin than in an open workshop, though petrol's own distinctive smell was allowed as a point in its favour, giving warning of a leak before it turns dangerous.[101] Sealed deep-cycle batteries answer part of the concern directly: modern sealed cells gas far less than older unsealed ones, can be mounted on their side or even upside down if needed, and fit into unusual spaces aboard a small boat — though the vented hydrogen they still produce must be accounted for in the installation regardless.[102]

The stern-quarter fuel-locker mock-up already on record (see [above](#petrol-safety-practice-and-two-aft-deck-fuel-compartment-proposals)) was restated in the same exchange, with a new mainsheet-routing detail added for it. Full account at [boats/johanna.md — a mainsheet question relayed to Glen Maxwell, and the foredeck's stubborn curve](/boats/johanna.md#a-mainsheet-question-relayed-to-glen-maxwell-and-the-foredecks-stubborn-curve).[103]

A harder-numbers critique followed, aimed at electric power as a sole propulsion-and-cooking system: solar panels alone cannot keep up with the combined demand of propulsion and cooking, and a wind generator capable of meaningful output needs blades of six feet or more — impractical on a boat the size of a Paradox. A pedal-powered generator was offered as a more realistic source, good for up to about 100 watts, alongside a water-powered generator towed astern under sail. For cooking specifically, a plain resistive immersion-heating element was argued the more efficient choice over a microwave — 100% efficient by nature against a microwave's roughly 50%, provided it heats water in a well-insulated container such as a thermos to avoid losing the advantage to heat loss; an electric cooker of any kind was allowed only as an occasional backup for conditions too rough to run the gimballed gas stove safely.[104] The same critique separately raised saltwater's own hazard to a battery bank: electrolysis of seawater reaching batteries and wiring can produce chlorine gas, arguing for a battery housing built genuinely waterproof and, ideally, jettisonable in a swamping emergency.[105]

A practical data point on cooking time came from testing a household microwave: a piece of fish cooked through in about three minutes wrapped in clingfilm with a little butter, and a portion of rice in about two — suggesting 100-200 watts and five to six minutes would suffice for a full meal aboard, consistent with the miniature marine unit's figures above. The same correspondent's own pedal generator — a modified exercise bike fitted with a home-built permanent-magnet generator, already an ongoing project by 2007 (see [a DIY pedal generator: converting an exercise bike](#a-diy-pedal-generator-converting-an-exercise-bike) above) — remained in daily use, ridden two to three hours a day for exercise as much as for charging.[106] A specific wind-generator product was named for the battery-ballast scheme: a 400-watt, 12-volt unit rated to that output in a 9 mph wind, found from discount retailers for as little as $500, judged adequate paired with four good deep-cycle batteries to replace a Paradox's lead ballast outright and cover the whole proposed electrical load.[107]

This 2008 exchange restates, rather than adds to, the batteries-as-ballast case already made in full at [weight, ballast, and displacement — gel-cell batteries as ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#gel-cell-batteries-as-ballast-an-electric-outboard-and-the-bird-watcher-ii-precedent).

## A newcomer's hybrid-electric proposal, and the Aquasub precedent

Choosing between Paradox and a competing "Black Skimmer" design, a newcomer ordered the Paradox plans, intending to begin building that autumn toward a summer 2009 launch, and reopened the electric-versus-gasoline debate: whether improved micro-generator technology could make an electric trolling motor a viable substitute for a gasoline outboard, and whether an 18 lb-thrust trolling motor, available for under $100, could move a Paradox at even 3 knots.[141] The standing case was restated in reply: there is little spare room aboard a 13.5 ft Paradox for a full hybrid system, even single-handed, though the idea is worth pursuing regardless, since some battery capacity is needed anyway for basic electronics whether or not a trolling motor is added — making a small trolling motor a realistic option for a boat this size, with a generator's own size and exhaust venting the main further considerations.[142]

A different small-boat design, the Aquasub, offered a worked precedent for a fuller hybrid system: two 6-volt golf-cart batteries, housed in a fully sealed, watertight enclosure fitted with a device to consume the hydrogen the batteries throw off, power both an exhaust fan and two trolling motors for a claimed four hours of continuous running. Mounting such a battery bank on a small plank able to slide between pre-positioned brackets would let it double as movable ballast, charged by a small movable wind turbine or a combined solar-panel-and-generator setup — restating, in a new form, the battery-as-ballast idea already covered in full at [weight, ballast, and displacement — gel-cell batteries as ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#gel-cell-batteries-as-ballast-an-electric-outboard-and-the-bird-watcher-ii-precedent). A small (roughly 500-1,000 W) portable generator, and a pedal-powered charger posted separately to the group's files section, were both floated as compact charging options for the same scheme.[143] Concrete Minn Kota consumption figures for a proven electric setup (see [above](#further-field-experience-with-a-side-mounted-outboard)) came as welcome, reassuring news to the same newcomer weighing the whole tradeoff.[144][145]

## Marine wind generators and a towed underwater generator, weighed against a plain outboard

A pedal-powered charger already on file was allowed as a reasonable option, if only because sailing a Paradox burns little enough energy to leave legs free for pedaling; more interest went to the idea of a small wind generator, though the accompanying link needed retyping directly into a browser before it would open.[148] A marine-rated 12/24-volt wind generator was suggested next, sold as better suited to the purpose than a generic model.[149] Reaction was mixed: a genuinely nice unit, capable of keeping batteries topped up overnight while waiting on sun, but possibly too large to manage within a Paradox's limited deck space.[150]

A towed underwater-propeller generator was floated as a further option: dragged astern on a line, one such unit begins charging at about 2.5 knots, with a model found delivering some 4 amps at 3 knots; mounting a wind turbine on the mast overnight was raised alongside it, the underwater unit's chief appeal being the ability to anchor in a fast stream or river and let the current do the charging.[151] Weighed against the whole menu of electric options, the settled judgment remained unchanged: a small 2-3 hp four-stroke outboard and a tank of petrol is hard to beat for cost and efficiency, however unglamorous, with a small solar panel on the battery bank enough on its own to keep a GPS and a handheld VHF radio alive.[152]

A **foredeck** mounting was recommended for any wind generator fitted to a Paradox — clear of the control lines and sails and in free air — at the cost of occasional green water coming aboard in the kind of conditions the boat is actually sailed in.[153] The underwater generator drew a specific caution in reply: its maker gives no drag figure at all, and a meaningful speed penalty under tow seems likely.[154]

Weighing that caution against the underwater generator's own appeal, it was accepted as probably too much drag for a boat this size under way, but worth keeping in mind for a different role: anchored overnight in a stream or fast river current, letting the flow do the charging rather than towing the unit while sailing. A mast-mounted wind turbine was floated alongside it for the same overnight, at-anchor use, with the sail stowed and out of the way — noting that many wind-turbine models bolt their blades to the chassis in a way that would let the blades be untightened and folded or removed for stowage — with both ideas framed as backup capacity for the rare stretches when solar alone falls short.[155]

## A wind generator's real output, and a historical fuel-versus-battery baseline

The 400-watt wind-generator figure named above only holds above roughly 28 mph of wind; at a more ordinary 9 mph the same unit delivers closer to 50-70 watts — a real but far smaller contribution than the flat 400-watt figure implies, though still worthwhile rather than negligible. A separate account of an electric catamaran offered a working precedent for panels carrying genuine propulsion load: a large battery bank backed by a modest number of solar panels, meant only as an adjunct to shore charging, in practice sustained about 6 knots on panel power alone in continuous good sun; more powerful, cheaper panels and wider acceptance of electric propulsion were expected within a few years.[108] Weighing the same battery-ballast idea further, informed by a thirty-year electronics background, a deep-cycle bank paired with a wind generator was judged enough to make genuine extras — a light radar set, a small microwave — realistic aboard a boat this size; the real bottleneck remained finding a genuinely decent electric outboard, without which liquid fuel stayed the practical choice, and a stretched or NOLI-sized hull was floated as making the whole all-electric concept easier to realize.[109][110]

A historical baseline for the same debate: Gerry Spiess's 1979 solo crossing of 3,800 miles of Atlantic in a 10-foot sailboat of his own design, taking a little over 54 days, ran on an outboard and its fuel plus a gimballed portable gas stove and gas cans for cooking — batteries were carried only to power a radio and tape recorder, not for propulsion or cooking.[111] Set against the same-day battery-ballast proposals above, even a genuinely minimal ocean crossing still relied on liquid fuel and flame, not batteries, for propulsion and cooking.

The hydrogen-gas caution above drew a second, independent confirmation, naming the product category directly: paying extra for a sealed battery — a Gel Cell or AGM — avoids the venting problem outright; one such AGM battery has no vents at all, is rated to be mounted at any attitude, and is claimed to work even submerged.[112]

## Further stern-quarter fuel-storage proposals: vented bins preferred over built-in tanks, and a copper-tank design

Returning to the original fuel-bin question, built-in tanks in the hull itself were discouraged in favor of vented bins holding removable cans, along the lines already under construction elsewhere (see [above](#petrol-safety-practice-and-two-aft-deck-fuel-compartment-proposals)); a similar bin arrangement had been considered for anchor stowage and rejected on ergonomic grounds — lifting a heavy anchor bent over and at full reach — and the one real drawback of a fuel bin in the same location is that deck access to it would be blocked by the stowed yuloh and sail/boom.[113] A firsthand data point from years of ownership: fuel stored simply on deck, with no problems, including outboard-only river runs of up to 150 miles at a stretch with no place to refuel along the way. Full account at [Whisper — deck-stowed fuel, and years without a problem](/boats/whisper.md#deck-stowed-fuel-and-years-without-a-problem).[114]

A caution belongs with any built-in **wet** fuel tank specifically, as distinct from a locker holding removable cans: modern gasoline blends commonly contain some percentage of ethanol, which degrades epoxy and polyester over time, and only vinylester resin is reported to withstand it — a wet tank built any other way is a matter of when, not if, it leaks. Full detail at [epoxy brands, chemistry, and cure behavior — ethanol's effect on epoxy and polyester fuel tanks](/construction/epoxy-brands-and-chemistry.md#ethanols-effect-on-epoxy-and-polyester-fuel-tanks).[115]

Closing out the stern-quarter proposals, a metal-tank alternative was floated along similar lines to the vented-locker approach above: tanks custom-made from **20-gauge copper sheet** to fit the odd shapes of the stern corners — a technique drawn from the published *Of Yachts and Men* — filled from filler caps set in the rear deck and vented through the stern, with the tanks cross-linked at their base to a central draw-off point through independent shutoff valves; the goal throughout remained the one driving the whole thread: keeping fuel odour out of the cabin.[116]

## Reactions to the copper-tank proposal, and refining a cap-and-pipe fueling method

The copper-tank proposal drew skepticism on its own terms: a built-in tank carries a leak risk that an ordinary portable **5-litre plastic fuel can** does not, since a leaking can can simply be picked up, moved clear, poured into the engine, or ditched over the side, while getting fuel from a built-in tank to the outboard at all would need a hand pump and pipework — itself a further point of failure.[117] A second, independent reaction reached the same "over-engineered" verdict: "sounds suitably complicated that something is eventually guaranteed to go wrong."[118]

The recurring problem behind the whole redesign — a lost outboard filler cap — drew three separate fixes on record: drilling a hole through the cap itself and fitting a pipe, so fuel can be pumped straight in without ever removing the cap;[118] a simpler cord lanyard securing the cap, and the filling funnel too;[119] and, on at least one outboard, a factory answer to the same problem already built in — a **Honda 2.3 hp** whose filler cap is fitted from new with an internal chain, making it impossible to lose short of applying real force.[121]

A built-in "supply tank" of any kind was questioned on placement grounds alone: it would need a secure location and, in time, its own replenishment, with no obvious spot found that would not intrude on the cabin's accommodation.[120] The proposed fix needs no built-in supply tank at all — the "supply tank" can simply be the fuel already carried in an ordinary 5-litre plastic can: cap off, a pipe inserted, and the fuel pumped straight across to the motor's own integral tank from inside the cabin rather than by reaching out over the deck, with a plug for the pipe's free end to keep water out — the same method attributed to Dave Bolduc's own practice.[122]

## Sealed-battery charging care, cost, and a light-aircraft cautionary tale

The hydrogen-gas caution and sealed-battery answer above (see [a wind generator's real output, and a historical fuel-versus-battery baseline](#a-wind-generators-real-output-and-a-historical-fuel-versus-battery-baseline)) drew a skeptical follow-up: a sealed type avoids spilled acid, but reportedly needs more careful charging and costs more; firsthand use of a sealed lead-acid (Yuasa) battery on a **microlight aircraft** found it did not last long, suspected to be because the aircraft engine's regulated charging output did not suit that battery type well.[123] General charging guidance for the same battery class: avoid both over-charging and over-discharging — repeatedly taking the battery below **10.5 volts** slowly degrades its ability to hold a charge, while consistent overcharging can kill even the more abuse-tolerant sealed types.[124] The tradeoff was underlined bluntly: an expensive sealed battery typically needs an equally expensive charger to look after it properly, since electric power is not cheap.[125]

A proposed explanation for the microlight battery's short life: a solid-state voltage regulator charging at a healthy **14.4 volts** at cruising speed, current flow high at first and dwindling to a trickle later — a charging profile an ordinary wet lead-acid battery tolerates but that can be "lethal" for some sealed gel-type cells, a risk that would carry over to charging a sealed battery from a boat's own alternator.[126] A cautionary aside from the same background: cleaning up after a normal wet lead-acid battery in a light aircraft that had been doing aerobatics made "a hell of a mess" — a small aircraft moves around jerkily much as a small boat does, part of the case for a sealed battery even at some cost to its working life.[126] Left open on record: whether a cheap solar panel could overcharge, and so damage, this class of sealed battery.[126]

## Real-world solar and wind performance, and a professional's skepticism toward battery-electric propulsion

A sealed AGM or Gel Cell battery was independently confirmed as the sensible boat-use choice (see [above](#a-wind-generators-real-output-and-a-historical-fuel-versus-battery-baseline)). Professional solar- and wind-power research work supplies a harder-edged skepticism behind that endorsement: published solar-panel performance figures are measured under ideal conditions, with a new panel facing the sun directly — a boat's own horizontal, pitching panel, shadowed by sails and spars and exposed to salt spray and cloud cover, is lucky to see 50% of its rated output. Wind generators fare no better under sail: with any wind at all while under way, the generator will not turn unless the boat is beating to weather, in which case the generator itself becomes a drag on boat speed; in port, larger boats and shoreside structures block the wind, on top of the inherent hazard the spinning propeller itself poses alongside a boat this small. A field data point from that same research background: powering test equipment required carrying a portable generator to run a microwave oven, since even the smallest microwave available at the time (800 W output) drew roughly 10 amps from a 120-volt household outlet — calling for a 1,000-watt nominal, 1,250-watt-peak generator, complete with its own built-in inverter and battery charger, at a good 40 lb. Set against that baseline, the miniature marine microwave discussed earlier in this thread — 600 W input for 350 W output — is judged not very efficient, its output barely above a household microwave's defrost setting, since converting electrical energy into heat is inherently one of the least efficient ways to cook. Whether a Paradox's deck holds enough photovoltaic area to recharge a depleted battery from empty at all is doubted — probably only enough to keep one topped up for small 12-volt electronics. The soundest solution on these numbers remains what has already proved out in practice earlier in this thread: a small petrol outboard, a 2 hp Honda by name, backed by a yuloh, and a small gimballed gas stove for cooking — leaving fuel storage, not power generation, as the one real remaining challenge (see [a 2008 recurrence](#a-2008-recurrence-a-published-account-quarter-tank-proposals-and-a-pivot-to-electric-power) above). The closing advice: weigh every potential disadvantage of solar, wind, and battery power carefully before committing to it, since fossil fuel remains by far the best-performing option by weight and cost per unit of energy delivered — summed up in a bet that a litre of petrol and a small outboard would carry a boat "a lot farther and faster" than eighty pounds of deep-cycle batteries and a trolling motor.[127]

A concrete trolling-motor data point sits alongside that verdict: a 25 lb battery paired with an electric trolling motor delivers about four hours at 2 knots, or two hours at 3 knots — workable for emergency use, but no substitute for the petrol-and-yuloh combination above. Absent onboard charging, the same setup relies on a mains charger topped up dockside at a marina as needed, a need that had not yet arisen in practice.[128]

## Little Cruiser and Swamp Thing's own electrical systems, and battery choice for continuous small loads

Weighing 12-volt power for a small boat's typical load — an occasional spotlight, running lights, a small deck light, a fishfinder, a VHF radio, and at-anchor entertainment gear — against a compact motorcycle battery's modest 14 amp-hour rating prompted a full electrical-system comparison drawn from two working boats.[129] On [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md), a 32 amp-hour deep-cycle SLA battery, recharged by up to 40 watts (2 amp-hours) from three solar panels, proves sufficient to power everything aboard including a 100-watt HF radio; Swamp Thing runs a far more minimal system by comparison — a 7.2 amp-hour gel cell charged by a single 10-watt (0.5 amp-hour) panel, intended only for charging or running a few small devices (a cell phone, a GPS, an iPod, a few LED lights) and not all at the same time. On cooking, a single one-pound gas canister typically lasts up to a week, with twelve canisters usually carried for a three-month cruise and often one or two brought home unused. On fuel, up to nine gallons of gasoline are carried at the start of a longer trip — about 36 hours of continuous motoring on a 3.5 hp outboard at an average 4.5 knots in light wind — stored inside the boat despite this being less than the ideal or recommended way to do it, with a standing precaution against lighting the stove or using any electrical device at all if fuel fumes are smelled, until the fumes clear and their source is found and removed. The standing lesson drawn from years of this experience: keep systems as simple as possible while cruising, since added complexity mostly just adds time spent maintaining and repairing it.[130]

Much of the small-device list above already carries its own internal battery, sidestepping the wiring question for those loads entirely — the simplest fix is to carry spares and buy more as needed rather than wiring every device into a boat's 12-volt system.[131] An ordinary (non-SLA, non-Gelcell) motorcycle battery is nonetheless a poor match for any of this duty regardless of its rated amp-hours: such batteries are not built for constant discharge to low levels, and fewer than half a dozen full-discharge cycles is enough to ruin one outright, where a true sealed-lead-acid battery type is often rated for several hundred.[132] Gel cells were endorsed specifically for a twin-hulled boat's duty cycle — one battery-and-panel set per hull, one for entertainment and one for navigation — being compact and, unlike wet-cell batteries, less prone to trouble from repeated deep discharging, pairing well with solar charging as a result. The same exchange favored solid-state flash-memory players over disc-based CD/DVD equipment for exactly this environment: no moving parts to fail, and enough storage on a device small enough to wear around the neck or carry in a phone to hold a season's worth of music, video, or photos — with a carried harmonica offered, half in jest, as the simplest entertainment option of all; its most memorable review on record: good portability, but "the picture is terrible (and the sound isn't much better)."[133][134]

## Aft-deck tiller clearance, an abandoned anchor well, and yoke or crank steering declined

The tiller sweeps under most of the aft deck, leaving room for a fuel compartment only in the spot already proposed above; a builder who tried at length to fit an accessible anchor well through the deck eventually accepted the idea as infeasible. The standing caution behind both verdicts: a boat is a compromise, and it is not worth destroying the things a builder likes about a design chasing some "improvement."[78] Yoke or crank steering was suggested as a way to reclaim some of that space, trading a swinging tiller for a steering mechanism left more exposed to the weather.[79] An aft-facing tiller was weighed as a middle path but rejected: repairing one in a remote anchorage would be far more complicated than the stock arrangement, it would need extra length to clear the mainsheet, and the resulting steering-rope angle would run too shallow — the stock tiller was kept.[80]

## Whether fitting a motor is worth it at all

Matt Layden's original design did not include a motor, a fact worth weighing before deciding to build one in. A sailor who judges a motor essential — weighing sailing skill, personal fitness, cruising area, and the time available on the water — may be better served choosing a design with a motor already built into it, and a free-draining, vented motor well is the safest and most convenient way to carry one.[81] See [alternatives for a larger sailor — a recommended alternative for a larger sailor](/similar-boat-designs/alternatives-for-a-larger-sailor.md#a-recommended-alternative-for-a-larger-sailor-michalaks-caroline-and-the-case-against-modifying-paradox-to-fit) for the follow-on debate over whether such a ready-made alternative design actually exists.

## A side-mounted emergency motor, and a caution against deck-stowed fuel in a seaway

A **side-mounted electric motor**, stowed inboard except for emergency use or motoring up-river at a trip's end, is one owner's answer to the whole question. Repeated green water washing over the decks, in weather he later judged himself unwise to have sailed into, left him wary of both a deck-stowed fuel can — which he doubts would stay put — and a transom-mounted motor, which risks being swamped outright.[83] The broader caution offered alongside it: Paradox is a great boat, but a builder should not get blinded by the hype — list what a boat actually needs to do, then find the boat that best fits those requirements, rather than trying to distort one design into doing what another already does better.[83]

## A folding outboard's mount, and fuel tied down between the rear cleats

Aboard "[Jill](/boats/jill.md)," a folding-mount outboard sits offset to one side, its gas can stowed between the two rear cleats and held down with a bungee cord looped over the cover to the cleat; the sheet stays clear of it unless too much slack is let out, in which case the rudder is usually the real culprit. The setup is judged not to strip the boat of her sailing ability or stability, ballast adjusted to compensate, and the outboard decision alone is not reason enough to rule out a Paradox.[82] A sturdier alternative tie-down uses a 1½ in ratchet strap, the same type used to strap lumber to a truck bed; a small, low-profile tank built directly into the rear deck itself — vented and drainable rather than a sealed internal compartment — was floated as an alternative to a portable can, and a folding mount that lifts the motor clear of the water has kept swamping from ever being a problem.[84]

## Electric outboards, and batteries doing double duty as ballast

An electric outboard is a workable alternative to a petrol motor, and appears elsewhere on this page in side-mounted installations on "[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)" and "[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)." Pairing an electric outboard's battery bank with the boat's fixed lead ballast — batteries doing double duty as ballast, with Phil Bolger's "Bird Watcher II" as a real precedent — is a design-level question in its own right, covered together with the rest of the ballast discussion at [weight, ballast, and displacement](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#gel-cell-batteries-as-ballast-an-electric-outboard-and-the-bird-watcher-ii-precedent).

## How much continuous power a human body can actually generate

The rowing-as-backup-propulsion idea invites a harder question: how much continuous horsepower a human body can really sustain, as distinct from a short burst. A published data point on a trained cyclist gives a useful upper bound: **200 watts for 20 minutes** (about **0.27 hp**) for a cyclist in good shape, on an efficient machine, using leg and torso power — with a top-level professional able to roughly double that output over the same span. Discounted for an ordinary sailor rather than an athlete, an out-of-shape rower's realistic *continuous* output falls well under that, plausibly closer to **1/6 hp** for the legs alone, with arm-only effort lower still. This bears directly on any human-powered or hybrid electric-with-manual-backup propulsion scheme: episodic rowing on the occasional weekend or day sail is not enough exercise to keep the relevant muscles conditioned for sustained use when actually needed underway, and no sailboat hull is particularly well suited to rowing or other human propulsion in the first place.[29][30][31]

A **100 amp-hour, 12V lead-acid deep-cycle battery** — the unit size behind the battery-ballast proposal above — weighs closer to **50 lb** than the 100 lb sometimes assumed.[29]

A real-world data point beyond the pure-cyclist figures above: in the right boat, with the right gear, one person's continuous output has carried a human-powered craft under pedal and oar power from Alberta to Nova Scotia via rivers and the coast, at **4 knots**, with no batteries involved.[32]

## A calorie-based case for human power, and its real-world limits

A 2008 exchange reframed the human-vs-motor propulsion question in terms of raw calories: a typical can of baked beans holds roughly 2,000 kJ (about 500 food calories, or 500 watt-hours) — enough, in theory, to run a small outboard drawing 15 amps at 12 volts (180 watts) for about 2.7 hours at 2 knots, or to let an average person sustaining 100 watts of output keep rowing, sculling, or working a yuloh for roughly 5 hours on the same energy budget; chocolate, with more than five times the calories of baked beans by volume, was suggested as the more space-efficient "fuel" for a small cruising boat.[156] Whether an off-the-shelf device already existed to convert that kind of human effort into usable power was also asked, without an answer recorded in this exchange — the closest match already on record is the DIY pedal generator described above.[158]

The comparison drew a detailed rebuttal: it ignores the energy the body itself spends digesting and assimilating food, and the continuous background cost of simply staying alive — circulation, respiration, thermoregulation, cell repair, and waste elimination — all of which eat into the food-calorie budget well before any of it reaches the muscles as useful work, making a human body a far less efficient converter of stored calories into propulsion than the naive comparison suggests. Set against a small four-stroke outboard and a few litres of gasoline, which holds an order of magnitude more usable energy per pound and is simpler to keep running, the combined weight, complexity, and expense of a full electric alternative — batteries, solar panels, a wind generator, and a trolling motor — was judged to lose on both cost and range; a generator adds its own carbon-monoxide and battery-hydrogen risks, and both solar and wind generation are hampered aboard a sailboat by shading from the sails and by the sheltered, low-wind berths typical of marinas, with panels additionally prone to fouling with salt and dust. None of that was meant to discourage anyone from actually trying it.[159]

A concrete hybrid setup illustrates the same tradeoffs: a 1,250-watt four-stroke genset, two deep-cycle batteries, four solar panels, and a controller/regulator and smart charger, needing only a trolling motor to complete the system — with a name-brand electric outboard noted, on reputation rather than firsthand use, as the current state of the art. Shore power adds a further cost beyond the electrical gear itself: a marina slip fee to plug into, needed at all only because solar alone cannot recharge a deep-cycle bank fast enough to keep a trolling motor supplied — a cost that can exceed whatever the solar charging was meant to save, especially with every slip taken and the batteries running down regardless. A running generator's constant-rpm drone was judged more intrusive out on open water than a small outboard idling at part throttle, though giving up a small gas outboard's familiar reliability for an all-electric system remained a hard trade to make in practice.[160]

One view expressed in the exchange held that Paradox is a boat designed to sail, with an outboard reserved for genuine emergencies or brief marina maneuvering rather than routine propulsion — failing either, "try the beans."[161]

A firsthand account settled how demanding the human-power side of the comparison really is in practice: rowing (sculling) "[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)" is a genuinely low-energy exercise even for a self-described non-athlete — a 3-mile session averaged just under 2 knots, and the longest continuous session ran 4 hours with room to keep going; only trying to push the boat fast tires a rower out quickly.[162] See [sculling and yuloh technique — performance](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md#performance) for the fuller data on sustainable sculling speeds. A closing word turned the same lesson back on the electric side of the comparison: a battery driven hard enough to go fast would be just as "knackered" in short order as a rower pushing the same pace.[164]

A follow-up conceded the calorie comparison had been oversimplified, but offered a different justification for using body power anyway: many of the body's own background "maintenance" functions keep drawing energy whether or not a sailor is actually exerting himself, much as an idling outboard burns fuel even when it is not driving the boat, so effort already being spent at rest might as well go toward rowing, sculling, or working the yuloh; the same follow-up agreed no readily available fuel matches petrol or gasoline's calorific value.[165]

A further reply agreed that a yuloh or oar belongs aboard any small sailboat that cannot easily be paddled or rowed, framing a motor as a backup safety device for particular situations rather than routine propulsion: approaching weather that calls for reaching a safe refuge quickly; a dying wind that will not get the boat home before dark; a strong tidal or river current, or heavy traffic, to be overcome; a fixed schedule to keep; conditions too rough to control the boat by sail or yuloh alone; or injury, illness, gear failure, or a simple misjudgment of one's own abilities. A motor buys real freedom and flexibility on those terms, but carries its own failure modes: a broken shear pin, or water in the fuel, while rounding a rocky point in a blow can turn into a panic situation and a crash landing in surf.[166]

Weighing the same tradeoff personally, one correspondent judged sail and yuloh sufficient for almost all situations, with an emergency backup good for some four to six hours of travel all that good seamanship and forward planning should ever need to call on — and, prompted by rising fuel prices and a wish to go greener, leaned toward trying an electric motor paired with a dedicated two-battery 6-volt golf-cart bank, backed by its own solar panel used only to keep those batteries topped up rather than for active recharging; lightweight, walkable flexible solar panels were noted as now available for the purpose.[167]

A further view drew the same line between routine cruising and a fixed itinerary from the opposite direction: a sailor content to scull at a steady two knots for hours needs no motor at all, but a skipper who must cover a set distance in a set time is, in effect, better served by a motor cruiser than by asking a boat like this to do that job — getting on the water being "Me time," with the destination always negotiable if the situation, usually the weather, changes. Full text and further detail on that builder's own boat at [Noli — propulsion philosophy, a narrow transom, and a home-built yuloh](/boats/noli.md#propulsion-philosophy-a-narrow-transom-and-a-home-built-yuloh).[168]

A reply to that view added a real-world grounding note: even a sailor devoted enough to scull for hours had, on a recent joint cruise, gladly accepted a tow under a companion's outboard when the chance arose — see [Little Jim — the 2008 Scilly Isles cruise](/boats/little-jim.md#the-2008-scilly-isles-cruise-little-jims-side-of-the-log) for the cruise this refers to.[169]

## An old outboard leg repurposed as an electric-motor mount, and a 24-volt reality check

The same thread drifted into golf-cart humor before landing on a genuine question: golf-cart batteries are already common in boats, prompting the idea of mounting a small electric motor on the leg of an old, otherwise-dead gas outboard.[170][171] Candidate motors floated for the job included a drill motor (judged too small), a starter motor (too big), and a windscreen-wiper motor, alongside a flexible drill-drive run through a tunnel to a 4 in pipe and a multi-bladed prop, and a lifting drive that would keep the propeller flush with the hull when grounded; waterproofing the motor itself was left an open question.[172]

The idea drew a grounding reply from a correspondent who had already worked through the same electric-versus-petrol tradeoff and settled on a small four-stroke: any genuinely useful electric power needs a 24-volt system — four golf-cart batteries and a sizeable solar array — and the small, cheap plastic props fitted to ordinary trolling motors are considered close to useless by anyone seriously pursuing electric boat propulsion. Given unlimited resources, a small motor-sailor built around a miniature biodiesel engine paired with a 90-amp alternator (echoing an existing camper-van conversion, "although the motor wasn't bio nor tiny") was floated as a more interesting long-term answer than a bolted-on trolling motor.[173]

## A firsthand case for the petrol outboard: tide, wind, and a drying mooring's deadline

A firsthand account made the case for keeping a petrol outboard from the opposite direction: a specific passage — two miles to windward against a force 4 wind with the flood tide running the same way, timed to be back at a drying home mooring by high water — would have been impossible under sail or yuloh alone, since the same wind and tide would instead have carried the boat away from home with no guarantee of a timely return; the engine, not routine propulsion, is what made the outing possible and pleasurable at all. The same account restates a standing view: handling Paradox among moored boats, or in open water once the wind reaches force 3 or more, is hard to do without an engine, with the yuloh "virtually useless" in that kind of close-quarters work — and that an engine's safety margin is worth having regardless, illustrated by two Paradoxes having already towed each other under outboard power earlier the same cruising season. Full account at [Faith — a tide-and-wind case for the outboard, on the Blackwater](/boats/faith.md#a-tide-and-wind-case-for-the-outboard-on-the-blackwater).[177] A follow-up asking how much fuel the outing took drew a concrete answer: about half a litre for an hour's running, working out to roughly six nautical miles per litre — a 5-litre can good for about 30 miles, two cans for about 60 in calm conditions.[178][179] Weighing petrol against electric power directly, the same account took petrol without qualification, on power, speed, and duration alike, and framed a petrol-fitted Paradox as a genuine motor-sailer capable of touring the canals of Europe wherever fuel is on hand — an ambition not to be attempted on yuloh power alone, which was judged useful on a Paradox only in near-calm conditions (see [sculling and yuloh technique — when wind overpowers the yuloh](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md#when-wind-overpowers-the-yuloh-rudder-position-and-a-failure-that-led-to-a-fall-overboard) for the full yuloh-in-wind case and the comparison drawn to Sven Yrvind's boats). The whole case drew unqualified agreement.[180]

## Adapting the pace of cruising to tide and wind, in genuinely engine-free conditions

A firsthand account of extended engine-free cruising in complex tidal waters supplies the other side of the ledger: in light or no wind, the ordinary time-distance-destination calculation stops working, and a sailor instead has to move toward a shallow, exposed bit of shore to wait out a tide change, or get under way at 3 a.m. to catch a favourable one — not difficult in itself, but a genuine change in the pace of life, one that in practice takes three or four days to settle into even with prior experience of it. Once that rhythm is found, the reward is real: more and better rest, and something close to a mystical experience of being governed by tide and wind rather than a schedule. An engine's real value shows up specifically where available time is finite: without one, a boat's practical cruising range is genuinely restricted, and an engine both extends that range and becomes a serious asset when conditions turn bad — but whether the added complication and lost space are worth it depends on how the boat is actually meant to be used, and in a micro-cruiser specifically, probably not.[181]

## A newcomer's own planned use case: short hops from a windy home coast

The same fuel-consumption figures reassured a prospective owner planning weekend hops between **Marina del Rey and Catalina Island**, off a stretch of California coast with near-constant wind: a motor looked likely to see real use at first, tapering off as sailing skill and confidence grew.[182]

## A deliberate no-motor choice, and the case for leaving it at home

Closing out the thread, one owner set out the case for carrying no engine at all beyond a side-mounted electric motor reserved for emergencies: deliberately going without a petrol fallback is what actually forces sail- and yuloh-handling skill to develop, since a sailor who can always start an engine when the going gets hard on the wind never gets the chance to learn to sail better in exactly those conditions. On that view, even "failing" to get home under sail is no real hardship — just another night spent aboard rather than at home mowing the lawn — and there is nothing quite like being a couple of miles from home on a falling tide and a fading wind to teach a sailor to get the best out of the boat. Full account, including the joking exchange it drew, at [Little Jim — a deliberate no-motor choice, and the case for leaving it at home](/boats/little-jim.md#a-deliberate-no-motor-choice-and-the-case-for-leaving-it-at-home).[183][184][185]

A reply to that view made the opposite case once more, arguing that carrying a motor does not automatically mean losing touch with nature or the water — a small motor still leaves plenty to contend with, illustrated by a river passage of some 12 miles upstream through rapids to reach open water, too far to paddle against and unthinkable to scull, so that most trips are instead planned to run downstream where possible. Full account, including the reply's closing reflection on tradition and small engines, at [Nels Arvidson — design and construction contributions](/people/nels-arvidson.md#design-and-construction-contributions).[186]

## A qualifier: when local current itself calls for an engine

A follow-up put a specific limit on the case for engine-free cruising made above: in some settings, going without an engine would severely restrict a sailor's ability to use the water at all, unless the boat itself is purpose-built for human power — a canoe or a rowing boat, and even then not always — making an engine worth having regardless of any general philosophy against one. A concrete illustration followed: a river running some 4-5 mph at flood stage, with a further stretch of grade 1-2 rapids upstream, rules out paddling against the current by canoe, let alone sculling, so cruising from that particular home water runs downstream rather than up. Full geography at [Nels Arvidson — design and construction contributions](/people/nels-arvidson.md#design-and-construction-contributions).[192][193]

The thread's last messages drifted into personal exchange — comparing homes near the coast, a shared taste in sea-going folk songs, and thanks for years of active participation across several Yahoo boating groups — without further engine-choice content.[187][188][189][190][191][194][195][196]

## Pedal power, and why a Mirage Drive does not suit Paradox

A pedal-drive alternative to the standard sculling oar was raised and settled against: fitting a Hobie Mirage Drive was considered, but the flat **6 ft x 3 ft 6 in cabin floor** — one of the boat's real advantages — would be blocked by the drive unit, and the centerboard-style trunk it needs would have to be unusually deep given the hull's **9-inch displacement**, enough to abandon the idea outright. The best mounting location found, if a pedal drive were fitted anyway, would be under the aft deck, which is shallower thanks to the rocker there, with the pedals facing backwards.[59] A Mirage-style drive would in any case not be effective on a boat as heavy as a Paradox, compared with the lighter craft such drives are normally fitted to — the reason a **yuloh** suits the boat instead comes down to the yuloh's **lever-arm length**, not to any property of a fin-and-pedal mechanism.[60] See [yuloh and stowed-rig retention](/components/yuloh.md) for the yuloh itself.

## An outboard bracket reused across ownership, and a Honda 2.3's real-world thrust

When a secondhand Paradox changed hands, the new owner fitted an outboard bracket through the very same holes in the transom the original builder had used for his own Honda 2.3, an older Honda 2 hp of similar dimensions and weight fitting the same position without difficulty — a data point that the bracket location on a stock hull is not especially sensitive to which particular small four-stroke ends up mounted there.[87] In calm, smooth water a **Honda 2.3 hp four-stroke** can propel a Paradox at **"3 plus knots"** with the throttle set at less than a third of full power, a concrete field figure for the outboard's real thrust at a low, economical setting, distinct from the wide-open-throttle hull-speed figures recorded elsewhere on this page (see [weighing a 2 hp four-stroke against a bigger motor, and whether Paradox can plane](#weighing-a-2-hp-four-stroke-against-a-bigger-motor-and-whether-paradox-can-plane)).[87] A stainless-steel adjustable outboard bracket — Plastimo model NV 51566, with a 370 mm height clearance — is one specific commercial product fitted to a Paradox; retracted, it takes up very little space and does not protrude past the rudder, so it does not stop a boat-and-trailer combination fitting a domestic garage.[88]

## Mainsheet snagging on the outboard: three fixes

Carrying an outboard at all creates the same recurring hazard already noted above (see [a transom-mounted petrol outboard, fuel stowage, and trim](#a-transom-mounted-petrol-outboard-fuel-stowage-and-trim)): the mainsheet snagging on the engine or its propeller. Freeing a tangled sheet means leaning over the stern and reaching directly for the propeller, "not for the fainthearted," and not something to attempt in a seaway. Three independent fixes for the same underlying problem are on record. A cage fitted around one outboard reduced, but did not fully prevent, the sheet tangling in the propeller; that boat's sheet lead was separately changed to run from the aft deck rather than the transom, reported to have "made a lot of difference" once tried. On another boat, a block fitted at the transom — where the sheet passes through a copper tube into the lazarette — gives a smooth transition from vertical to horizontal so the sheet runs freely rather than binding on the tube's end, backed by a preventer made from three bungees joined to a single hook that clips onto the engine's cowling, keeping the sheet clear whether the engine is raised or lowered and with the propeller left just clear of the water when sailing.[89][90]

## Repeated engine failures through a 2008 cruise, and the fault eventually diagnosed

A Honda outboard that had performed reliably on shorter trips grew unreliable across a longer, multi-week cruise. Reluctance to start was diagnosed by a mechanic as **"'foul' or 'gone off' fuel"** taken on at a previous refuelling stop, treated with a spark-plug swap and a fresh fill (a wrongly-sized replacement plug sold at the same visit was later exchanged for the correct one) — yet the engine still failed on leaving harbour that same day, again suspected fuel starvation, and failed a second time two days later attempting a short passage, before finally running reliably the following day. Through this stretch the outboard was, in its own owner's words, **"a temperamental machine willing only to work when it decided."**[91][92] The same cruise supplied a blunt verdict on the outboard's overall value to the passage as a whole: **"Without the engine this cruise would not have been possible."**[93]

## Starting technique: finding compression, then a smart pull

A recurring starting difficulty on a small four-stroke traced to technique rather than the engine itself: not pulling the starting cord correctly. The fix, learned from a mechanic, is to first find a compression load, then pull the cord smartly, rather than a single continuous pull from slack.[94] For a cold start specifically, the recommended sequence is to use about half choke, start with the engine in its raised position to get a good, unimpeded pull on the cord, then immerse the propeller for cooling water flow and reduce the choke gradually as the engine warms.[95]
