# Cabin heating for cold-weather sailing

The Paradox's small, enclosed [cabin](/paradox.md) — which already houses a cabin stove for cooking — retains heat effectively, whether from body heat and solar gain alone or from the stove run low. There is no dedicated cabin heater in general use; owners instead rely on a handful of practice-tested techniques, balanced against the carbon-monoxide risk of any combustion appliance in a sealed space.

## Carbon monoxide risk from unvented combustion heaters

Any heating setup in an enclosed space like the Paradox's cabin must account for proper disposal of the products of combustion — CO, CO₂, and H₂O. There are known cases of people dying while trying to keep warm in an enclosed space (a van) using Coleman-type gas lights or radiant heaters, i.e. unvented combustion appliances not designed as cabin heaters. Small coal- and pellet-burning heaters designed for boats do exist, but are relatively expensive. A compressed-gas camping light (the kind Coleman makes) produces a surprising amount of heat as a side effect of producing light, but is not a substitute for a proper vented heater. A vented alternative exists in principle — Zodi's "Hot Vent" product (zodi.com) puts the heat/flame source outside the space being warmed rather than burning fuel inside the cabin, directly avoiding the combustion-byproduct problem — though no report of a Paradox owner adapting or installing one is recorded.

## Body heat, solar gain, and the stove run low

Matt Layden has tried just about every low-tech option in his own boats. One winter he deliberately froze [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) into a saltwater pond on Cape Cod just to experience what it was like, and found that his own body heat plus sun coming in through the windows kept the boat comfortable enough — no auxiliary heater at all. He eventually sailed her back out through the ice to end the winter's entombment; the episode came to light only afterward, on stripping paint from Little Cruiser's sides for a refit, when cuts up forward in the shape of a bow wave were noticed — physical evidence, preserved under the paint, of that exit.

A tested cold-weather practice, working in a boat (stored outside) during winter, is to run the cabin stove on its very lowest setting and leave it on — it gets surprisingly hot inside. A crack left open at the hatch is essential for ventilation, since without it the heat itself (not just fumes) can drive you back out. Underway in cold weather, the gas cooking stove is instead run on low for a few minutes at a time, always with the hatch cracked to avoid carbon monoxide buildup. Most cold-weather time aboard Little Cruiser is spent at the dock rather than underway, where sunlight through the windows plus body heat alone usually keeps the cabin tolerable; when it gets too cold even for that, the safest method on land has been a ceramic heater. Warm clothing and a winter sleeping bag make a real difference to comfort on a cold cruise.

Little Cruiser is fully insulated with about two inches of foam, credited for comfort at both extremes — "When it's hot we're cool and when it's cold we're warm" — and as a direct result the crew sleep aboard the boat routinely rather than camping ashore. The cooking/heat source is a GAZ propane stove burning 1-lb cylinders small enough to stow in the bilge: "Ten cylinders get us through a whole winter trip!" Foam fitted in the windows (a technique Matt Layden also used aboard Little Cruiser, and which owners adopted on their own boats) gives a three-way benefit — cooler in hot weather, warmer in cold weather, and a dramatic reduction in condensation. See [provisioning and water](/operations/provisioning-and-water.md) for the related cabin/stores philosophy.

## The inverted-flower-pot heat retainer

An ordinary clay flower pot placed upside-down over the stove retains heat: the clay absorbs and holds the heat longer than the bare open flame would radiate alone, giving a longer-lasting warm glow after (or instead of) turning the stove up. It is not safe to fall asleep with the stove burning — for an overnight, a good sleeping bag is the safer way to stay warm rather than relying on a lit stove.

The technique has been field-tested aboard "Zoë" on a cold Cumberland Sound cruise: with the overnight temperature in the low 30s°F, the flower pot was run on the stove for about five minutes — "all I could stand" — after which the cabin stayed comfortable for some time, helped by a down sleeping bag. The only drawback was heavy condensation on the windows and overhead the next morning, wiped up with a synthetic-chamois "Absorber," attributed partly to having plugged all the vent openings overnight — a concrete real-world data point on the ventilation/condensation trade-off of sealing up a Paradox-style cabin tightly for warmth.

A hazard worth noting with this technique: a hot pot resting loose on the stove can end up dislodged elsewhere in the cabin if knocked free, so it is worth securing or watching rather than leaving unattended.

## Window condensation: causes and mitigation

Condensation on the cabin windows and overhead is a recurring side effect of a sealed-up, heated cabin. On "Zoë," no insulation is fitted, and condensation has not been a serious problem: a lip surrounding the cabin already collects any condensation running off the windows, and the gutter itself stays dry — only the windows and overhead get wet. Leaving some through-ventilation carries the moisture out of the air, at the cost of a colder cabin — the same heat-vs.-ventilation trade-off the flower-pot technique runs into.

Two further approaches address the same problem from different angles. Fitting a drain channel in the window frame, below the Lexan, would let condensation running off the window drain away rather than pool — a mitigation-by-drainage approach not confirmed as built on a Paradox. Foam fitted in the windows (see above) instead prevents condensation from forming in the first place, giving a dramatic reduction in practice. A third approach, from outside the Paradox family: on the **BRIS** microcruiser (see [blue-water suitability](/design/blue-water-suitability.md)), window condensation is deliberately channelled to a tube and collected in a bottle, doubling as a source of drinking water.

## The cabin stove's gimbal axis

A Paradox galley stove is typically gimbaled for roll (port-to-starboard) only, not for pitch: on "Zoë," the mount allows motion on two axes because the boat pitches very little at anchor or underway. This is the only recorded description of how a Paradox galley stove is actually mounted, though it does not specify stove type in general — see [provisioning and water](/operations/provisioning-and-water.md) for the separate GAZ propane stove aboard Little Cruiser.

## Choosing a galley cooking stove: the Force 10 Seacook and the Forespar alternative

Separately from the heating question above, the **Force 10 Seacook** — a single-burner, gimballed propane stove — has been considered as a possible addition to "[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)."[14] Force 10's other products are rated good quality and value generally, though no direct experience with that particular model is on record; the suggested comparison is the Seacook against the **Forespar Mini-Galley 2000** before deciding.[15] Weighing the two, the Forespar came out preferred.[16]

## Cabin fans for cooling and quiet

A recurring request is for a small, quiet cabin fan — the complaint against a previous unit was noise bad enough to "drive you crazy" on an extended trip (see [people/david-beard.md](/people/david-beard.md#citations)).[269] Quiet and small are generally in tension: one recommendation, from direct use in larger boats, is to skip the miniature "quiet" fans altogether in favor of a roughly 14 x 14 in three-speed box fan, on the reasoning that a bigger fan can spin more slowly for the same airflow and is therefore quieter — a household box fan is bulkier than a dedicated marine unit but trades size for genuine quiet.[270] The same larger-is-quieter logic applies to 12V computer-style cabin fans: an inexpensive computer fan around 4.75 x 4.75 in, run on 12V DC, is quieter than a smaller unit turning faster for the same output; wiring a Zener diode in to drop the supply to about 9V slows the fan further for an even quieter cabin.[271] The better 12V brushless computer fans are built in aluminum housings; fitting a grille or a simple wire screen over the blades protects fingers and gear.[272] The same big-fan-slow-speed principle scales up further with 220V AC computer/appliance-cooling fans roughly 9-10 in square, which can be run at a reduced voltage (110V in the US) for a very quiet, high-volume breeze — by contrast, small modern CPU fans are built to move a lot of air through a small opening and are correspondingly loud. Automotive radiator fans are a further quiet option, their blades deliberately staggered to avoid a consistent hum.[273] A battery-powered alternative sidesteps wiring a boat fan into the electrical system entirely: a portable camping fan running on 4 D-cell batteries (sold as the "Porta Breeze") is quiet and runs for a long time on one set of batteries.[274] A further reply on the same thread reinforced the size/speed tradeoff from the buyer's-market side: the newest, largest computer fans on the market run about 6 in in diameter and are very quiet, while cheaply built machines still use smaller 4.75 in or under fans that spin faster and louder for the same airflow — an ordinary full-size desktop PC with a front intake grille and a roughly year-old home-entertainment PC (running quietly even with its case fans on low speed) were both offered as everyday examples of the size-versus-noise principle at work.[275] The thread's opener settled on a purpose-built marine fan rather than any of the suggested computer-fan or box-fan alternatives: a three-speed "Bora" fan from West Marine (made by Caframo), reported to fit the Paradox cabin well, run quietly, and move plenty of air — pricy at around $70 but judged worth it.[276]

## A gimballed lamp as a heat source: open question

Whether a gimballed kerosene lamp could serve as a practical mild heat source aboard a Paradox — as one had aboard a 20 ft canoe yawl elsewhere — remains an open question in the archive.[9] Candidate designs discussed include a small Welsh miner's-style gimballed lamp (reasoned to work well given how easily an insulated Paradox cabin warms around even a small heat source) and a metal chimney with baffles/coils, which might produce more heat than a plain lamp chimney at the cost of more soot.[11][12] "little Jim"'s cabin has nowhere above eye height to mount a lamp, so any lamp fitted would tend to dazzle whoever was aboard; one solution sidesteps the problem rather than solving it, using LEDs set into the cabin roof — well above where a hanging lamp could go, and, unlike a flame, with no dazzle, heat, or soot to manage.[10] This is the first recorded description of a Paradox using LED cabin lighting rather than a gas or kerosene lamp, though it does not address the underlying interest in a lamp specifically as a heat source. See [design/rig.md](/components/rig/index.md) and [boats/little-jim.md](/boats/little-jim.md) for the first-sail account that prompted the lamp question in the first place.

A small candle lamp holding a "survival candle" of the kind sold for power-outage preparedness has separately been offered as a simpler heat-and-light source — it throws off useful heat as well as light to read by, with a drawer beneath for spares; a holder for two or more smokeless, dripless candles burning together has been suggested as a further step up in output for a chillier night.[291] Two further published candle-heater designs have been pointed to for the same purpose: one built from stacked clay flower pots over the candle flame, radiating stored heat much as the inverted-flower-pot stove technique above does, and a second built from tin cans, alongside a magazine article on the technique.[292]

## A charcoal or kerosene can heater, and the transom-cook-stove alternative

A one-gallon-can charcoal stove with an airtight chimney pipe has been described elsewhere as usable and safe on a small boat, though no example is recorded as ever built for a Paradox; a commercially made charcoal boat stove once sold under the name "the Pansy" is judged easy to reproduce at home. A separate charcoal-specific cabin-heater design, the "Lenihan Charcoal Cabin Heater," associated with the Triloboat community, is a further design worth consulting, alongside a discussion and photograph of a small boat heater found partway down an external boating-forum thread. A dedicated heater built around a metal can as the heat-retaining body, with a chimney run through the cabin roof and the burner/wick assembly salvaged from a large kerosene lantern fitted into the can's base, has been proposed as a cleaner kerosene-fired alternative to charcoal — no ash to deal with.

The alternative already in common use is simply running the existing cook stove — normally sited in the lazarette and vented out the transom — for cabin warmth as well as cooking; how much of that heat actually reaches forward into the cabin is unclear, welcome in winter but unwanted in summer. A dedicated heater is hard to justify space for in so small a cabin, and cook stoves are not built to be left running unattended for warmth. The chief hazard, beyond fire risk, is poisonous combustion emissions: kerosene needs to be adjusted to burn with a clean, soot-free blue flame, and any heater's chimney has to strike a balance between clearing combustion products from the cabin air and not carrying away all of the heat with them.

A cabin the size of Paradox's needs surprisingly little heat: a single ordinary kerosene lamp is sometimes enough on its own, two lamps better still. One liveaboard's wood-burning cabin stove was built from a length of six-inch steel pipe (not galvanized), both ends welded shut, with a small hinged firebox door cut into the side using only a drill and a jigsaw fitted with a metal-cutting blade; the door itself was cut from a second length of six-inch pipe and hung on ordinary hardware-store hinges, with about four air holes drilled near the base. A three-inch stovepipe formed the stack, with a two-foot section fitted only at anchor; underway the stack was simply capped where it passed through the cabin top, and the stove was not normally run while sailing. Charcoal is best avoided regardless of stove design: beyond the smell and fume danger already noted, it leaves a fine grey dust that blows around the cabin in the slightest breeze.[286] Whatever heater is used, the hatch should be cracked open about an inch for ventilation.[283]

A further heater precedent comes from a larger, similarly small-cabin boat: a small cast-iron stove burning compressed-coal briquettes, fitted aboard a 23 ft sailing cruiser and lived with through a full winter at anchor, kept that cabin reliably warm — but at about a foot high and nine inches wide, with a length of scaffold pole for its flue, was judged too large to fit a Paradox. A scaled-down equivalent was proposed instead: a tiny heater built from 3-4 in diameter copper or stainless steel tubing with roughly a 1 in flue pipe — small enough for the cabin, with the same caution as above to keep any spark clear of an inflatable mattress.[298]

A wood-burning stove precedent comes from a 21 ft cruising sailboat, run too hot until the technique was learned: keeping the fire small inside the firebox and feeding it one small stick at a time every 5 to 10 minutes, manageable without much trouble when seated close by. Five weeks spent living aboard through a Vancouver November/December — reckoned among the coldest, wettest months there — passed in reasonable comfort on that technique; a Paradox's smaller cabin is guessed to need nothing more than a couple of candles and some sort of heat diffuser.[299]

A chimney run through the cabin roof needs to clear the hatch's own travel — a stack sliced off by an opening hatch is a real, reported hazard — and one proposed way to route it without that risk is to fatten and hollow out one of the four cabin posts, insulated, though which corner best suits the modification is an open question; elsewhere a one-inch diameter chimney has been seen paired with a small solid-fuel bulkhead heater.[284][287]

Direct cold-weather experience bears out how little heat a Paradox cabin actually needs: over one night at about -2°C a sleeping bag alone was warm enough, and on waking, boiling a mug of coffee warmed the cabin to a comfortable temperature before the heat dissipated, enough to get dressed in comfort; the boat's own ventilation is generous enough to supply a single burner stove even with the hatch shut.[285]

A ready-made heater body has also been proposed: a commercially made cast-iron pot-bellied barbecue, about six inches in diameter with a three-inch flue and a roughly four-by-three-inch door, the legs unbolting to bring the height under a foot; such barbecues are available from UK DIY retailers or eBay for around £20. A similar but narrower-flued, more pot-bellied version with two doors rather than one could be converted to top-loading by discarding the barbecue's own lid in favor of a flue adapter.[288][289][290]

## Heat storage and insulation alternatives to a heater

A heat-retaining alternative to an open flame stores heat rather than generating it: a small hinged wooden box, vented with holes top and bottom, holds a soapstone block or brick heated beforehand on the cook stove, releasing its warmth slowly and safely inside the insulating box — cooler to the touch than an open heat source and unlikely to start a fire if the boat is knocked about by a wake or a gust.[293]

Any heater's actual output is governed by fuel-burn rate, not by "concentrating" or "radiating" the flame differently: a candle in particular is a poor heat source, since it burns fuel inefficiently, gives off partially-combusted soot particles (the source of its glow) that are unhealthy to breathe, and produces a fair amount of water vapor besides — any combustion heater should be vented, either through a dedicated chimney or by cracking the hatch. For simple heat storage, plain water beats a brick or similar solid for the amount of heat held per unit volume: filling a hot water bottle from the kettle and putting it inside a sleeping bag is an effective, low-tech way to stay warm overnight in a drafty cabin, a technique credited to Charles Stock, who has sailed three decades' worth of seasons this way. A kerosene wick stove, vented through a simple copper pipe, is a separate, more deliberate answer for cooking warmth in cold weather specifically.[294] A thermos of a favorite hot beverage, made up before turning in, gives the same benefit twice over — warmth overnight, then a hot drink on waking, still inside the sleeping bag.[295]

An alternative to any heater at all is simply keeping more heat in: thicker insulation and better draught exclusion. Owners of the BRIS/Sofia microcruiser (see [blue-water suitability](/design/blue-water-suitability.md)) with its 2 in-thick foam core report maintaining an 18-20°C cabin in sub-zero outside temperatures on body heat alone with two people aboard.[296] In practice, a cabin already insulated with 2 in of foam may need no heater at all: stuffing a towel into the ventilator on a cold night cuts the cooling draught further, and a sailor unused to dinghy or small-yacht sailing tends to underestimate just how much warmer it is simply out of the wind — ordinary sailing clothes have proven comfortable even in weather that went below freezing overnight.[297]

## Open-flame lighting and heat versus electric: a deliberate minimal-electrics choice

Not every builder wants an electrically-dependent cabin. One minimal-electrics fit-out chose a **paraffin (kerosene) anchor lamp and a candle lantern** for interior lighting and heat, keeping electrics to a bare minimum — just enough for charging batteries and running a GPS, depth finder, and radio.[17] Against the warning that open flame is a real risk ("just one more way you might end up swimming home"),[18] the considered defense of the choice runs: light, heat, and cooking need not be electrically powered, and being wholly battery-dependent is its own kind of compromise. Informed by surviving both a near-drowning and more than one unexpected fire (including once having to leave a burning tent in a hurry), the reasoning is that a source of open flame should never sit between the crew and the only way out — which is why the stove was ruled out behind the cabin's aft bulkhead — and that the design's stove position is in fact the right place to keep the fire extinguisher. With reasonable precautions, sound equipment, and backup safety gear (a fire blanket and extinguisher) in place, the risk is judged acceptable: if the worst happened one would simply swim ashore.[19]
