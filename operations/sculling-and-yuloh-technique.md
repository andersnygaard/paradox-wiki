# Sculling and yuloh technique

Where [yuloh and stowed-rig retaining loops](/components/yuloh.md) documents how the yuloh is carried aboard a Paradox-family boat, this page documents the technique of actually using one — drawn almost entirely from a first-person account of learning to scull [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) and [Enigma](/matt-layden/enigma.md), published on microcruising.com as "Sculling With a Yuloh" within the site's Watertribe "Lessons Learned" material. It is engine-free auxiliary propulsion in practice: how a 1,600-lb-plus micro-cruiser gets moved by one person, by hand, in calms, foul tides, shoals, and — under real time pressure — mid-race.

## Origin: a tow that convinced them to carry a yuloh

The habit did not start as a purist's choice. During a prolonged calm far from shore, Little Cruiser's outboard failed, and its crew discovered they could not move the **1,600-pound boat** easily or very far using only a dinghy paddle — they had to accept a **tow** to the nearest harbor. That failure was the direct trigger for carrying a yuloh at all: "next trip we will remember to bring our yuloh along since we can scull a lot faster than we can paddle." A paddle, on a boat this heavy, simply is not auxiliary power; a yuloh is.

## Technique: a whole-body, one-handed stroke

The yuloh is a long, curved sculling oar worked over the stern, and its central practical advantage over a conventional oar is that it can be worked **facing forward, with one hand**, leaving the other hand free — used in practice to hold a **water bottle**, work the **tiller**, read a **GPS**, or even talk on a **phone** while navigating twisty channels at night (done during the 2010 Everglades Challenge working out of Chokoloskee Bay).

The stroke itself is a full-body motion, not an arm exercise: feet spread, knees bent, push with the arms, and **drive forward with the back leg**, leaning into the oar. To reverse, the blade is **re-pitched** and pulled back using the legs and body rather than the arms alone. In waves or rough water the technique is simply to **keep the blade down deep**. Over long sculling stretches the stroke can be refined into a more rhythmic **body-lean**, using body weight rather than pure arm-and-leg force to conserve energy — it produces more roll but does not cost speed, and makes multi-hour sessions sustainable. Off-season training with kayak paddling and cycling a few times a week builds the fitness for it.

Beyond straight propulsion, the yuloh's other advantage over regular oars is in the **shallows**: it converts instantly between sculling oar and push-pole without changing gear, useful for poling across skinny shoals, fending off groundings, or pushing off obstacles at a moment's notice — a dual role regular oars do not offer.

Practicing outside of easy conditions makes the technique reliable rather than a fair-weather trick: sculling in **small breaking surf**, against **strong foul tides** in inlets, and at **night** around anchorages — the last useful for moving a boat silently away from noisy neighboring boats without starting the engine, one-handed with a cup of coffee in the other on calm mornings when sculling is preferable to motoring at all.

A genuine tension runs through the record over whether a yuloh can be reversed at all. In principle the stroke above runs backward as readily as forward: reversing is simply a matter of re-pitching the blade and pulling back with the legs and body, the same as any other stroke direction. In practice, however, one owner reports flatly that "a sculling oar has no reverse and no brake" — a claim not reconciled with the re-pitching technique above, and plausibly the difference between a maneuver worked out deliberately over several strokes and one needed in the seconds before a collision, when there is no time to set one up. Either way the consequence is absorbed structurally rather than avoided: the [stem post](/components/stem.md) is 100 mm (4") thick, so no damage is done to the boat itself when something is hit.[42]

## A figure-eight stroke does not work; a horizontal twist does

A first attempt at yulohing a Paradox, sculling in the punt-style figure-of-eight motion the hands make sculling a small boat with a single blade, did not work. What did work: moving the shaft **horizontally** and **twisting it at the end of each stroke** — a distinct motion from punt-sculling that generates power on a yuloh specifically, confirmed over two days learning the technique from scratch.[29]

## Keeping the yuloh on its ball: a captive-line retainer

A yuloh pivoting on a **bronze ball** can work its way off the ball in use, and doing so is one of the first things a new builder is likely to fight. On the December 2001 [Cedar Key to Honeymoon Island sea trial](/boats/zoe.md#sea-trials-route-cedar-key-to-clearwater) some twenty minutes were lost to this at the very outset, to the point that replacing the ball with a **sculling notch** on returning home was seriously contemplated. The fix arrived instead within the hour and needed no hardware: a line was secured to the **base of the pivot** so as to leave a long tail on each side, the yuloh mounted, and the line tied around its shaft so the oar was held **captive to the ball while still free to move in every direction**. Referred to thereafter as the "training device," it was proven within two days, when an anchorage had to be shifted under scull in a rising chop **without** the shaft being tied down and the oar stayed put regardless.[43]

The same trip supplies the clearest single demonstration of **why** sculling proficiency is worth acquiring on a boat with no engine. On the final morning, two tacks were needed to fetch the centre span of the Honeymoon Island causeway bridge, and all wind was lost on passing under it while a foul tide ran at its strongest. Sculling as hard as possible from a standing start, the boat **stemmed the current and cleared the bridge**, then fell off far enough to gather way under sail again — an outcome credited directly to the week's accumulated yuloh practice.[43]

## Performance

Using the full-body technique, Enigma could be driven at **3 knots flat out** in calm conditions; ordinary cruising speed, sustained, was closer to **2 knots**. The yuloh itself is no faster over the water than a regular oar would be on a similarly sized small boat — its advantages are in one-handed forward-facing control and its shallow-water versatility, not raw speed.

Gear evolution cut the other way on at least one occasion: a **new fir yuloh for Enigma with more curve** was later built, modeled partly on the self-feathering properties of [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md)'s own oar — but GPS-measured top speed with the new, more-curved oar was **somewhat reduced** compared with the old yuloh, a data point against assuming more curve is simply better.

A UK Paradox owner's experience matches the same ~2-knot cruising figure on a different Layden boat: "[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)" will push at a comfortable **2 knots all day** under the yuloh. On the Dinghy Cruising Association's annual **Thames raid** it sculled roughly **50 of the ~55 miles**, arriving without the blisters that afflicted the crews of the conventional rowing boats — because sculling lets the body keep shifting position rather than repeat one fixed rowing stroke (see [AYRS meeting talk](/references/ayrs-meeting-video.md)).

## Watertribe evidence: a micro-cruiser can be cruised motor-free under race pressure

The clearest demonstration that this is a viable primary propulsion method, not just a calm-weather party trick, comes from results across several **Watertribe** events, entered partly out of curiosity and partly to test micro-cruising techniques under pressure — following the example of [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md), who had entered Watertribe events year after year to field-test his own designs.

- **~2008, WCTSS Cedar Key gathering** — the outboard was left behind entirely aboard Little Cruiser, found unnecessary, and the boat was sculled to Atsena Otie Key and other nearby islands during the calms.
- **2009 NC Challenge (inaugural)** — sculled about **11 miles** to finish **first in class** with Enigma fully loaded for cruising at roughly **400 lb**, averaging close to 2 knots except against tide and headwind out of Beaufort.
- **2010 Everglades Challenge** — the first **300-mile** Watertribe Challenge completed, finishing first in class. The hardest test was **Gasparilla Pass at night**: a first attempt against a foul tide and **5-foot breaking seas** failed, forcing hours of waiting before sculling through successfully at the tide change. The 300-mile distance approximates the mileage of the usual Angelfish Creek-to-Georgetown winter cruising route.
- **2010 NC Challenge** — the hardest rowing test yet: **14 miles / 7 hours** of sculling on the first day alone, well beyond a usual roughly two-hour training session, yet still finishing a few hours faster than the prior year.

The conclusion these results support directly: even someone of average fitness can coastal-cruise a micro-cruiser without a motor, even under race time pressure — not only in the leisurely conditions of a winter Bahamas cruise.

## A fully engine-free solo passage (2013)

The technique's furthest expression was a **2013 solo Bahamas trip** aboard Enigma, made deliberately **without an outboard at all** — "a voyage for the purist with sail and oar only," inspired by mentors **Sven Yrvind** and Matt Layden. The same influences — Yrvind, and **Lin and Larry Pardey**, who "traveled all over the world with just an oar as auxiliary power" — stand generally behind the case that engine-free auxiliary propulsion is a serious, viable approach rather than a novelty.

Sculling in this sense sits alongside, not in place of, an [auxiliary outboard](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md): most builders carry both, but the Watertribe results and the 2013 passage show the yuloh can substitute for the motor entirely when a sailor commits to the technique.

## Matt Layden's own yuloh speed, and the straight-vs-curved-blade trade-off

During the Everglades Challenge he won, **Matt Layden** himself averaged about **2 knots on the calm section** under yuloh power — the only yuloh-speed figure in the record tied specifically to that race (see [boats/original-paradox.md](/boats/original-paradox.md)), and consistent with the general ~2 knot cruising-speed figure recorded above.

The fullest direct comparison of yuloh design and its practical effect on power and physical strain comes from sculling Little Cruiser on Bahamas trips: **1.5-2 knots** once used to the technique, a best sustained one-mile average of about **2.5 knots**, and short sprints of **3.0 knots**. On a visit to Matt Layden after the Everglades Challenge came a chance to scull the original Paradox, with Matt coaching that the **whole body** go into the stroke. Paradox by then carried a **new sculling oar** — straighter than the plans' design and with a **wider, longer blade** — which proved **more powerful** (easier to control the blade's pitch) but required **much more wrist action**, enough to make the right wrist start hurting; Matt Layden reported that his own wrist had hurt during the first few days of the Everglades Challenge using the same new oar. Weighing the trade-off, the verdict favored Little Cruiser's own **more curved** oar. Photos taken afterward showed the tips of both the new oar and the rudder reinforced with **bronze rod**. This is the record's fullest direct comparison of yuloh design (straight vs. curved blade) and its practical effect on both power and physical strain, plus a rare admission that even the designer himself felt a real physical cost from his own new gear during the race documented in [boats/original-paradox.md](/boats/original-paradox.md). Over a year later, a second source independently confirmed Matt's oar remained straight and, per Matt himself, still worked well but was harder on the arm and wrist than a curved blade — corroborating the account and resolving the earlier uncertainty over whether the new oar was more or less curved than the original (see [yuloh and stowed-rig retention](/components/yuloh.md#building-the-yuloh-itself-blade-curvature-and-the-pivot-boltsocket)).

Having by then used both oars aboard Matt's own boat, that same preference for the original, more curved oar was restated: the extra curve makes the blade scull more automatically, whereas the newer, straighter oar — while more powerful and easier to control for blade pitch — demands noticeably more wrist effort, the same trade-off independently confirmed from Matt himself.

## Matt Layden's "motors are a luxury" philosophy, put into practice

Matt Layden's own stated view on engines — that motors are fine so long as a sailor understands they are a **luxury**, not something to be relied on to get out of trouble — has been deliberately tested by other builders. Returning from an overnighter on "[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)" with the outboard deliberately left at home, one builder rigged a makeshift yuloh from a **kayak paddle** that "worked ok" and planned a proper yuloh as a next project. Applying Matt's philosophy directly, it became clear the outboard had been compensating for inexperience under sail — a crutch experienced sailors wouldn't need — and what could be managed without it was a pleasant surprise.

A more qualified view holds that a motor is worth keeping as "another 'tool,'" useful against strong contrary currents and on windless days, but weighed against the cost of disrupted trim, carrying "6+ gallons of explosive fuel," and the maintenance burden — concluding it's "really a personal choice," and that a sailor who can do without an engine "will most likely be a better sailor" for it. Sailing alongside Matt Layden for **two months in the Bahamas**, "we almost always sailed together," and that time taught the finer points of tides, wind shifts, and sail trim. That view went further than Matt's own "luxury" framing for the **yuloh** specifically, calling it "a MUST" aboard "our little boats" — not only for propulsion at 1-2 knots, but as "a great push pole in the shallows" and a fender against rocks and docks; given a straight choice between a motor and a yuloh, the yuloh wins, though Little Cruiser carries both and uses each for its own purpose (sail when there's wind, scull when it's calm and a short distance needs covering, motor "and choke on the fumes" when speed is needed on a windless day).

A strikingly similar experience came around the same time from "little Jim"'s Portland cruise (see [boats/little-jim.md](/boats/little-jim.md)): **3 hours** fighting wind and tide within 2 miles of a safe haven (never in real danger, though the weather could have worsened), and wind and tide again for the final mile up the river to the boatyard — "gives me lots of practice with the yuloh, but I doubt if I shall be able to move my arms tomorrow." Despite this, the resolve held to stay motor-free for the season at least, allowing only that the launch site might need changing to make that more practical. These near-simultaneous accounts stand as the clearest double data point for Matt Layden's motors-as-luxury philosophy being deliberately put into practice, under real difficulty, by two different builders in the same week.

## Whether Paradox can be rowed with conventional oars

Distinct from yuloh sculling, whether a Paradox can be rowed at all with conventional oars — accepting a non-optimal result — was raised as an open question, drawing on experience rowing rafts professionally and crossing flatwater stretches with alternating forward strokes "like hitting a punching bag," and picturing a barstool-height seat on the cabin floor with extension arms holding oar pins roughly a foot off each side.[30] Standing and facing forward to row hits a real obstruction: the cabin top is in the way, and extending oar locks would be needed on either side of the boat, with oars about the same length as the yuloh; in practice, the yuloh doubles as a paddle in a pinch, though it tires the arms quickly over any real distance.[31] A photograph from the "Aussie Paradox" album, showing the hull afloat with her ground plate visible, suggested standing rowing looked "pretty doable," possibly even without oar-lock extensions, though clearing the oars from the water between strokes might still need them.[32]

One rowlock design floated for the purpose is the "north woods" type, pinned through both the oarlock and the oar so the blade cannot feather, with the oar itself curved down toward the water to clear the cabin top — the same curved oars stowing neatly on deck where the yuloh normally sits; fitting such high-set oarlocks would let a builder dispense with the plans' two deck loops used for stowing the sail rig and yuloh.[33]

A further, untried oar concept proposes rowing standing up and facing forward, with oarlock brackets fitted to the sides of the hatch opening once it is open, well forward — reasoning there would be room to swing the oars through a large enough arc for adequate propulsion, and that the approach would address two recurring yuloh drawbacks: holding the head to wind once it pipes up, and stopping or reversing at close quarters.[34][35] A second oar-port variant, going through the hull sides rather than the hatch, was judged workable in principle but with real drawbacks: the storage shelves on either side would need to change to make room, and plugging the ports when not in use risks the hull's waterproof integrity; rowlocks sited either side of the cabin top close to the aft end of the open hatch — rather than at the hatch opening itself — were suggested as an alternative siting, again with the oarsman standing and facing forward to push the oars for momentum.[36]

A further data point on doing without an outboard entirely: one owner fitted no motor at all to "[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)" in her first season, specifically to force himself to learn to sail, coming to the boat mainly from a motor-boating background — the same deliberate discipline documented for "Whisper" above — which pushed him to use the yuloh for non-sailing maneuvering until he became quite proficient; a small electric trolling motor was added only afterward, stowed awkwardly enough that it is used only once sail and yuloh have both failed.[35]

## Carrying a spare yuloh: Smith's mass-and-momentum case against it

Whether to carry a **spare yuloh** — reasoning by analogy from canoeing, where a spare paddle is considered essential in case the primary is lost or broken — is bound up with a related doubt: given Paradox's weight, would she even scull well at all? A paddler's intuition suggested not, on the theory that a long, skinny, lightly built racing hull gets far more "carry" between strokes than a heavy displacement hull ever could, which would feel instead like pushing too much water and decelerating quickly.

The general physics of mass and momentum runs the other way, with the intuition backwards: a **heavier** vessel carries **farther**, not less far, between strokes — the same principle that let old-time schooners work their way up rivers and around bends under momentum alone. Illustrated with paired examples at matched speed: a Hobie 18 doing 15 knots stops almost the instant its sails come down, while a supertanker at the same 15 knots will coast on for a long time after "all stop"; a skateboard and a hundred-car freight train, both on level ground, show the same contrast. A solo paddler in a kevlar canoe must maintain a constant rhythm to keep moving, but the same canoe loaded with a crew and two weeks of provisions and camping gear will carry through a pause in paddling. What a **light** hull gains instead is **acceleration**, not carry — the quality behind a catamaran's sudden burst of speed or a racing crew's snap off the start line — though even there, an eight-man rowing scull, with its tremendous power and low wetted surface on a long waterline, will ultimately carry farther than the accelerating catamaran.

On the spare-yuloh question specifically, a spare was judged unnecessary: no more needed than a spare diesel engine aboard a sailing auxiliary, since the yuloh is **auxiliary** propulsion, not primary, unlike a canoe paddle, where losing the only paddle genuinely strands the paddler. Plenty of yuloh how-to material exists online, but none of it worth the price of the Paradox plans — which already include **yuloh plans** as standard, confirmed separately.

## Slalom-stroke sculling oars, and whether they improve on the yuloh's falling-leaf stroke

A Japanese VE-SCULL sculling-oar design, said not to stall the blade's leading edge on the return stroke and reportedly simple enough that a trained monkey can use it, was raised as a possible improvement on the standard yuloh.[37][38] Whether that is a genuine improvement is doubtful: a first reaction judged it might be nothing more than an oar built for the slalom stroke rather than the falling-leaf stroke a yuloh uses,[37] a second correspondent, no better at conventional sculling himself, offered only the same "even a monkey can use it" reassurance in reply without settling the question,[40] and a closer technical look agreed that whether a blade stalls is a function of its foil section, angle of attack, and how fast it is worked — factors any slalom-scull designer would already be accounting for, so the VE-SCULL's claimed advantage may not mark a genuine difference from a well-shaped yuloh blade at all.[39] The slalom stroke itself is understood to be the conventional Western sculling technique, with the yuloh's falling-leaf stroke historically the oriental system by contrast.[41]

The accompanying idea — fitting a fence to the underside of the blade, parallel to the waterline, to stop water breaking away and causing tip vortex[38] — runs up against how a yuloh is actually worked in practice: the shaft's angle over the stern is already varied by the sculler depending on the maneuver, steeper to accelerate hard or move fast for a few moments, more horizontal for ordinary cruising or in shallow water, so a fixed fence would need to be adjustable across that whole range to stay useful.[39] A fence at the blade's tip would still be useful for one purpose regardless of the tip-vortex question: stopping the blade's end from sinking into the mud when the yuloh is used as a quant/push-pole in the shallows.[39]

The slalom blade does carry one clear advantage over the falling-leaf yuloh blade: it can be used as a rudder while "freewheeling," and it allows quicker turns to be made — a real, if narrow, point in its favor even where the stalling and laminar-flow claims turn out not to matter much.[39]

See also [yuloh and stowed-rig retaining loops](/components/yuloh.md) for how the yuloh is stowed aboard, and [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md), whose own Watertribe participation and sculling-over-towing preference (declining a tow across the Gulf Stream in favor of sailing or sculling Paradox) set the example other builders followed.
