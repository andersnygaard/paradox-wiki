# Sculling and yuloh technique

Where [yuloh and stowed-rig retaining loops](/design/yuloh-and-stowed-rig-retention.md)
documents how the yuloh is carried aboard a Paradox-family boat, this page
documents the technique of actually using one — drawn almost entirely from
**David Bolduc**'s first-person account of learning to scull
[Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) and
[Enigma](/matt-layden/enigma.md), published on microcruising.com as
"Sculling With a Yuloh" within the site's Watertribe "Lessons Learned"
material. It is engine-free auxiliary propulsion in practice: how a
1,600-lb-plus micro-cruiser gets moved by one person, by hand, in calms,
foul tides, shoals, and — under real time pressure — mid-race.

## Origin: a tow that convinced them to carry a yuloh

The habit did not start as a purist's choice. During a prolonged calm far
from shore, Little Cruiser's outboard failed, and David and
[Mindy Bolduc](/people/bolduc/mindy.md) discovered they could not move their
**1,600-pound boat** easily or very far using only their dinghy paddle — they
had to accept a **tow** to the nearest harbor. That failure was the direct
trigger for carrying a yuloh at all: as David put it, "next trip we will
remember to bring our yuloh along since we can scull a lot faster than we
can paddle." A paddle, on a boat this heavy, simply is not auxiliary power;
a yuloh is.

## Technique: a whole-body, one-handed stroke

The yuloh is a long, curved sculling oar worked over the stern, and its
central practical advantage over a conventional oar is that it can be worked
**facing forward, with one hand**, leaving the other hand free — used in
practice to hold a **water bottle**, work the **tiller**, read a **GPS**, or
even talk on a **phone** while navigating twisty channels at night (done
during the 2010 Everglades Challenge working out of Chokoloskee Bay).

The stroke itself is a full-body motion, not an arm exercise: feet spread,
knees bent, push with the arms, and **drive forward with the back leg**,
leaning into the oar. To reverse, the blade is **re-pitched** and pulled
back using the legs and body rather than the arms alone. In waves or rough
water the technique is simply to **keep the blade down deep**. Over long
sculling stretches the stroke can be refined into a more rhythmic
**body-lean**, using body weight rather than pure arm-and-leg force to
conserve energy — it produces more roll but does not cost speed, and makes
multi-hour sessions sustainable. Off-season training with kayak paddling and
cycling a few times a week builds the fitness for it.

Beyond straight propulsion, the yuloh's other advantage over regular oars is
in the **shallows**: it converts instantly between sculling oar and push-pole
without changing gear, useful for poling across skinny shoals, fending off
groundings, or pushing off obstacles at a moment's notice — a dual role
regular oars do not offer.

Practicing outside of easy conditions makes the technique reliable rather
than a fair-weather trick: sculling in **small breaking surf**, against
**strong foul tides** in inlets, and at **night** around anchorages — the
last useful for moving a boat silently away from noisy neighboring boats
without starting the engine, one-handed with a cup of coffee in the other on
calm mornings when sculling is preferable to motoring at all.

## Performance

Using the full-body technique, David Bolduc could drive Enigma at **3 knots
flat out** in calm conditions; ordinary cruising speed, sustained, was closer
to **2 knots**. The yuloh itself is no faster over the water than a regular
oar would be on a similarly sized small boat — its advantages are in
one-handed forward-facing control and its shallow-water versatility, not raw
speed.

Gear evolution cut the other way on at least one occasion: David later built
a **new fir yuloh for Enigma with more curve**, modeled partly on the
self-feathering properties of [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md)'s
own oar — but GPS-measured top speed with the new, more-curved oar was
**somewhat reduced** compared with the old yuloh, a data point against
assuming more curve is simply better.

A UK Paradox owner's experience matches the same ~2-knot cruising figure on a
different Layden boat: [Alastair](/people/alastair.md) said he will push
"[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)" at a comfortable **2 knots all day** under
the yuloh. On the Dinghy Cruising Association's annual **Thames raid** he
sculled roughly **50 of the ~55 miles**, arriving without the blisters that
afflicted the crews of the conventional rowing boats — because sculling lets the
body keep shifting position rather than repeat one fixed rowing stroke (see
[AYRS meeting talk](/references/ayrs-meeting-video.md)).

## Watertribe evidence: a micro-cruiser can be cruised motor-free under race pressure

The clearest demonstration that this is a viable primary propulsion method,
not just a calm-weather party trick, comes from David Bolduc's results
across several **Watertribe** events, entered partly out of curiosity and
partly to test micro-cruising techniques under pressure — following the
example of [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md) ("my long-time sailing
mentor"), who had entered Watertribe events year after year to field-test
his own designs.

- **~2008, WCTSS Cedar Key gathering** — David left the outboard behind
  entirely aboard Little Cruiser, found it unnecessary, and sculled to
  Atsena Otie Key and other nearby islands during the calms.
- **2009 NC Challenge (inaugural)** — sculled about **11 miles** to finish
  **first in class** with Enigma fully loaded for cruising at roughly
  **400 lb**, averaging close to 2 knots except against tide and headwind
  out of Beaufort.
- **2010 Everglades Challenge** — the first **300-mile** Watertribe
  Challenge completed, finishing first in class. The hardest test was
  **Gasparilla Pass at night**: a first attempt against a foul tide and
  **5-foot breaking seas** failed, forcing hours of waiting before sculling
  through successfully at the tide change. The 300-mile distance approximates
  the mileage of the Bolducs' usual Angelfish Creek-to-Georgetown winter
  cruising route.
- **2010 NC Challenge** — the hardest rowing test yet: **14 miles / 7 hours**
  of sculling on the first day alone, well beyond a usual roughly two-hour
  training session, yet still finishing a few hours faster than the prior
  year.

The conclusion these results support directly: even someone of average
fitness can coastal-cruise a micro-cruiser without a motor, even under race
time pressure — not only in the leisurely conditions of a winter Bahamas
cruise.

## A fully engine-free solo passage (2013)

The technique's furthest expression was David Bolduc's **2013 solo Bahamas
trip** aboard Enigma, made deliberately **without an outboard at all** — "a
voyage for the purist with sail and oar only," inspired by his mentors
**Sven Yrvind** and Matt Layden. The same influences — Yrvind, and **Lin and
Larry Pardey**, who "traveled all over the world with just an oar as
auxiliary power" — are credited generally with the case that engine-free
auxiliary propulsion is a serious, viable approach rather than a novelty.

Sculling in this sense sits alongside, not in place of, an
[auxiliary outboard](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md): most builders
carry both, but the Watertribe results and the 2013 passage show the yuloh
can substitute for the motor entirely when a sailor commits to the
technique.

## Matt Layden's own yuloh speed, and the straight-vs-curved-blade trade-off

During the Everglades Challenge he won, **Matt Layden** himself averaged
about **2 knots on the calm section** under yuloh power — the only
yuloh-speed figure in the record tied specifically to that race (see
[boats/original-paradox.md](/boats/original-paradox.md)),
and consistent with the general ~2 knot cruising-speed figure recorded above
from David Bolduc's own technique write-up.

**Dave Bolduc** provided the fullest direct comparison of yuloh design and
its practical effect on power and physical strain. Sculling Little Cruiser
on Bahamas trips, he found **1.5-2 knots** once used to the technique, a
best sustained one-mile average of about **2.5 knots**, and short sprints of
**3.0 knots**. Visiting Matt Layden after the Everglades Challenge, Dave got
to scull the original Paradox himself, with Matt coaching him to put his
**whole body** into the stroke. Paradox by then carried a **new sculling
oar** — straighter than the plans' design and with a **wider, longer
blade** — which Dave found **more powerful** (easier to control the blade's
pitch) but requiring **much more wrist action**, enough that his own right
wrist started hurting; Matt Layden reported that his own wrist had hurt
during the first few days of the Everglades Challenge using the same new
oar. Weighing the trade-off, Dave still preferred Little Cruiser's own
**more curved** oar. Photos taken afterward showed the tips of both the new
oar and the rudder reinforced with **bronze rod**. This is the record's
fullest direct comparison of yuloh design (straight vs. curved blade) and
its practical effect on both power and physical strain, plus a rare
admission that even the designer himself felt a real physical cost from his
own new gear during the race documented in
[boats/original-paradox.md](/boats/original-paradox.md). Over a year later,
[David Beard](/people/david-beard.md) independently confirmed Matt's oar
remained straight and, per Matt himself, still worked well but was harder on
the arm and wrist than a curved blade — corroborating Dave Bolduc's account
from a second source and resolving Smith's own uncertainty, at the time, over
whether the new oar was more or less curved than the original (see [yuloh
and stowed-rig
retention](/design/yuloh-and-stowed-rig-retention.md#building-the-yuloh-itself-blade-curvature-and-the-pivot-boltsocket)).

Dave Bolduc, having by then used both oars himself aboard Matt's own boat,
restated his own preference for the original, more curved oar: in his
judgment the extra curve makes the blade scull more automatically, whereas
the newer, straighter oar — while more powerful and easier to control for
blade pitch — demands noticeably more wrist effort, the same trade-off
[David Beard](/people/david-beard.md) had just independently confirmed from
Matt himself.

## Matt Layden's "motors are a luxury" philosophy, put into practice

Matt Layden's own stated view on engines — that motors are fine so long as a
sailor understands they are a **luxury**, not something to be relied on to
get out of trouble — has been deliberately tested by other builders.
[David Beard](/people/david-beard.md), returning from an overnighter on
"[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)" during which he deliberately left the
outboard at home, rigged a makeshift yuloh from a **kayak paddle** that
"worked ok" and planned a proper yuloh as his next project. Applying Matt's
philosophy directly, he realized he had been using his own outboard to
compensate for inexperience under sail — a crutch experienced sailors
wouldn't need — and was pleasantly surprised at what he could manage once he
had to.

Dave Bolduc offered a more qualified view: he likes using a motor as
"another 'tool,'" useful against strong contrary currents and on windless
days, but weighed against the cost of disrupted trim, carrying "6+ gallons
of explosive fuel," and the maintenance burden — concluding it's "really a
personal choice," and that a sailor who can do without an engine "will most
likely be a better sailor" for it. Sailing alongside Matt Layden for **two
months in the Bahamas**, "we almost always sailed together," Dave credited
that time with teaching him and Mindy the finer points of tides, wind
shifts, and sail trim. Dave went further than Matt's own "luxury" framing
for the **yuloh** specifically, calling it "a MUST" aboard "our little
boats" — not only for propulsion at 1-2 knots, but as "a great push pole in
the shallows" and a fender against rocks and docks; given a straight choice
between a motor and a yuloh, he said he would take the yuloh, though Little
Cruiser carries both and uses each for its own purpose (sail when there's
wind, scull when it's calm and they want to cover a short distance, motor
"and choke on the fumes" when they need speed on a windless day).

[Alastair](/people/alastair.md), returning around the same time from "little
Jim"'s Portland cruise (see
[boats/little-jim.md](/boats/little-jim.md)),
described a strikingly similar experience: **3 hours** fighting wind and
tide within 2 miles of a safe haven (never in real danger, though the
weather could have worsened), and wind and tide again for the final mile up
the river to the boatyard — "gives me lots of practice with the yuloh, but I
doubt if I shall be able to move my arms tomorrow." Despite this, he
remained determined to stay motor-free for the season at least, allowing
only that he might need to change his launch site to make that more
practical. David Beard's and Alastair's near-simultaneous accounts stand as
the clearest double data point for Matt Layden's motors-as-luxury philosophy
being deliberately put into practice, under real difficulty, by two
different builders in the same week.

## Carrying a spare yuloh: Smith's mass-and-momentum case against it

Whether to carry a **spare yuloh** — reasoning by analogy from canoeing,
where a spare paddle is considered essential in case the primary is lost or
broken — is bound up with a related doubt: given Paradox's weight, would she
even scull well at all? A paddler's intuition suggested not, on the theory
that a long, skinny, lightly built racing hull
gets far more "carry" between strokes than a heavy displacement hull ever
could, which would feel instead like pushing too much water and decelerating
quickly.

[David C. Smith](/people/david-c-smith.md) answered with the general physics of mass and momentum,
arguing the intuition has it backwards: a **heavier** vessel carries
**farther**, not less far, between strokes — the same principle that let
old-time schooners work their way up rivers and around bends under momentum
alone. Illustrated with paired examples at matched speed: a Hobie 18 doing 15
knots stops almost the instant its sails come down, while a supertanker at
the same 15 knots will coast on for a long time after "all stop"; a
skateboard and a hundred-car freight train, both on level ground, show the
same contrast. A solo paddler in a kevlar canoe must maintain a constant
rhythm to keep moving, but the same canoe loaded with a crew and two weeks of
provisions and camping gear will carry through a pause in paddling. What a
**light** hull gains instead is **acceleration**, not carry — the quality
behind a catamaran's sudden burst of speed or a racing crew's snap off the
start line — though even there, an eight-man rowing scull, with its
tremendous power and low wetted surface on a long waterline, will ultimately
carry farther than the accelerating catamaran.

On the spare-yuloh question specifically, Smith judged one unnecessary: he would
no more carry a spare yuloh than a spare diesel engine aboard a sailing
auxiliary, since the yuloh is **auxiliary** propulsion, not primary, unlike a
canoe paddle, where losing the only paddle genuinely strands the paddler. He
noted plenty of yuloh how-to material exists online, but none of it, in his
judgment, worth the price of the Paradox plans — which already include
**yuloh plans** as standard, confirmed separately by
[Alastair](/people/alastair.md).

See also [yuloh and stowed-rig retaining loops](/design/yuloh-and-stowed-rig-retention.md)
for how the yuloh is stowed aboard, [Dave Bolduc](/people/bolduc/dave.md) for
his fuller biography, and [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md), whose own
Watertribe participation and sculling-over-towing preference (declining a tow
across the Gulf Stream in favor of sailing or sculling Paradox) set the
example other builders followed.
