# Anders Nygaard's Miramis interview

Anders Nygaard's **Miramis** interview is a solicited, first-person primary source in which the builder of the 10%-enlarged Norwegian Paradox "[Miramis](/boats/miramis.md)" answers the archive directly about the boat's design rationale, its construction, and how he intends to use her. It is the **first source in the archive gathered by direct interview** rather than compiled from an existing web, email, or social-media record, and it is the fullest single account of a **10% Paradox scale-up** as a considered whole — the "why," not only the "what."

The interview was conducted on **2026-07-23** as a text interview, in Norwegian, over five rounds of questions, and is written up by topic rather than as raw question-and-answer. Because it is a solicited account from the builder himself, it carries first authority on his own boat; because that boat is unfinished, much of what it records is intention as well as accomplished fact, and it is written up and cited as such.

## What the interview adds

Nygaard's build is otherwise documented in the [Paradox Sailboat Facebook group](/references/paradox-facebook-group.md), where his own posts from 2020 onward form a dated build log (the source for most of the [Miramis](/boats/miramis.md) page). The interview complements that record rather than repeating it: where the Facebook posts show the build *happening*, the interview supplies the reasoning and the parts of the story the posts never stated. Chiefly:

- **Why 10%, and the name.** The boat is named **"Miramis"** after Mio's white horse in Astrid Lindgren's *Mio, min Mio*; and the choice of exactly 10% is explained as the largest scale-up that stays trailerable, against [Eerik Mee](/people/eerik-mee.md)'s dock-kept 20% "[THU](/boats/thu.md)."
- **The kept scantlings, glassed to compensate.** Rather than scale plank thickness up with the hull, he kept the thinner [Simas Butavičius](/people/simas-butavicius.md) dimensions and made up the difference by glassing the hull inside and out.
- **The one deliberate departure.** A 3D-printed, carbon-clad rudder carried in a **cassette that braces the blade from both sides** in the manner of **John Welsford**'s boats — the single place he consciously breaks with the stock design, for strength.
- **Trailer, and intended waters.** A 1250 kg / 18 ft trailer cleared with Norway's road authority; a boat meant for the **Skagerrak**, with a tongue-in-cheek plan for the shallow mountain lake **Møsvatn** and standing Dinghy Cruising Association invitations to the **Limfjord** and **Koster**.
- **The builder, and the effort.** His first boat build; a furniture-maker and former sailboat owner; his estimate of ~2,000 hours and ~$10,000 to a good standard; and his advice that a beginner should start with an **Oz Goose**.

## Its evidentiary value and limits

Its value is that it is **direct and specific from the one person who knows the boat best**, and that it settles matters the Facebook record leaves implicit — the reasoning behind the 10% figure, the name, the scantling-and-glass trade, and the rudder-cassette departure. As a solicited source it is also **current**, reaching the build's 2026 state (the rudder cassette, the forged-carbon tack strop) a year past where the dated Facebook posts stop.

Its limits follow from the same facts. It is **self-reported** and **uncorroborated except where the Facebook log independently covers the same ground** (which it does for the scantlings, the ballast, the chine runners, the systems, and the sail). And it describes a boat **still under construction**: figures such as the final ballast, the ready-to-sail weight, the self-righting behaviour at the larger scale, and a launch date are stated as intention or left open, and are cited that way. Nothing here has been tested on the water.
