# The "Building Paradox" MAIB series — construction content

**"Building Paradox"** is a **sixteen-installment construction series by Don Elliott**, published in [Messing About In Boats](/references/messing-about-in-boats.md) from **vol. 14 no. 9 (September 15, 1996)** through **vol. 14 no. 24 (May 1, 1997)** — Parts 1 through 15 followed by a "Conclusion." It documents, step by step, the ground-up build of Elliott's own Paradox (later known as ["The Edge"](/boats/edge.md)), from design rationale and the bill of materials through hull assembly, rudder, rig, hatch, and finishing. This page captures the **content** of the articles; the articles as an obtainable product — Elliott's mail-order booklet, its price, and its later printing defect — are covered separately at [Building the Paradox articles](/references/building-the-paradox-articles.md).

The series is the fullest single construction account of the Paradox in the record, and much of it corroborates or predates the mailing-list knowledge captured elsewhere in this bundle. Its practical value is qualified in one respect: an engineer at the epoxy maker Gougeon Brothers published a **detailed technical rebuttal** of several of its epoxy claims (see [Reception and the epoxy-accuracy controversy](#reception-and-the-epoxy-accuracy-controversy)), so its epoxy specifics are recorded here as **what Elliott advised**, cross-checked against that correction where it applies.

The full run of sixteen installments matches the "16 articles" figure by which the series was later sold and remembered (see [Building the Paradox articles](/references/building-the-paradox-articles.md)); a single mailing-list post once cited only the first "five parts (issues 9-13)," which undercounted it. **All sixteen installments, including Part 11 (vol. 14 no. 19), are accounted for below.**

Much of the series' technique content has since been broken out into standalone articles that carry the detail this page only summarises: [the builder's toolkit](/construction/builders-toolkit.md), [fibreglassing technique](/construction/fiberglassing-technique.md), [puttying and fairing](/construction/puttying-and-fairing.md), [painting preparation and paint-system choice](/construction/painting-preparation.md), [casting lead ballast](/construction/casting-lead-ballast.md), [solvents and epoxy clean-up](/construction/solvents-and-cleanup.md), and [epoxy safety and personal protection](/construction/epoxy-safety-and-ppe.md); the [hatch](/components/hatch.md) as a component in its own right; [beam, freeboard, and stability](/design/beam-and-stability.md); the unbuilt ["Best Open Beachcruiser"](/design/variants/best-open-beachcruiser.md) design request addressed to Matt Layden; and [the small-boat library](/references/small-boat-library.md) the series drew on.

## The published design particulars

Reprinted with the series (Part 10) and first shown on the July 15, 1995 cover feature, the design's own **specifications box** — titled "Paradox / 4.2 m Coastal Cruiser / Matt Layden · 7/94 · Drawing #1" — gives the canonical published figures:[10][1]

| | | | |
|---|---|---|---|
| LOA | 4.2 m / 13′10″ | Sail area | 9.3 m² / 100 ft² |
| LWL | 4.1 m / 13′5″ | Displacement | 640 kg / 1,410 lb (to DWL, sailing trim) |
| Beam | 1.29 m / 4′0½″ | Prismatic coefficient | 0.58 |
| Draft | 23 cm / 9″ | Displacement/length | 270 |

Full building plans — **14 pages of drawings for $35** — were sold by **David & Mindy Bolduc** (then P.O. Box 14833, Greensboro NC); Matt Layden was "reluctant to draw up the plans... and only through their encouragement did he do so."[10][6] The plans' own **"Sad Commentary"** disclaims any guarantee and states the plans are "a guide, not... a complete or comprehensive manual." The materials spec calls for **carefully selected A-C exterior fir plywood** (marine "may produce a stronger, fairer, longer lasting boat"), medium-density solid wood (fir, cypress, luan, mahogany, spruce — **avoid oak, teak, maple, birch, poplar**), epoxy or resorcinol glue, and **bronze ring nails** (Gripfast/annular) as "permanent backup to glue bonds."[10] These figures extend the bundle's [hull scantlings](/design/hull-scantlings.md), [weight and ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md), and [building plans](/references/building-plans.md); the prismatic coefficient (0.58) and displacement/length ratio (270) are quantitative particulars not otherwise in the record.

## Design philosophy: what Paradox eliminates and provides (Part 1)

The opening installment frames Paradox as "the result of a design process of elimination." It lists **seven things Matt Layden did away with** — the cockpit (room, swamping, falls overboard), keel/centerboard/daggerboard (useless in shoal water), the jib (unhandy, needs going forward), sail track and bolt-rope slot (jam when lowering), the tall mast (suited to ballasted keelboats, not light cruisers), rigging stays (a coastal boat lowers its mast often), and reef points ("there's a better way") — and **seventeen things provided in their place**, among them full helm control from within a watertight cabin, reefing/raising/lowering the sail from inside, the **chine runners** ("the magic step for Paradox"), a 180°-rotating kick-up rudder, full insulation doubling as flotation and soundproofing, **13 separate stowage areas**, two 70-litre rain-fillable water tanks, a 3′×6′ sleeping sole, lightning protection, roller-reefing, drying out level on a tough bottom, ventilation throughout, and 360° visibility from within.[1] Elliott proposes calling the type a **"chine runner sharpie."**[4] See [sharpie lineage](/design/sharpie-lineage.md) for the fuller question of what the record does and doesn't settle about Paradox's place in the type. His early figure for self-righting — "**about 120 degrees**" — is lower than the ~165° the mailing-list record later settled on (see [weight and ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md)), and is recorded here as a dated 1996 estimate.[1]

## Materials, plans, and the epoxy method (Parts 2–3)

Paradox needs **no lofting and no strongback** — "Matt controls the shape through careful dimensioning of parts."[11] Elliott built from **Douglas-fir exterior plywood** ("get Boise-Cascade," equal-thickness veneers, all his plywood cost $200) rather than marine grade, holding that epoxy-sealed exterior ply is sound; his rule: "if you don't epoxy inside and out buy marine, if you epoxy, exterior grade is fine" — a position later argued at length on the list (see [plywood grade and foam-flotation debate](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md)).[11] He used **System Three epoxy** (2:1, easy-sanding, from Merton's) over WEST SYSTEM, and specifically found System Three would clear its epoxy for the potable water tank where WEST would not.[11][17]

The build uses a **two-step gluing method** (no pre-coating: wet out both faces, then thicken the remaining epoxy with Cab-O-Sil and butter one face) and treats epoxy's **gap-filling ability** as the hinge of the whole modern method — "the Gougeon brothers in the 70s invented a completely new method of boatbuilding," and traditional planes, spokeshaves, and draw-knives are set aside for the table saw, disc grinder, and Surform.[12] Cab-O-Sil is the everyday thickener; microfibres for structural bonds and fillets; microspheres/microballoons/**Microlight (WEST 410)** for sandable fairing.[11] A running **"Screw-Up Section"** documents epoxy repairs to gouges, splits, and cup-holes.[12] These techniques underlie the bundle's [epoxy coating technique](/construction/epoxy-coating-technique.md), [fastening and gluing](/construction/fastening-and-gluing.md), and [epoxy brands and chemistry](/construction/epoxy-brands-and-chemistry.md).

## Hull parts: bulkheads, side panels, stem, and scarphs (Parts 3–5, 7, 10)

Accuracy matters only at layout and assembly of the four bulkheads, one transom, and the two side panels — "1.5 mm to 2 mm," dead-accurate, because "no strongback [means] the main parts must be right on."[11] The **14 laminated deck and cabin beams** are dry-heat-bent over shop-built jigs before gluing.[11] Bulkheads are epoxy-coated (by squeegee, then a metal putty knife on the second coat, under a **"No Sanding"** doctrine that avoids grinding off costly epoxy) and set to cure hard before filleting.[12][14]

The **side panels** are laid out full length on sawhorses from Drawing #6 and cut just outside the line, then **scarphed** — Elliott follows Harold Payson's method, adding a metal edge-strip to keep control of the scarph slope, then chalk-and-sand to check.[13][15] The panels are joined on the floor against fixed blocks and a chalk baseline, resisting "slipping and sliding" with the cast lead pigs as weights, and cured a full week.[15] Chines and sheers, likewise, are **scarphed from standard 8-ft stock** in a fifteen-minute jig routine, then a 6″ cut-off of each is kept as a hull-assembly guide.[10] The **stem is built up in layers** — the middle layer first, from five cardboard patterns, with 29° side bevels — then the bow curve is cut only after the three layers are glued.[15] The **3/4″ bottom panel is scarphed** (four hours' work with grinder, belt sander, and plane) and glued on only after the sheers and chines are set into slots cut in the bulkhead corners, the hull is turned over, and the water-tank and food-store separators are faired in both directions against a taut string; crucially, **before the bottom goes on, a template of the hull-side curve is taken where the chine runners will mount** — "vital to the success of making a good chine runner."[16] See [scarf joints](/construction/scarf-joints.md), [panel layout](/construction/panel-layout.md), [hull planking](/construction/hull-planking.md), and [stem notching](/construction/stem-notching.md).

## The rudder, ballast, and filleting (Parts 6–9)

Paradox's **kick-up rudder blade** — two plywood pieces epoxied over a dowel alignment pin — pivots through 180° on a deliberately loose bolt, hauled up by a line in a V-groove and held down by lead cast into a pocket at its rear; its face is shaped to a **NASA airfoil section** (radiused leading edge, maximum thickness about a third back, ending in "not a sharp tip, but a small flat" whose slight drag "pulls the water onto the blade's surface" and prevents trailing-edge turbulence).[6][7][8] Its **lower pintle is a custom part the builder moulds** — chopped fibreglass and microfibres packed into a wooden mould "like a pile of wet hair" — because a conventional pintle can't sit close enough to the hull for the blade's 180° swing to port and starboard; the blade is then fibreglassed by the series' **"don't float the cloth" method** (squeegee the resin thin, never pool it, and add no more epoxy until the first coat gels).[16] See [rudder](/components/rudder.md).

**Ballast:** Paradox takes between 70 and 180 kg (154–397 lb) of lead, cast as ≈40-lb "pigs" — Elliott advises making eight to start — poured into buried bread-pan moulds from lead **begged as used tyre-balancing weights** (the steel clips float off and are skimmed).[6] This is the magazine-published counterpart to the ballast detail on [weight and ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md).

**Filleting:** Part 9 introduces Elliott's **"Spherical Filleting"** — a technique using a ball-ended tool (a marble on a dowel) run along a masking-tape-bounded joint, in a "Superfast" and a "Right-Now" variant ("I've finished a 20′ fillet in under five minutes"), which he prints under an "All Rights Reserved" notice claiming the *concept* as his own.[9] That originality claim was directly contested (see below). The structural fillet is built in three passes — pure epoxy, then epoxy + microfibres + Cab-O-Sil, then epoxy + Cab-O-Sil — with Cab-O-Sil in every mix to stop sag.[9]

## Deck, cabin, hatch, mast, and windows (Parts 10, 12–15)

The hull is assembled **without a strongback** in an eleven-step trial fit — bulkheads located by hot-glued blocks, drawn together with Spanish windlasses and Quick-Grips gripping sheet-metal screws, squared to a centreline string, then disassembled and finally glued and ring-nailed.[10] The **vent trunk** is described as "actually a plenum chamber": deck openings for the mast and control lines "are not required to be watertight," because water runs around them and back overboard rather than into the cabin — a defining Paradox feature.[10] Excess epoxy is carried between coating grids by an **"Epoxy Flip"** of the putty knife.[10]

The two **fresh-water tanks are built for drinking water** to System Three's instructions — five interior epoxy coats over cloth, then a five-day regimen of white-vinegar wipe and warm-water wash to remove residue; WEST SYSTEM declined to recommend its epoxy for potable tanks because its hardener "keeps releasing a residue that's harmful if ingested."[17] See [water tank lining](/construction/water-tank-lining.md).

The **windows are Lexan**, sealed only with **GE Silicone II** ("it has to be the II not I" — other sealants craze it), cut and drilled on a shop-made jig with close to 200 screws.[17][20] Elliott **sewed his own sail** from a $217 Sailrite kit ($330 if factory-sewn), needing a strong machine to punch seven cloth layers, and regretted being talked down from 6 oz to 4.2 oz cloth — citing Ian Nicolson that a 16-ft cruiser wants ≈5–5.7 oz.[17] See [sailmaking](/construction/sailmaking.md).

Paradox's **hatch has no hatch door** — "another item eliminated" — sealing instead like "a submarine" via a compressed EVA-foam strip on the side rails, a sandwiched-plus-vinyl-sweep front seal, and a rear lift-and-drop over a traversing beam.[20] The **mast is a hollow close-grain Douglas-fir box**, glued up in stages, carrying its masthead-light wire and a copper lightning strap foamed inside so they "won't keep you awake slapping"; Elliott advises epoxy-and-paint over varnish, and confirms it stows inside the boat through the aft port.[20] Because Paradox has **no metal mast, standing rigging, or engine** — "the only metal of any size on board is the anchor, bolts in the cleats and the upper pintle" — Layden nonetheless designed in detailed lightning protection.[20] See [mast sealing and fit](/construction/mast-sealing-and-fit.md), [window glazing materials](/construction/window-glazing-materials.md), and [lightning protection](/design/lightning-protection.md).

## Finishing, systems, and the reader Q&A (Conclusion)

The final installment answers reader questions and lists last tasks. Its most-cited exchange validates the **chine runners**: asked whether they work, Elliott quotes **Dave Bolduc**, who "after sailing Paradox against Little Cruiser (which has a centerboard) for over two months... did not find enough difference in the windward performance between the two boats to suggest altering Paradox," and recommends building to the plans before modifying.[21] Elliott adds that chine runners are "more like a scoop" than a low-aspect keel, tested via radio-control models, with the deep-sitting hull side itself counting as lateral resistance — see [chine runners](/chine-runners/index.md).[21] On modifications generally he defers to the designer: "On any changes to the boat you should consult the designer, I'm only the builder."[21]

Last systems and finishing: the **battery** rides in a vented, strapped-down plastic box (≈50 lb, trimmed with a lead pig opposite), with Matt Layden recommending a solar panel and voltage regulator; the **rudder is steered by a continuous looped control line** on four corner blocks, lockable by a yo-yo-like clamp so the solo sailor can steer "from anywhere in the cabin" (see [self-steering](/operations/self-steering.md)); the **boom and yard** are laminated, the reef spool fibreglass-wrapped, the boom rotating on an internal metal tube (see [furling system](/components/rig/furling.md)); painting uses **Pettit High-Build undercoat and Easy Epoxy topcoat**, foam-rolled and tipped off by a second person (see [topside painting](/construction/topside-painting.md)); and fittings are bedded in **3M 5200 or Sikaflex 231** by a "double seal" bead system, to be reopened and inspected "every couple of years."[21] The series closes: "**Matt Layden has elevated the concept of the small systemized coastal cruiser to a new level.**"[21]

## Reception and the epoxy-accuracy controversy

Because MAIB is an independently edited periodical, the series' reception is on the record — and it was pointed. **James R. Watson, a technical advisor at Gougeon Brothers (WEST SYSTEM epoxy)**, published a detailed correction: that WEST SYSTEM and Cab-O-Sil trademarks were used incorrectly; that **vinegar is unsafe for cleaning epoxy off skin** — it "slightly dissolves" epoxy and carries it into the subdermal tissue, promoting allergic sensitisation (safe only for cleaning *tools*); that the "spherical filleting" tool was **not original** (Freeman Mfg. had sold an identical fillet tool for ~50 years, and Gougeon had made their own for 20); and that Elliott confused **"creep" with "slump."** Watson allowed the articles had "much merit" and "some great tips," but warned of "errors, misinformation and untested opinions which can be very misleading to one using that information as gospel."[18]

Reader letters split sharply — one reader was "dismayed by erroneous information" and judged Elliott "not in the expert category," another answered "for every expert there is an equal and opposite expert" and found WEST SYSTEM "a little stuffy," and a third stopped reading "after about stage six" finding the style "pedantic."[19] Elliott replied with a sidebar, **"In Defense of 'Building Paradox'"**: he "stood corrected" on the trademarks, but denied the rest — he had **never** advised cleaning skin with vinegar (only tools, "just keep it off your skin"), never claimed to *invent* the spherical tool (the copyright "is to protect the text and method" for a planned book), and maintained "every word and advice given... is accurate based on thorough research or personal experience." He closed by turning Watson's own trademark lecture back on him: "you misspelled my name, it's spelled with two t's. It's a valuable asset to me and should be used with care and accuracy."[20] The **Conclusion** quietly incorporated part of the correction, restating (per a System Three rep) that vinegar is fine on tools and "never advised in 'Building Paradox'" for skin.[21] The whole episode — a public dispute over Elliott's authority and accuracy, met with combative self-defence — prefigures his later mailing-list conflicts; see [Don Elliott](/people/don-elliott.md).

## Biographical glimpses of Matt Layden (from the series)

Writing before Layden was widely known, Elliott gathered what he could: he "first heard his name mentioned in *Small Boat Journal* over ten years ago" (≈1986) and again in Ida Little & Michael Walsh's [*Beach Cruising and Coastal Camping*](/references/beachcruising-book.md); the July 15, 1995 cover shows "Matt and the boat in the Bahamas in 30 knots of wind"; Layden's boat "is without a motor and if necessary he sculls all day," and he built Paradox "without the aid of transportation other than his bicycle and a small trailer."[6] Part 9's issue also carried a reader's **"Layden Sightings"** — two 1983–84 log entries recording a chance meeting with young Matt Layden single-handing his self-built cat-boat **"Terrapin of New London, CT"** off Florida and Georgia: "about the size of a Beetle cat but with leeboards and a fully battened sail... could be completely closed in and oars could be stuck out the sides... the helmsman sat aft with his head out a hatch."[8] These firsthand-date [Terrapin](/matt-layden/terrapin.md) to 1983–84 and enrich the [Matt Layden biography](/matt-layden/biography.md).

## Citing this source

This series is print-only, carried in [Messing About In Boats](/references/messing-about-in-boats.md); there is no separate URL for "Building Paradox" itself. The stable identifier is the **installment** — Part N (or "Conclusion") — together with its MAIB volume, number, and date, per [Messing About In Boats — Provenance and citing](/references/messing-about-in-boats.md#provenance-and-citing). Cite an installment in the form used throughout the citations below:

> [Messing About In Boats](/references/messing-about-in-boats.md), vol. 14 no. N (date), "Building Paradox — Part N," Don Elliott (pp. X–Y) — [detail].

For the booklet-form compilation of the same articles as a purchasable product, see [Building the Paradox articles](/references/building-the-paradox-articles.md).
