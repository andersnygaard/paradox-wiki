# "Paradox" — DCA Bulletin 193 (Duncan Gilchrist)

A signed article, **"Paradox,"** subtitled as an evaluation of the Matt Layden-designed 13'10" (4.2 m) micro-cruiser, ran across pages **45–47** of ***Bulletin* 193** of the **Dinghy Cruising Association** — the same issue that carries the owner's own appraisal at pp. 48–49.[1] The two pieces are complementary and were evidently placed together, but they are **different articles by different hands**, and the archive holds them as separate sources.

What makes this one distinct is its standpoint. The author was not an owner, not a builder, and not a partisan: he approached the design from **declared scepticism** about the central claim that a keel-less, board-less flat-bottomed box could work to windward, arranged a day and a half of sailing specifically to test it, and wrote the result up.[1] That makes it the archive's **only independent sail trial of a Paradox published in print** — an outside evaluation rather than an owner's testimony, a builder's log, or a designer's account.

## The trial itself

The sail took place at **Chew Valley Lake Sailing Club** in **October 2006**, aboard the UK Paradox "[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)," after an earlier encounter with the boat on the DCA pontoon at the **Beale Park Show 2006**.[1] The author had by then obtained and studied a set of plans and the building instructions, so the piece combines a sail trial with a plans review.[1]

Conditions were poor for the purpose and the article says so: wind ranged from **flat calm to about 4 mph** for most of the day, with one useful spell of roughly **8 mph** after lunch.[1] The windward verdict — tacking through 90°, close to hull speed — therefore rests on a **single short period of usable breeze**, a limitation the article states plainly rather than obscuring. The scepticism of onlookers is recorded too: the sailing club's president had a rescue boat stand by for the launch.[1]

The trial is also the source of a **negative** finding that is rare in the record: the boat completed every tack that day, but goes into a tack quickly, then drops level onto her flat bottom and slows as the wind pressure comes off the sail, so that her weight only just carries her round — leaving the author unconvinced about beating up narrow rivers.[1] This independently corroborates the owner's own listed disadvantage in the companion article, from a different observer on a different day.

## Why this source dates Bulletin 193

The companion article's reference page records the issue as **undated on the captured pages**, with "three seasons" of sailing since the April 2003 launch used to infer a probable 2005 or 2006. This article settles the question from the same issue: it describes the **Beale Park Show 2006**, states that plans ordered by airmail arrived **in October 2006**, and carries an editorial note dating the photographs to **Chew Valley Lake, October 2006**.[1][2]

*Bulletin* 193 therefore cannot have been published before **late 2006**, and most likely appeared in the winter of **2006–07**. This is firmer than the inference drawn from the companion piece alone, and it is internal to the issue rather than external to it. See ["Sailing The Paradox" — DCA Bulletin 193](/references/dca-sailing-the-paradox.md), whose dating section is now qualified by this finding.

## The forename spelling, in the same issue

This article names the owner of "Little Jim" in full, in running text and in the editor's photo credit, spelling the forename **"Alastair"** throughout.[1][2] The companion article two pages later bylines the same person **"Alistair."**

A single issue of one magazine thus prints the name **both ways**, which is direct evidence that "Alistair" is a publication-side variant rather than a competing spelling in use by its owner — the reading the archive had already adopted on the strength of self-identification in the mailing-list record. It also adds a **third independent print attestation of the surname Law**, alongside the companion article and *[Messing About in Boats](/references/messing-about-in-boats.md)*. See [Alastair Law](/people/alastair-law.md) for the full naming evidence.

## A note on the degree sign

Throughout the article, heel angles and field of view are printed with a **percent sign where a degree sign is meant** — heel figures of "20%," "35%" and "55%," and "360% vision" from inside the closed hatch.[1] The intended values are unambiguous from context, and one angle in the same article ("45°", "90°") is set correctly, confirming the substitution is a typesetting or character-mapping fault rather than the author's meaning.

The archive therefore records these figures **as degrees**, and this note exists so the reading is traceable rather than silent.

## Provenance

The source is a three-page PDF, held locally at `source-articles/gilchrist/paradox-by-duncan-gilchrist/source.pdf` with a full transcription and three extracted figures at `source-articles/gilchrist/paradox-by-duncan-gilchrist/index.md`. All three pages carry the running foot **"DCA Bulletin 193 : 45"** through **": 47."**

The PDF is published by **dinghycruising.life** and was verified byte-identical to the locally held copy. The article begins **partway down p. 45**, after the unrelated conclusion of a separate cruise-log piece, which is not part of this article and is excluded from the transcription.

The figures are two photographs of "Little Jim" — afloat at the water's edge, and bow-on on her road trailer — and a **captioned cutaway diagram** of the design, labelling roller reefing, chine runners, foam insulation and buoyancy, solar panel, sculling yuloh, interior steering, the line to raise the rudder, battery, food locker, gimballed stove, water tanks, and anchor storage.[3]
