# The small-boat library behind the Paradox

Most boatbuilding books never have anything new to say — one repeats another, showing a notch already explained ten times before, or a technique nobody would ever use; library shelves carry outdated books depicting materials no one uses any more and unaware of a decade of new methods, and the genre's most injurious fault is omission, since these books fail to answer the real problems that come up constantly while building.[7] The standard proposed against that failure is that a boatbuilding book should include complete building information, covering every step, making certain there are no omissions.[7] The list below — assembled across the [sixteen installments](/references/building-paradox-series.md) of the "Building Paradox" series carried in [Messing About In Boats](/references/messing-about-in-boats.md) — is idiosyncratic against that standard: it mixes small-boat voyaging accounts kept as proof against a size-equals-safety assumption, type-history references consulted for a beachcruiser design brief, construction manuals and epoxy-maker literature drawn on step by step, and the two places Matt Layden's own boats first reached print.

## Small-boat voyaging accounts and precedents

The series holds that size does not determine seaworthiness — a claim it says has been proven wrong many times — and names small boats as the evidence: a small boat is more uncomfortable, not as fast, not as weatherly, but unsafe has been proven otherwise.[1] Robert Manry, *Tinkerbelle*, recounting a 13 ft converted daysailer taken across the Atlantic, is named among the precedents.[1] Gerry Spiess (the transcription's spelling; standard spelling "Gerry Spiess"), *Alone Across The Atlantic*, recounting a 10 ft homemade boat, is likewise cited.[1] Webb Chiles, *Open Boats Across The Pacific*, is cited with the note that Chiles almost lost his boat, attributed to its centreboard.[1] Named in the text without an accompanying book are Yankee Girl, Mermaid, and Chidiock Tichborne, and — as the most extreme case offered — Sergio Testa's Acrohc Australis.[1] Judson Abbott, "Almost To Hell and Back," *Small Boat Journal* #57, rounds out the references list on the same point.[1]

## Beach cruising and shoal-water practice

Ida Little & Michael Walsh, [*Beach Cruising and Coastal Camping*](/references/beachcruising-book.md), appears in the same nine-item references list; the book was drawn on for shoal-water, drying-out-on-the-flats cruising practice and is elsewhere in the record one of the places [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/index.md)'s boats were first encountered in print.[1]

## Sharpies and small-craft type history

Reuel Parker (the transcription gives both "Reuel" and, in the Part 4 references list, "Ruel"; standard spelling is "Reuel"), *The Sharpie Book*, is cited twice: once in Part 1's references list, and again in Part 1's running text for its detailed description of how to wait for the right weather window on a passage, the point being illustrated by Little Cruiser's and Paradox's own Bahamas passages.[1] Howard Chapelle, *American Small Sailing Craft*, is paired with Parker in the Part 4 references list.[2] John Gardner, *Building Classic Small Craft*, is named for its sharpie chapter — "A Sharpie" — held up as an ideal starting profile and used as the opening reference point for a design brief for "The Best Open Beachcruiser," addressed within the series to Matt Layden.[4]

## Construction technique

H. (Harold) Payson's description of how to make a scarph, found at the back of one of his books, is called the best available and worth following, with the further clarification that Payson's key discovery was the need for a piece of metal at the scarph's edge.[3] The Gougeon Brothers' *The Gougeon Brothers on Boat Construction* and WEST SYSTEM's *Wooden Boat Restoration & Repair* (#002-970) are named together in a Part 3 forward-reference to upcoming epoxy coverage.[6] System Three's *The Epoxy Book* is cited twice — once alongside the WEST SYSTEM restoration manual, and again in the Part 12 references list.[6][5] WEST SYSTEM's *Epoxyworks* newsletter is cited twice in the Part 13 references: No. 4 (Spring 1994, p. 18) for PVC bonding-method tests, and No. 7 (Spring 1996, p. 10), the source for both the house-primer adhesion tests and a plywood-joint study finding butt-block joints only about 20% as strong as the plywood itself.[8] *WoodenBoat* #129 (April 1996) is cited for dry-heat wood bending.[9]

## Sails and canvas work

Ian Nicolson (the transcription's running text spells it "Nicholson," but the Part 12 references list gives "Nicolson," the standard spelling), *Roving in Open Boats*, is quoted at length on cruising sail cloth weight: "in our sail lofts we are saddened every year when we see inadequate sails which are sold with new dinghies... these sails are too light, poorly finished and not designed to stand up to the hurry burly of life afloat," continuing that cruising cloth weight should be high — "typically, a 16-foot boat should have cloth around 5 oz. weight and for really extended cruising it should be 5.7 oz."[5] Frank Rosenow, *Canvas and Rope Craft*, is cited as the source of the square patch used to lock up the aft end of a sail's panel seams.[5] See [sailmaking](/construction/sailmaking.md).

## Design and stability

John Teale, *How to Design a Boat*, is quoted on a narrow, high-sided boat's advantage in a knockdown: "in the last analysis and a total knock-down the slimmer vessel is likely to right herself, where as the beamier boat may remain capsized and upside down," and again on freeboard: "high freeboard, however undesirable for windage and whether caused by high top sides or high deck work, does marvels for maximum righting moments and range of stability."[10] Teale's book is also listed as the third item in the Part 13 references, alongside the two *Epoxyworks* issues.[8] See [blue-water suitability](/design/blue-water-suitability.md).

## Where Matt Layden's boats were first written about

Malcolm Howes, "Evolution of a Microcruiser," *Small Boat Journal* #75, appears in the Part 1 references list among the small-boat-seaworthiness citations.[1] The Bolducs' own *Messing About In Boats* articles are cited in the same list: "Cruising In Little Cruiser" (July 15, 1995) and "Cheating Winter" (June 15, 1993), by Dave and Mindy Bolduc.[1]

## Manufacturer literature

West Marine's free booklet *Grounding and Lightning Protection Equipment*, available by calling 800-538-0775, is given as the reference for the series' lightning-protection coverage.[11] See [lightning protection](/design/lightning-protection.md).

## Citing this source

This page is a **bibliography**: each entry's own book, magazine-article, or manufacturer-literature title is the stable identifier — e.g. Reuel Parker's *The Sharpie Book*, Ian Nicolson's *Roving in Open Boats*, WEST SYSTEM's *Epoxyworks*. None of these titles is available at a public URL; they are print sources, cited here exactly as Don Elliott's own "Building Paradox" series cites them. Cite an entry in the form:
