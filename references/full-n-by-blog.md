# Full n By (Sean Mulligan's blog)

**Full n By** (`https://fullnby.blogspot.com/`) is the Blogger blog of **[Sean Mulligan](/people/sean-mulligan.md)**, the Lake Havasu, Arizona owner of the [Paradox](/paradox.md) **"[Scout](/boats/scout.md)."** Its Paradox content is the first-person record of **finishing, launching, and learning to sail one boat** — the stock Paradox that [Lezlie Henson](/people/lezlie-henson.md) built and named **"Tardis,"** which Mulligan bought almost complete, finished, renamed **"Scout,"** and sailed. It is the **authored, first-person primary source** for the sailing life of a hull the archive had otherwise known mainly at second hand, and it is the direct sequel to [Lezlie's World](/references/lezlie-henson-blog.md): where Henson's diary is the two-year *build*, this is the *completion and the first sailing*.

Its title is a sailing term — to sail **"full and by"** is to hold a course close-hauled, sails full and as near the wind as the boat will point — and Mulligan, who **founded the Havasu Pocket Cruiser's Convention (HPCC)**, writes as much about the pocket- and micro-cruising outlook as about the boat itself.

## What the blog is worth as evidence

Its value is that it is **contemporaneous and first-hand about a boat the bundle had only heard summarised.** Everything the archive previously carried about "Scout" came through a UK observer, **Bill Serjeant**, reporting at second hand — that she was "built to a high standard," bought and renamed, and sailed hard.[1] Mulligan's own blog is the source underneath that: it records, in the owner's words and photographs, exactly what he did to finish Henson's almost-complete hull, when and how he first launched her, and how he set her up to sail and scull single-handed on a desert lake.

It is also **specific where the second-hand record is general.** It fixes the launch to a dated post; it itemises the completion work — the mast, the battery and forward compartment, the sail and spars, the mainsheet hardware, the cabin ventilation and masthead wiring — that no other source describes; and it gives the first-hand account of **re-working the yuloh** that other sources only note Mulligan "depends on."[1] Alongside the boat, it is a rare owner's-voice statement of the **micro-cruising outlook** from the founder of the largest US trailer-sailer gathering of its day.

Its limits should be read with that. It is one owner's account, written for a general small-boat readership rather than for builders, and its several essays — on sailing with a partner, on GPS versus fundamental navigation, on "two-foot-itis in reverse" — are opinion, clearly Mulligan's own and cited as such. The boat is a **stock** Paradox, so the blog adds completion narrative, outfitting choices, and a sailing record rather than design data. And the Blogger run captured here **ends in January 2014**, before the 2014 Texas 200 and the heavy-wind video record that the bundle already draws from Bill's Log; those later events are outside this corpus.[1]

## Provenance and dating

The blog is a conventional dated-post Blogger site; its run is **43 posts from 10 August 2013 to 6 January 2014**, when Mulligan announced he was moving off Blogger to a self-hosted site (`fullnby.com`). Those later, self-hosted posts are not part of this record. **Twelve** posts are (or contain) YouTube videos, linked from Mulligan's channel. Nine images embedded in the posts are not the boat's own — eight are clip-art and stock pictures Mulligan hot-linked from other sites into two essays, and one is an expired Facebook-CDN photo; all of "Scout's" own photographs are otherwise intact.

## What it adds: finishing "Tardis," and the first sailing of the hull

The blog is drawn out in full on the boat's and owner's pages; in outline, it supplies:

- **The launch.** "Scout" was rigged and sailed for the first time on **12 September 2013** — the hull's first-ever launch, since Henson sold her unsailed.[2]
- **Mulligan's completion work.** He bought her about 95% complete and finished her over the summer of 2013: he found the mast built with a stainless eyebolt in its side rather than the plans' internal halyard sheave; removed the plans' removable bow partitions and moved the battery forward and on-centre to trim her; extended the sail's yard about eight inches and re-set the outhaul (Henson's sail was sewn from a Sailrite kit, which he took to be the later Mark-II Paradox mainsail); made aluminium brackets for the mainsheet cam-cleat and a yuloh storage bracket; and later added transom mainsheet fairleads, a PVC cabin air scoop, above-deck masthead wiring, and pre-rigged dock lines for single-handing.[3][4][5]
- **The yuloh.** "Scout" is sculled in calms; Mulligan reports the yuloh built for him had its blade foil cut backwards, and describes re-working it — flipping the blade and relocating a sharper curve in the loom above the pivot so it self-orients on both strokes — to lift his sustained sculling speed from about 1.75 knots toward 2.[6]
- **Sailing on Lake Havasu.** A season of day-sails and overnight rendezvous at Steamboat Cove, and of tuning the boat, in winds up to 15–25 knots.[7]

## The essays

Several posts are stand-alone micro-cruising essays rather than boat reports: seven tips for sailing with a spouse or partner; a piece on GPS as a tool rather than a crutch and on keeping fundamental dead-reckoning skills; and "Siti-Toof 2 disease," on wanting ever-*smaller* boats — "two-foot-itis in reverse" — after a lifetime of moving up in size. These are cited as Mulligan's own outfitting and cruising opinion, not as design fact.[8]
