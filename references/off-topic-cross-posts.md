# Off-topic cross-posts (non-Paradox)

Several thin, off-topic items circulated on `paradoxbuilders` over the
years, none containing Paradox-specific design, construction, or sailing

## "The Aegre" (Caledonia Yawl website) — 1973 micro-voyage account

An external, non-Paradox-specific website
(`http://users.bigpond.net.au/caledonia_yawl/`) was cross-posted to
`paradoxbuilders`, `Smallboats`, and `Microcruising` alike as being of likely
interest to all three audiences.

The site recounts, in serialized installments, a **1973 voyage of roughly
10,000 miles** from the north of Scotland to the South Pacific aboard **"The
Aegre,"** a **21 ft Shetland foureen** — a small, open/flush-decked traditional
boat sailed by the site's author (23 at the time) and a partner, Julie (20).
The boat had:

- a **standing lug rig**,
- **no engine or electrics**, and
- a flush deck giving only about **4 ft of headroom below**,

undertaken in the pre-GPS, pre-email, pre-weatherfax era — an extreme
historical example of minimalist small-boat voyaging, relevant to the
microcruising/Paradox community's own small-boat, self-sufficient cruising
ethos (see [Texas 200](/operations/texas-200.md) and
[is Paradox a suitable first boat?](/operations/first-boat-suitability.md) for
Paradox's own comparatively modern equivalents). The site's separate material
about the "Caledonia Yawl" boat itself is also of interest, and offers an
email-notification signup for new installments.

## Bahamas beach-cruising charter cross-post

A commercial charter offering **8 days of beach cruising in the Bahamas**
aboard a **Sea Pearl 21' cat ketch sharpie** (an open boat, unrelated to
Paradox), for **$1,750 per person** (double occupancy, airfare extra), was
cross-posted from `http://www.exuma-bahamas.com/`.

The real point was a cost comparison: [Dave and Mindy
Bolduc](/people/bolduc/dave.md) likely spent **less overall** buying [Little
Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) outright and taking their **first
multi-month cruise** than a single charter guest would spend on this one
8-day trip — an offhand data point favoring owning a Layden-family
microcruiser over paying for a guided charter.

A cheaper (if still costly) alternative circulated in response: combined
one-way airfare (Georgetown outbound, Nassau–New York return) for the Sea
Pearl charter cost almost as much as the charter itself, but a Florida-based
outfit offered a **$1,000-for-6-days** trip flying guests from Nassau to the
Exuma Sea Park and touring **north from there by Klepper II folding kayak**,
rigged with **leeboards** but no outrigger.

This thread is tangential microcruising color — no Paradox-specific design,
construction, or sailing content — retained as minor reference color per this
concept's own precedent.

He had previously built only a "stitch and goo" plywood lapstrake pram from a

was judged "a good choice, with a fine pedigree," likely to "last a
lifetime" (short of a "raft of friends wanting to go sailing"), with a UK
boating-magazine article on record about a home-built Golant Gaffer sailing
around Europe.

The boat's designer, **Roger Dongray** — also designer of the **Cornish
Crabber** range — reported that a Golant Gaffer had cruised Europe's canal
system as far as Turkey, likely the voyage the magazine article covered; the
boat's interior space was praised as generous for its under-20 ft length.

### The magazine citations, and a Classic Boat vs. Watercraft aside

Two specific citations were tracked down, both in **Classic Boat** magazine:
the Europe-to-Turkey voyage article (Weymouth, southern England, via the
European inland waterways and the Black Sea to Istanbul) in the **September
2001 issue, number 159**; and an earlier review of the Golant Gaffer itself
in the **July 1998 issue, number 121** — the review that had first sparked
interest in the boat, originally received as a photocopy from Roger Dongray
when study plans were ordered by mail. Old Classic Boat back issues proved
hard to find locally, and Classic Boat's own website gave no contact e-mail
address for back-issue orders.

A recurring aside contrasts the two UK boating magazines: **Classic Boat**
was seen by some as having chased "the fat cat's wallet" (like WoodenBoat in
the US), with **Watercraft** magazine preferred instead for having more
articles to taste and for making more effort to serve the US subscriber
market (back issues and subscriptions serviceable from the US).

## "Tropicstar" — a friend's Star-class-hull power-sharpie conversion

A "Trailer pictures" thread (see [trailering](/operations/trailering.md))
drifted off-topic around a photo captioned only **"Trailer's for Tropic
Star, Little Cruiser and Paradox,"** showing a yellow sharpie alongside the
two Layden boats.

The boat, named **"Tropicstar,"** was described by [Dave
Bolduc](/people/bolduc/dave.md) — not a Paradox or any Layden design, but a
power conversion of an old **Star-class sailboat**, built by a friend of
his, **Steve**:

- Steve **cut off most of the keel**, leaving only about a foot for
  directional stability.
- A **cabin was added**, built from **3/4 in plywood** with **tempered-glass
  windows** — construction Dave judged made the boat **"a bit top heavy."**
- Power came from a **four-stroke 8 hp Honda outboard**.
- Steve had originally planned to take Tropicstar to the **Bahamas**, but
  ultimately judged she needed more sea trials than a short trip would give
  her; instead he spent **six weeks cruising the Florida Keys** in her, where
  she "worked very well" thanks to her shallow **1 ft draft**.
- Steve was reportedly considering **cutting the cabin off** and building a
  **lighter, longer** replacement to make her more seaworthy.
- As she stood, Tropicstar would make "a great river or canal boat"; with the
  planned cabin modification, she would also suit Bahamas cruising, provided
  the crew brought along a **2 hp kicker** (backup outboard).

This is a purely off-topic, non-Paradox boat description — included here only
because it was shared with and discussed on the `paradoxbuilders` list, in
the same thread and same photo as two Layden-design boats.

## Tracking down a mystery Bolger Birdwatcher builder near Saskatchewan

A "Re: Amelie" sub-thread drifted from Paradox design comparisons into a
purely off-topic search for the builder of an old, modified **Bolger
Birdwatcher** somewhere near **Saskatchewan**, Canada — prompted by
[Nels](/people/nels.md) mentioning his own **Bolger Micro** was built in
Saskatchewan, and a query on whether he was anywhere near **Radisson**,
Saskatchewan, trying to locate the builder of an old Birdwatcher in that
area.

An earlier, independent attempt to track the same builder down some eight
years prior had failed, without even the builder's name recalled, beyond a
sense that he had used some material other than plywood.

Construction photos of various Birdwatchers from a separate boat-design
group turned up one unusual example built with a **plywood hull but an
aluminum deck structure**, its sides raised and its cabin top lower than a
standard Birdwatcher's. An e-mail contact address beginning "aeneasprecht"
traced to a matching **Aeneas Precht** in the Saskatchewan phone book,
though only an answering machine answered the first call. Whether the
modified boat had ever appeared in a boating magazine remained an open
question, with a possible sighting recalled in a fiberglass-boatbuilding
book's sidebar, alongside curiosity about what had motivated the builder's
altered lines.

The same Precht had in fact been called once before as well — "not an easy
name to remember" — with a guess that the article in question ran in a
**Common Sense Newsletter**, published by **Benie Wolfard**.

Precht was eventually reached directly by phone: recently back from an
extended trip to China, he asked to be called again in two weeks once
resettled, promising to answer all questions then. The only further request
on record was that Precht be told another Saskatchewan resident was
interested in seeing his Birdwatcher sometime.

No further follow-up on the mystery Birdwatcher or Aeneas Precht is
recorded.

## Derek Clark's secondhand Wee Rob Roy canoe

A non-Paradox secondhand-boat question: whether to buy a used **Ian
Oughtred "Wee Rob Roy"** canoe — 12'6" long, 28 in beam, 11¼ in depth, 30 lb
— whose owner claimed a **180 lb displacement** while himself weighing 182
lb with "plenty of freeboard" left. The displacement figure as stated drew
a correction: displacement means the full weight a hull can support at its
design waterline (boat plus contents), and 180 lb sounded too low for a
canoe of that size unless it were far smaller than described.

A design very similar in size and weight was estimated to comfortably carry
**220 lb** (boat plus load) while drawing only 4 in, with a 9 in midship
depth and needing an additional 85 lb to sink her a further inch — implying
the Wee Rob Roy in question could likely handle over 200 lb safely, with
reduced freeboard and more careful handling in rougher water as the
trade-off; adding a spray cover would let such a canoe double as a small
kayak. [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md#the-14-ft-rob-roy-based-canoe-and-the-2005-everglades-challenge)
is on record as loving the Rob Roy design, which was the direct inspiration
for the canoes he used in his last two Everglades Challenge entries.

Portability was the deciding factor weighed against a two-person canoe
already on hand, which needs two people to lift on and off a car (harder
still with a bad back), against the Wee Rob Roy's one-handed portability;
the same seller also offered a version with a side outrigger ("ama") for
extra fishing stability. Fitting a pair of buoyancy bags fore and aft was
confirmed as a straightforward way to add a further safety margin to a
canoe of this kind — half in earnest and half in jest, a compressed-air
cylinder from a paintball gun, with an aerator on the end of the hose, was
floated as a way to supply a "private bubbling spa" if she were ever
swamped.

This is a purely off-topic, non-Paradox secondhand-boat item, included here
only because it was discussed on `paradoxbuilders`.

## A secondhand cat-ketch cruiser spotted on eBay, and a transatlantic price gripe

A non-Paradox secondhand-boat item: a UK-based list member flagged a
US eBay listing for a **cat-ketch centerboard cruiser** as a good basis
for a microcruiser, using it to note a recurring transatlantic price gap —
comparable secondhand boats routinely cost roughly twice as much in the UK
as in the United States. A specific want was floated alongside it: a
**Freedom 35 cat-ketch, centerboard version,** ready to sail for about
$5,000.

a similarly styled **Nordic-design sloop**, admired from advertisements,
proved on inspection at a boat show to have a cabin so cramped — room for
only two berths — that a visitor "almost had to lay down to get inside,"
making a rainy afternoon confined below a discouraging prospect.

## Canada Day, and an Alberta road trip

A one-day, purely off-topic **Canada Day** greeting to the list's Canadian
members drew a reply describing a recent westward road trip through
Alberta: Crowsnest Pass and up Highway 22, an overnight stop at Longview
(with a purchase at a shop associated with musician Ian Tyson), on to
Calgary and Banff, then two days photographing the Drumheller badlands —
plentiful wildlife along the way (horses, antelope, bison, mule deer, elk,

timed just ahead of flooding that later affected the region. The reply
drew a closing exchange placing a second correspondent just outside Olds,
Alberta — not, in his own description, "a sailing mecca," though the
province has some sailing clubs on lakes near Banff and Calgary that
prefer members to own a "yacht"; he favors home-built designs of the
Jim-Michalak type instead, and toasted the day with a Labatt Blue in a
longneck bottle.
