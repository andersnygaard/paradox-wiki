# Eerik Mee's blog (paradoxbuild.blogspot.com)

**Eerik Mee's blog** (`https://paradoxbuild.blogspot.com/`, byline "Eerik") is a Blogger build log of one boat: **"[THU](/boats/thu.md),"** a [Paradox](/paradox.md) built **20% larger than the plans** and rigged as a **Chinese junk**, built from 2015 and launched in 2017. It is an **authored, first-person primary source**, and it is the only one of its kind in the archive on two counts at once: the only first-person account of a **scaled-up Paradox actually built and sailed**, and the only account of a **purpose-designed cambered junk rig** on the design — as distinct from the stock lug *converted* to junk on [Planktos](/boats/planktos.md), the only other built-and-sailed junk-rig Paradox on record.

That makes it disproportionately valuable, because it puts a completed boat under two of the archive's longest-running "argued but never built" threads — the [larger Paradox](/design/larger-paradox.md), discussed on the mailing list roughly forty times without a completed example, and the [junk rig](/design/ideas/junk-rig.md), argued over for years in the abstract.

## What the blog is worth as evidence

Its value is that it is **specific and contemporaneous about departures the archive had only theorised**. The scale-up is stated exactly — "I multiplied just all measurements with 1.2 … LOA turned out about 5.05 m and width about 1.5 m"[7] — landing almost exactly on the worked 20% figures the mailing list had carried since 2008 (5.04 m / 1.48 m; see [larger Paradox](/design/larger-paradox.md#a-worked-20-scale-up-dimensions-sail-area-and-displacement)). The rig is worked through in build detail: a six-panel **cambered junk sail** built to **Arne Kverneland**'s Junk Rig Association method, on a **6 m mast moved forward of bulkhead 2**.[2] And the boat was **launched and sailed**, so the blog records what the theory could not: that it **tacks without tricks**,[5] where the balance and sheeting fears in the list-era junk-rig discussion went untested.

Its limits should be read with that. It is one builder's account of one heavily modified boat, so its figures describe **THU**, not the stock Paradox — sail area, displacement, ballast, and helm balance are all a scaled-up boat's. The sailing record is thin: the boat was **day-sailed on an inland Estonian lake** a handful of times (four in 2017, more in 2019), never cruised, so the blog documents that the boat works but not how it holds up over distance.[5][6] And the rig was still being tuned when the record ends — boom twist and sheeting were unresolved, and windward pointing is explicitly left unconcluded.[5]

## Provenance and dating

The blog was live and public at retrieval. It is a **Blogger site built from static "pages"** rather than dated posts, so its narrative runs across six pages — *Building the hull*, *Mast and sail*, *From decking till painting*, *Finishing the hull and hoisting sail*, *The launch*, and *Recent progress* — each internally dated, together spanning **July 2015 → August 2019**.

Retrieved and mirrored for this bundle on **2024-10-31**, held under `source-web/eerik-mee/` as **six page PDFs**, one per blog page, each preserving the page text, its inline photographs, and its reader-comment thread.[1] The comment threads are part of the source: several carry the builder's own answers on the scale-up, the rig, the mast position, and the ballast.

## The build, in outline

- **The hull (2015–16).** First cut July 2015; hull, chine runners, rudder (with cast-lead ballast in the blade), and the 6 m birdmouth mast built through to mid-2016 — everything scaled ×1.2, with copper-powder/epoxy antifouling.[1]
- **The rig (2015–16).** A hollow birdmouth mast and a scratch-sewn **cambered junk sail** — six batten panels, 8% camber, built to Arne Kverneland's method with help from the **Junk Rig Association**, on Odyssey sailcloth with aluminium battens and boom.[2]
- **Decking to paint (2016).** Deck, cabin trunk, ventilation trunk with the mast partners forward of bulkhead 2, rudder hung on doubled pintles for the heavier blade, built-in bow fairlead — primed but unlaunched at the end of the 2016 season.[3]
- **Finishing and first hoist (2016–17).** Laminated-glass and car-windshield windows, cabin sole and hatches, a fold-down seat, insulation, and the first **sail hoist in the yard** in June 2017.[4]
- **The launch (5 July 2017).** Trailered to a lake, mast raised, **christened "THU"**; sailed on first trials — **tacking without tricks** — and out about four times before the season ended.[5]
- **Recent progress (2018–19).** Ballast cast as twelve lead pigs, a full electrical system (solar, LiFePO₄ battery, inverter, depth sounder, LED lighting) and an **electric outboard** fitted, and more lake sailing in 2019, with rig tuning and lee-helm correction ongoing.[6]

## Why the departures matter to the archive

Two threads in the bundle turned on the absence of a built example, and this blog supplies one for each:

- **The scale-up.** [Larger Paradox](/design/larger-paradox.md) records Matt Layden's in-principle sanction of a 20% enlargement and a worked set of dimensions, but notes no one had built one and reported back. THU is that build, at essentially those dimensions.[7]
- **The junk rig.** [Junk rig](/design/ideas/junk-rig.md) collects a decade of list argument — mast balance, sheeting a sail that overhangs the transom, the need for **built-in camber** rather than flat panels — none of it tested on the water there. THU tests it: the mast was moved forward, the sail was given camber, and the boat tacks and sails, though sheeting and boom twist remained work in progress.[2][5]

The full extraction of the boat is at [THU](/boats/thu.md), and of the builder at [Eerik Mee](/people/eerik-mee.md).
