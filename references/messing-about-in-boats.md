# Messing About In Boats (MAIB) magazine

**Messing About In Boats** (MAIB) is a long-running American small-boat magazine, published roughly semimonthly since the early 1980s and edited by **Bob Hicks**. It is the **print venue in which Paradox and Matt Layden's wider family of micro-cruisers were first, and most continuously, documented** — from a 1991 reader letter through to Cedar Key photo-essays of the 2010s. Where the [paradoxbuilders mailing list](/references/mailing-list.md) is a many-voiced email archive beginning in 2001, and [microcruising.com](/references/microcruising-website.md) is the Bolducs' authored website, MAIB is the contemporaneous published record: the magazine that carried the boat to a national small-boat readership years before the mailing list existed, and the original home of the ["Building Paradox" articles](/references/building-the-paradox-articles.md) whose full construction content is set out at [the Building Paradox series](/references/building-paradox-series.md).

MAIB's coverage matters for three reasons. It **predates the mailing-list archive by a decade**, preserving the earliest public account of Matt Layden and his boats. It is where the **Paradox design particulars and plans-ordering information first appeared in print** (July 1995, and again alongside the 1996–97 series). And it is an **independently edited periodical** — its feature articles, letters columns, and reader reactions record praise, correction, and controversy that a designer's or seller's own site does not, including a technical rebuttal of the "Building Paradox" series by an epoxy manufacturer's engineer.

## Provenance and citing

**MAIB is a print-only magazine with no public online archive or per-issue URL.** A set of **54 issues** relevant to Paradox/Layden, spanning roughly **1991 (vol. 9) to 2025 (vol. 43)**, underlies the coverage history and citations below. Each issue's own printed masthead — not any filename or digital metadata — is the authority for its date, so **every citation below is dated from the issue's own masthead**. Quoted figures were checked against the page images where they mattered, and uncertain readings are flagged where they occur on the detail pages.

Because there is no URL to resolve to, an issue's own locator — volume, number, cover date, article title, author, and page number — is the complete, citable identifier. Issues are cited in the form:

> [Messing About In Boats](/references/messing-about-in-boats.md), vol. X no. Y ([date]), "[Article title]," [author] (p. N) — [detail].

# Paradox and Matt Layden in MAIB — a coverage history

## Before Paradox: Matt Layden and the Bolducs' Little Cruiser (1991–1995)

Matt Layden reached MAIB's readers years before Paradox existed, chiefly through the cruising of **Dave and Mindy Bolduc** in their Layden-built [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md). The earliest reference on record is a **1991** reader letter proposing a "micro cruiser rally," which cites Layden's having sailed his 15-foot micro-cruiser from Connecticut to the Bahamas and points back to a still earlier MAIB item on Layden from February 1991.[1] Layden had in fact turned up on MAIB's pages earlier still, as an unnamed "Flying Dutchman" — a photographer met his tiny cruising sailboat on Cape Cod's Pleasant Bay one late fall while Layden was cruising New England and heading home to Connecticut for winter.[3]

The Bolducs' **June 1993 cover feature** told the full story of cruising Little Cruiser from Florida across the Gulf Stream to the Bahamas and back — a 15-foot lug-rigged sharpie, 4½ ft wide, 9-inch draft, water-ballasted with a rain-fed catchment, sailed and handled entirely from below with no cockpit, "heavily built of plywood."[2] The article drew more reader mail than anything else MAIB ran that year, and a **September 1993** editorial follow-up gathered what little was then known of the "mystery man" designer: that he had built "a half-dozen or so" 13–16 ft minimal cruisers that had voyaged to the Bahamas, that he "designs on old envelopes," was then building a boat in Maine, and had entered a design in *Cruising World*'s 1982 Design Competition, published there in July 1983.[4] Further Little Cruiser cruise reports and Layden-plan inquiries followed through **1993–1995**,[5][6][7] including a March 1995 memoir noting that Don Musante owned another Layden boat.[7]

## The design's debut and building data (July 1995)

Paradox itself was introduced to MAIB's readers in the **July 15, 1995 issue**, whose cover showed Matt Layden sailing Paradox in the Bahamas and whose Bolduc feature "Cheating Winter" described a Key Largo-to-Bimini cruise with Layden's new micro-cruiser.[8] That same issue reproduced a **Paradox plans-and-specifications sheet** — a "14 ft / 4.2 m Coastal Cruiser" drawing dated 7/94 — the design's first appearance of build data and plans-ordering information in print.[8] The full design-particulars box (LOA 4.2 m, sail area 100 ft², displacement 1,410 lb, and the rest) was reprinted with the construction series and is set out in full at [the Building Paradox series](/references/building-paradox-series.md#the-published-design-particulars).

## Don Elliott's "Building Paradox" construction series (1996–1997)

The magazine's centerpiece Paradox content is **Don Elliott's "Building Paradox," a sixteen-installment construction series** running from **vol. 14 no. 9 (September 15, 1996)** through **vol. 14 no. 24 (1997)** — Parts 1 through 15 followed by a "Conclusion," documenting the ground-up build of Elliott's own Paradox. It was trailed a fortnight ahead in the vol. 14 no. 8 "In Our Next Issue" box (which misspelled both principals as "Don Eliott" and "Matt Leyden").[9] The series' full part-by-part construction content, the published design-particulars box, and its reception are captured at [**the Building Paradox series**](/references/building-paradox-series.md); it is the same body of articles later sold direct by Elliott as a booklet, covered as a distributable product at [Building the Paradox articles](/references/building-the-paradox-articles.md).

## Reception and the epoxy-accuracy controversy (1997–1998)

Being an independently edited periodical, MAIB also recorded the series' mixed reception. Reader letters ranged from "the Paradox series [was] helpful" to a complaint that "Building Paradox" was "the most boring thing ever published."[10][11] More consequentially, **James R. Watson, a technical advisor at Gougeon Brothers (WEST SYSTEM epoxy)**, published a detailed correction of the series' epoxy technique and trademark usage — that WEST SYSTEM and Cab-O-Sil were being cited incorrectly, that vinegar is unsafe for cleaning epoxy from skin (it drives epoxy into the skin and promotes allergic sensitization), and that the "spherical filleting" tool the series claimed as an original invention had been sold commercially for some fifty years — while allowing the articles had "much merit."[full text and citation at the [series page](/references/building-paradox-series.md#reception-and-the-epoxy-accuracy-controversy)] Readers took sides in the Commentary column, Elliott answered with an "In Defense of 'Building Paradox'" sidebar, and echoes of the debate (chiefly over fillet "overkill") ran on into 1998.[12] The exchange prefigures Elliott's later mailing-list disputes over plans-fidelity and his own credentials — see [Don Elliott](/people/don-elliott.md).

## Later mentions: booklet ads, sea trials, and Cedar Key (2001–2007)

After the series, MAIB kept a standing classified for Elliott's "Building Paradox" article booklet (running at least 2001–2003)[13] and carried his later, unrelated "Capsize, a Study of an Adventure" series (2001), which drew on his own Paradox — its 20-gallon water tank and rain catchment, and Dave Bolduc's tip to replace water ballast with stones if the rain ran out.[14] A Layden **GJAC** was advertised for sale by Don Musante of Groton, Connecticut in June 2001.[15]

In **August 2003** MAIB published **Glen Maxwell's first-person "Paradox Sea Trials,"** an account of the December 2001 shakedown of his Paradox "[Zoë](/boats/zoe.md)" from Cedar Key to Honeymoon Island, Florida — crediting the design to Matt Layden and noting the chine runners' roll-damping effect.[16] From **2004 onward** the annual **Cedar Key** small-boat gathering became a recurring venue for Layden sightings in MAIB's event reports: attending and "sharing ideas late into the night" (2004);[17] sailing a new open-topped, chine-log minimalist design not yet fitted with a cabin, alongside Maxwell's "Zoe" (2005);[18] approving a friend's sailing-kayak hull and showing "his latest single paddle, trim tab steered, sailing kayak" (2006);[19] and hosting the cookout at which he introduced guest speaker **Sven Yrvind** (2007).[21] The same period's **2007 Everglades Challenge** report describes Layden — WaterTribe name "Wizard" — racing his 8-foot [Sand Flea](/matt-layden/sand-flea.md) solo, pausing to rescue a capsized kayaker, and finishing less than half a day behind the author's crewed boat.[20]

## A British Paradox in a DCA cruise account (July 2009)

The **July 2009** issue (Vol. 27 No. 3) carried "With Great Britain's Dinghy Cruisers: Faith's Cruise," an abridged first-person account of a roughly **500-sea-mile** cruise from the River Crouch to the Isles of Scilly and back to Plymouth — sailed initially solo in the Paradox "[Faith](/boats/faith.md)" and, for its final Scillonian leg, in company with the Paradox "[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)."[29] It is the archive's clearest instance of MAIB carrying **British** Paradox cruising, routed through the Dinghy Cruising Association rather than the American small-boat scene, and it closes on the practical business of recovery: hauling out at Plymouth, then "unstepping the mast, emptying the water ballast, and making her ready for the road" in forty-five minutes.[29]

An editorial note attached to the article names the second skipper in full — one of the print attestations of a surname the mailing-list archive never records, though it spells the forename "Alistair"; see [Alastair Law](/people/alastair-law.md) for the naming evidence, and ["Sailing The Paradox" — DCA Bulletin 193](/references/dca-sailing-the-paradox.md) for the other.[29]

## The Layden lug and chine runner in others' designs (2011–2013)

By the 2010s Layden's ideas circulated in MAIB as adopted techniques. A **2011** feature credited him as the originator of the "chine runners" its author adapted to an unrelated barge-hulled design,[22] and a **2013** article referred to a furling balanced "Layden Lug main," crediting Matt Layden and tying the rig to his Paradox.[23] A July 2013 Cedar Key photo-essay caption noted "Matt Layden sailing his original Paradox design."[24]

## Modern coverage (2014–2017)

Later issues carry scattered but genuine Paradox content: a 2014 note of a "Matt Layden Paradox Scout" being built for a boating festival;[25] a recurring columnist, **Michael Beebe**, who built two Paradoxes and repeatedly referenced Layden's lug rig across his 2016 "Meanderings" columns;[26] a reviewed cruising book listing "Matt Layden's Paradox" among notable dinghies;[27] and a 2017 Cedar Key essay noting "Matt Layden came calling in a fine little kayak he designed."[28]

## Don Elliott's other MAIB series (disambiguation)

Don Elliott was a recurring MAIB author, and two of his other series are **not** about Paradox and should not be confused with it: **"Capsize, a Study of an Adventure"** (2001, which does reference his own Paradox in passing) and **"Building Sleeper"** (2004, a Van Loan design). Only "Building Paradox" (and the Paradox references within "Capsize") concern the Layden design.
