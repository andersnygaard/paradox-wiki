# "Sailing The Paradox" — DCA Bulletin 193

A signed article, **"Sailing The Paradox,"** subtitled *"The owner's experiences of sailing Little Jim,"* appeared across pages 48–49 of ***Bulletin* 193** of the **Dinghy Cruising Association**, written by the builder of the UK Paradox "[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)."[1] It is a deliberate, structured appraisal rather than a cruising narrative: after a short preamble the piece divides into two lists — **fifteen advantages and eleven disadvantages** — offered "in no particular order," with the stated aim of being "completely impartial" and of helping "anyone considering building one of these remarkable boats."[1]

This makes it a distinct kind of source from the rest of the record. The [mailing-list archive](/references/mailing-list.md) captures the same builder's views as they formed, scattered across hundreds of messages answering other people's questions; the [AYRS talk](/references/ayrs-meeting-video.md) is a retrospective delivered to a research society seventeen years on. This article sits between them — a **considered, edited, published summary** compiled while the boat was still comparatively new, and the only place the design's drawbacks are set out as a deliberate itemized list by someone who had built and cruised one.[1]

## Provenance and dating

The source is a two-page PDF. Both pages carry the running foot **"DCA Bulletin 193 : 48"** and **": 49"**.

The *Bulletin* issue is **not dated within the article's own two pages**, and no cover or masthead is available to date it directly. The article's own opening — "three seasons" of sailing — dates the writing to roughly **three seasons after the April 2003 launch**, so most likely **2005 or 2006**; this is an inference from internal evidence, not a stated date, and is recorded as such.[1] An editorial footnote refers the reader to "Mike Knott's letter p.12" in the same issue, which lies outside this article's two pages.[1]

The issue has since been dated more firmly from **inside the same issue**. A separate article at pp. 45–47 describes the **Beale Park Show 2006**, records plans ordered by airmail as arriving **in October 2006**, and carries an editorial note dating its photographs to October 2006.[3] *Bulletin* 193 therefore cannot predate **late 2006**, and most likely appeared in the winter of **2006–07** — later than the "three seasons" inference alone suggested, and resting on stated dates rather than on a count of seasons. See ["Paradox" — DCA Bulletin 193](/references/dca-paradox-gilchrist.md).

The byline prints the forename as **"Alistair."** The archive treats this as a publication-side variant: the same sender's own mail account carries the display name "Alastair" across more than three hundred messages, and every archive message signs off "Al, Yeovil, England." The companion article two pages earlier settles the point from the print side, spelling it **"Alastair"** throughout and giving the full name in an editorial photo credit — so a **single issue sets the name both ways**, which is direct evidence of a typesetting variant rather than a competing spelling.[3] The surname **Law** is confirmed here in print, independently of the AYRS talk and of *[Messing About in Boats](/references/messing-about-in-boats.md)*; see [Alastair Law](/people/alastair-law.md) for the full naming evidence.

## The reproduced sail plan

Page 49 carries, below the article text, an editorially added reproduction of **Matt Layden's Paradox sail plan** — hand-lettered "SAIL PLAN / PARADOX / 4.8m COASTAL CRUISER / MATT LAYDEN · 7/94 · DWG #1," scale 1:20.[2] The editor's note sources it to `http://home.triad.rr.com/lcruise/plans1.htm` and marks it as included "for reference," so it is **not** part of the owner's own submission.[2] The specification block survives despite the scan quality: it is low-resolution and hand-lettered, so several imperial figures are best-effort reads and the metric values are the more reliable of the two columns.[2] The one figure that matters most — **640 kg / 1400 lb displacement** — is legible and agrees with the article text.[1][2]

Note the drawing's own "4.8m" label against an LOA the archive elsewhere records as 4.2 m; the sail-plan sheet is a **1994 drawing**, and this discrepancy is inherited from the source drawing rather than introduced by the *Bulletin*. It is flagged here rather than silently reconciled.
