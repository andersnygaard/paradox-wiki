# Materials sourcing links

This page consolidates the external-link-only materials-sourcing references for Paradox builders: epoxy/fiberglass sourcing and the Kevlar cost debate, plexiglass/Lexan glazing sources, general (non-Paradox-specific) boatbuilding community links, and West System Epoxy website pointers.

# Online metal ordering (onlinemetals.com)

**`onlinemetals.com`**, a Seattle-based online metal supplier specializing in small quantities, is a sourcing option for any builder needing metal stock (chine runner shoes, fittings, etc.) in less than mill-order quantities.

# Plywood, lumber, epoxy, and hardware sourcing (M.L. Condon, Harbor Sales, South Jersey Lumberman's, Hamilton Marine)

A broader supplier list circulated in response to a New Jersey-based builder's materials questions while his plans order was still pending — a reminder that compiling a bill of materials is a sensible way to use the waiting time. **M.L. Condon** (White Plains, NY, near the Hudson) carries, by a wide margin, the best plywood available in North America, if the budget allows; **Harbor Sales** (Maryland) is a comparable high-quality alternative; and, depending on which part of New Jersey a builder is in, either M.L. Condon or **South Jersey Lumberman's** can supply suitable stock, with Douglas fir serving for most of the structural lumber. **FGCI** and, for UK-based builders, **MAS** were both named as competitively priced epoxy sources. For hardware and fittings not made of wood, **Hamilton Marine** (Searsport, ME) is a further named source.[29][30][31]

# Marine plywood sourcing in the Los Angeles area

For a Los Angeles-area builder, **Allied Plywood/Veneer** (`alliedveneer.com/marineplywood`) is a recommended top marine-plywood source.[16] Marine ply runs expensive there in practice, even for the supplier's best-grade stock bought in quantity.[17]

# Epoxy and fiberglass sourcing (raka.com), and the Kevlar cost debate

**raka.com** supplies epoxies, fiberglass cloths and tapes, fillers, and pigments, and is a materials-sourcing option for Paradox builders.

## Kevlar vs. fiberglass: a cost comparison

Raka.com's prices support a cost comparison that stands as one more reason not to use Kevlar on a Paradox (relevant to any builder considering a Kevlar-glass bottom — see [hull planking — the stitch-and-glue debate](/construction/hull-planking.md)):

- 60 in wide, 5 oz **Kevlar** costs several times as much per yard as equivalent-weight fiberglass cloth from the same supplier
- 60 in wide, 6 oz **fiberglass cloth** is the far cheaper alternative
- A biaxial-weave Kevlar product costs even more per yard than plain-weave Kevlar

By this figuring, even **triple-sheathing** a bottom in fiberglass still costs meaningfully less than a single Kevlar layup — and since a Paradox needs only about **5 yards** of material either way, the absolute Kevlar premium is modest in real terms even if steep in percentage terms. Nylon peel ply (smoothed over a final epoxy coat for a fine finish, then peeled off once cured) is also available from the same supplier in 60 in width.

A practical, cost-driven counterpoint to buying Kevlar at retail: a Paradox bottom needs only about **5 sq metres** of material, well within reach of scrounging rather than buying at full retail price. A yard building high-tech racing boats is a plausible source of **offcuts**, obtainable informally and off the books from someone with a bit of spare Kevlar to sell, since such yards often have leftover roll-ends going to waste — pram dinghies have been built entirely from tear-offs discarded into scrap bins during professional mould-shop layups, material that would otherwise be thrown out or sold cheaply to whoever asked at the door. Scrounging materials this way is part of the appeal of home boatbuilding for cost-conscious builders, and the same opportunity plausibly exists in the US as in the UK. This doesn't change the underlying Kevlar-handling difficulty (hard to wet out, cut, and sand) flagged elsewhere in the archive — see [a Kevlar-handling caution](/people/david-leblanc.md).

A separate, unresolved sourcing question concerns **braided carbon-fiber sleeve** suitable for wetting out over a birdmouth wood spar mandrel: an aerospace/aircraft-industry source would be preferable to the orthopedic-supply source already identified — see [mast — a birdmouth carbon-fiber composite spar](/components/rig/mast.md#a-birdmouth-carbon-fiber-composite-spar-as-an-alternative-to-the-plans-rig) for the full technique and citation.

# Inexpensive plexiglass and Lexan for glazing

Paradox's steering-station glazing is valued for the **good lighting and visibility** it gives the helmsman, while itself using only a **minimal amount** of plexiglass or Lexan compared with some other designs — retail plexiglass or Lexan from a chandlery like West Marine or BoatUS runs expensive in quantity, so cheaper glazing sources are worth knowing. The general approach: check the yellow pages for local **plastics dealers** and ask whether they have any **drops or damaged sheets**, which usually carry a good discount and still leave plenty of usable material for a small-boat project. A US supplier serving the central-East-Coast area, **`harborsales.net`**, is one named source.

Beyond the boat itself, Paradox's design **ingenuity** — its unapologetic single-mindedness in serving its intended purpose, without regard for convention or convention-minded criticism — has drawn interest from builders of other small craft; its **inside steering station** in particular has been borrowed as an idea for a small catamaran design, on the reasoning that an enclosed helm makes just as much sense on a multihull, where higher speed brings stronger apparent wind and denser spray, as it does on Paradox.

Further sourcing angles for cheap glazing, drawn from outside the US:

- In the **UK**, a local plastics firm often has **offcuts** left over from other jobs; sizing parts to fit available offcuts earns a further discount off the standard per-square-metre price. Sending a spouse or child in to ask tends to get **better discounts, and sometimes free material** — one scrap dealer who held out for a higher price on a spare aluminum cooking pot (eyed as an anchor-warp bucket) gave the same pot away free a few days later.
- A local **glass supplier** (as opposed to a plastics dealer) is another source of scrap offcuts, and even full sheets bought there are priced well below a marine chandlery.
- Six pieces of **1/4 in scratch-resistant Marguard Lexan**, bought from an eBay seller ("Plasticsfolks", 100%-rated over 525 transactions at the time), reglazed all of Little Cruiser's windows in one order, with shipping combined across the pieces; the sheets arrived slightly larger than advertised and in good condition. Online offcut sellers' pieces are usually genuine offcuts, but added shipping cost is the one real downside — worth comparing a local source against an online one before ordering.

# General boatbuilding resource links (cedar-strip site, newsgroup, forum)

Three general small-boat-building resources, unconnected to Paradox specifically, are worth knowing to any builder:

- **A cedar-strip boatbuilding site**, `rutuonline.com` — a favorite reference on cedar-strip construction, with posting activity also on the **rec.boats.building** newsgroup.
- **rec.boats.building** — a Usenet newsgroup, accessible via Google Groups.
- **The WoodenBoat Forum** — an online discussion board, at the time hosted via a Hypernet/UBB Ultimate.cgi bulletin-board URL.

These are general amateur-boatbuilding community pointers rather than Paradox-specific content — see also [boat-building articles](/references/dave-carnell-articles.md).

More generally, beyond the links above, on the order of a hundred other discussion groups cover boatbuilding in various materials. For a prospective builder choosing among them, buying plans matched to a preferred building method and boat type gives by far the best chance of success, rather than modifying an existing design to fit a different material or method — sticking with a design as sold keeps the designer's own support available if problems come up during the build, a resource lost the moment a builder starts modifying.

# Peel ply: what it is, and US availability

**Peel ply** is a fabric that epoxy resin will not bond to. Stretched over the top of a fresh fiberglass layup while the resin cures, it compacts the layup and leaves a smooth finished surface once cured; once the resin has fully cured, the peel-ply fabric itself is simply peeled off, taking its name from the technique — leaving the smooth surface underneath it needs no further sanding to accept the next coat. It must be rolled out with a small roller as it is stretched over the layup. Nylon peel ply is available in the US (see [Kevlar vs. fiberglass: a cost comparison](#kevlar-vs-fiberglass-a-cost-comparison) above for its price alongside fiberglass and Kevlar), though buying fabric sold specifically as "peel ply" carries a premium.[18][19][20][21]

Peel ply is essentially **Dacron fabric**, and a cheaper source than a marine chandlery is a home-built-airplane supplier — Wicks Aircraft or Aircraft Spruce & Specialty, among others — buying the lightest-weight covering cloth they stock, generally a sailplane-covering grade. Dacron dress-lining material from an ordinary fabric supply store has also given good results for the same purpose. The reason either works is the same reason peel ply works at all: nothing bonds to Dacron, not even paint.[21]

A cheap retail source is a local small boat builder willing to sell a roll on as a favor rather than at full mail-order markup.[22] Peel ply is not recommended for use directly over polyester gelcoat, since it would still leave a visible imprint of its own weave on the finished outside surface; what it actually does is compact the bulk of resin and cloth left after heavy rolling, giving a smooth outside finish with only a faint imprint of the cloth itself.[23][24]

Peel ply is **not reusable**: the cured epoxy residue left on its surface sticks to the fabric, and its very fine weave — the same property that stops resin soaking through during a layup — stops it being cleaned off and used again. One further practical benefit beyond the smooth finish itself: once the peel ply is removed, sanding does not clog the grit paper with blush residue the way it otherwise would, an advantage largely eliminated by using an epoxy with little or no amine blush in the first place.[25][26]

# West System Epoxy resources (moisture barriers and a sharpie-building article)

The **West System Epoxy** website (`www.westsystem.com`) serves Paradox builders for two distinct purposes.

## Moisture-barrier coatings and penetrating epoxy

For **moisture-barrier coatings** and **penetrating epoxy** (relevant to the refit thread — see [checking a used Paradox for rot](/construction/rot-inspection-ice-pick.md)), **West Marine** is a retail source and the **West System Epoxy** website an informational one, though the latter is not always fully up to date as an informational source.

## "Building a Wood/Epoxy Sharpie" article

A construction article on the same site is reached from the West System homepage by clicking **"Epoxyworks Magazine"**, then **"Epoxyworks Back Issues"**, then **"Epoxyworks Number 15, Spring 2000"** — the article is called **"Building a Wood/Epoxy Sharpie."** The construction shown **is not exactly like a Paradox**, but it may still hold useful information for Paradox builders. The same magazine issue covers the **largest wood/epoxy ship ever built**: a **213 ft, three-masted barque** — of interest to anyone wanting a "more ambitious project."

# Tools for swaging Nicopress sleeves onto stainless-steel wire

For a builder wanting to secure Nicopress compression sleeves onto stainless-steel wire, the tool options found on the market range widely in price and mechanism: a Bosun's Supply swaging tool resembling bolt cutters (an inexpensive copy of the genuine Nicopress No. 1 tool); a "Swage-It" tool resembling a walnut cracker, tightened with a wrench; the genuine Nicopress No. 1 swaging tool, also bolt-cutter-shaped; a Nicopress bench-mounted hydraulic tool; and bench-mounted manual Nicopress No. 510 or No. 300 tools.[27] Stainless-steel wire rigging has little place on a stock Paradox, which is built and rigged with rope rather than wire.[28]
