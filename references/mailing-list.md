# Paradox builders mailing list

The primary community for Paradox builders and sailors, and the source archive
for this bundle.

# History

| Era | Group | Notes |
|-----|-------|-------|
| 2001– | `paradoxbuilders` (Yahoo Groups) | Founded 7 July 2001 by [Glen C. Maxwell](/people/glen-maxwell.md) to exchange building and sailing information. |
| later | `paradoxbuilders2` (Yahoo Groups) | Second Yahoo group, first proposed in March 2004 as a dedicated file-upload group once the original group's photo storage ran short — see [Glen Maxwell — a second group considered, and the first mention of a dedicated website](/people/glen-maxwell/list-founder-and-moderator.md#a-second-group-considered-and-the-first-mention-of-a-dedicated-website) for the proposal and Glen's initial response (archiving old files, building a separate website); actually founded on 10 May 2004 by [David C. Smith](/people/david-c-smith.md), acting moderator during Glen Maxwell's Atlantic crossing, once the storage limit was reached in earnest — see [the photo-storage limit, and the founding of paradoxbuilders2](#the-photo-storage-limit-and-the-founding-of-paradoxbuilders2) below. |
| 2019– | `paradoxsailboat` (groups.io) | Created 5 Nov 2019 by the list owner **"Mattsplatt"** (signing "Matt") to replace both Yahoo groups; content migrated over. |

The 2019 groups.io owner posted under the screen name **"Mattsplatt"** and
signed "Matt". This is **plausibly, but not confirmed, to be designer
[Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)** — the identity is pending
corroborating evidence from the archive and is not stated here as established
fact.

The group's **Files area** is organised into folders — e.g. **construction**,
**stove**, and **Study Plans** — hosting construction photos and a
guide/description of the study plans (uploaded early on). The dedicated "stove"
folder points to a cabin stove being a fitted item on these boats. See
[Study Plans](/references/study-plans.md).

# Sibling and successor groups

Beyond the `paradoxbuilders`/`paradoxsailboat` lineage itself, two related but
separate Yahoo groups came up as cross-references for Paradox builders:

- **[Micro Cruising](/references/micro-cruising-group.md)** — a broader
  micro-cruising discussion group started by
  [Dave Bolduc](/people/bolduc/dave.md) around September 2001, which
  Paradox builders were repeatedly encouraged to also join; still an active
  cross-reference as late as April 2002.
- **Smallboats** (`http://groups.yahoo.com/group/Smallboats`) — a separate
  Yahoo group [Don Elliott](/people/don-elliott.md) redirected Paradox
  questions to after resigning from `paradoxbuilders` in October 2001,
  stating he would "not belong to any group that promotes unsafe
  practices." See
  [plywood grade and foam flotation debate](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md)
  for the exchange that preceded this. Smallboats' scope, membership, and
  activity are not otherwise documented in this archive.

# The photo-storage limit, and the founding of paradoxbuilders2

`paradoxbuilders`' Yahoo Groups **photo storage limit** — first flagged as a
display-resolution problem in March 2004 (see [Glen Maxwell — a second group
considered](/people/glen-maxwell/list-founder-and-moderator.md#a-second-group-considered-and-the-first-mention-of-a-dedicated-website))
— was reached in earnest the following month, while founder
[Glen C. Maxwell](/people/glen-maxwell.md) was away on his Atlantic crossing
aboard "Hogfish Maximus" (see
[Matt Layden eyewitness — an Atlantic crossing](/people/glen-maxwell/matt-layden-eyewitness.md#an-atlantic-crossing-aboard-hogfish-maximus)).
New "Whisper" photos uploaded during this window triggered a recurring group
error that locked the uploader out of the photo section entirely, a problem
independently confirmed by another member the same day.

**[David C. Smith](/people/david-c-smith.md)**, filling the administrative gap in Glen's absence,
diagnosed the underlying cause: the group had hit its Yahoo-imposed storage
ceiling outright, rather than just a display-resolution limit as in March. As
an immediate step Smith deleted his own large-format photos from the "Swash"
album, then founded a genuinely new group, **`paradoxbuilders2`**, on 10 May
2004, dedicated to photo and file storage overflow — asking members to keep
posting to `paradoxbuilders` itself and treat the new group purely as backup
capacity, so the discussion itself would not split across two groups, and
restricting the new group's photos and files to material specific to Paradox
and other Matt Layden designs. This settles when and by whom
`paradoxbuilders2` was founded: Smith, acting in Glen's absence, once the
storage limit had gone from a nuisance to a hard block.

Even after the new group existed, the photo section remained inaccessible for
removing the offending pictures. Further investigation found that **deleting**,
rather than merely resizing, three photos was needed to bring the section back
under its limit — chosen for being the least Paradox-specific of the group's
content rather than for any fault of their own: a steel dinghy, a Bris rudder,
and a "water in my backyard" photo. With those removed, general access to the
photo section was restored. A division of labor followed: use
`paradoxbuilders2`'s **files** section (rather than its photos section) for
posting photos in folders, since the original group's files area had been kept
free of photos, while reserving the original group's own files section for
data.

With access restored, new "Whisper" photos continued over the following days —
first a further batch to `paradox2`, then a complete set of sailing photos to
`paradoxbuilders2` itself, taken with a chase boat to capture the boat "on all
points of sail."

A year later, the original group's files section again neared capacity,
raising the question of a `paradoxbuilders2`-style overflow group — answered
by the fact that `paradoxbuilders2` already existed, with its files section
still empty, confirming that Smith's May 2004 overflow group had seen little
actual use for its intended purpose. The exchange also prompted encouragement
for [Kjell Karlsson](/people/kjell-karlsson.md) to keep his "ParaPlastic"
build photos coming (see [the "BRIS Paradox" — window test-fitting in
polycarbonate](/boats/bris-paradox.md#window-test-fitting-in-polycarbonate)).

# List culture

The list also fielded occasional comparisons to other small-craft designs.
Against Jim Michalak's "AF3" — a similarly beach-landable design offering
more interior space, including room for a dog — the judgment on record is
that the AF3 would not be as stable as a Paradox, though it might suit lying
out in the sun better; a capsize-recovery account for the AF3 was separately
linked for context. Weighed against that comparison, a Paradox has been
judged the right boat on the strength of others' experience, but still too
small for some, prompting the suggestion of a small catamaran as an
alternative.

A later comparison weighed Michalak's "Robbsboat" (published on Duckworks) as
a possible Paradox alternative, judged wider for its length than a Paradox and
generally reflective of Michalak's habit of reworking other designers' hulls —
he has produced numerous Phil Bolger redesigns, among them the "Cormorant," a
31 ft light expansion of his own "Caprice" design, framed as an answer to
perceived shortcomings in Bolger's AS29: the AS29, though probably more
seaworthy, slaps badly at anchor, carries a lot of weight, does not beach
well, and steers poorly under power because its motor sits aft of the rudder
post. The Cormorant, by contrast, is light, dries out on a beach without
crushing its bottom (a lighter load and a different entry shape avoid the
slapping), and locates its motor well forward of the rudder post for more
authoritative steering; a long slotted cabin top similar to the Birdwatcher's,
with a removable forward panel, gives through-ventilation and standing
headroom without a Bolger box's high windage. The comparison judged Michalak's
simple, iterative approach to solving these problems — light weight,
beachability, and modest seaworthiness for coastal and Bahamas-style
cruising — increasingly persuasive on its own terms, without displacing
Paradox itself in the comparison.

The Robbsboat pointer resurfaced again in mid-2006, offered to a newcomer
considering the [plywood grade a Paradox
build needs](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#a-july-2006-recurrence-and-a-recommendation-for-mdo-plywood)
as a cheap, simple alternative buildable in 3/8 in plywood and, in the
poster's estimation, possibly roomier inside than a Paradox, though none of
the handful then under construction had yet been finished; Michalak's own
building book for the design was again recommended as highly regarded.[44]
Writing directly to Jim Michalak about his own boats drew a blunt verdict
from the designer himself: he considers his designs **"lake boats, not sea
boats."**[45] That verdict was answered with a counterexample rather than
accepted outright — a Michalak "Frolic2" had been entered in the WaterTribe
Everglades Challenge (the same ocean-and-mangrove race Paradox and Little
Cruiser have both completed) and finished in good shape, suggesting the
lake/sea distinction is not absolute.[46] Asked directly whether Michalak's
designs are proven for long-distance, single-handed coastal cruising — on
the reasoning that a boat suited to its purpose is surely the better
starting point — the reply conceded the point "somewhat," while maintaining
that a sailor can do anything in anything, given enough skill.[47] Pressed
further on whether the Frolic2's Everglades finish undercut the "lake boat"
verdict, the fuller answer was that the two entrants never really left
sheltered water or faced serious seas — Frolic2 remains a comfortable,
capable design on its own terms — and that Michalak, like Bolger, tends to
describe his own small boats conservatively as a matter of course, plausibly
for liability reasons, even though several are self-righting from a
90-degree-plus knockdown and unsinkable unless holed or caught with the
hatches open; both designers hold that a boat's seaworthiness is inseparable
from her captain's skill and judgment, not a property of the hull alone.[48]

**Matt Layden's own view of offshore work informs that same caution.**
Michalak himself has said he would not take a Paradox to sea, but by the
same reasoning he is not the offshore sailor **Matt Layden** has proven
himself to be: Matt is credited with having sailed, rowed, sculled, and
paddled **over 25,000 miles** in small boats, including one winter spent
aboard a boat in ice and snow, and is understood to remain reluctant to
publish plans himself for liability reasons — the same caution attributed to
Michalak and Bolger above.[48] The general lesson drawn is to take capability
claims one step at a time, matched against an honest evaluation of the
sailor's own experience rather than either bravado or a designer's
disclaimer.

A **Robbsboat-specific technical exchange** followed, considering what it
would take to adapt Michalak's cheap, simple hull toward Paradox's own
offshore-capable formula: adding [chine runners](/chine-runners/index.md)
(with or without a leeboard) and the Paradox sail rig was judged a
reasonable starting point, along with substituting a steel bottom plate for
internal ballast; Matt's [reefing system](/design/rig.md), which can button
the boat up as safe as "a cork in a bottle" (Bolger's own phrase for the
storm strategy) when the hatches are fully closed, was judged the piece
still missing from a scratch-built alternative, and not something to trust
without first learning a new hull's own capabilities incrementally. Set
against Paradox, a Robbsboat would be faster and cheaper to build, offers
more interior sleeping room, but is neither as strong nor as heavy — a real
disadvantage for offshore jaunts, where a collision is more likely to end
badly for the boat.[49]

The Panama City buyer weighing a Robbsboat build separately noted a
Bolger-designed **"Black Skimmer"** looked promising but hard to right after
a capsize, and observed in passing that the Bolducs' own microcruising.com
site had "become very popular," judging from translations he had seen it in
several languages; he wondered whether the Bolducs might one day set up a
more direct way to order plans online.[50] Nels's own closing advice on
Bolger designs specifically: check with Bolger before starting a Black
Skimmer build, since he has issued upgrades since the original plans (still
the version Dynamite Payson sells) — that original design is comparatively
complex to build, has low cabin space, and carries a tall, heavy mast that
is not easy to step. Bolger's **"Martha Jane,"** which supersedes the Black
Skimmer, was recommended as more practical, more trailer-friendly, and
roomier, if less handsome to look at — fitted with tabernacled masts and
water ballast, and, in its own later-upgraded form, a pilothouse with
standing headroom down the central slot plus sponsons on either side of the
cockpit for self-righting from a 90-degree-plus knockdown, along with an
improved leeboard design. A Robbsboat, though, would still get a builder out
on the water sooner.[51]

**A separate sharpie-history correction** ran alongside the Robbsboat/Michalak
exchange: Paradox's hull shape was judged closer to century-old original
sharpies (with more freeboard for her length) than to Phil Bolger's sharpies,
and distinct from the specific hulls built by Ralph "the Commodore" Monroe —
his "Kingfisher," built to a local design, and her more flared, dory-like,
double-ended successor "Egret."[52]

**Duckworks Magazine** (`duckworksmagazine.com`), run by Chuck Leinweber, sells
Michalak's plans alongside John Welsford's and others', and was the outlet
that published the Robbsboat design discussed above; Leinweber himself
competed in a Frolic2 in the WaterTribe Everglades Challenge.[53]

## Whether WaterTribe racing tests the boat or the crew

A recurring question about Matt Layden's WaterTribe results — is a race like
the Everglades Challenge really a test of the boat, or mainly of the crew? —
drew answers on both sides. A skeptical aside dismissed WaterTribe racing as
an easy "blow up canoe" contest, testing crews rather than boats.[55] That
was answered, first, with a pointed rebuttal on the course itself: the Gulf
of Mexico leg is far from trivial — a large, deep body of water that can
carry a 35 mph wind, is only rarely as calm as a "duck pond," and turns
genuinely dangerous in a hurricane.[56] The remark's author then clarified
he had only meant that Steve "Chief" Isaac, WaterTribe's organizer,
thoroughly vets the boats and equipment entered, not that the race itself
was easy.[57] Separately, a fuller answer conceded that the month-long
Ultimate [Florida] Challenge probably does test paddlers more than boats,
though even so the Everglades Challenge has weeded out boats through
mechanical failure, and a swamped racing boat with a lead keel sinks fast
while a small boat built with positive flotation does not; a further
parallel was drawn to an English Channel race where a storm forced crew
rescues while the abandoned racing boats themselves stayed afloat.[54]

**Most competitors never actually leave sight of land** — the course runs
mostly through sheltered, shoal water, and many entrants are kayakers or
canoeists with no offshore sailing experience at all. **Matt Layden was among
the first WaterTribe competitors to work the deeper water** rather than hug
the shoal coastline, on the reasoning that deeper water is often calmer than
the shallows closer in.[57] Two further factors, drawn from Gary
Blankenship's account of Matt's races, were judged to matter as much as boat
choice: not needing to land and make camp each night (landing and departing
through surf, and sleeping with the bugs, cost real time compared with
heaving-to or drifting through a nap afloat), and a cabin or sprayhood's
shelter from wind, rain, cold, and sun alike. Matt himself raced in shorts, a
T-shirt, and bare feet, with a fleece top only on cold mornings, carrying
rain gear and footwear mainly for the overland portages, and never wore a
high-tech life vest — only a couple of old ten-dollar horse-collar types, to
Chief's own amazement. Being able to rest aboard and stay sheltered from the
elements were credited with the energy reserves that let him keep sailing
home after one race's finish, rather than stopping at the finish line
itself — see [Matt Layden's WaterTribe racing
record](/matt-layden/biography.md#the-watertribe-years-enigma-sand-flea-and-elusion)
for the full racing history.[57]

**A correction to the record on which race used which boat:** the
inflatable-kayak result credited above to a Florida Challenge was actually
sailed in the **Michigan Challenge** (August 2003); in the 2004 Florida
Challenge itself, Matt instead used a small kayak of his own building called
the "Rob Royoid," built to make the Everglades' inland portage sections more
bearable — both already covered in full at [the 14 ft Rob Roy-based canoe,
and the 2005 Everglades
Challenge](/matt-layden/biography.md#the-14-ft-rob-roy-based-canoe-and-the-2005-everglades-challenge).
WaterTribe describes its own purpose as encouraging "the development of
boats, techniques, skills, equipment, and fitness for safe and efficient
coastal expeditions and wilderness tripping using minimal impact human and
wind powered watercraft based on kayaks, canoes, and small multihulls."[58]

# Spam and moderation

By late September 2002 the list had absorbed six separate bare-link "spam"
postings from a rotating cast of one-off handles (see
[Glen C. Maxwell — the "spammed" pictures thread](/people/glen-maxwell/list-founder-and-moderator.md)).
[Glen C. Maxwell](/people/glen-maxwell.md), the list's founder and sole
moderator at the time, proposed requiring **moderator approval** before a new
member could post; the proposal was adopted within 24 hours, with
**[Dave Bolduc](/people/bolduc/dave.md)**, **[Paul V.](/people/paul-v.md)**,
and later David LeBlanc volunteering as co-moderators alongside Glen Maxwell,
letting the group close to new-member applications and eliminate the spam.

## Photo-album upload privileges are a moderator-controlled setting

A recurring point of confusion for members trying to add photos to their own
album within the group: Yahoo Groups' photo tools give the **moderator**,
not the member, control over who may upload. A moderator can open uploading
to every member, restrict it to a limited set of members, reserve it to
themselves alone, disable member uploads outright, or require every photo to
be approved before it appears — meaning a member denied "privileges" when
trying to add a picture needs the group's own moderator to change that
setting, not a fix on the member's own account. [Bill Serjeant](/people/bill-serjeant.md),
running a comparable setup on his own `smallsailboats` group at the time,
described allowing all members open upload-and-delete access to their own
pictures as his preferred policy there.

## The original group reaches its member capacity

By February 2006, the original `paradoxbuilders` Yahoo group had filled to
its **member capacity** — a separate limit from the photo/file **storage**
ceiling reached in 2004 (see [the photo-storage limit, and the founding of
paradoxbuilders2](#the-photo-storage-limit-and-the-founding-of-paradoxbuilders2)
above). Both David Beard and Glen Maxwell independently directed a member
troubleshooting an unrelated photo-upload problem to post to
`paradoxbuilders2` instead, confirming the original group could take no
further new members by that point.
