# Paradox builders mailing list

The primary community for Paradox builders and sailors, and the source archive for this bundle.

# History

| Era | Group | Notes |
|-----|-------|-------|
| 2001– | `paradoxbuilders` (Yahoo Groups) | Founded 7 July 2001 to exchange building and sailing information. |
| later | `paradoxbuilders2` (Yahoo Groups) | Second Yahoo group; a dedicated file-upload group was first proposed in March 2004, once the original group's photo storage ran short, with an initial response of archiving old files and considering a separate website; actually founded on 10 May 2004, by the acting moderator during a founder's Atlantic crossing, once the storage limit was reached in earnest — see [the photo-storage limit, and the founding of paradoxbuilders2](#the-photo-storage-limit-and-the-founding-of-paradoxbuilders2) below. |
| 2019– | `paradoxsailboat` (groups.io) | Created 5 Nov 2019 by the list owner **"Mattsplatt"** (signing "Matt") to replace both Yahoo groups; content migrated over. |

The 2019 groups.io owner posted under the screen name **"Mattsplatt"** and signed "Matt". This is **plausibly, but not confirmed, to be designer [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)** — the identity is pending corroborating evidence from the archive and is not stated here as established fact.

The group's **Files area** is organised into folders — e.g. **construction**, **stove**, and **Study Plans** — hosting construction photos and a guide/description of the study plans (uploaded early on). The dedicated "stove" folder points to a cabin stove being a fitted item on these boats. See [Study Plans](/references/study-plans.md).

# Sibling and successor groups

Beyond the `paradoxbuilders`/`paradoxsailboat` lineage itself, two related but separate Yahoo groups came up as cross-references for Paradox builders:

- **[Micro Cruising](/references/micro-cruising-group.md)** — a broader micro-cruising discussion group started around September 2001, which Paradox builders were repeatedly encouraged to also join; still an active cross-reference as late as April 2002.
- **Smallboats** (`http://groups.yahoo.com/group/Smallboats`) — a separate Yahoo group Paradox questions were redirected to after a resignation from `paradoxbuilders` in October 2001, stating a refusal to "belong to any group that promotes unsafe practices." See [plywood grade and foam flotation debate](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md) for the exchange that preceded this. Smallboats' scope, membership, and activity are not otherwise documented in this archive.

# The photo-storage limit, and the founding of paradoxbuilders2

`paradoxbuilders`' Yahoo Groups **photo storage limit** — first flagged as a display-resolution problem in March 2004 — was reached in earnest the following month, while the founder was away on an Atlantic crossing aboard "Hogfish Maximus" (see [an Atlantic crossing](/people/glen-maxwell/matt-layden-eyewitness.md#an-atlantic-crossing-aboard-hogfish-maximus)). New "Whisper" photos uploaded during this window triggered a recurring group error that locked the uploader out of the photo section entirely, a problem independently confirmed by another member the same day.

The acting moderator filling the administrative gap diagnosed the underlying cause: the group had hit its Yahoo-imposed storage ceiling outright, rather than just a display-resolution limit as in March. As an immediate step, large-format photos were deleted from the "Swash" album, then a genuinely new group, **`paradoxbuilders2`**, was founded on 10 May 2004, dedicated to photo and file storage overflow — asking members to keep posting to `paradoxbuilders` itself and treat the new group purely as backup capacity, so the discussion itself would not split across two groups, and restricting the new group's photos and files to material specific to Paradox and other Matt Layden designs. This settles when and by whom `paradoxbuilders2` was founded: the acting moderator, once the storage limit had gone from a nuisance to a hard block.

Even after the new group existed, the photo section remained inaccessible for removing the offending pictures. Further investigation found that **deleting**, rather than merely resizing, three photos was needed to bring the section back under its limit — chosen for being the least Paradox-specific of the group's content rather than for any fault of their own: a steel dinghy, a Bris rudder, and a "water in my backyard" photo. With those removed, general access to the photo section was restored. A division of labor followed: use `paradoxbuilders2`'s **files** section (rather than its photos section) for posting photos in folders, since the original group's files area had been kept free of photos, while reserving the original group's own files section for data.

With access restored, new "Whisper" photos continued over the following days — first a further batch to `paradox2`, then a complete set of sailing photos to `paradoxbuilders2` itself, taken with a chase boat to capture the boat "on all points of sail."

A year later, the original group's files section again neared capacity, raising the question of a `paradoxbuilders2`-style overflow group — answered by the fact that `paradoxbuilders2` already existed, with its files section still empty, confirming that the May 2004 overflow group had seen little actual use for its intended purpose. The exchange also prompted encouragement to keep "ParaPlastic" build photos coming (see [the "BRIS Paradox" — window test-fitting in polycarbonate](/boats/bris-paradox.md#window-test-fitting-in-polycarbonate)).

By February 2006 the original group also reached Yahoo's separate member-capacity limit — distinct from the 2004 storage ceiling — after which new members were directed to `paradoxbuilders2` instead.

## Whether WaterTribe racing tests the boat or the crew

A recurring question about Matt Layden's WaterTribe results — is a race like the Everglades Challenge really a test of the boat, or mainly of the crew? — drew answers on both sides. A skeptical aside dismissed WaterTribe racing as an easy "blow up canoe" contest, testing crews rather than boats.[22] That was answered, first, with a pointed rebuttal on the course itself: the Gulf of Mexico leg is far from trivial — a large, deep body of water that can carry a 35 mph wind, is only rarely as calm as a "duck pond," and turns genuinely dangerous in a hurricane.[23] A clarification followed that the "blow up canoe" remark had only meant that WaterTribe's organizer thoroughly vets the boats and equipment entered, not that the race itself was easy.[24] Separately, a fuller answer conceded that the month-long Ultimate [Florida] Challenge probably does test paddlers more than boats, though even so the Everglades Challenge has weeded out boats through mechanical failure, and a swamped racing boat with a lead keel sinks fast while a small boat built with positive flotation does not; a further parallel was drawn to an English Channel race where a storm forced crew rescues while the abandoned racing boats themselves stayed afloat.[21]

**Most competitors never actually leave sight of land** — the course runs mostly through sheltered, shoal water, and many entrants are kayakers or canoeists with no offshore sailing experience at all. **Matt Layden was among the first WaterTribe competitors to work the deeper water** rather than hug the shoal coastline, on the reasoning that deeper water is often calmer than the shallows closer in.[24] Two further factors were judged to matter as much as boat choice: not needing to land and make camp each night (landing and departing through surf, and sleeping with the bugs, cost real time compared with heaving-to or drifting through a nap afloat), and a cabin or sprayhood's shelter from wind, rain, cold, and sun alike. Matt himself raced in shorts, a T-shirt, and bare feet, with a fleece top only on cold mornings, carrying rain gear and footwear mainly for the overland portages, and never wore a high-tech life vest — only a couple of old ten-dollar horse-collar types, to the organizer's own amazement. Being able to rest aboard and stay sheltered from the elements were credited with the energy reserves that let him keep sailing home after one race's finish, rather than stopping at the finish line itself — see [Matt Layden's WaterTribe racing record](/matt-layden/biography.md#the-watertribe-years-enigma-sand-flea-and-elusion) for the full racing history.[24]

**A correction to the record on which race used which boat:** the inflatable-kayak result credited above to a Florida Challenge was actually sailed in the **Michigan Challenge** (August 2003); in the 2004 Florida Challenge itself, Matt instead used a small kayak of his own building called the "Rob Royoid," built to make the Everglades' inland portage sections more bearable — both already covered in full at [the 14 ft Rob Roy-based canoe, and the 2005 Everglades Challenge](/matt-layden/biography.md#the-14-ft-rob-roy-based-canoe-and-the-2005-everglades-challenge). WaterTribe describes its own purpose as encouraging "the development of boats, techniques, skills, equipment, and fitness for safe and efficient coastal expeditions and wilderness tripping using minimal impact human and wind powered watercraft based on kayaks, canoes, and small multihulls."[25]
