# John Gilbert ("JG")

**John Gilbert**, posting under the handle **`gilberj55`** and signing "JG" (his real name surfaces directly in the `From:` header on several of his later messages), was a recurring non-building technical commentator on the `paradoxbuilders` list from December 2006 to September 2008. He owned no [Paradox](/paradox.md) and reported no build of his own, but contributed substantive, varied technique across more than a dozen topics — ballast physics, hull scaling, rig classification, sculling and the yuloh, hull construction and finishing, self-steering, and cabin heating — earning a place in the archive on that volume and breadth alone. He is a different person from **Jack Gardiner** (`alex29`/`tidybowlmann`), builder of the Paradox "[Jill](/boats/jill.md)" — see [Disambiguation](#disambiguation-vs-jack-gardiner) below.

## Ballast physics, weight, and trailering figures

Lead runs a little over 11 times the density of water, against roughly 13 times for gold and 21 for platinum; concrete comes in under 3 times water's density, needing almost 4 times the volume of lead for the same ballast weight, and rebar-reinforced concrete or iron pigs need roughly 2-3 times the volume — scrap wheel-balancing weights from tire shops are the recommended cheap source, and melting about 1,000 lb of such scrap into 10 lb pigs is "a relatively pleasant job... with some cool beer nearby." See [weight, ballast, and displacement — ballast material choice](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#ballast-material-choice-lead-against-concrete-iron-and-other-alternatives).[1][2]

On why ballast spread across the beam, as Paradox's is, slows her roll: weight spread lengthwise reduces pitch (faster in calm water, wetter and slower in rough) while weight spread athwartship should instead damp roll — the same radius-of-gyration principle a figure skater uses pulling the arms in to speed a spin, offered as a further factor alongside the chine runners in Paradox's well-known resistance to rolling. See [weight, ballast, and displacement — ballast placement: outboard vs. centered](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#ballast-placement-outboard-vs-centered).[3]

A worked trailer-weight breakdown starting from the design displacement: a boat built to spec and loaded exactly to the design waterline displaces 1,410 lb; towed fully loaded except for a roughly 200 lb skipper, trailer weight comes to about 1,210 lb, and with the water tanks also emptied, about 1,060 lb — built boats seldom hit the exact design figure, and a Paradox would likely not show a difference from 100 lb either way. See [trailering — converting a boat's own displacement into an actual trailer load](/operations/trailering.md#converting-a-boats-own-displacement-into-an-actual-trailer-load).[4]

## Scaling and an enlarged Paradox

A worked 20%-scale-up of the design (16' 6.42" x 4' 11.3" x 10.8", about 2,310 lb) came with a construction shortcut of his own: a butt-block joint in place of the plans' scarfed side-panel overlap, adding the enlargement's extra length concentrated mainly in the living cabin rather than spread evenly along the hull. See [crew size and cabin fit — why Paradox is fundamentally a solo boat, and one builder's case for a removable cabin instead](/operations/crew-size-and-fit.md#why-paradox-is-fundamentally-a-solo-boat-and-one-builders-case-for-a-removable-cabin-instead).[5][6]

During the January 2007 "Why not scale?" thread, he asked whether a separate 17 ft Paradox/Little-Cruiser-style hull under discussion was the same project as a smaller, 13' 11" x 3' 11½" Enigma-style boat with a small forward "v" in the bottom panels, seen elsewhere on the web as "Jeffs New Boat" — the two turned out to be unrelated designs. See [stretched Paradox — a 17 ft Paradox/Little-Cruiser-style hull, developed independently of "PARANIGMA"](/design/ideas/stretched-paradox.md#a-17-ft-paradoxlittle-cruiser-style-hull-developed-independently-of-paranigma).[7]

## Rig and sail: classifying the Paradox lug

Called the rig a version of a standing lug — a strict standing lug has its tack at the mast, and the Paradox tack sits close to, but not on, the mast, "whatever works is ok" — noting a true balanced lug carries more sail forward of the mast than Paradox's rig does, and describing the reefing/furling system as slackening the halyard and hauling in the reefing line ("refurling??"). See [rig and sail — balanced lug, standing lug, or dipping lug?](/components/rig/index.md#balanced-lug-standing-lug-or-dipping-lug).[8]

## Hull construction: sheer clamp, bulkhead 1, and scantlings

Confirmed the sheer clamp's notch near the stern clears the steering-line lead blocks, and that bulkhead #1 must be left open for the mast to stow inside — with sheet 7 showing a fixed-port alternative for a builder who prefers to keep the bulkhead solid — and that an open bulkhead #1 should carry limber holes so water can drain back to the bilge. See [sheer strakes — sheer clamp notch near the stern](/components/sheer-strakes.md#sheer-clamp-notch-near-the-stern) and [interior — design intent: watertight bulkhead, opening is optional](/components/interior.md#design-intent-watertight-bulkhead-opening-is-optional).[9]

On build weight generally: building really light is not automatically a good idea — light weight also means a less robust structure, and while modern composite technique can build strong and light, it is more critical to get right, and shows wear from beaching and years of use faster than a stouter structure; other things equal, a heavier structure is usually more seaworthy, and genuinely light weight suits a car-topper, an easy tow, or a racer more than a cruiser. See [hull scantlings — the case for over-construction](/design/hull-scantlings.md#the-case-for-over-construction-field-evidence-hazards-and-a-counter-argument).[10]

Estimated a stripped Paradox, without ballast or water, at 550-650 lb — a little heavy for a car roof, judging even the lighter Enigma borderline — and put the practical car-topping limit at 75-80 lb, by analogy with carrying two roughly 40 lb kayaks against one 80 lb-plus boat: the harder the loading and unloading, the less likely a sailor is to actually go out. See [car-topping conversion ideas — a stripped Paradox's own car-topping weight, and roof-rack loading technique](/design/ideas/car-topping-conversion-idea.md#a-stripped-paradoxs-own-car-topping-weight-and-roof-rack-loading-technique).[11]

On a failed paint job over epoxy: recommended either a paint specifically formulated to bond to epoxy, or etching/scoring the epoxy surface first for a mechanical key, still wanting a UV-inhibited topcoat over the epoxy either way. See [epoxy coating technique — interior finish](/construction/epoxy-coating-technique.md#interior-finish-bare-wood-epoxy-varnish-cetol-or-paint).[12]

## Chine runners: against a ballasted wing, and grounding wear

In the 2007 revisiting of casting lead directly into the chine runners, argued against both that idea and a proposed bolt-on steel ballast "wing": anything added to the bottom without increasing draft only works near the bow or stern, since elsewhere it adds drag and loses ballast value to the added buoyancy of whatever is bolted on; Paradox's shallow draft, low center of gravity, and low-aspect-ratio chine rails already work as an efficient lateral plane, like end plates; and casting lead onto the rails themselves would spread weight outboard, slowing roll rate in a way that could help in some sea states but risks synchronizing dangerously with wave period in others. Proposed running lead in pieces along the outside centerline instead, as on Nathanael Herreshoff's sharpie *Meadowlark* (roughly 15 x 6 x 1 in pieces laid end to end, adding about an inch of draft while protecting the bottom), without calculating the effect on roll — and concluded against departing from the stock ballast arrangement. See [chine runner construction — a 2007 revisiting, and the case against a bolt-on steel "wing"](/chine-runners/construction.md#a-2007-revisiting-and-the-case-against-a-bolt-on-steel-wing).[13]

Separately, on a lighter foam-core chine-runner proposal, noted the rails project past the sides of the bottom and take the first impact when a boat grounds, so should be built moderately substantial regardless of core material. See [chine runner construction — a closed-cell foam alternative, and a 2007 durability exchange](/chine-runners/construction.md#a-closed-cell-foam-alternative-and-a-2007-durability-exchange).[14]

## Sculling and the yuloh

His deepest single topic, spread across three posts in one thread. Traced the yuloh to Chinese junks and sampans, where the handle is traditionally lashed to the deck with a rope doing most of the propulsive work — judged unnecessary on a boat as light as Paradox, hand weight alone being enough — and confirmed the less-cambered face is the top/aft one, the more-curved face the bottom/forward one; noted some scullers instead use vertical, scimitar-shaped blades doubling as a steering or trailing tow-oar, but judged the yuloh still the better choice for Paradox since its curve stows more conveniently on deck. See [yuloh and stowed-rig retention — blade camber, the "for'd" nomenclature, and pivot socket dimensions](/components/yuloh.md#blade-camber-the-ford-nomenclature-and-pivot-socket-dimensions).[15]

Argued oarlocks are actually better suited to plain sculling than a yuloh's ball-and-socket pivot, since an ideal sculling oar's blade curves up at the tip to work with the sweep's natural twist, where a yuloh needs the opposite, downward curve — each setup takes some getting used to — but still judged the yuloh the better fit for Paradox given her transom height, stowage, and hull weight. On the pivot socket itself, drew on an older cutter's yuloh (a lignum-vitae pin and a socket built into the yuloh's own stalk) to suggest a rounded-head bolt as the pin and an inset hardwood block, replaceable when worn, as the socket, alongside a small universal joint with an insertable pin as a possible alternative. See [yuloh and stowed-rig retention — ball-joint versus oarlock, and pivot-socket wear over time](/components/yuloh.md#ball-joint-versus-oarlock-and-pivot-socket-wear-over-time).[16][17]

## Self-steering

Reported using a weighted-shaft self-steering method on two sailing models: a roughly 2 ft Tahiti Ketch model with strong inherent course stability held its heading well once the rig was balanced, varying only slightly even in gusty conditions, while a second, shorter-keeled cruising model instead used the mainsheet led to an aft-pointing tiller with an elastic damper — workable, but somewhat less capable in gusty weather. See [self-steering — a pendulum-weight self-steering idea, and its limits](/operations/self-steering.md#a-pendulum-weight-self-steering-idea-and-its-limits).[18]

Recalled a photo of a "tillermaster" electric autohelm mounted on "Zoë" and asked how it had worked out, drawing the answer that it worked fine over a short cruise but drained its battery faster than it could be replaced. See [self-steering — sea trial and damage at the 2007 Cedar Key gathering](/operations/self-steering.md#sea-trial-and-damage-at-the-2007-cedar-key-gathering).[19]

## Cabin heating

Had a "Little Cod" wood-burning stove aboard a 21 ft cruising sailboat, initially too hot until learning to keep the firebox fire small and feed it one stick at a time every 5-10 minutes; spent about five weeks living aboard through a Vancouver November/December, reputedly the coldest, wettest months there, in reasonable comfort on that technique, and guessed a Paradox would need nothing more than a couple of candles and some sort of heat diffuser. See [cabin heating — a charcoal or kerosene can heater, and the transom-cook-stove alternative](/operations/cabin-heating.md#a-charcoal-or-kerosene-can-heater-and-the-transom-cook-stove-alternative).[20] Agreed the candle-and-diffuser guess carried a real carbon monoxide risk, and recommended fitting a CO monitor, or at minimum a colour-changing oxygen-level test card, in any enclosed space alongside good ventilation and an exhaust path. See [cabin heating — a carbon monoxide caution for candle and diffuser heaters](/operations/cabin-heating.md#a-carbon-monoxide-caution-for-candle-and-diffuser-heaters).[24]

## Auxiliary power: a firsthand case for adapting to tide and wind

Drawing on years of engine-free cruising in complex BC coastal waters, gave the archive's fullest firsthand account of what genuinely no-engine sailing demands: in light or no wind, the ordinary time-distance-destination calculation stops working, calling instead for moving toward a shallow, exposed bit of shore to wait out a tide change, or getting under way at 3 a.m. to catch a favourable one — a real change in the pace of life that in practice takes three or four days to settle into, even with prior experience of it, but rewards with better rest and something close to a mystical rhythm once found. Judged an engine's real value shows up specifically where available time is finite, extending a boat's practical range and becoming a serious asset when conditions turn bad, but weighed against its added complication and lost space, probably not worth fitting to a micro-cruiser specifically. See [auxiliary outboard power — adapting the pace of cruising to tide and wind, in genuinely engine-free conditions](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#adapting-the-pace-of-cruising-to-tide-and-wind-in-genuinely-engine-free-conditions).[25] A follow-up put a specific limit on that view: in a place with genuine current running past it, going without an engine would severely restrict a sailor's ability to use the water at all, unless the boat itself is purpose-built for human power — a canoe or a rowing boat, and even then not always. See [auxiliary outboard power — a qualifier: when local current itself calls for an engine](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-qualifier-when-local-current-itself-calls-for-an-engine).[26]

## A buoyage lesson for a newcomer

Replying to a first-time builder's plans-reading question about the abbreviation "P&S," gave the fullest buoyage explanation on record: an international standardization attempt by the IMO some thirty years earlier failed when enough countries refused to change, leaving a lateral (red/green, "red right return") system alongside a cardinal (compass-point) system both in use across North America, and explained the actual logic behind "red right return" — the red, starboard-hand buoy belongs on a vessel's right only when travelling in the direction of the flood tide, a rule that becomes genuinely confusing on a coast, such as British Columbia's own, where the flood's direction is not obvious among the islands. See [Paradox building plans — reading the plans](/references/building-plans.md#reading-the-plans-a-first-time-builders-backlog-of-questions) for the fuller mnemonic thread this capped off.[27]

## Life takes a turn: settled in Nanaimo, not building after all

Congratulating Stephen Yahn on nearing Ironsides' completion, reported that his own life had "taken a turn" ruling out building a Paradox or micro-cruiser for the foreseeable future, and that he had since relocated to **Nanaimo**, on Vancouver Island's east coast — while still hoping to connect with Stephen in person one day to see a finished boat firsthand. See [Ironsides — nearing completion, a September 2008 launch expected](/boats/ironsides.md#nearing-completion-a-september-2008-launch-expected-and-registry-congratulations) for the fuller thread this closes out.[28]
