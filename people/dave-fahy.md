# Dave Fahy

**Dave Fahy** is a boatbuilder on the **Gold Coast, Queensland**, Australia, who spent about **eighteen months** building an [Enigma](/matt-layden/enigma.md) 3.6, launching and christening her "**Goliath**" on **7 March 2021** — one of the archive's fullest first-person records of building and sailing Matt Layden's smaller Paradox sibling.[1] He first appears in the group already well into the hull work, in April 2020, and that August asked whether anyone else was building an Enigma 3.6 in Australia; the question drew no reply on record, leaving him the archive's only documented Australian Enigma builder-sailor.[2]

## Building Goliath

By April 2020 Fahy had installed the boat's lightning protection — a black wire run to the mast — an anchor-light wire, and solid wood blocks at the head, foot, and partners, then sealed the hull up around them, wiring and lightning protection built in before the deck went on rather than added afterward.[3] That July he routed a faux-laid deck, cutting individual plank lines with a router rather than laying real strips, and chose to varnish rather than paint it, anticipating the ongoing brightwork upkeep that decision would bring.[4] He built in plywood rather than the foam-core construction some other members experimented with, telling one poster that money was the reason he had not built his Enigma in foam instead.[5] Ballast went in that October: he melted and cast 50 kg of lead in five ingots.[6] By late October he was through a first coat of Northane epoxy high-build primer, sanding between coats; by December, painting and varnish were finished and he had moved on to installing hardware.[7][8] "Goliath" was launched on 7 March 2021, after an eighteen-month build.[1]

## A tender hull, and a mid-build ballast increase

Early sailing showed the Enigma tender, and Fahy progressively added ballast beyond the original 50 kg to steady her. By late April 2021 he had recast the lead into two 30 kg pieces fixed to each side plus a 15 kg ingot on top of each, for 40 kg a side, and by August 2021 had settled on roughly 45 kg of lead on each side, amidships — a substantial increase on the original casting.[9][10] The added weight steadied the boat measurably, and afterward he preferred to sit on the lee side, finding it minimised weight transfer in gusts.[10] He also made a point of keeping the forward compartment empty, valuing the buoyancy it gave over the extra stowage.[10]

## Sailing lessons

Fahy's early sailing turned up two separate lessons about running with the wind. In April 2021, reaching in the main channel on the Gold Coast with too much sail up as the wind gusted to 20 knots, he ran off downwind and eased the sheet too far; the boom blew up and around the front of the mast, and it took a stressful few minutes — the sail still furlable throughout — to get it back on the right side. His fix was to tie a stopper knot so the boom cannot swing forward of the mast again.[11] The following month, sailing in 13 knots gusting to 17 with the newly increased ballast working well, he had a second, different mishap: an accidental gybe when the boom blew clean vertically from port to starboard in a gust.[12] That August he installed a bow roller, and by the end of the month was cruising Goliath overnight, logging almost 40 nautical miles on one trip.[13][14]

## A reference point for other Enigma builders

Fahy became a regular voice answering other Enigma owners' questions in the group, drawing on his own experience with the design. He compared ballast weights with a fellow Enigma builder, André-François Bourbeau, as each worked out how much lead their hulls needed.[9][10] When Andreas Westerberg — building a [Paradox](/paradox.md) — asked whether anyone had glassed the inside of their hull, Fahy reported he had glassed his own Enigma's interior, judging the extra strength and protection worth the few extra kilograms of weight.[15] He kept sailing Goliath for years afterward: a September 2025 post records him beating north up the Gold Coast waterways before running down to Browns Bay for the night, then sailing up the Coomera River the next morning before returning to Hollywell — evidence that the boat, and his sailing of her, outlasted the build thread by a long way.[16]
