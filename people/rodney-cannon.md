# Rodney Cannon

**Rodney Cannon** (Yahoo handle `rdnycannon`, signing "Rod") is a Paradox builder from **Venice, California**, a few blocks from the ocean. A former professional magician and self-described electronics hobbyist, he introduced himself to the list on 23 June 2008 having just ordered Paradox plans over a competing "Black Skimmer" design, new to both boatbuilding and sailing itself. He began construction on 7 August 2008, three months ahead of his own stated schedule. His boat is not named anywhere in the archive itself; a later, external source — [Bill Serjeant's 2014 worldwide builders' census](/references/builders-and-owners-census-2014.md#the-table) — gives her name as **"Wizard Spawn,"** still listed as "in build" six years on. Full construction account: [boats/wizard-spawn.md](/boats/wizard-spawn.md).

## Learning to sail alongside learning to build

New to sailing as well as building, Rodney prepared by signing up for ASA sailing classes, reading *Chapman Seamanship and Small Boat Handling* and *Learn to Sail in a Weekend*, and practicing on a paid copy of the *Virtual Sailor 7.01* simulator — noting that even the simulator's own point-of-view controls function as ballast, just as in real life. He asked whether a Paradox has ever been hove to, since she carries no jib, and hoped, half in jest, for a Paradox model in the simulator. See [heavy weather tactics — a 2008 recurrence: heaving to without a jib](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#a-2008-recurrence-heaving-to-without-a-jib).

## An auxiliary-power debate, and cruising plans

Rodney opened a wide-ranging auxiliary-power debate spanning several batches: a hybrid-electric proposal citing RJ Riley's "Aquasub" precedent (sealed golf-cart batteries doubling as movable ballast), a mast-mounted wind turbine idea, and a settled view that Paradox is designed to sail with an outboard reserved for emergencies only. He got firsthand Minn Kota electric-motor and rowing/sculling figures from established builders in reply, and separately named his own cruising ambitions — extended coastal cruising down to Mexico, and later fresh-water lake and stream cruising inland — which shaped his preference for keeping the built-in water tanks free of routine salt water. Full account: [operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-newcomers-hybrid-electric-proposal-and-the-aquasub-precedent) and [construction/water-tank-lining.md](/construction/water-tank-lining.md).

## Starting construction

Rodney announced starting his build on 7 August 2008, then weighed practicing epoxy and glassing technique on a simple secondary boat (a Puddle Duck Racer, or an unnamed single-sheet 8-footer) before risking mistakes on the Paradox itself, ultimately leaning toward testing on scrap plywood instead. The next day he moved to the deck and cabin beams — rigging the laminating jigs, working through the plans' sizing instructions with help from established builders, and discovering along the way that beams are conventionally laminated plywood rather than the solid mahogany he had bought (a stock in fact meant for the interior, following another builder's example — see [boats/wizard-spawn.md](/boats/wizard-spawn.md#mahogany-for-the-interior-inspired-by-another-builders-finish)). Full account, including the beam dimensions and encouragement he received from Stephen Yahn, Pete Martin, and Al Law: [boats/wizard-spawn.md](/boats/wizard-spawn.md).

Locating the mainsheet on the plans came next, and settling on a rerouted lead borrowed from Glen Maxwell's own boat rather than the stock design, a choice that also left him weighing how far off his own self-steering wind vane still was. Full account: [boats/wizard-spawn.md — locating the mainsheet, and adopting Glen Maxwell's rerouted lead](/boats/wizard-spawn.md#locating-the-mainsheet-and-adopting-glen-maxwells-rerouted-lead).[10]

## Registering the boat, and crediting the archive

Rodney also asked, early in construction, about the administrative side of building a boat in California — whether the state imposes build restrictions and what agency issues a hull number — learning the process is essentially the same, under Coast Guard law, in every US state. Full process: [operations/registration-and-hull-numbering.md](/operations/registration-and-hull-numbering.md).[7][8] A few days later, well into the beam work, he publicly credited the archive's own accumulated build photographs and documentation with making his own progress far easier than it would have been otherwise.[9]

## A masthead-light idea, and a good-natured close to a thread

Still working through practical build questions, Rodney floated making the masthead light plug into a removable socket, with a lightning rod similarly sleeved and secured by a butterfly screw for quick removal — "just a Mcgyver thought."[15] He welcomed the suggestion of hoisting a light temporarily in the topping lift as a simple alternative to a dedicated fixture, guessing it might be a while after his own boat was finished before he had the nerve to sail at night; by way of comparison, he noted three preteen sailors were about to each single-hand an 8 ft open boat from Marina del Rey to Catalina Island the following month, chase boats standing by.[16] Needled for sounding overly cautious about trailering — "starting to sound like Butters" — Rodney took the ribbing in stride, professing no idea who Butters was.[17]

## Asking how to get the new sail made

Rodney asked whether the material in the group's "New Sail Plan" folder was enough on its own to take to a sailmaker or send to Sailrite for a sail or kit, or whether more would be needed — left open on record. See [Sail — buying vs. building a sail](/components/rig/sail.md#buying-vs-building-a-sail-recommendations-and-firsthand-accounts) for the standing guidance on briefing a sailmaker.[18]

## A further sailmaker lead, and a Sailrite question

Rodney separately offered a sailmaker lead of his own — one of the older, established sail lofts in his own marina area, a short walk from a regular stop of his — and offered to ask there in person by the end of the week, while also asking whether a US sailmaker recommendation had been tried at Sailrite. He later reported building his own beam-laminating jigs after seeing another builder's site, calling the result much easier than the manual's own method; that inspiration is credited on [boats/cachivache.md](/boats/cachivache.md#continued-progress-into-the-patagonian-winter). Full account of the Sailrite listing that followed: [components/rig/sail.md — buying vs. building a sail](/components/rig/sail.md#buying-vs-building-a-sail-recommendations-and-firsthand-accounts).[19][20]

## Settling on the original sail plan

Once Sailrite listed both Paradox sail patterns in September 2008, Rodney chose the original sail plan over the newer, taller-peaked one for his own order, having corresponded separately with its Sailrite submitter: the original sail furls more easily on the boom, and since neither of them planned to race, the newer sail's extra performance did not seem worth losing that ease of handling — a newer sail could always be ordered later if he changed his mind. See [Sail — choosing between the two 2008 Sailrite listings](/components/rig/sail.md#choosing-between-the-two-2008-sailrite-listings).[21] Even after settling the question, he kept turning the comparison over: closer study found the newer sail's shorter boom was not, in the end, that much shorter than the old one's, while the smaller original sail's lower price remained the more persuasive point — and, after visiting five lumber yards over the course of a month, he finally found one carrying the "good stuff" for his build. See [Wizard Spawn — sourcing plywood locally](/boats/wizard-spawn.md#sourcing-plywood-locally).[24]

## Reconsidering a Sailrite kit's cost, and deciding to sew it himself

Rodney had not realized that having a sail professionally sewn added its own cost on top of a kit's price, having assumed the difference was only $50-100; once corrected that a made-up sail runs over double a kit's cost, he decided to sew the Sailrite kit himself. See [Sail — choosing between the two 2008 Sailrite listings](/components/rig/sail.md#choosing-between-the-two-2008-sailrite-listings).[25]

## A topping-lift question, and a small-workshop build strategy

Rodney asked whether a topping lift is used only during reefing and released afterward, or kept under tension while running with the sail partly reefed — wondering whether that explained "Zoë"'s tidier reefed profile against older sail-plan photographs, or whether it instead came down to her shorter boom. See [Furling system — anticipating an upcoming reef](/components/rig/furling.md#anticipating-an-upcoming-reef-leaving-the-topping-lift-set) for the answer.[26] Working in a small build area, Rodney planned to do every task he could complete before joining the hull together, sewing the new sail's Sailrite kit among them, so that the day the hull itself was finished he could go straight sailing. See [Wizard Spawn — a build-sequencing strategy](/boats/wizard-spawn.md#a-build-sequencing-strategy-the-sail-kit-before-hull-assembly).[27]

## Asking what "P&S" means

Working through the plans, Rodney asked what the abbreviation **"P&S"** — appearing throughout, usually after a measurement for a cleat or similar fitting — actually stood for, an FAQ answered the same way as far back as 2005. See [Paradox building plans — reading the plans](/references/building-plans.md#reading-the-plans-a-first-time-builders-backlog-of-questions).[28]

## Continuing the mnemonic thread, and a fall sailing class

The question spun off a run of further mnemonics over the next two days: Rodney offered his own version tying the color to right-of-way rather than position, then asked how a British correspondent's "IN THE CAN" buoyage shorthand actually identifies a buoy's shape — having picked up the green/starboard/right association mainly from watching Star Trek — a question left unanswered on record. He mentioned, in passing, signing up for sailing classes that autumn so as not to look too much like a landlubber. See [Paradox building plans — reading the plans](/references/building-plans.md#reading-the-plans-a-first-time-builders-backlog-of-questions) for the full mnemonic exchange this fed into.[29][30]

## A running joke about never second-guessing Matt

In the parallel "Sailrite kits" thread, Rodney answered a one-off correspondent's list-shorthand maxim — "never second-guess Matt, refer to rule one" — with a one-line pun of his own, "rules of my road," playing off the simultaneous p&s exchange rather than adding anything substantive. See [Modifying Paradox and derivative identity — the case for building to plan](/design/modifying-paradox.md#the-case-for-building-to-plan).[31]

## Thanking John Gilbert for the buoyage lesson

Following the "p&s huh?" thread's return a week later, Rodney thanked the correspondent who had explained the flood-tide logic behind "red right return," admitting the point about flood direction hadn't sunk in the first time — "too much epoxy these days." See [Paradox building plans — reading the plans](/references/building-plans.md#reading-the-plans-a-first-time-builders-backlog-of-questions) for the full buoyage explanation this thanks answered.[32]

## Congratulating Stephen Yahn, and praising Puget Sound

Confirmed to a newcomer that he was himself in the beginning stages of building a Paradox, with no modifications planned, as a new boat builder building strictly to plan, and praised Puget Sound as an excellent cruising ground for the design given the region's frequent light drizzle and short, one-to-three-month sunny season — having found open-cockpit boating there unpleasant firsthand from his own powerboating and rafting trips — predicting "Ironsides" would draw her builder plenty of envious looks. See [boats/ironsides.md — nearing completion, a September 2008 launch expected](/boats/ironsides.md#nearing-completion-a-september-2008-launch-expected-and-registry-congratulations) for the fuller thread.[33]

## A newbie's ocean-passage spares question

Reading a firsthand Force 8 gale account and its recommended bare-minimum offshore spares, Rodney asked, as a newcomer still "struggling thru my first cup of coffee," what a "spacer" for the tack arrangement actually is, and whether a second halyard is meant as a straight backup for the first or helps it some other way. See [heavy-weather tactics — clarifying the offshore spares list](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#clarifying-the-offshore-spares-list-the-tack-arrangement-spacer-and-the-furling-axles-field-record) for the answer this drew.[22] Thanking the builder who answered, Rodney joked good-naturedly about the physical toll of a first build — a sore back, fingers "like fat sausages," and "eggs that taste like epoxy" — while genuinely glad the design had already been improved on by the builders who came before him.[23]

## A distant cruising dream, and a proposed Paradox gathering

Picking up a fellow builder's recommendation of *Self-Steering Under Sail*, Rodney planned to buy a copy — and a second book, *101 Rigs* — on his next bookstore trip, and asked in passing whether that builder had been able to go sailing yet, recalling him as nearly finished with his own build (unanswered on record).[11] The self-steering interest traced back to a lingering hunch: once his own boat was finished and weekend trips to Catalina lost their novelty, he suspected he might someday find himself preparing for a trip to Hawaii in it — or, short of that, trailering her to the Gulf coast and on to the Bahamas — and floated the idea of a large Paradox gathering somewhere, hoping for 2010.[12] The idea drew a reply wondering, half in jest, whether he might already be in Hawaii by then;[13] restating the Hawaii daydream a few days later, Rodney credited the exchange with having "ignited the ember," while still expecting to use the boat close to home for some years first, and could easily see himself sailing her to the Bahamas eventually to attend such a gathering.[14] See [merits/gatherings.md — a proposed all-Paradox gathering](/merits/gatherings.md#a-proposed-all-paradox-gathering-2010) for the wider community echo.

## Naming the alternative boat behind the distant cruising dream

The "Swaggie" floated above as a fallback boat for a bigger, more open-ended ocean cruise turned out to be John Welsford's 18 ft design — bigger than Paradox and sharing most of its traits, though without a rain-replenished water supply — which Rodney judged worth building himself in a few years for that purpose, while still favoring Paradox for its size, cost, and shallow draft in the near term. Full design comparison: [similar-boat-designs/comparisons.md — John Welsford's Swaggie](/similar-boat-designs/comparisons.md#john-welsfords-swaggie).[34] He separately echoed another correspondent's wish to duplicate cruises into the Bahamas associated with Matt Layden.[35] The exchange settled into some good-natured banter about actually getting to a Bahamas start line from the UK — allowed to be the hardest part of the whole trip — which Rodney answered with a tongue-in-cheek proposal of his own: fit the boat with a blimp, so long as the nights stayed calm.[36][37] He also passed on his own further elaboration of the Swaggie-trailering comparison, having seen video of the design's own first launch. Full detail: [similar-boat-designs/comparisons.md — John Welsford's Swaggie](/similar-boat-designs/comparisons.md#john-welsfords-swaggie).[38] Once a fuller set of Swaggie figures came back from a fellow owner, Rodney clarified what he had actually meant by his own earlier comparison — the appeal lay in also being sailed and reefed from inside with similar stability, not simply in being small for her capability — then, a day later, corrected his own account of the trailerability verdict as resting on video only, not firsthand experience, and admitted he could no longer recall for certain which of two fellow owners had first made the matching-draft comparison. Full detail: [similar-boat-designs/comparisons.md — John Welsford's Swaggie](/similar-boat-designs/comparisons.md#john-welsfords-swaggie).[40][41]

## Choosing Paradox over Robbsboat and Black Skimmer

Rodney's own choice of design had come down to three candidates — Black Skimmer, Robbsboat, and Paradox — with Black Skimmer ruled out first for her size. Paradox won out over Robbsboat "because of a fluke": reading Don Elliott's account of Matt Layden's own story left him "all teary eyed," and, as a beginner, Paradox offered far more information and an established, supportive builder community online than Robbsboat did at the time — a different, more experienced builder, he allowed, might well have chosen otherwise. Coming originally from Kansas, he was glad of the choice regardless, joking that a design detailed enough to specify "how you should wipe your butt" was exactly what a landlocked Midwesterner needed, since there are no corncobs to be had at sea.[39]
