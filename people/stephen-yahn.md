# Stephen Yahn

**Stephen Yahn** (Yahoo handle `yahns`/`yahn101a`, signing "Steve Yahn" or "Steve") is a Paradox builder from **Manchester, Washington**, on Puget Sound, a half-mile from his nearest boat launch. Before Paradox he sailed a **Picaroon**, a boat he found too large and awkward to trailer, which he gave as his own main reason for choosing a small, trailerable design he could tow widely across the western United States.

## Pre-build questions across the fleet's rigging and systems

Through the first half of 2006, while still accumulating plans and materials, Stephen asked a long, wide-ranging series of detailed technical questions covering deck-loop fastening and spar storage (see [installing the pipe stowage rails](/components/deck.md#installing-the-pipe-stowage-rails) and [storing the mast, boom, and sail when not sailing](/components/deck.md#storing-the-mast-boom-and-sail-when-not-sailing)), the tack strop's clearance over the deck and general mainsheet leading (see [design/rig.md](/components/rig/index.md)), 12V wire gauge and insulation choices (see [radio and electronics](/operations/radio-and-electronics.md)), rudder pintle/blade clearance (see [design/rudder.md](/components/rudder.md)), and how much lead ballast to carry, which he turned into a group poll (see [weight and ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#installing-the-lead-ballast)). He bought his plywood — exterior fir, since the boat would spend 99% of its life on a trailer — in mid-July 2006 and began work shortly after.

## Building "Ironsides"

Stephen started his Paradox, **"Ironsides,"** in Manchester in July 2006, widening bulkhead 3 with steel reinforcement for extra sleeping width, fabricating a side-mounted outboard motor mount, building the vent trunk, machining rudder gudgeons from Boeing surplus aluminum, and floating a pendulum-weight self-steering idea. Full construction detail: [boats/ironsides.md](/boats/ironsides.md).

## Continued build progress: grounding, hull-condition testing, and the tiller

Stephen weighed in on a newcomer's plywood-hull-deterioration question with a practical **tap test**: lightly tapping all over a glassed plywood hull with a small hammer (or a big screwdriver's plastic handle) and listening for the tone — in his own experience, a hull built well and never wetted through can last well beyond 30 years. See [checking a used Paradox for rot — an alternative, tool-free technique: the tap test](/construction/rot-inspection-ice-pick.md#an-alternative-tool-free-technique-the-tap-test) for the fuller exchange. He also opened a **tiller-shape** question — how to derive the tiller's curve and whether a 1.5 in square cross-section would suit — answered with a nail-and-lamination jig technique and a bent-galvanized-pipe alternative; the tiller's actual length and curve, which the plans do not spell out, remained open. See [design/rudder.md — tiller construction](/components/rudder.md#tiller-construction-laminated-wood-versus-bent-metal-tube) for the full technique.

Separately, replying to a newcomer's idea for a scaled-down "mini" Paradox, Stephen recalled an idea of his own along similar lines — fitting a Paradox deck, cabin, chine runners, and sail rig, scaled down, onto a Bolger Brick hull instead of scaling the Paradox hull itself. See [a scaled-down "mini" Paradox (speculative)](/design/variants/scaled-down-idea.md) for the full exchange.

Days later, still fitting out the rudder, Stephen asked whether the rudderstock, gudgeons, and pintles should mount on the transom's centerline, or whether the rudder blade itself should be centered instead — the answer noted that centerline-mounted pintles put the blade very slightly to starboard and the tiller very slightly to port. See [design/rudder.md — rudder pivot point on the transom centerline](/components/rudder.md#rudder-pivot-point-on-the-transom-centerline).

On the "Paradox sails" thread, Stephen — his own boat still far from the rig stage — asked whether a stationary housing or shroud around the furling drum, as on commercial roller-furling gear, might keep the furling line from falling off and hold the sail over on the boom; the reply explained why a fence does not work and why the stock system's line does not come off in practice, and drew thanks for it — a simpler drum with fewer parts to build. See [furling system — the Mk1/Mk2 tack-angle difference, and why the sail can't be fenced onto the drum](/components/rig/furling.md#the-mk1mk2-tack-angle-difference-and-why-the-sail-cant-be-fenced-onto-the-drum).[9][10]

On the recurring "Steel Paradox?" question, pointed to Thomas E. Colvin's *Steel Boatbuilding* (ISBN 1-888671-02-5) for a clear-cut sizing method, and gave a concrete figure: 1/8 in steel plate weighs about **5.1 lb per square foot**. See [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative)](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md#gauge-weight-and-the-case-for-steel-over-wood-generally) for the fuller steel-construction debate.[11]

With his hands too old and sore for yuloing, Stephen fitted a side-mounted 2 hp Honda outboard near bulkhead #4 rather than on the transom — full fabrication detail on [boats/ironsides.md](/boats/ironsides.md#a-side-mounted-outboard-fabricated-to-spare-his-hands). He also opened a **tiller-rope-lock** question, proposing a plywood V-notch clamp; the replies offered working friction-clamp designs along with a case that a lock is rarely necessary, and he left the question to be settled empirically under sail. See [self-steering — locking the tiller rope, and why most builders don't bother](/operations/self-steering.md#locking-the-tiller-rope-and-why-most-builders-dont-bother).[12][13] Two years into running the Honda 2 hp, Stephen reported it entirely trouble-free — topping up its oil sump with a squeeze bottle and judging the throttle cable unlikely ever to need replacement — and praised another builder's outboard-bracket design, wishing he had the same powerhead clearance. See [auxiliary outboard power — a Honda 2 hp's spare-parts and reliability record](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-honda-2-hps-spare-parts-and-reliability-record).[14][15][16][17]

## A dead-drum drag proposal, and its answer

Planning the camcleats for his own vent trunk, Stephen proposed adding a winch-style dead drum for extra drag on the slack halyard or furling line, by analogy with how a conventional sailboat winch lets a line slip over a stationary drum. The idea was judged unnecessary — Matt Layden already gets sufficient drag from the line's own bend at the vent trunk's exit lip, and needs his foot on both lines regardless since he fits no topping lift. See [design/rig.md — why Matt Layden's foot-drag furling method needs no extra hardware](/components/rig/index.md#why-matt-laydens-foot-drag-furling-method-needs-no-extra-hardware) for the full exchange.[18][19]

Stephen decided to split his own floor hatches ahead of the seat after all. He also reported his wife's refusal to drink water stored in an epoxy-lined tank prompted him to rule out drinking from his own ballast tank too, and that the high retail price of lead ballast from tire shops was pushing him toward scrap steel plate from Boeing surplus as a cheaper alternative; he judged the foot-drag technique's variable pressure likely the best solution regardless of any dead-drum fitting. See [construction/water-tank-lining.md — fixing the tank tops in place](/construction/water-tank-lining.md#fixing-the-tank-tops-in-place) for the community's wider drinking-water-versus-ballast-only practice.[20]

His scrap-steel-over-lead remark drew a car-battery suggestion, which Stephen turned down: modern recycling economics mean his local dump now takes old batteries, oil, and scrap metal for free before selling it on to industrial recyclers, so there is no longer any reward for breaking a battery open himself — a health risk to him and his wife he had no reason to take. See [weight, ballast, and displacement — sourcing lead cheaply, and why scrap has largely replaced it](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#sourcing-lead-cheaply-and-why-scrap-has-largely-replaced-it) for the fuller exchange.[21][22]

## A rowlock idea, and a note on the deck stowage loops

On a newcomer's question about rowing a Paradox with conventional oars, Stephen floated a "north woods" style rowlock, pinned through both the oarlock and the oar so the blade cannot feather, with the oar curved down toward the water to clear the cabin top — the same curved oars stowing neatly on deck where the yuloh normally sits. Fitting such high, purpose-built oarlocks would let a builder skip the plans' two deck loops used to stow the sail rig and yuloh. See [sculling and yuloh technique — whether Paradox can be rowed with conventional oars](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md#whether-paradox-can-be-rowed-with-conventional-oars) for the full rowing exchange this contributed to.[36]

## A vent trunk drain pipe sizing explanation

Answering a UK builder's worry that leftover 22 mm bathroom PVC tube might be too small for a Paradox's vent trunk drain against Matt Layden's 1 in nominal pipe specification, Stephen explained that US "pipe" sizing is not the same measurement as "tube" sizing: a nominal 1 in pipe measures 1.315 in (33.4 mm) on the outside diameter, with the true figure depending on the pipe's Schedule — a rating tied to pressure capacity rather than a fixed size, with Schedule 40 the common household-plumbing standard at about 33.78 mm outside diameter and heavier Schedule 80 reserved for higher-strength applications. Pipe under 12 in is sized nominally by inside diameter, and larger pipe by outside diameter instead — a holdover from when pipe was made only of iron, some plastic-pipe manufacturers later adopting the old iron sizes and others not; tubing, unlike pipe, is always sized by outside diameter. See [boats/johanna.md — vent trunk drain pipe sizing](/boats/johanna.md#vent-trunk-drain-pipe-sizing-metric-tube-against-the-plans-1-in-nominal-pipe) for the full exchange, including Bill Serjeant's own 22 mm drain proven in a brief force 6 aboard "Faith."[39]

For a builder who had already glued the vent-trunk blocks together before hearing of the grooved-block technique, cutting the assembled blocks in half, routing a passage for the drain, and gluing them back together was offered as a workable alternative to drilling, avoiding an expensive drill bit, the difficulty of drilling straight, and the risk of voids where the pipe passes through the glue line.[40] Full construction detail for his own vent box, including the groove-cutting and epoxy-fill technique, is on [boats/ironsides.md](/boats/ironsides.md#building-the-vent-trunk-drain).

## A Gorilla Glue suggestion, and an acrylic-latex paint question

Answering a fellow builder's epoxy skin-rash question, Stephen recommended **Gorilla Glue** as a faster-curing alternative with a different base chemistry from epoxy — it stains skin black or dark, calling for rubber gloves, and can be sanded off skin once built up thickly enough. See [epoxy brands, chemistry, and cure behavior — responses to a skin sensitivity case](/construction/epoxy-brands-and-chemistry.md#responses-to-a-skin-sensitivity-case-alternative-brands-resin-types-and-glovemask-technique).[42] Days later, having always used oil-based paints on boats the way his father had, Stephen asked the list about **acrylic-latex paint**'s durability on a boat — what kind of primer it needs, whether gloss or satin finishes better, and whether it hardens enough to walk on for a deck — expecting it to resist fading well as an acrylic, but wary given his own inexperience with the product. See [topside and hull painting choices — an acrylic-latex deck-paint caution](/construction/topside-painting.md#an-acrylic-latex-deck-paint-caution) for the answer this drew.[43]

On the epoxy-in-a-cold-garage question, Stephen recalled a Canadian epoxy called **COLD-CURE**, used long before and found to work well in cold conditions; for his own hull's outer skin of fiberglass he planned to use polyester resin instead, having five gallons left over from another project. See [epoxy brands, chemistry, and cure behavior — cold-weather cure and heating a workspace](/construction/epoxy-brands-and-chemistry.md#cold-weather-cure-and-heating-a-workspace) for the wider brand/temperature record this adds to. ## Windvane-boom interference, and flexible vane materials

Contributing to a renewed windvane discussion, distinguished vertical-axis vanes (always unforgiving in a boom collision) from horizontal-axis vanes (knocked around by the boom but mostly recovering), and recommended flexible fiberglass rods and tubes made for stunt-kite construction as a suitably resilient vane material, citing two published references consulted together. See [self-steering — boom interference, vane-blade materials, and a commercial precedent: the QME](/operations/self-steering.md#boom-interference-vane-blade-materials-and-a-commercial-precedent-the-qme).[44]

## A charcoal-stove question, and a kerosene can-heater decision

Wondering about cabin heat for a longer cruising season aboard "Ironsides," Stephen opened a "Charcoal Stove" thread asking after a design he had read about, built from a one-gallon can with an airtight chimney pipe. Weighing the suggestions that came back, he settled on a kerosene/paraffin lamp-based heater instead of charcoal, judging it cleaner — no ashes — with a metal can serving as heat dissipater and chimney base, and the burner/wick assembly salvaged from a large lantern fitted into the can's bottom. He also floated fattening and hollowing one of the four cabin posts, insulated, as an alternative chimney route that avoids the hatch's swing, undecided which corner to use. See [cabin heating — a charcoal or kerosene can heater, and the transom-cook-stove alternative](/operations/cabin-heating.md#a-charcoal-or-kerosene-can-heater-and-the-transom-cook-stove-alternative) for the full exchange.[46][47][48]

## A brick or soapstone heat-retainer idea

Still working through cabin-heat options for "Ironsides," Stephen floated an alternative to an open flame: a small, hinged wooden box vented with holes top and bottom, holding a soapstone block or brick heated beforehand on the cook stove and left to release its warmth slowly and safely — cooler to the touch and less of a fire risk if the boat were knocked about, and aimed specifically at cutting window condensation and warming a cold or rainy day aboard. See [cabin heating — heat storage and insulation alternatives to a heater](/operations/cabin-heating.md#heat-storage-and-insulation-alternatives-to-a-heater) for the fuller discussion this drew.[49]

## An oil-based enamel for interior finish

Answering a newcomer's interior-finish question, Stephen described his own practice: inexpensive Rustoleum oil-base enamel paint applied directly to bare wood once building is complete but before the deck goes on, filleting nowhere except the vent trunk and the vent baffle at the transom — judged harder and more durable than acrylic latex. See [epoxy pre-coating technique — interior finish: bare wood, epoxy, varnish, cetol, or paint](/construction/epoxy-coating-technique.md#interior-finish-bare-wood-epoxy-varnish-cetol-or-paint).[51]
