# Stephen Yahn

**Stephen Yahn** (Yahoo handle `yahns`/`yahn101a`, signing "Steve Yahn" or
"Steve") is a Paradox builder from **Manchester, Washington**, on Puget
Sound, a half-mile from his nearest boat launch. Before Paradox he sailed a
**Picaroon**, a boat he found too large and awkward to trailer, which he
gave as his own main reason for choosing a small, trailerable design he
could tow widely across the western United States.

## Pre-build questions across the fleet's rigging and systems

Through the first half of 2006, while still accumulating plans and
materials, Stephen asked a long, wide-ranging series of detailed technical
questions that drew answers from [Alastair](/people/alastair.md), [Glen C.
Maxwell](/people/glen-maxwell.md), and others — deck-loop fastening and spar
storage (see [installing the pipe stowage
rails](/design/yuloh-and-stowed-rig-retention.md#installing-the-pipe-stowage-rails)
and [storing the mast, boom, and sail when not
sailing](/design/yuloh-and-stowed-rig-retention.md#storing-the-mast-boom-and-sail-when-not-sailing)),
the tack strop's clearance over the deck and general mainsheet leading (see
[design/rig.md](/design/rig.md)), 12V wire gauge and insulation choices (see
[radio and electronics](/operations/radio-and-electronics.md)), rudder
pintle/blade clearance (see [design/rudder.md](/design/rudder.md)), and how
much lead ballast to carry, which he turned into a group poll (see [weight
and
ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#installing-the-lead-ballast)). He
bought his plywood — exterior fir, since the boat would spend 99% of its
life on a trailer — in mid-July 2006 and began work shortly after.

## Starting "Ironsides," and a widened bulkhead 3

Stephen opened the archive's "Bulkhead 3" thread proposing to narrow that
bulkhead's vertical plywood webs to gain extra sleeping width, reinforced
with steel rather than more plywood; the resulting builder discussion is
recorded in full at [crew size and cabin
fit](/operations/crew-size-and-fit.md#modifying-bulkhead-3-to-gain-room-for-sleeping-two).
By late July 2006 he had begun construction in earnest, naming his boat
**"Ironsides"** and reporting the modification built as steel, not plywood:
a vertical web widened to 1.5 in, reinforced with steel weldments (4.8 lb
each) sealed in epoxy, fastened with hot-dip galvanized bolts likewise
assembled and sealed in epoxy. He planned cloth, rather than plywood, bins
in place of the standard fixed bin panels. He was doing the trial assembly
of the sides and bulkheads, and found fitting the stem "character
building."

## Continued build progress: grounding, hull-condition testing, and the tiller

In early August 2006 Stephen chose a **one-inch copper pipe**, doing double
duty as the vent trunk drain, to also carry his mast's lightning-ground
conductor — remarking only on the pipe's price. He separately weighed in on
a newcomer's plywood-hull-deterioration question with a practical **tap
test**: lightly tapping all over a glassed plywood hull with a small hammer
(or a big screwdriver's plastic handle) and listening for the tone — in his
own experience, a hull built well and never wetted through can last well
beyond 30 years; see [checking a used Paradox for rot — an alternative,
tool-free technique: the tap
test](/construction/rot-inspection-ice-pick.md#an-alternative-tool-free-technique-the-tap-test)
for the fuller exchange, including a "Jeff UK" tone-quality and rot-cause
addition (a different, unconfirmed-identity correspondent, not [Jeff
(UK)](/people/jeff-doryman.md) of `people/jeff-doryman.md`). He also opened a **tiller-shape**
question — how to derive the tiller's curve and whether a 1.5 in square
cross-section would suit — answered by [Alastair](/people/alastair.md) and
[Derek Clark](/people/derek-clark.md) with a nail-and-lamination jig
technique and a bent-galvanized-pipe alternative; the tiller's actual
length and amount of curve, which the plans do not spell out, remained an
open question on the record. See [design/rudder.md — tiller
construction](/design/rudder.md#tiller-construction-laminated-wood-versus-bent-metal-tube)
for the full technique.

Separately, replying to a newcomer's idea for a scaled-down "mini" Paradox,
Stephen recalled an idea of his own along similar lines — fitting a
Paradox deck, cabin, chine runners, and sail rig, scaled down, onto a
Bolger Brick hull instead of scaling the Paradox hull itself. See [a
scaled-down "mini" Paradox
(speculative)](/design/variants/scaled-down-idea.md) for the full exchange.

Days later, still fitting out the rudder, Stephen asked whether the
rudderstock, gudgeons, and pintles should mount on the transom's
centerline, or whether the rudder blade itself should be centered instead —
answered by Alastair, whose own centerline-mounted pintles put the blade
very slightly to starboard and the tiller very slightly to port. See
[design/rudder.md — rudder pivot point on the transom
centerline](/design/rudder.md#rudder-pivot-point-on-the-transom-centerline).

On the "Paradox sails" thread, Stephen — his own boat still far from the
rig stage — asked whether a stationary housing or shroud around the furling
drum, as on commercial roller-furling gear, might keep the furling line from
falling off and hold the sail over on the boom; Alastair explained why a
fence does not work and why the stock system's line does not come off in
practice, and Stephen thanked him for it — a simpler drum with fewer parts to
build. See [furling system — the Mk1/Mk2 tack-angle difference, and why
the sail can't be fenced onto the
drum](/design/furling-system.md#the-mk1mk2-tack-angle-difference-and-why-the-sail-cant-be-fenced-onto-the-drum).[11][12]

On the recurring "Steel Paradox?" question, pointed to Thomas E. Colvin's
*Steel Boatbuilding* (ISBN 1-888671-02-5) for a clear-cut sizing method, and
gave a concrete figure: 1/8 in steel plate weighs about **5.1 lb per square
foot**. See [aluminum and steel Paradox
(speculative)](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md#gauge-weight-and-the-case-for-steel-over-wood-generally)
for the fuller steel-construction debate.[13]

With his hands too old and sore for yuloing, and having already fitted a
2 hp Honda outboard, Stephen floated mounting it outside the hull near
bulkhead #4 rather than on the transom or through a cabin panel, so it
could be started, refueled, and throttled from the hatchway and unshipped
for stowage when not needed. See [auxiliary outboard power — a side-mounted
outboard, to keep the cabin layout
unchanged](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-side-mounted-outboard-to-keep-the-cabin-layout-unchanged).[14]
Thanked the builders who answered, confirmed he would go ahead with the
side-mount plan, and reported he could now start fitting blocks inside the
hull for the motor mount, though the foam flotation was not yet in and the
rudder/tiller setup was still his more immediate task.[15] Responding to
Derek Clark's own transom-mounted-petrol account, Stephen added two further
details for his own fuel setup regardless of mount position: keeping the
fuel supply in an outside plastic jerrycan rather than bottles stowed near
the cooking area, and setting the outboard's anticavitation plate flush with
the hull bottom to keep the propeller from running deeper than, and
damaging, the chine runners. See [auxiliary outboard power — a
transom-mounted petrol outboard, fuel stowage, and
trim](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-transom-mounted-petrol-outboard-fuel-stowage-and-trim).[16]
He also opened a **tiller-rope-lock** question, proposing a plywood V-notch
clamp, which drew both working friction-clamp designs from other builders and
a case that a lock is rarely necessary at all — he ultimately left the
question open to be settled empirically under sail rather than building
anything right away. See [self-steering — locking the tiller rope, and why
most builders don't
bother](/operations/self-steering.md#locking-the-tiller-rope-and-why-most-builders-dont-bother).[17][18]
Posting photos of his finished side motor mount for "Ironsides," Stephen
gave its full construction detail — a sized-to-fit wooden pad for the Honda 2
hp motor, a hitch pin, a ball valve against flooding, a bracing shelf, and a
copper (rather than plastic) grounding pipe doing double duty as a cable
groove — and, prompted by Alastair's caution, realized fitting the mount
afloat risked shipping water as he leaned over the side to align the motor
shaft. See [auxiliary outboard power — a worked side-mount installation, and
the risk of shipping water while fitting it
afloat](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-worked-side-mount-installation-and-the-risk-of-shipping-water-while-fitting-it-afloat).[19][20]
Two years into running his own Honda 2 hp, Stephen reported it entirely
trouble-free, and separately praised Alastair's published outboard-bracket
photos, wishing he had the same powerhead clearance. On the practical side,
he shared a squeeze-bottle technique for topping up the motor's oil sump, and
judged a spare throttle cable unlikely ever to be needed given how little
stress the cable sees in normal use. See [auxiliary outboard power — a Honda
2 hp's spare-parts and reliability
record](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-honda-2-hps-spare-parts-and-reliability-record).[21][22][23][24]

## A dead-drum drag proposal, and its answer

Planning the camcleats for his own vent trunk, Stephen proposed adding a
winch-style dead drum for extra drag on the slack halyard or furling line,
by analogy with how a conventional sailboat winch lets a line slip over a
stationary drum. Alastair judged the idea unnecessary — Matt Layden already
gets sufficient drag from the line's own bend at the vent trunk's exit lip,
and needs his foot on both lines regardless since he fits no topping lift.
See [design/rig.md — why Matt Layden's foot-drag furling method needs no
extra
hardware](/design/rig.md#why-matt-laydens-foot-drag-furling-method-needs-no-extra-hardware)
for the full exchange.[25][26]

Thanking Alastair, Stephen decided to split his own floor hatches ahead of
the seat after all. He also reported his wife's refusal to drink water
stored in an epoxy-lined tank prompted him to rule out drinking from his own
ballast tank too, and that the retail price of lead ballast from tire shops —
about $0.45 a pound — was pushing him toward scrap steel plate from Boeing
surplus as a cheaper alternative; he judged the foot-drag technique's
variable pressure likely the best solution regardless of any dead-drum
fitting. See [construction/water-tank-lining.md — fixing the tank tops in
place](/construction/water-tank-lining.md#fixing-the-tank-tops-in-place) for
the community's wider drinking-water-versus-ballast-only practice.[27]

His scrap-steel-over-lead remark drew a car-battery suggestion from a UK
correspondent, which Stephen turned down: modern recycling economics mean his
local dump now takes old batteries, oil, and scrap metal for free before
selling it on to industrial recyclers, so there is no longer any reward for
breaking a battery open himself — a health risk to him and his wife he had no
reason to take. See [weight, ballast, and displacement — sourcing lead
cheaply, and why scrap has largely replaced
it](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#sourcing-lead-cheaply-and-why-scrap-has-largely-replaced-it)
for the fuller exchange.[28][29]
