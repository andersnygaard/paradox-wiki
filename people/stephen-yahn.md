# Stephen Yahn

**Stephen Yahn** (Yahoo handle `yahns`/`yahn101a`, signing "Steve Yahn" or "Steve") is a Paradox builder from **Manchester, Washington**, on Puget Sound, a half-mile from his nearest boat launch. Before Paradox he sailed a **Picaroon**, a boat he found too large and awkward to trailer, which he gave as his own main reason for choosing a small, trailerable design he could tow widely across the western United States.

## Pre-build questions across the fleet's rigging and systems

Through the first half of 2006, while still accumulating plans and materials, Stephen asked a long, wide-ranging series of detailed technical questions covering deck-loop fastening and spar storage (see [installing the pipe stowage rails](/components/deck.md#installing-the-pipe-stowage-rails) and [storing the mast, boom, and sail when not sailing](/components/deck.md#storing-the-mast-boom-and-sail-when-not-sailing)), the tack strop's clearance over the deck and general mainsheet leading (see [design/rig.md](/components/rig/index.md)), 12V wire gauge and insulation choices (see [radio and electronics](/operations/radio-and-electronics.md)), rudder pintle/blade clearance (see [design/rudder.md](/components/rudder.md)), and how much lead ballast to carry, which he turned into a group poll (see [weight and ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#installing-the-lead-ballast)). He bought his plywood — exterior fir, since the boat would spend 99% of its life on a trailer — in mid-July 2006 and began work shortly after.

Stephen started his Paradox, **"Ironsides,"** in Manchester in July 2006, widening bulkhead 3 with steel reinforcement for extra sleeping width, fabricating a side-mounted outboard motor mount, building the vent trunk, machining rudder gudgeons from Boeing surplus aluminum, and floating a pendulum-weight self-steering idea. Stephen decided to split his own floor hatches ahead of the seat after all. He also reported his wife's refusal to drink water stored in an epoxy-lined tank prompted him to rule out drinking from his own ballast tank too, and that the high retail price of lead ballast from tire shops was pushing him toward scrap steel plate from Boeing surplus as a cheaper alternative; he judged the foot-drag technique's variable pressure likely the best solution regardless of any dead-drum fitting. His scrap-steel-over-lead remark drew a car-battery suggestion, which Stephen turned down: modern recycling economics mean his local dump now takes old batteries, oil, and scrap metal for free before selling it on to industrial recyclers, so there is no longer any reward for breaking a battery open himself — a health risk to him and his wife he had no reason to take. ## A vent trunk drain pipe sizing explanation

Answering a UK builder's worry that leftover 22 mm bathroom PVC tube might be too small for a Paradox's vent trunk drain against Matt Layden's 1 in nominal pipe specification, Stephen explained that US "pipe" sizing is not the same measurement as "tube" sizing: a nominal 1 in pipe measures 1.315 in (33.4 mm) on the outside diameter, with the true figure depending on the pipe's Schedule — a rating tied to pressure capacity rather than a fixed size, with Schedule 40 the common household-plumbing standard at about 33.78 mm outside diameter and heavier Schedule 80 reserved for higher-strength applications. Pipe under 12 in is sized nominally by inside diameter, and larger pipe by outside diameter instead — a holdover from when pipe was made only of iron, some plastic-pipe manufacturers later adopting the old iron sizes and others not; tubing, unlike pipe, is always sized by outside diameter. See [boats/johanna.md — vent trunk drain pipe sizing](/boats/johanna.md#vent-trunk-drain-pipe-sizing-metric-tube-against-the-plans-1-in-nominal-pipe) for the full exchange, including Bill Serjeant's own 22 mm drain proven in a brief force 6 aboard "Faith."[39]

For a builder who had already glued the vent-trunk blocks together before hearing of the grooved-block technique, cutting the assembled blocks in half, routing a passage for the drain, and gluing them back together was offered as a workable alternative to drilling, avoiding an expensive drill bit, the difficulty of drilling straight, and the risk of voids where the pipe passes through the glue line.[40] Full construction detail for his own vent box, including the groove-cutting and epoxy-fill technique, is on [boats/ironsides.md](/boats/ironsides.md#building-the-vent-trunk-drain).

## A cautionary sea story on anchor-fitting reinforcement

Answering a fellow owner's question about attaching an anchor rode at the bow, recalled a sea story of a couple whose stern cleats survived a severe storm off Madagascar intact while the surrounding deck tore away instead — a cautionary illustration that a fitting bolted strong enough to hold can still fail by taking the hull structure around it with it. Fitted broad washers under both his own cleat bolts and his stainless stem U-bolt accordingly, reserving the U-bolt for trailer duty and the cleats for anchor duty. See [ground tackle and anchoring — attaching ground tackle at the bow](/operations/ground-tackle.md#attaching-ground-tackle-at-the-bow-cleats-a-stem-u-bolt-and-chafe-protection) for the full exchange.[77]

## A childhood memory of walnut-shell non-skid, and a paint-based method

## Encouraging a newcomer just starting his own build

Answering a new builder's announcement that he had begun construction, Stephen framed epoxy as simple and nearly foolproof, since nails and screws provide a permanent mechanical backup to the glue joint regardless, and suggested the rudder, mast, boom, yard, and yuloh as good parts to start on. He also passed along his own alternative to solvent-based hand cleaning: a box of cheap surgical rubber gloves, stripped off and replaced rather than cleaned with MEK, acetone, or vinegar, a habit he extended to painting and varnishing too. ## A proposed larger-diameter furling spool

Reasoning that a fatter furling spool would need less line tension to roll up the sail, Stephen proposed building up its diameter beyond that of the boom itself with a fiberglass-reinforced wooden doughnut split in half and glued or screwed on, asking builders with finished boats how best to match whatever Matt Layden does to the same end. An answer came back favoring winding many turns of the reefing line directly onto the drum as a simple fix that works well enough, while agreeing the wooden doughnut was the neater approach — with a further benefit, that the tack-lashing holes could be drilled straight through the doughnut rather than through the boom itself.
