# Stephen Yahn

**Stephen Yahn** (Yahoo handle `yahns`/`yahn101a`, signing "Steve Yahn" or "Steve") is a Paradox builder from **Manchester, Washington**, on Puget Sound, a half-mile from his nearest boat launch. Before Paradox he sailed a **Picaroon**, a boat he found too large and awkward to trailer, which he gave as his own main reason for choosing a small, trailerable design he could tow widely across the western United States.

## Pre-build questions across the fleet's rigging and systems

Through the first half of 2006, while still accumulating plans and materials, Stephen asked a long, wide-ranging series of detailed technical questions covering deck-loop fastening and spar storage (see [installing the pipe stowage rails](/components/yuloh.md#installing-the-pipe-stowage-rails) and [storing the mast, boom, and sail when not sailing](/components/yuloh.md#storing-the-mast-boom-and-sail-when-not-sailing)), the tack strop's clearance over the deck and general mainsheet leading (see [design/rig.md](/components/rig/index.md)), 12V wire gauge and insulation choices (see [radio and electronics](/operations/radio-and-electronics.md)), rudder pintle/blade clearance (see [design/rudder.md](/components/rudder.md)), and how much lead ballast to carry, which he turned into a group poll (see [weight and ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#installing-the-lead-ballast)). He bought his plywood — exterior fir, since the boat would spend 99% of its life on a trailer — in mid-July 2006 and began work shortly after.

## Starting "Ironsides," and a widened bulkhead 3

Stephen opened the archive's "Bulkhead 3" thread proposing to narrow that bulkhead's vertical plywood webs to gain extra sleeping width, reinforced with steel rather than more plywood; the resulting builder discussion is recorded in full at [crew size and cabin fit](/operations/crew-size-and-fit.md#modifying-bulkhead-3-to-gain-room-for-sleeping-two). By late July 2006 he had begun construction in earnest, naming his boat **"Ironsides"** and reporting the modification built as steel, not plywood: a vertical web widened to 1.5 in, reinforced with steel weldments (4.8 lb each) sealed in epoxy, fastened with hot-dip galvanized bolts likewise assembled and sealed in epoxy. He planned cloth, rather than plywood, bins in place of the standard fixed bin panels. He was doing the trial assembly of the sides and bulkheads, and found fitting the stem "character building." Built without bins at all, the finished modification yields a **41 in** cabin sole width — see [crew size and cabin fit — a newcomer's basic-interior-dimensions FAQ, answered](/operations/crew-size-and-fit.md#a-newcomers-basic-interior-dimensions-faq-answered) for the fuller comparison this figure fed into.[59]

## Continued build progress: grounding, hull-condition testing, and the tiller

In early August 2006 Stephen chose a **one-inch copper pipe**, doing double duty as the vent trunk drain, to also carry his mast's lightning-ground conductor — remarking only on the pipe's price. He separately weighed in on a newcomer's plywood-hull-deterioration question with a practical **tap test**: lightly tapping all over a glassed plywood hull with a small hammer (or a big screwdriver's plastic handle) and listening for the tone — in his own experience, a hull built well and never wetted through can last well beyond 30 years; see [checking a used Paradox for rot — an alternative, tool-free technique: the tap test](/construction/rot-inspection-ice-pick.md#an-alternative-tool-free-technique-the-tap-test) for the fuller exchange, including a tone-quality and rot-cause addition from an unconfirmed-identity correspondent (not [Jeff (UK)](/people/jeff-doryman.md) of `people/jeff-doryman.md`). He also opened a **tiller-shape** question — how to derive the tiller's curve and whether a 1.5 in square cross-section would suit — which was answered with a nail-and-lamination jig technique and a bent-galvanized-pipe alternative; the tiller's actual length and amount of curve, which the plans do not spell out, remained an open question on the record. See [design/rudder.md — tiller construction](/components/rudder.md#tiller-construction-laminated-wood-versus-bent-metal-tube) for the full technique.

Separately, replying to a newcomer's idea for a scaled-down "mini" Paradox, Stephen recalled an idea of his own along similar lines — fitting a Paradox deck, cabin, chine runners, and sail rig, scaled down, onto a Bolger Brick hull instead of scaling the Paradox hull itself. See [a scaled-down "mini" Paradox (speculative)](/design/variants/scaled-down-idea.md) for the full exchange.

Days later, still fitting out the rudder, Stephen asked whether the rudderstock, gudgeons, and pintles should mount on the transom's centerline, or whether the rudder blade itself should be centered instead — the answer noted that centerline-mounted pintles put the blade very slightly to starboard and the tiller very slightly to port. See [design/rudder.md — rudder pivot point on the transom centerline](/components/rudder.md#rudder-pivot-point-on-the-transom-centerline).

On the "Paradox sails" thread, Stephen — his own boat still far from the rig stage — asked whether a stationary housing or shroud around the furling drum, as on commercial roller-furling gear, might keep the furling line from falling off and hold the sail over on the boom; the reply explained why a fence does not work and why the stock system's line does not come off in practice, and drew thanks for it — a simpler drum with fewer parts to build. See [furling system — the Mk1/Mk2 tack-angle difference, and why the sail can't be fenced onto the drum](/components/rig/furling.md#the-mk1mk2-tack-angle-difference-and-why-the-sail-cant-be-fenced-onto-the-drum).[11][12]

On the recurring "Steel Paradox?" question, pointed to Thomas E. Colvin's *Steel Boatbuilding* (ISBN 1-888671-02-5) for a clear-cut sizing method, and gave a concrete figure: 1/8 in steel plate weighs about **5.1 lb per square foot**. See [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative)](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md#gauge-weight-and-the-case-for-steel-over-wood-generally) for the fuller steel-construction debate.[13]

With his hands too old and sore for yuloing, and having already fitted a 2 hp Honda outboard, Stephen floated mounting it outside the hull near bulkhead #4 rather than on the transom or through a cabin panel, so it could be started, refueled, and throttled from the hatchway and unshipped for stowage when not needed. See [auxiliary outboard power — a side-mounted outboard, to keep the cabin layout unchanged](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-side-mounted-outboard-to-keep-the-cabin-layout-unchanged).[14] Thanked the builders who answered, confirmed he would go ahead with the side-mount plan, and reported he could now start fitting blocks inside the hull for the motor mount, though the foam flotation was not yet in and the rudder/tiller setup was still his more immediate task.[15] Responding to another builder's own transom-mounted-petrol account, Stephen added two further details for his own fuel setup regardless of mount position: keeping the fuel supply in an outside plastic jerrycan rather than bottles stowed near the cooking area, and setting the outboard's anticavitation plate flush with the hull bottom to keep the propeller from running deeper than, and damaging, the chine runners. See [auxiliary outboard power — a transom-mounted petrol outboard, fuel stowage, and trim](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-transom-mounted-petrol-outboard-fuel-stowage-and-trim).[16] He also opened a **tiller-rope-lock** question, proposing a plywood V-notch clamp, which drew both working friction-clamp designs from other builders and a case that a lock is rarely necessary at all — he ultimately left the question open to be settled empirically under sail rather than building anything right away. See [self-steering — locking the tiller rope, and why most builders don't bother](/operations/self-steering.md#locking-the-tiller-rope-and-why-most-builders-dont-bother).[17][18] Posting photos of his finished side motor mount for "Ironsides," Stephen gave its full construction detail — a sized-to-fit wooden pad for the Honda 2 hp motor, a hitch pin, a ball valve against flooding, a bracing shelf, and a copper (rather than plastic) grounding pipe doing double duty as a cable groove — and, prompted by a caution from another builder, realized fitting the mount afloat risked shipping water as he leaned over the side to align the motor shaft. See [auxiliary outboard power — a worked side-mount installation, and the risk of shipping water while fitting it afloat](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-worked-side-mount-installation-and-the-risk-of-shipping-water-while-fitting-it-afloat).[19][20] Two years into running his own Honda 2 hp, Stephen reported it entirely trouble-free, and separately praised another builder's published outboard-bracket photos, wishing he had the same powerhead clearance. On the practical side, he shared a squeeze-bottle technique for topping up the motor's oil sump, and judged a spare throttle cable unlikely ever to be needed given how little stress the cable sees in normal use. See [auxiliary outboard power — a Honda 2 hp's spare-parts and reliability record](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-honda-2-hps-spare-parts-and-reliability-record).[21][22][23][24]

## A dead-drum drag proposal, and its answer

Planning the camcleats for his own vent trunk, Stephen proposed adding a winch-style dead drum for extra drag on the slack halyard or furling line, by analogy with how a conventional sailboat winch lets a line slip over a stationary drum. The idea was judged unnecessary — Matt Layden already gets sufficient drag from the line's own bend at the vent trunk's exit lip, and needs his foot on both lines regardless since he fits no topping lift. See [design/rig.md — why Matt Layden's foot-drag furling method needs no extra hardware](/components/rig/index.md#why-matt-laydens-foot-drag-furling-method-needs-no-extra-hardware) for the full exchange.[25][26]

Stephen decided to split his own floor hatches ahead of the seat after all. He also reported his wife's refusal to drink water stored in an epoxy-lined tank prompted him to rule out drinking from his own ballast tank too, and that the retail price of lead ballast from tire shops — about $0.45 a pound — was pushing him toward scrap steel plate from Boeing surplus as a cheaper alternative; he judged the foot-drag technique's variable pressure likely the best solution regardless of any dead-drum fitting. See [construction/water-tank-lining.md — fixing the tank tops in place](/construction/water-tank-lining.md#fixing-the-tank-tops-in-place) for the community's wider drinking-water-versus-ballast-only practice.[27]

His scrap-steel-over-lead remark drew a car-battery suggestion, which Stephen turned down: modern recycling economics mean his local dump now takes old batteries, oil, and scrap metal for free before selling it on to industrial recyclers, so there is no longer any reward for breaking a battery open himself — a health risk to him and his wife he had no reason to take. See [weight, ballast, and displacement — sourcing lead cheaply, and why scrap has largely replaced it](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#sourcing-lead-cheaply-and-why-scrap-has-largely-replaced-it) for the fuller exchange.[28][29]

## A self-steering weight idea

Stephen floated a pendulum-weight self-steering concept: a horizontal shaft fitted aft of the rudder axis, above the waterline, carrying a movable weight that shifts to leeward as the hull heels, offsetting the round-up tendency created as the sail's center of effort moves to leeward of the hull's center of resistance — an idea he traced to a century-old model-sailboat tactic, and one he thought fit naturally with the lead weight already cast into the plans' own rudder blade. Separately, recalling a casting pool at Lincoln Park in Santa Monica where he had sailed model boats, he asked whether the big iron rudder straps seen on some traditional full-size sailboats served a similar self-steering purpose; the answer identified them as plank-reinforcement hardware unrelated to self-steering. Locking the steering line down while sailing, the normal practice, was pointed out as already negating the pendulum idea's effect; Stephen had not yet fitted his own rudder's lead weight, leaving the idea untested on the water. See [self-steering — a pendulum-weight self-steering idea, and its limits](/operations/self-steering.md#a-pendulum-weight-self-steering-idea-and-its-limits) for the full exchange, including gilberj55's model-boat precedents and Alan's and Bill Serjeant's assessments.[42][43][44][45]

## Rudder gudgeons and deck keepers, and a first attempt at powder coating

Continuing work on "Ironsides," Stephen machined a pair of rudder gudgeons from thick aluminum plate bought at Boeing surplus, and made the arched deck keepers from steel — flat discs cut from 1/4 in plate brazed to 1 in-diameter tubing bent from electric conduit fittings. To stop corrosion on the steel parts, he tried powder coating for the first time. On a suggestion, he changed the arched deck keepers' shape to a lower, stronger profile less prone to snagging rope, settling on 4 in high.[46]

## A rowlock idea, and a note on the deck stowage loops

On a newcomer's question about rowing a Paradox with conventional oars, Stephen floated a "north woods" style rowlock, pinned through both the oarlock and the oar so the blade cannot feather, with the oar curved down toward the water to clear the cabin top — the same curved oars stowing neatly on deck where the yuloh normally sits. Fitting such high, purpose-built oarlocks would let a builder skip the plans' two deck loops used to stow the sail rig and yuloh. ## A vent trunk drain pipe sizing explanation

Answering a UK builder's worry that leftover 22 mm bathroom PVC tube might be too small for a Paradox's vent trunk drain against Matt Layden's 1 in nominal pipe specification, Stephen explained that US "pipe" sizing is not the same measurement as "tube" sizing: a nominal 1 in pipe measures 1.315 in (33.4 mm) on the outside diameter, with the true figure depending on the pipe's Schedule — a rating tied to pressure capacity rather than a fixed size, with Schedule 40 the common household-plumbing standard at about 33.78 mm outside diameter and heavier Schedule 80 reserved for higher-strength applications. Pipe under 12 in is sized nominally by inside diameter, and larger pipe by outside diameter instead — a holdover from when pipe was made only of iron, some plastic-pipe manufacturers later adopting the old iron sizes and others not; tubing, unlike pipe, is always sized by outside diameter. See [boats/johanna.md — vent trunk drain pipe sizing](/boats/johanna.md#vent-trunk-drain-pipe-sizing-metric-tube-against-the-plans-1-in-nominal-pipe) for the full exchange, including Bill Serjeant's own 22 mm drain proven in a brief force 6 aboard "Faith."[51]

Separately, on his own vent box for "Ironsides," Stephen glued together blocks of wood with a groove cut to accept the pipe liner, bonded in place with thickened epoxy, and used copper tubing for grounding his crystal radio, the pipe doing double duty. For a builder who had already glued the vent-trunk blocks together before hearing of the technique, cutting the assembled blocks in half, routing a passage for the drain, and gluing them back together was offered as a workable alternative to drilling, avoiding an expensive drill bit, the difficulty of drilling straight, and the risk of voids where the pipe passes through the glue line.[52][53] A follow-up added the groove-cutting detail: two saw cuts, then a chisel to flatten the groove's bottom; the groove itself can be filled around the pipe with epoxy thickened with sawdust or flour — Stephen's own long-term results with ordinary kitchen wheat flour were good — leaving the tubing proud and trimming it flush once cured.[55]

## A Gorilla Glue suggestion, and an acrylic-latex paint question

Answering a fellow builder's epoxy skin-rash question, Stephen recommended **Gorilla Glue** as a faster-curing alternative with a different base chemistry from epoxy — it stains skin black or dark, calling for rubber gloves, and can be sanded off skin once built up thickly enough. See [epoxy brands, chemistry, and cure behavior — responses to a skin sensitivity case](/construction/epoxy-brands-and-chemistry.md#responses-to-a-skin-sensitivity-case-alternative-brands-resin-types-and-glovemask-technique).[56] Days later, having always used oil-based paints on boats the way his father had, Stephen asked the list about **acrylic-latex paint**'s durability on a boat — what kind of primer it needs, whether gloss or satin finishes better, and whether it hardens enough to walk on for a deck — expecting it to resist fading well as an acrylic, but wary given his own inexperience with the product. See [topside and hull painting choices — an acrylic-latex deck-paint caution](/construction/topside-painting.md#an-acrylic-latex-deck-paint-caution) for the answer this drew.[57]

## Windvane-boom interference, and flexible vane materials

Contributing to a renewed windvane discussion, distinguished vertical-axis vanes (always unforgiving in a boom collision) from horizontal-axis vanes (knocked around by the boom but mostly recovering), and recommended flexible fiberglass rods and tubes made for stunt-kite construction as a suitably resilient vane material, citing two published references consulted together. ## A charcoal-stove question, and a kerosene can-heater decision

Wondering about cabin heat for a longer cruising season aboard "Ironsides," Stephen opened a "Charcoal Stove" thread asking after a design he had read about, built from a one-gallon can with an airtight chimney pipe. Weighing the suggestions that came back, he settled on a kerosene/paraffin lamp-based heater instead of charcoal, judging it cleaner — no ashes — with a metal can serving as heat dissipater and chimney base, and the burner/wick assembly salvaged from a large lantern fitted into the can's bottom. He also floated fattening and hollowing one of the four cabin posts, insulated, as an alternative chimney route that avoids the hatch's swing, undecided which corner to use. See [cabin heating — a charcoal or kerosene can heater, and the transom-cook-stove alternative](/operations/cabin-heating.md#a-charcoal-or-kerosene-can-heater-and-the-transom-cook-stove-alternative) for the full exchange.[61][62][63]

## A brick or soapstone heat-retainer idea

Still working through cabin-heat options for "Ironsides," Stephen floated an alternative to an open flame: a small, hinged wooden box vented with holes top and bottom, holding a soapstone block or brick heated beforehand on the cook stove and left to release its warmth slowly and safely — cooler to the touch and less of a fire risk if the boat were knocked about, and aimed specifically at cutting window condensation and warming a cold or rainy day aboard. See [cabin heating — heat storage and insulation alternatives to a heater](/operations/cabin-heating.md#heat-storage-and-insulation-alternatives-to-a-heater) for the fuller discussion this drew.[64]

## An oil-based enamel for interior finish

Answering a newcomer's interior-finish question, Stephen described his own practice: inexpensive Rustoleum oil-base enamel paint, about $7 a quart, applied directly to bare wood once building is complete but before the deck goes on, filleting nowhere except the vent trunk and the vent baffle at the transom — judged harder and more durable than acrylic latex. See [epoxy pre-coating technique — interior finish: bare wood, epoxy, varnish, cetol, or paint](/construction/epoxy-coating-technique.md#interior-finish-bare-wood-epoxy-varnish-cetol-or-paint).[66]
