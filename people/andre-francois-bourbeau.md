# André-François Bourbeau

**André-François Bourbeau** is a boatbuilder and sailor based near **Lac Kénogami** in Québec's Saguenay region, who came to sailing already an experienced wilderness paddler and a builder of traditional birchbark and spruce-bark canoes, and who owns two **[Paradoxes](/paradox.md)**, designed the **[Gorfnik](/similar-boat-designs/gorfnik-paraduck.md)**, and in 2021 scratch-built a twin-masted **[Enigma](/matt-layden/enigma.md)** ketch of his own design.[1][2] He launches from the boat ramp beside the church at **St-Cyriac**, on Lac Kénogami, and his builds are documented in detail, post by post, in the **[Paradox Sailboat Facebook group](/references/paradox-facebook-group.md)**.[2]

## Paradox and Gorfnik

He keeps two Paradoxes, lending the second one out to friends specifically so he always has company on the water, and by 2021 had six years' experience sailing both the Paradox and his own Gorfnik design, with a longest Paradox trip of five weeks and a longest Gorfnik trip of four weeks.[1] He finds the Gorfniks see more use because they are smaller and easier to launch, and he likes travelling with two or three of them loaded on the same trailer so friends can come along, but he still rates the Paradox above the Gorfnik for its performance and its margin of safety.[1] He traces much of his boatbuilding approach to years of wilderness canoe travel and building traditional birchbark and spruce-bark canoes, above all his insistence on knot-free, straight-grained wood for load-bearing members such as gunnels — black ash, in his case, cut from adjacent sections of the same tree with one piece reversed, which let him hold a hull within an eighth of an inch of the centreline even without bulkheads.[3]

## Question — an Enigma ketch of his own design

In March 2021 he began building, without any plans, a boat based on the Enigma hull but reworked with ideas drawn from years of Paradox and Gorfnik trips, describing the project at the time as an Enigma/Gorfnik combination meant to combine the best features of both boats.[1][4] Laying out the partitions took real thought: he wanted to slide straight into sleeping position, as on the Paradox and Gorfnik, without first emptying the rear storage locker, to have room for his folding Brompton bicycle, and to be able to step the mast from inside the cabin, all of which pushed him to relocate the mast and rework the sail plan and the boat's balance.[4] He carried several trip-tested ideas over from the Paradox and Gorfnik: an accessible anchor, fishing-gear, and muddy-boots locker on the starboard side (his bike lives to port) with drain holes, sitting under the folding hatch so it doubles as a cooking platform; a rear deck lowered three inches and sloped aft to shed water, which also gives him a flat, railed spot for gear or a wind-sheltered second cooking counter, an easier step up from the swim ladder, and lets him keep the tiller and steering lines above deck rather than running them through a hole in the transom; and a long, narrow aeration gap between the flat and rounded parts of the foredeck, matching the screened rear window that had already proven itself on the Gorfnik.[5] Working out the cabin roof and windows by trial and error, since he had no plan, he settled on a 17-degree rake for the front window, 6 degrees on the side windows, and a 1.5-degree slope from back to front so rainwater runs off.[6] He launched the finished boat at the Lac Kénogami ramp beside the St-Cyriac church on 18 July 2021, with a champagne christening.[2] Asked on the spot what he had named her, he had not yet settled on anything and answered that it was a good question — so he named her **Question**, which he reckoned to be the fourth Enigma launched.[7]

### Beaching wheels, rudder, hatch, and rig

Question carries integrated, demountable beaching wheels: half-inch axles run through standard copper pipe let into the chine wings, set at roughly 5 degrees of positive camber, an angle he arrived at by building a temporary test buggy that also served to move the boat around on land.[8] Because the axle holes sit underwater, he sealed the inner pipe ends with epoxy against infiltration and thickened the three-quarter-inch wings with added ash strips to support the pipe; unsure whether aluminium would resist salt corrosion any better and unable to find stainless pipe, he used ordinary copper left over from building his house, unworried after keeping an earlier Gorfnik in salt water for two full seasons without a sign of corrosion.[9] He also glued in wooden support blocks, drilled to the exact depth needed, before epoxying the pipes permanently into place and fibreglassing the chines, which left each axle supported along five and three-quarter inches of pipe rather than the two and a half inches the wing thickness alone would give.[10] The rudder is a balanced design, worked out from scratch with cardboard mock-ups since he had no plan to follow, with roughly 30 percent of the blade forward of the pivot to lighten the steering; he built the rudder stock and swim step from solid ash and the blade from hardwood plywood, into which he had already cast lead while pouring his ballast bricks, reasoning that a design good enough for **[Matt Layden](/matt-layden/index.md)** was good enough for him.[11] He glued an eighth-inch skin of hardwood plywood to each face of the three-quarter-inch blade to build up a wing-shaped cross-section, checked the 30 percent balance against a one-inch-gridded photo before drilling the pivot hole, and set the pull-up rope hole as high as possible to keep it from vibrating.[12] The rudder stock pivots on a removable aluminium tube riding on Teflon washers rather than conventional pintles, a system he prefers because it lets the whole rudder assembly rise when it touches bottom or the boat is beached, so the hull takes the load rather than the eyebolts; a small wood stop keeps the rudder from swinging forward of vertical.[13] The support piece beneath it, which doubles as a swim step, is shaped like an airfoil section underwater and built strong enough to take the boat's weight when she is hauled up a beach.[14] The folding, rimmed hatch that doubles as a cooking platform is sealed watertight with a bicycle inner tube.[15] The twin aluminium masts are raked about four inches aft, a figure he settled on after testing how much the aluminium bent under sail tension and consulting his sailmaker friend Benoit Tremblay of Concept Air, who made the sails.[16]

## Build technique

Some of his technique carries over from boat to boat. While the side panels are still flat and easy to reach, he draws the waterline on them — trivial while flat, in his view, and a real headache once the sides are curved up — and glues an extra eighth-inch layer of plywood into the front four feet of each side panel, where impacts are most likely.[17] For ballast, he casts his own removable lead bricks: he collects old wheel weights, melts them over a propane camp stove (he found butane does not run hot enough), skims the floating iron clips off with a slotted spoon, and pours the molten lead into a mould while wearing heavy gloves, since the lead sets faster than the wooden mould can burn.[18] The bricks came out to a bit over 60 lb per side on Question, set into the bottom of the two side storage bins.[19] He padded the bricks with Ensolite foam so they cannot bang around and add a little flotation, and totalled the boat's ballast at roughly 30 kg (66 lb) of lead per side, plus a battery pack on one side, a spare anchor on the other, and water jugs on each side.[20]

## Paradox, Enigma, and Gorfnik compared

He put Question through a deliberate capsize test soon after her first sail: he could not tip her sitting down, but standing and hiking out he laid her over until water briefly crossed the cabin window and still recovered; hiking out further, he fell out of the boat entirely and she flipped back upright on her own, and he climbed back aboard on the swim step and bailed her out with a bucket. He was clear that the Enigma, much smaller than the Paradox, does not share the Paradox's extreme, hard-to-capsize stability — on a Paradox, he notes, you can hang your whole weight out on the mast without coming anywhere close to going over.[21] Sailing the two boats together in 13 knots of wind, with an experienced racing friend aboard the Enigma and himself on the Paradox, he found the heavier, more stable Paradox could carry its full 130 square feet of sail while the more tender Enigma, leaned past 45 degrees, topped out around 66 square feet; the Enigma pointed at least as high as the Paradox, but the Paradox was faster, and sailing it at 13 knots felt relaxed enough that leaning the wrong way barely mattered, where the Enigma demanded constant attention.[22] After several multi-day sails and two camping trips in the Enigma — one in light air, one in a puffy 20 knots — sailed alongside a friend's Gorfnik, he judged the Enigma tender at first but stiffening as she heels, forgiving enough at 20 to 25 degrees that he was comfortable sailing her there; downwind the lighter Gorfnik keeps pace, but upwind the Enigma's pointing and hull shape leave the Gorfnik well behind. For camping he found the Gorfnik roomier and so stable that he could lean out and wash his hands while cooking, where the Enigma is stable enough to cook aboard but demands care not to move too fast; on safety, he rated the Gorfnik's nil secondary stability a real limit — leaned too far, or caught by a hard puff without releasing the sheet, she can flip instantly and without warning — against the Enigma, which he found would right herself from at least 50 degrees of heel, giving him time to react, and which he reckoned would spill him out before she filled with water; for beaching he preferred the Gorfnik, though the Enigma managed fine too. Paddling the Enigma disappointed him a little, making only about 2 knots at a normal pace against the Gorfnik's 1.75, a small enough margin that he found the Gorfnik's paddling position — leaning back against the cabin — more comfortable, and thought the Enigma's tenderness made her twitchier and harder to hold straight. His summary verdict was that the Gorfnik amounts to a square live-aboard canoe and the Enigma to more of a sailboat, while the Paradox remained, for him, the fastest and most relaxing of the three. The ketch rig itself left him unconvinced: once he had experienced the Paradox's infinite roller reefing, he found tying off reefing lines and shifting sail position between two masts a step backward, especially since the long chine runners make the boat comparatively insensitive to sail position anyway; the main reason he had chosen a ketch, freeing the cabin of an intrusive single mast, no longer felt necessary once the boat was built, and he considered reverting to a single lug sail with roller reefing.[23]

## Seamanship shared with the group

He has also passed on practical advice drawn from his own trips. To help a Paradox come about cleanly in strong wind, he keeps two gallons of water and a heavy anchor in the far-forward locker, a trick that works up to about 25 knots, above which he simply circles the boat around the other way instead.[24] He notes that because the Paradox's mast sits off-centre, uneven steering between tacks can be balanced by shifting the halyard's position on the yard rather than by moving the mast itself.[25] On multi-day trips through Québec's frequent stretches of rain, he keeps a Paradox's cabin dry by gluing a foam layer to the inside roof to cut condensation, stepping out only under an umbrella (also useful for moving the boat with a yuloh in light drizzle), opening the hatch about 18 inches to pass in and out without letting rain in, leaving wet footwear on the rear deck, and going through a roll of shop towels a week.[26]
