# Eerik Mee

**Eerik Mee** (byline **"Eerik"** on his blog) is a boatbuilder in **Tartu, Estonia**,[7] and the builder of **"[THU](/boats/thu.md),"** a [Paradox](/paradox.md) built **20% larger than the plans** and rigged as a **Chinese junk**, launched in 2017. He documented the whole build on a personal blog, `paradoxbuild.blogspot.com`, which is preserved as a first-class source at [references/eerik-mee-blog.md](/references/eerik-mee-blog.md); this page draws the builder and the build out of it.

He is notable in the archive for having actually built two things it had otherwise only argued about. THU is the **only scaled-up Paradox on record built and sailed** — the recurring "[larger Paradox](/design/larger-paradox.md)" realised, at almost exactly the 20% figures the mailing list had worked out years earlier — and it carries the **only purpose-designed cambered junk rig** on the design, as against the stock lug *converted* to junk on [Planktos](/boats/planktos.md).

## The build

Mee built THU from **July 2015**, scaling every measurement on the plans by **1.2** — "I multiplied just all measurements with 1.2, so yes, it is also wider and higher. LOA turned out about 5.05 m and width about 1.5 m."[1] The hull, chine runners, rudder, and a 6 m birdmouth mast were built through 2015–16; the cabin, deck, and fit-out followed, and the boat was **launched and christened "THU"** on an inland lake on **5 July 2017**.[2][3] Fitting-out continued for two more years — the ballast cast as lead pigs, a full electrical system and an electric outboard added — with more lake sailing in 2019.[4]

The build was **repeatedly paused for overseas work**: his projects took him away for long stretches — a Thailand project that "took about half a year longer than initially planned," later work on Bali and elsewhere in Indonesia, and a spell in Norway — each of which cost him a building or sailing season.[4] He built at home in **Estonia**, doing much of the work outdoors in a garden and garage and waiting on warm-enough weather for epoxy; the blog's own references fit an EU builder, citing EU rules on handling lead and the difficulty of sourcing the right aluminium alloy "in this country."[4]

## What he changed, and why

Mee's departures from the plans are the substance of his contribution, and he explained his reasoning in the blog's comment threads:

- **The 20% scale-up.** He sized the boat up uniformly to fit himself, arriving at **LOA ≈ 5.05 m, beam ≈ 1.5 m** — within centimetres of the worked 20% scale-up the list had carried since 2008.[1] The stock scantlings were kept; the spars scaled with the hull.
- **The cambered junk rig.** Rather than convert the stock lug, he designed a **six-panel cambered junk sail** from scratch, following **Arne Kverneland**'s method and drawing on the **Junk Rig Association**.[5] He first tried a flat-cut sample plan, "but I didn't like the looks of the boat; so I modified it towards Reddish type at roach. The HML is actually flat cut sail with not so good winward performance. It's the AK's camber that improves it."[5]
- **The mast moved forward.** To carry the taller (6 m vs ~4.8 m) junk rig in balance, he moved the mast **from aft of bulkhead 2 to as far forward of it as possible**, wanting more mast below deck and clearance between the transom and the upper sheet.[5] He intended the boat to be **moored, not trailered**, accepting the heavier mast's rig-up time in exchange.[5]
- **A worked-out ballast and systems boat.** THU carries **~240 kg of ballast** (twelve cast-lead pigs plus water ballast) in a compartment aft of bulkhead 3, and — unlike most Paradoxes — a **full electrical system and an electric outboard**.[6][4]

His full account of how she was built, equipped, and sails is at [THU](/boats/thu.md); the source itself at [Eerik Mee's blog](/references/eerik-mee-blog.md).

## The record's limits

Mee was **never a mailing-list correspondent** — his build ran a decade after the list era and was reported only on his own blog. The blog answers a few readers' questions and links out to the Junk Rig Association, but it is a single builder's record of a single, heavily modified boat, and the sailing it documents is a handful of day-sails on a lake rather than a cruise.[2][3] What survives is a completed, launched, and repeatedly sailed boat, tuned but not finished tuning when the record ends in 2019.[4]

He was, however, an active member of the [Facebook group](/references/paradox-facebook-group.md) from soon after it started, posting sailing videos and the first-hand sailing experience the blog's 2019 endpoint doesn't reach, and helping other builders — for instance advising a builder on rudder-gudgeon geometry — with that material folded into [THU](/boats/thu.md).[8][9]
