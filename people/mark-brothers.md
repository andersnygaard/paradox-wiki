# Mark Brothers

**Mark Brothers** is a Paradox owner in **Newcastle, New South Wales, Australia**, who returned to sailing after a 20-year absence by buying and refurbishing a secondhand [Paradox](/paradox.md) in 2022 — at the time one of only about four in the whole of Australia — and went on to become one of the most active modern owners recorded in the [Paradox Sailboat Facebook group](/references/paradox-facebook-group.md).[1]

A former competitive sailor whose racing career ran from dinghies through to J24 keelboats, he has called the Paradox the most fun boat he has owned and one that can go almost anywhere, and counted four Paradoxes in Australia at the time plus a single Enigma, his own being the only one in New South Wales.[2]

He came across the design while looking for a boat small enough to launch and sail single-handed and to fit the tight gap beside his home, found that Paradox had no Australian kit supplier or builder, and then found an old post on the Paradox mailing list suggesting one of the country's boats might have been offered for sale the previous year; a message to the apparent owner got a same-day yes, and since the boat was in South Australia — some 1520 km and a week's round-trip drive away — the seller agreed to tow it partway to meet him at the New South Wales border at Mildura. The boat he collected was seven years old and in generally good condition, and he set to work refurbishing her that same weekend, aiming to have her ready by Easter.[1]

## Refurbishing and the trailer

Once refurbished, the boat rides on a trailer converted from a jet-ski trailer, its drawbar considerably strengthened to take her weight; Brothers has put Paradox's practical towing weight at around 1200 lb, more than the 1000 lb a fellow prospective owner had assumed when shopping for a trailer.[3]

The boat came to him with a rigid foam layer glued under the decks but otherwise no built-in insulation. He retrofitted the main cabin's sides with EVA foam cut from exercise mats, applied over double-sided tape across the whole surface — the same technique he had already used successfully on the floorboards — chosen deliberately so the foam could be peeled back to inspect for rot if ever needed, though he judged the risk low since the whole interior is epoxied. He also fitted a small USB-powered dehumidifier to help control moisture inside whenever the hatch is shut.[4]

## Cruising

By October 2022 the boat was cruising: a 20-nautical-mile outing on **Lake Macquarie**, New South Wales, during which his racing background left him dissatisfied with twist in the sail — made worse by leading the sheet off the top of a post to keep it clear of the outboard through tacks and gybes — so he fitted a roller bearing to the boom end to carry the extra load of a vang system; logged by Navionics, the boat touched 6.2 knots and cruised consistently in the high 4s and 5s.[5] The vang itself hooks onto a wire hawse running along the rubbing strake, adjustable fore-and-aft for maximum downward pull, and is tacked from side to side by unhooking, easing, and re-hooking it, with a forward shockcord keeping the whole assembly clear of a gybe.[6]

The same month he took the boat onto open water on **Broken Bay**, reporting that she handled two hours of bouncing on the open sea better than her skipper did.[7]

He sailed repeatedly with the **Sydney RAID group**, including a weekend out in November 2022,[8] a Rathmines–Bolton Point–Pulbah Island raid on Lake Macquarie in December 2023 that he combined with local WWII history,[9] and another weekend away in November 2024.[10]

In January 2023, answering persistent criticism of the Paradox's windward ability, he sailed a full circle without tacking around the Captain James Cook Memorial Fountain on Lake Burley Griffin in Canberra.[11]

Further passages followed: a 13-nautical-mile coastal leg from **Swansea to Newcastle** in March 2023, sailed entirely upwind into a breeze that built from 12 to 30 knots;[12] a trip up the **Manning River** on the New South Wales mid-north coast in June 2023;[13] an October 2023 overnight raid to the Sand Islands that, once his companions turned back, became the longest day-sail of his life, during which he finally got the boat self-steering well enough to cook a steak and vegetables underway;[14] a run up the **Hawkesbury River** north of Sydney in August 2024;[15] and, in January 2024, his longest passage yet — more than 22 hours from Throsby Creek at Carrington, up Newcastle Harbour and the Hunter River through Tomago, Hexham, Heatherbrae, and Raymond Terrace to the old river port of Morpeth and back the next morning, through winds ranging from a measured 28 knots down to calm and every point of the compass, with wind-against-tide in the narrow reaches — an outing he judged had taught him more about handling a Paradox than the previous twenty months combined.[16]

## Rig tuning, weight, and the Mk3 sail

In December 2022 he cut the boat's sailing weight by around 150 kg — replacing a 150 amp-hour lead-acid gel battery with a 100 amp-hour lithium iron battery, discarding redundant gear, and carrying only 16 litres of water — and reported the boat transformed, making over 4 knots downwind in 10-15 knots of wind and 3.9 knots upwind in similar conditions the next day.[17]

In April 2023 he fitted a new, larger sail built specifically to lift the boat's light-wind performance without changing spars — a post dated the same day the sailmakers **Really Simple Sails** published their account of a custom **[Mk3 sail](/components/rig/mk3-sail.md)** built for a Paradox owner they identified only by first name, embedded in a video titled for the boat **Pamela B**.[18] Two posts elsewhere in the group's own record close that gap: one from fellow member **Anders Nygaard**, sketching his own new sail against Michael Storer's design for Mark Brothers's boat Pamela B as his benchmark,[19] and a comment under it from **Sylvain Bernier**, who had bought a Mk III sail from Really Simple Sails and identified it as the same design Storer built for Brothers.[20] Together they identify Brothers's boat as Pamela B and him as the reference owner for the Storer/Really Simple Sails sail — a notable modern alternative to Matt Layden's original standing-lug rig.

He kept tuning from there: engaging the boat's electric outboard at idle when sailing upwind for roughly a full knot of extra speed without losing the quiet of sailing, cutting the water ballast he carried by half, and enlarging the sail by lengthening the yard's leech — while, against a claim that a longer waterline alone would solve the boat's speed, working out that Paradox's roughly 13 ft waterline gives a hull speed near 4.8 knots and that a 25% longer waterline would lift that only to about 5.6 knots, well short of a proportional gain.[21]

## Other contributions

He engineered an anchoring system worked entirely from inside the cabin: the rode runs from a bow roller, where the anchor sits ready underway, back to a snapshackle at the aft end of the cabin, with the rope stowed in a rope-tidy bag on the rear deck, so dropping anchor is just a matter of releasing the snapshackle pin without going on deck at all — a broken tack strop once forced him forward anyway, to jury-rig a repair after anchoring in a sizeable seaway.[22]

He fitted one of the smaller Porta Potti-style toilets, stowed against the transom, where its two halves — seat/water tank and holding tank — fit the transom's internal beam almost exactly.[23]

Within the group he also served as an informal locator of Australia's small Paradox fleet, naming himself as the closest owner to a Melbourne-area enquirer from his home near Newcastle and counting, as of October 2022, two more boats in Perth and one in Brisbane, plus a separate Enigma on the Gold Coast.[24]
