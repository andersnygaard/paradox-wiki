# Tim McKinney

**Tim McKinney** is a Texas sailor who started the "**Paradox Sailboat**" Facebook group in **February 2019** — its founding statement, "a group interested in the Paradox Sailboat designed by Matt Layden, a little cruiser full of surprises," is the group's own charter, and its first real member was fellow owner [Sean Mulligan](/people/sean-mulligan.md).[1][2] He is not a builder but an **owner**: he acquired an already-built [Paradox](/paradox.md) in the fall of 2018, and over the following months he gave the group — and now the archive — its first candid, modern owner's assessment of what the boat is actually like to sail, modify, and live aboard.[3]

## The boat

McKinney's Paradox was built by **Michael Beebe** in **Rockport, Texas**, and was Beebe's **second** Paradox — Beebe being, on this record, a Texas builder of at least two of the boats.[3] Beebe built it as a **"convertible,"** with a **removable hardtop** and **conventional tiller steering** in place of the stock rope-and-yoke arrangement, and he had enjoyed sailing it with the top off and a short tiller in fair weather.[3][7]

## Steering experiments

McKinney found the inherited conventional steering uncomfortable to sail with once the aft window was back in and the tiller shortened to suit, and he set about redesigning it.[4] He weighed a push-pull tiller with a yoke at the rudder post against a yoke-and-rope arrangement, favoring the push-pull as closer to what he was used to on a sailboat while conceding rope steering would let him lock down a course, and he explicitly ruled out converting the boat back to Matt Layden's original rope steering.[4] What he built was a tiller in **square tubing with round tubing inside**, designed to clear the aft window when needed or pass through it for minor course changes.[7] The redesign did not work out: after his Texas 200 attempt he judged the non-standard steering a failure and concluded the boat needed to be **reengineered back to the original rope steering as designed**.[10][11]

## Electrical system

Ahead of his 2019 Texas 200 attempt, McKinney fitted a **20-watt solar panel** and, for a battery, a **lawnmower battery** as a stopgap.[6][9] The panel performed well, bringing the battery from 12.7 to 14.7 volts in three hours, and on the strength of that he planned to build a **lithium battery** with greater capacity.[9]

## The 2019 Texas 200

McKinney set out on the **2019 Texas 200** — a roughly 200-mile, mostly downwind coastal raid along the Texas coast — having gotten his newly acquired Paradox out on the water only once beforehand; he had run the event the previous year in a Compac 16 and had sailed the area before that in a Balboa 16.[5] The trip was **cut short by a snake bite** to his leg.[10][11] His report on the boat itself was candid rather than glowing, and stands as the archive's earliest first-hand modern owner's verdict on sailing a Paradox: the boat **sailed well and felt relatively fast**, adjusting sail area on the fly to match a companion's speed was "neat," and left to herself she would **stall head-to-wind and simply sit there** while he attended to other things.[11] Set against that, his non-standard steering **did not work at all**, boarding from muddy shores was awkward and made it hard to remove and rinse muddy boots before entering the cockpit, and he judged that a **wind scoop** would markedly improve sleeping comfort on warm nights.[10][11] He also noted the boat showed some reluctance tacking to one side, which he attributed to a mix of light wind, his leg injury, and the steering he had installed rather than to the boat itself.[10]
