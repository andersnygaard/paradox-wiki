# Andreas Westerberg

**Andreas Westerberg** is a Swedish amateur builder who built a [Paradox](/paradox.md) between late 2021 and 2024, working from plans he bought from Wanda Elliott after failing to reach Dave Bolduc by email.[1] He stands out in the archive for a **digital-first build method** no other recorded builder used: he **3D-modelled the entire boat from the plans' drawings** and **CNC-routed the bulkheads and other parts** to make the physical assembly easier, starting with a test-fitted set of bulkhead outlines within weeks of buying the plans.[2] That approach — modelling first, cutting by machine, proving fit digitally before committing wood — makes him the archive's exemplar of a CNC/3D-modelled Paradox build.

## 3D-modelling and the bulkhead curve

Early in the modelling work Westerberg ran into the plans' **5-7mm convex curve on the sides of bulkheads 1-3**, which he took to be a deliberate stiffening feature and which complicated the 3D model because, in his understanding, a true compound surface cannot be flattened for CNC cutting.[3] Other builders in the group corrected the premise rather than the measurement: the curve is not a compound surface at all but the natural, automatic result of the slight twist built into the side panels wherever a panel's bending axis is not parallel to the bulkhead edge — it follows from the designed shape rather than being drawn in as a separate feature.[3] He also 3D-modelled the mast components before cutting them, finding it harder than the hull to picture the parts correctly from the drawings alone.[4]

## Plywood, glassing, and construction choices

A regional **plywood shortage** left Westerberg unable to source 18mm stock for the bottom, so he bought eight sheets of 12mm instead; he judged the difference — about 1.4 kg/m² over 9mm — small enough that 12mm was fit even for the deck, a view other builders in the thread shared, noting that a lighter hull is fine provided it is glassed to the plans' schedule.[5] He **glassed the inside of the side panels and bottom before assembling the hull**, gaining laminating experience on flat panels ahead of the harder work on the assembled shape.[6] Bending the **chine logs** to the bow proved difficult — he could not get them to take the curve without breaking — so for the aft half he cut plywood to the finished shape instead of bending solid stock, a problem other builders met too, with suggestions ranging from softer construction-grade pine to splitting the log and laminating it in place.[7] He **scarfed the bottom sheets** himself with a simple router jig, roughing out the joint with the router and finishing by hand-sanding.[8]

On the outside bottom he built up a laminate of two layers of 600g biaxial glass and a layer of Diolen polyester cloth for abrasion resistance, backed by a single layer of 300g rowing weave on the inside, then primed with **Hempel Light Primer** ahead of painting.[9] He ran a small experiment on **resin-starved joints** — clamping a dab of unthickened West System epoxy as hard as he could between two pieces of fir plywood, on the reasoning that this should produce the worst-case starved joint if starving were a real risk — and posted the result to the group; other members' consensus was that clamping epoxy too hard is itself counterproductive, since it squeezes resin out of the joint rather than testing whether the substrate had absorbed it.[10]

## A watertight, transom-free layout

Partway through the build Westerberg departed from the plans' aft arrangement, sealing the **aft compartment** to serve as a flotation tank — adding, by his own estimate, over 300 litres/kg of buoyancy there and 400kg+ combined with the already-sealed forward compartment — specifically so he could avoid any holes in the transom.[11] That forced a re-designed steering system: with no tiller-shaft hole available, he ran block-and-tackle lines from each side of the rudder through sealed pipes into the cockpit, a reversed-throw arrangement he mocked up and refined with the group's input before building it into the boat.[12] For ballast he salvaged an old steel drill rod, roughly 250kg, that had been buried on his property since the 1980s, planning to cut it into bars for mounting brackets in the hull — a choice that also led him to confirm with compass manufacturers that a steel-ballasted hull's compass could still be compensated and calibrated.[13]
