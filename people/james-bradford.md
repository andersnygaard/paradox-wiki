# James (Bradford, Yorkshire, UK)

**James** (Yahoo handle `howgegoboat`; Bradford, Yorkshire, UK) began cutting bottom plywood in January 2008 for a lightened, car-topping, Enigma-style Paradox variant of his own design — the outcome of over a year floating and refining the same idea on the list. His own introduction to small, enclosed-cabin sailing came aboard a "sea tramp" — a Hartley ferro-cement hull with a deckhouse, moored at Hamble — later likened to "paradox except for scale." He already owns a 15 ft "summer breeze"-type boat of his own, "Summer Breeze," decked over with higher freeboard, similar to a boat called "Day Dreamer," and tried an early flexible-window pop-up-roof idea on her before adapting it to the Paradox/Enigma hybrid.

## Building a car-topping, Enigma-style Paradox

By January 2008 the years of floated car-topping-Paradox refinements (below) had become an actual build: bottom plywood was bought, and within days being cut.[1][2] The chine runners ("winglets") were planned in 3/4 in plywood, about 6 in wide and 3 ft long, sized for the higher aspect ratio Matt Layden has recommended; a soft top in the style of Enigma, with timber rails, was planned for the cabin, with a possible later conversion to a birdwatcher-style top; and a chine-runner science website was sought out and found for reference.[2][3] The link, on catamaran "anti-vortex panels," drew a reply distinguishing them from Paradox's own chine runners — see [how chine runners work — distinguished from catamaran anti-vortex panels](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md#whether-a-flat-bottom-is-essential-to-the-mechanism). A fellow UK builder asked what plan he was following and where he was based; no answer is recorded.[4] A rig question went along with the build news — whether a 65 sq ft sail was the right size, or whether trimming a foot off the boom would suit strong summer winds better, with a light spinnaker floated for calmer days — and a group meet, possibly at Poole Harbour, was proposed to the UK builders. See [Little Jim — a boom-trim question, a proposed Poole meet, and 2008 cruise plans with Bill Serjeant](/boats/little-jim.md#a-boom-trim-question-a-proposed-poole-meet-and-2008-cruise-plans-with-bill-serjeant) for the answer.[2]

A worked weight estimate followed: plywood at roughly 25 lb per 1/4 in sheet gives about three sheets' equivalent for the bottom and three more for the sides and decks, six sheets totaling 150 lb (75 kg); adding ten pieces of solid timber for chines and frames brought the estimate to about 100 kg (220 lb) — judged "not unrealistic" for car-topping if the loading system lifted only the bow or stern at a time, with stops fitted at each end of the roof-rack crossbar (a trailer was kept as the fallback). Non-structural interior fittings were left off at first, to see what was actually needed; buoyancy and ballast were planned as 5-litre and 20-litre containers.[5] Asking after Paradox's and Enigma's respective hull depths, and presuming Enigma the shallower of the two, the build's own 2 ft hull depth (already settled in the [bolt-off, invertible cabin-top](#a-bolt-off-invertible-cabin-top-the-originating-idea) revision above) was restated as a lot closer to Enigma's depth than to stock Paradox's — with the stated goal of combining Paradox's cabin comfort with the sailing performance and light weight of a Michael Storer "Goat Island Skiff."[6]

## A bolt-off, invertible cabin top: the originating idea

In October 2007 James proposed the design this build now follows: a lighter Paradox/Enigma hybrid with the cabin sides and roof bolting off the hull and inverting to stow inside for car-top carrying, refined over several follow-up messages — thinner plywood, bolted-and-sealed or hinged-and-folding glazing, and finally a reduced hull (five 1/4 in ply sheets, 15 ft long, 5 ft beam, 2 ft depth, a standing lug sail on an 11 ft mast) trading away the inside-steered rudder, inside furling, and watertight vent snorkel to save weight. See [car-topping conversion ideas (speculative)](/design/ideas/car-topping-conversion-idea.md) for the full development, including a UK builder's own competing removable-hardtop concept.

## Other contributions

- **Questioning Enigma's beam and chine wings.** Asked why the 4.6 m Enigma wasn't given a 4 ft beam at the chine, and whether that would let her sail faster lightly laden or plane on occasion, noting her higher-aspect chine wings and that the spray dodger works well. See [Enigma — the Enigma family: the 360 and the plywood-cabin 460](/matt-layden/enigma.md#the-enigma-family-the-360-and-the-plywood-cabin-460).
- **A windvane pointer.** Passed along a link to a simple, easy-to-understand self-steering vane design that works well on most points of sail but not on a run. See [self-steering](/operations/self-steering.md).
- **A barbecue-conversion cabin heater.** Described his own barbecue (narrower-flued, more pot-bellied, two doors) as a candidate cabin heater, convertible to top-loading with a flue adapter, and posted the specific model — a Landmann cast-iron barbecue. See [cabin heating — a charcoal or kerosene can heater, and the transom cook-stove alternative](/operations/cabin-heating.md#a-charcoal-or-kerosene-can-heater-and-the-transom-cook-stove-alternative).
- **Naming and refining a scaled-up Paradox.** Preferred "Paradox Max" as a name for a 20%-enlarged Paradox, corrected the worked length figure for plywood curvature, floated a 4 ft-chine/5 ft-deck beam-widening alternative, worked out pop-bottle ballast weight, and judged Matt Layden's Enigma 460 too narrow in the beam. See [Building Paradox larger — correcting the 20% figures for plywood curvature, and a beam-widening alternative](/design/larger-paradox.md#correcting-the-20-figures-for-plywood-curvature-and-a-beam-widening-alternative).
- **What makes a Paradox a Paradox.** Asked what the design's main constituent elements actually are, offering a five-point list: the inside sailing position, internal ballast, the simple lug rig, chine runners, and heavy displacement (except Enigma). See [paradox.md](/paradox.md#citations).
- **A UK "explorer boat" aside.** Asked in passing about a small explorer boat then sailing solo around the UK and whether it would make an interesting race. See [blue-water suitability](/design/blue-water-suitability.md#citations).
- **Paradox as an all-weather philosophy, not solely an offshore requirement.** Agreed Paradox is the pre-eminent micro-cruiser — "the minature HR" — while judging that crossing the North Sea in a force nine is "not our cup of tea" for many builders; the Paradox philosophy's essential elements are just as much the interior sailing position, simplicity of design, and self-righting ability as any specific offshore requirement.[7]
- **Weighing water ballast against Paradox's fixed heavy bottom, and an Enigma alternative.** Questioned the logic of carrying a heavy fixed bottom around on the trailer, in light winds, or up a beach, when the same righting effect could be substituted with water ballast instead; concluded that an all-weather micro-cruiser still favors Paradox, but that for some builders Enigma is the way to go instead. See [weight, ballast, and displacement — ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#ballast) for the fuller water-ballast-versus-fixed-weight discussion.[8]
