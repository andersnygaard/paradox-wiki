# Pete Martin

**Pete Martin** (Yahoo handle `randoneur`, e-mail `pdmartin@…`, u=25214471) is a UK builder in **Cheltenham, England**. After introducing himself to the list in October 2006, pricing UK plywood suppliers, and ordering plans the same day, he started construction on **1 December 2006** — a boat he intends to name **[JoHanna](/boats/johanna.md)**.

## Pre-build questions, and relaying Enigma 460 news

Ahead of starting, Pete weighed ballast material and water-tank choices with the list; the full exchange, and his own settled ballast plan, are recorded on [boats/johanna.md — ballast: lead against concrete, and a bladder-tank alternative](/boats/johanna.md#ballast-lead-against-concrete-and-a-bladder-tank-alternative). Pricing UK marine plywood and epoxy ahead of cutting in March 2007, Pete also relayed news, via the separate EnigmaBuilders Yahoo group, of Matt Layden's newly designed 4.6 m **Enigma 460**, then under prototype construction — see [Enigma — the Enigma family](/matt-layden/enigma.md#the-enigma-family-the-360-and-the-plywood-cabin-460).[9] His own plywood-pricing and MAS-epoxy/filler shopping questions are recorded in full on [boats/johanna.md — pricing UK marine plywood, and an MAS epoxy and filler choice](/boats/johanna.md#pricing-uk-marine-plywood-and-an-mas-epoxy-and-filler-choice).

Laying out bulkhead #3 once his plywood arrived in April 2007, Pete asked whether the plans' straight-drawn deck area on that bulkhead should be cut straight or given the same curve as the other bulkheads, and whether the rudder stock could safely be cut from the same sheet — both answered, along with a UK-plywood-width workaround for the rudder halves and a technique for bending stiff 18 mm ply to the bottom rocker. See [boats/johanna.md — bulkhead #3's deck-area curve, and the rudder-stock cutting order](/boats/johanna.md#bulkhead-3s-deck-area-curve-and-the-rudder-stock-cutting-order).[14][15][16][17]

## Timber sourcing and ring-nail sizing, May 2007

A local timber yard's failure to actually stock Douglas Fir, and a ring-nail sizing question, sent Pete first to Robbins of Bristol and then to another builder's own hardware experience. Full account on [boats/johanna.md — sourcing Douglas Fir, and sawn versus planed timber](/boats/johanna.md#sourcing-douglas-fir-and-sawn-versus-planed-timber) and [boats/johanna.md — ring-nail sizing, and a bronze-nail offer](/boats/johanna.md#ring-nail-sizing-and-a-bronze-nail-offer).

Over the following Bank Holiday weekend, Pete finished cutting all of JoHanna's bulkheads and panels and dry-mocked them up, a result encouraging enough to suggest the hull would indeed fit together at final assembly — a milestone that drew unsolicited advice on bottom-panel thickness for a stitch-and-glue build, which Pete declined in favor of building close to the plans as bought, pointing instead to the Enigma 460 as the option for a genuinely lighter boat. See [boats/johanna.md — all bulkheads and panels cut, and a dry mockup](/boats/johanna.md#all-bulkheads-and-panels-cut-and-a-dry-mockup) and [hull planking — how thick does the bottom really need to be?](/construction/hull-planking.md#how-thick-does-the-bottom-really-need-to-be).[23][24]

## A settled ballast plan, and a Milford Haven maiden voyage

Weighing in on a fellow UK owner's tank-leak troubles, Pete confirmed he had decided against the built-in tanks as designed long before, planning instead to use either a flexible bladder tank or two, or simply old soft-drink bottles, making up the rest of the ballast weight with lead and stores.[25] For cleaning a flexible bladder tank, flushing it through periodically with a bleach such as Milton was offered as the practical answer, reasoning that with no breather to let air in as the tank drains, the water inside should stay comparatively fresh — the same reason wine and beer boxes keep longer once opened than a bottle does; a screw-down plate sized to remove the bag itself for cleaning was the planned fitting.[26]

The exchange also carried Pete's own maiden-voyage planning: with a build not expected to reach the water until around 2009, he was weighing **Milford Haven** for a shakedown cruise, and asked in passing whether Chichester Harbour dries out extensively at half tide the way he understood it to.[26] Milford Haven was endorsed as a good choice, alongside a competing recommendation for Poole Harbour instead, for combining plenty of quiet corners with large enough open stretches of water to try every point of sail without being confined to narrow channels, and unusually accommodating tides.[27][28]

## Construction started, 1 December 2006

Pete built a jig for laminating deck beams on 1 December 2006, the date he recorded as his build's official start — for a boat he expects to become **"JoHanna."**[4] The announcement drew congratulations from [Jeff UK](/people/jeff-doryman.md), who asked whether the build would be at home and in a garage or outside.[5] For Pete's workspace, early construction questions (cabin-beam length, tiller offsets, sheer-clamp notch, bulkhead #1, cabin-beam jig bevels), and the answers they drew from Bill Serjeant, gilberj55, and Alastair, see [boats/johanna.md](/boats/johanna.md). Pete also asked whether any video existed of a Paradox under sail, having found Enigma and Little Cruiser footage but none of a Paradox; Jack Gardiner offered to repost torrent links he had previously listed. See [boats/johanna.md — a query about Paradox video](/boats/johanna.md#a-query-about-paradox-video).[8] After a long gap, Pete finally sorted a boat shed and cut cardboard bulkhead mock-ups to size, raising a run of cutting, beveling, and interior-wood questions answered in full — including a Mary Rose shipbuilding anecdote — at [boats/johanna.md — a boat shed sorted, and open cutting and bulkhead-curve questions](/boats/johanna.md#a-boat-shed-sorted-and-open-cutting-and-bulkhead-curve-questions).[11] Once the bulkhead mock-ups were actually set out and joined together in their correct positions, the boat's true size came to life for Pete; with plywood ordered and his boat shed proving workable once its doors were opened for airflow on a hot weekend, he turned to deciding between West and MAS epoxy for the real build. See [boats/johanna.md — the cardboard mock-ups joined, and an epoxy decision pending](/boats/johanna.md#a-boat-shed-sorted-and-open-cutting-and-bulkhead-curve-questions).[12]

With his bulkheads largely finished, Pete turned to the vent trunk, asking how its top attaches to bulkhead 2 and how the drain pipe is installed; full answer and citations at [rig and sail — attaching the vent trunk top to bulkhead 2](/components/rig/mast.md#attaching-the-vent-trunk-top-to-bulkhead-2) and [boats/johanna.md — the vent trunk top and drain pipe](/boats/johanna.md#the-vent-trunk-top-and-drain-pipe).

## A vent trunk drain pipe question

Weeks later, weighing UK metric PVC tube against the plans' 1 in nominal drain-pipe specification, Pete asked whether leftover 22 mm bathroom tube would be adequate or whether the harder-to-drill 32 mm size was worth the trouble — settling, after a US pipe-sizing explanation and a firsthand report of 22 mm holding up under real sailing conditions, on the 22 mm tube already in hand. Full exchange at [boats/johanna.md — vent trunk drain pipe sizing](/boats/johanna.md#vent-trunk-drain-pipe-sizing-metric-tube-against-the-plans-1-in-nominal-pipe).[43]

## A shrinking workspace, and congratulating a new North London builder

Pete raised his own sheer and chine strakes' beveling question the same week — see [sheer strakes — bevel the sheer strake and chine log after fitting, not before](/components/sheer-strakes.md#bevel-the-sheer-strake-and-chine-log-after-fitting-not-before) for the answer.[31] Congratulating [Ian Lang](/people/ian-lang.md) on starting his own build, Pete sympathized directly over the shrinking workspace a growing pile of plywood brings — he was by then tripping over himself all week trying to shape his own chine and sheer strakes, laid out flat and occupying the full length of his building space, and needing to rearrange everything into a more shipshape stack before he could move freely, a task repeatedly interrupted by rain whenever he tried clearing up outside. He suggested Ian stall his competing china-cabinet project until winter, since the weather would likely be unsuitable for that kind of joinery in any case.[32]

## A hull-gluing-sequence question

With his cleats bevelled and locating nails already in use, Pete asked the list whether a Paradox hull needs to be glued up in a single sitting or can safely be done in stages without losing symmetry — answered in full with two complementary techniques (a one-session glue-up working outward from bulkhead 3, and a wire-nail tooling-pin method for staged assembly) at [hull assembly sequence — gluing up the hull](/construction/hull-assembly-sequence.md#gluing-up-the-hull-one-sitting-or-several-and-locating-the-bulkheads-with-temporary-pins) and [boats/johanna.md — hints on gluing up the hull](/boats/johanna.md#hints-on-gluing-up-the-hull). Pete confirmed he was already using nails as locators and expected reassembly with glue to go smoothly once everything was checked true at the dry-fit stage, with the vent trunk and an epoxy coat on the bulkheads and side panels still to finish first.[33][34][35][36][37][38]

The epoxy-coating question itself was answered separately: the bulkheads and side panels can just as easily be coated after the wet assembly as before, provided the deck is not yet fitted, giving a cleaner bonding surface for the epoxy joints, chine logs, and sheer strakes. See [hull assembly sequence — epoxy-coating the bulkheads and side panels: before or after the wet assembly](/construction/hull-assembly-sequence.md#epoxy-coating-the-bulkheads-and-side-panels-before-or-after-the-wet-assembly).[39]

## Answering a skin-irritation question

Answering Ian Lang's epoxy skin-rash question, Pete reported no problems of his own using **MAS epoxy**, wearing a mask only some of the time depending on ventilation, and asked whether Ian was certain the epoxy itself, rather than a latex-glove allergy, was to blame. See [epoxy brands, chemistry, and cure behavior — responses to a skin sensitivity case](/construction/epoxy-brands-and-chemistry.md#responses-to-a-skin-sensitivity-case-alternative-brands-resin-types-and-glovemask-technique) for the fuller thread.[45] JoHanna's sheer strakes were installed the same week; see [boats/johanna.md](/boats/johanna.md#sheer-strakes-installed).

## A water-tank and rear-sole-plate question

Weighing the built-in water tanks against the standing case against drinking from an epoxy-lined tank, Pete had by January 2008 collected enough 2-litre pop bottles to keep him watered for well over a week, and asked whether Faith's own site showed a different rear-sole-plate arrangement than the plans' specified continuous brass hinge; also still tempted to fit a drop-in washboard at the rear of the cabin trunk, as on Little Cruiser and NOLI. Full account and citations at [boats/johanna.md — water tanks reconsidered, and a rear-sole-plate question](/boats/johanna.md#water-tanks-reconsidered-and-a-rear-sole-plate-question).[51]

Thanking Al and Bill for their answers, Pete settled on leaving the built-in tanks out in favor of lifting or hinged sole plates, remained undecided between a V-shaped and a parallel-sided washboard pending a scrap-ply mock-up, and floated building two sealed, vented aft-quarter lockers for 5-litre fuel cans against a possible future outboard — doubling, if never used for fuel, as 5 litres of sealed reserve buoyancy. Considering a Lexan washboard along the lines of Little Cruiser's, he judged a double washboard not really worth it on Paradox given its 260 mm board height. Full account and citations at [boats/johanna.md — washboard design](/boats/johanna.md#washboard-design-a-vee-shape-a-divided-board-and-a-tapered-opening) and [boats/johanna.md — drop board options](/boats/johanna.md#drop-board-options-a-photographed-technique-and-a-lexan-alternative-considered).[52][53]

## Deck-beam fairing, corner posts, and paint choices closed out

Pete's earlier open questions on the low deck beam at bulkhead 1, the sheet-10 corner-post detail, and interior paint were all answered within a few days; he settled on a Dulux "Super Grip" primer over the Bulls Eye 1-2-3 alternative already recommended, available through his wife's staff discount, and went looking for cheap camping mats to line JoHanna's cabin sides. Full account and citations at [boats/johanna.md — deck-beam fairing, corner posts, and interior paint answered](/boats/johanna.md#deck-beam-fairing-corner-posts-and-interior-paint-answered).[54]

Days later, Pete confirmed he would get the camping mats regardless of their exact source, settled on battery-powered LED lighting over wired cabin electrics, and answered a fellow builder's own masthead-light search with a solar-garden-light suggestion. Full account and citations at [boats/johanna.md — camping mats, battery-powered lighting, and a masthead solar light suggested](/boats/johanna.md#camping-mats-battery-powered-lighting-and-a-masthead-solar-light-suggested).[55]

Raising the transom-mounted-mainsheet-and-motor question again, Pete relayed a rerouted mainsheet setup reducing the fouling considerably and asked for further comment, and separately reported a temporary deck fit turning up a stubbornly stiff foredeck curve. Full account and citations at [boats/johanna.md — a mainsheet question relayed to Glen Maxwell, and the foredeck's stubborn curve](/boats/johanna.md#a-mainsheet-question-relayed-to-glen-maxwell-and-the-foredecks-stubborn-curve).[56]

Following up, Pete pointed to the group's files section for the vane-gear photo, restated his agreement that the boom-end attachment is the real fouling culprit, and pressed on with the foredeck's tight curve near the stem — having left no "wings" of his own for leverage, giving a "Turkish slipper" profile that called for more grinding, with a steamer session considered as a next step. Full account and citations at [boats/johanna.md — a mainsheet question relayed to Glen Maxwell, and the foredeck's stubborn curve](/boats/johanna.md#a-mainsheet-question-relayed-to-glen-maxwell-and-the-foredecks-stubborn-curve).[57]

## A 2013 trailer and outboard-bracket footnote

By 2013 Pete's own JoHanna carried a **De Graaff SB 730/2 ADJ** road trailer, by then discontinued, fitted with flat rubber rollers to spread the load better than a standard roller trailer — information Pete passed on to a fellow UK owner sourcing an equivalent trailer for a different Paradox.[58] Pete had also fitted his outboard with a cage to cut down the mainsheet snagging the propeller, a fix judged only partly effective, and had since moved his sheet leads from the transom to the aft deck to help solve the problem further.[59]
