# William H. Longyard

**William H. Longyard**, of Winston-Salem, NC, has no [Paradox](/paradox.md) of
his own. Like [David LeBlanc](/people/david-leblanc.md), he earns a place among
the bundle's people on the strength of recurring, substantive technical
contributions rather than a build or purchase. His distinctive value is a
firsthand knowledge of Swedish micro-cruiser designer **Sven Yrvind** and his
boats, and he is separately the author of the small-boat-voyaging book
***A Speck on the Sea***.

## Contributions

- **Sven Yrvind expertise.** Longyard is the readiest source of firsthand
  knowledge on how Yrvind's "Bris" boats compare to Matt Layden's Paradox. He
  owns a copy of Yrvind's book in Swedish (read to him by a Swedish friend) and
  had spoken with Yrvind personally about his building process. The two
  designers' hulls are hydrodynamically similar and both are deliberately
  under-powered, but Yrvind's are technologically far more complex — a
  composite fabric/resin skin built to flex and "bounce off" a floating log or
  container at sea rather than crack, and a sail plan split into multiple
  small component sails rather than Layden's single simple rig. Longyard later
  received his own copy of Yrvind's new book, KONSTRUKTOREN — independently of
  Kjell Karlsson's chapter translation — and posted its illustrations to the
  group, including one of Sven's current project drawn by Matt Layden; see [the
  "BRIS Paradox" — Sven Yrvind's book, translated: the Matt Layden/Paradox
  chapter](/boats/bris-paradox.md#sven-yrvinds-book-translated-the-matt-laydenparadox-chapter).
  See also
  [blue-water suitability and comparison to Sven Yrvind's boats](/design/blue-water-suitability.md)
  and [the "BRIS Paradox"](/boats/bris-paradox.md): substituting epoxy for
  Yrvind's specified resin risks working against the hull's intended
  flexibility, with vinylester proposed as a better substitute (Divinycell's
  manufacturer is the recommended source of advice). The fuller mechanical
  reasoning behind that flex is that a Divinycell/glass/flexible-resin
  sandwich lets a Yrvind hull rebound rather than crack when struck, the same
  principle behind a flexing airliner wing — while Paradox's plain plywood
  hull has no rigidity problem of its own to solve; if extra strength is
  wanted regardless, glassing the inside as well as the outside gives an
  I-beam effect. See
  [the "BRIS Paradox" — why Yrvind specified a flexible, not rigid, sandwich](/boats/bris-paradox.md#why-yrvind-specified-a-flexible-not-rigid-sandwich).
  Yrvind's own website, `www.yrvind.com`, is a further source of photos of his
  boats and building work. Yrvind's own account of the integrated
  rudder-transom system planned for his new boat judged it suitable for
  Paradox as well — see
  [rudder and chine experiments — Sven Yrvind's own integrated
  rudder-transom system](/design/rudder.md#sven-yrvinds-own-integrated-rudder-transom-system).
  A group project to translate Yrvind's full book into English drew a reality
  check from Longyard's own conversations with Yrvind, who was too busy to
  translate or co-author an English edition himself; three of Longyard's own
  contacts joined the volunteer-translator pool, with his own copy of the book
  offered for photocopying. Yrvind ultimately chose a professional translator
  over the volunteer crowd-effort, at which point Longyard proposed offering
  his own publisher the translated Paradox chapter as a sample — judging a
  finished, volunteer-translated manuscript far better economic sense to
  present to a publisher than a from-scratch proposal, a view Kjell Karlsson
  agreed closed out the group's translation project. See [the "BRIS
  Paradox" — a proposed group project to translate Sven Yrvind's full book
  into English](/boats/bris-paradox.md#a-proposed-group-project-to-translate-sven-yrvinds-full-book-into-english).
- **Auxiliary outboard fuel conversion.** A generic small-engine method for
  running an outboard from an external tank: the tank feeds a 12V electric
  fuel pump and a fuel-pressure regulator plumbed ahead of the carburetor. See
  [auxiliary outboard power](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md).
- **Thin-ply reassurance.** In the debate over a secondhand Paradox built with
  undersize (6 mm rather than 12 mm) side ply, the encouraging precedent of
  **"Shrimpy,"** Shane Acton's very thinly planked circumnavigating sloop
  (Acton also wrote *Shrimpy Sails Again* about a UK-to-Brazil voyage). See
  [Help! — undersize side panels](/construction/undersize-side-panel-ply.md).
- **Hammerite paint sourcing.** Hammerite works well as a safe-handling
  coating on freshly cast lead ballast bricks — 15 years of use found it
  "bulletproof" — but it is hard to find in the US, with eastwoodco.com the
  only known supplier, and only for the spray version. See
  [chine runner construction](/chine-runners/construction.md#casting-lead-ballast-into-the-runners).
- **Spray-in foam sourcing.** `TekSupply.com` is a source for high-volume
  urethane spray-in insulation/flotation foam in refrigerant-type containers,
  an alternative after canned "Great Stuff" and canned two-part expanding foam
  both proved unsatisfactory. See [plywood grade and foam
  flotation debate — spray-in foam versus sheet foam for
  insulation](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#spray-in-foam-versus-sheet-foam-for-insulation).
- **Bronze hardware.** Bought leftover bronze rod and pipe stock from
  [Glen C. Maxwell](/people/glen-maxwell.md)'s June 2002 offer to the group
  (priced at **$14.30** plus tube and postage). See
  [lower rudder fitting — bearing material options](/construction/rudder-bearing-materials.md).
- **UK boating-magazine recommendation.** Two UK small-boat magazines,
  **Watercraft** and **Practical Boat Owner**, cover boats and boatbuilding
  more relevant to a small-boat budget and interests than the "big American
  glossies" like WoodenBoat — echoing
  [David LeBlanc](/people/david-leblanc.md)'s own earlier preference for
  Watercraft over Classic Boat on similar grounds. See
  [off-topic cross-posts — a Classic Boat vs. Watercraft aside](/references/off-topic-cross-posts.md#the-magazine-citations-and-a-classic-boat-vs-watercraft-aside).
- **Whole-hull steel construction.** The fullest technical case on record for
  building a Paradox entirely in steel calls for 12-gauge plate with added
  stringers/frames over thicker 3/16 in, steel-tube bulkheads that could open
  up the mast bulkhead, and square-tube gunnels — trading simpler, faster
  construction for a hull harder to damage or hole. See
  [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative) — whole-hull steel
  construction](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md#whole-hull-steel-construction-gauge-framing-and-damage-tolerance).
  Field-repair and fabrication detail rounds this out: a small oxy/acetylene
  kit permits gas-welding away from shore power; steel boats as small as 9 ft
  cruise Amsterdam's canals, confirming a 14 ft Paradox "unquestionably
  suitable"; cheap plasma cutters allow fast plate-cutting; and ordinary red
  oxide primer, not epoxy, suffices for finishing. See [aluminum and steel
  Paradox (speculative) — field welding, and confirmation from real small
  steel
  boats](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md#field-welding-and-confirmation-from-real-small-steel-boats).
  The square-foot weight figures underpinning the debate: 12-gauge steel runs
  4.375 lb/sq ft against ~1 lb/sq ft for 1/4 in plywood, offset by needing
  much less material for equal strength. A parallel with 1903-1933 aircraft
  construction is instructive — steel fuselages displaced wood there on the
  strength of weight-adjusted strength and build speed — and the term "oil
  canning" traces to early-20th-century English petrol tins whose thin folded
  sides would pucker. Composite iron/wood hulls were commonplace by the
  mid-18th century, and modern warships still pair steel hulls with aluminum
  superstructures; Sven Yrvind's flexible composite construction is judged
  the better answer to collision protection generally, though slower to build
  than a steel hull, which could be fabricated in as little as a couple of
  days. See [aluminum and
  steel Paradox (speculative) — gauge, weight, and the case for steel over
  wood
  generally](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md#gauge-weight-and-the-case-for-steel-over-wood-generally)
  and [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative) — composite wood/steel
  hulls, and steel's fabrication
  practicalities](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md#composite-woodsteel-hulls-and-steels-fabrication-practicalities).
- **Auto-body-gauge comparison corrected.** Most auto-body panels run 20 or
  22 gauge steel, where the 12-gauge (.10 in) plate proposed for a Paradox
  hull is roughly three times thicker — spray-in insulation remains a good
  idea regardless. See
  [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative) — DC's rebuttal: why a steel
  Paradox would be "a horrible
  mistake"](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md#dcs-rebuttal-why-a-steel-paradox-would-be-a-horrible-mistake).
- **A real steel-dinghy photo.** A 9 ft Dutch steel dinghy — a common
  motor-barge tender on the Rhine and Danube and throughout Holland, where
  small steel boatbuilding is itself a common trade — stands as concrete
  real-world precedent for a steel Paradox. See [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative)
  — a real-world precedent: small steel dinghies on European
  canals](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md#a-real-world-precedent-small-steel-dinghies-on-european-canals).
  Steel poses construction challenges "no more so than in wood," making the
  choice a matter of personal preference rather than one correct answer — a
  fear of experimenting with design changes would have left boatbuilders
  "still using coracles." See [aluminum and steel Paradox
  (speculative) — a builder's case against steel, on design-philosophy
  grounds](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md#a-builders-case-against-steel-on-design-philosophy-grounds).
  **Hammerite** — the coating used on North Sea oil rigs — serves as a
  simple, non-exotic top coat for steel boat builders generally. See
  [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative) —
  the design-philosophy objection escalates into a personal
  dispute](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md#the-design-philosophy-objection-escalates-into-a-personal-dispute).
- **Buoyancy fundamentals, and a steel/wood-deck construction caution.** A
  basic rule of thumb: about 55 lb of buoyancy per cubic foot of foam. For a
  steel-hull-with-wood-deck design, a rough steel panel-weight estimate runs
  4.3 lb/sq ft, and wood and steel must never be left in direct contact,
  needing full coverage with glue/bedding compound or heavy tape at any
  joint between them. See [plywood grade and foam
  flotation debate — buoyancy fundamentals: positive, negative, and
  neutral](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#buoyancy-fundamentals-positive-negative-and-neutral)
  and [plywood grade and foam flotation debate — wood decks over a steel
  hull, and material-contact
  cautions](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#wood-decks-over-a-steel-hull-and-material-contact-cautions).

The steel debate ultimately reframes the whole material question as a
non-issue: plans-built, stitch-and-glue, or steel all work fine because the
underlying Paradox design is sound — a view extending even to ferro-cement or
animal hides, since designers **Phil Bolger, Paul Fisher, and Jay Benford**
have all offered plans letting builders pick their own building material.
Stitch-and-glue stands as steel's practical construction alternative. See
[aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative) — a stitch-and-glue Paradox as
steel's practical
alternative](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md#a-stitch-and-glue-paradox-as-steels-practical-alternative).

A tutorial exists for converting **Hull program** output files — the nesting
software introduced to the list by Glen Maxwell — into **AutoCAD**; the
Paradox's own offsets were converted via VRML/DXF into IntelliCAD and posted
for builders, with a DXF file judged **easier for most builders to work
with** than the native Hull file. See [panel layout and nesting — software for
nesting and converting the offsets](/construction/panel-layout.md#software-for-nesting-and-converting-the-offsets-hull-vrml-and-cad).
See also [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative) —
epilogue](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md#epilogue-why-materials-questions-draw-pushback-and-a-belated-apology).

A low-effort alternative to a scratch-built wooden mast is **irrigation
tubing** — used at 3 in diameter with excellent results on an earlier 17 ft
boat — cheap, light, and "ready to use" straight from a yellow-pages
irrigation supplier. See [rig and
sail — mast material choices](/design/rig.md#mast-material-choices).

A twin-rudder proposal aimed at mounting an outboard on the centerline is not
worth the added complexity and drag on a transom as narrow as Paradox's,
given the rare use a motor gets. See [rudder and chine experiments — a twin-rudder proposal for
a centerline outboard](/design/rudder.md#a-twin-rudder-proposal-for-a-centerline-outboard-judged-not-worth-the-complexity).

On self-steering, the fullest comparison on record between electric
auto-pilot and wind-vane self-steering favors auto-pilot generally for its
lower cost, simplicity, and modest power needs, while conceding a
wind-powered vane suits longer voyages better despite the reliability and
complexity problems simple designs tend to develop on small boats. Fitting a
trim tab to Paradox's specifically **folding** rudder calls for a Bowden-type
flexible cable to operate the tab without sacrificing the fold-up feature.
See
[self-steering — auto-pilot versus wind-vane self-steering: a practical
comparison](/operations/self-steering.md#auto-pilot-versus-wind-vane-self-steering-a-practical-comparison)
and [self-steering — a trim-tab wind vane and Paradox's folding
rudder](/operations/self-steering.md#a-trim-tab-wind-vane-and-paradoxs-folding-rudder).

A secondhand aluminum Skipper 12 dinghy mast is workable as a Paradox spar —
an aluminum mast of roughly equivalent diameter to the plans' wooden mast
does the same job, provided its minor-axis width matches the plans' mast
width — but the whole length should be checked for signs of prior weakness
first (circumferential holes, a straightened former bend). The general case
for aluminum masts: lighter, more reliable, no maintenance, easy hardware via
pop-rivet. A practical wiring tip: tie off any mast wire into "Y" shapes with
cable ties roughly every foot to stop it "pinging" against the mast at
night. See [rig and sail — reusing a secondhand dinghy
mast](/design/rig.md#reusing-a-secondhand-dinghy-mast) and [rig and sail — a
proposed birdsmouth-building
tutorial, and existing published
guides](/design/rig.md#a-proposed-birdsmouth-building-tutorial-and-existing-published-guides).

On a proposed seatbelt for heavy weather: many cabin injuries come from
**flying objects**, not from the body sliding around, so any locker built to
house restraint gear needs to be secure enough to stay shut in a knockdown —
sharp locker corners are better rounded with a router and padded (pipe
insulation is one option) rather than left square. See [heavy-weather
tactics — a seatbelt idea, floated but not
built](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#securing-the-crew-during-a-knockdown).

Paradox's keel-less, sharpie/dory-type hull suits riding out steep waves for
the same reason old dory fishermen valued the lack of a keel: a **keeled**
dory slipping sideways down a wave's face can "trip" over its own keel, a
risk Paradox's flat, keel-less hull does not share. The same keel-less logic
has an ocean-rowing precedent in **John Ridgway and Chay Blyth**, who rowed
the Atlantic in 1966 in their dory-like rowboat *English Rose* without a
keel. See [heavy-
weather tactics — why a keel-less, dory-like hull resists tripping in steep
waves](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#why-a-keel-less-dory-like-hull-resists-tripping-in-steep-waves).

For a steel Paradox's weight calculations, an alternative to solid round
stock or square-tube framing dispenses with tubing altogether: bend frames
from 0.10 in plate on a brake into 2 in x 1 in "L" shapes, weld them together
into a complete frame, and weld the hull skin to the flange's outer edge —
an approach with no enclosed cavity to trap water, unlike a tube frame's
hollow interior. See [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative) — a welded
angle-flange framing method, with no water
traps](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md#a-welded-angle-flange-framing-method-with-no-water-traps).

On sizing cabin windows for a more open-water-capable boat, a Siren 17 refit
showed that windows get stove in regardless of material thickness (Kenichi
Horie's own story is a further example), so several smaller windows are a
more practical choice than a few large ones, and aluminum angle brackets
with a rubber shock buffer add real protection to long windows that are
kept. See [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative) — stretching the cabin
top and sizing windows for open-water
use](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md#stretching-the-cabin-top-and-sizing-windows-for-open-water-use).

An alternative anchor-locker approach to [DC](/people/dc.md)'s uses a
conventional bow anchor well with the chain stowed low for stability, a
split forward window to reach the foredeck without going out on it, and a
mast-trunk drain with an in-line strainer. See
[ground tackle and anchoring — a flush anchor well, weight distribution,
and drainage](/operations/ground-tackle.md#a-flush-anchor-well-weight-distribution-and-drainage).

Recommended reading for Paradox builders includes three boat-construction
books by the English amateur boatbuilder and writer Percy Blandford —
*Practical Boatman*, *Build Your Own Boat*, and *Modern Sailmaking*, all
written between the late 1940s and 1970s and out of print, but findable
through used-book sites such as abe.com and alibris.com. Blandford is "the
quintessential English amateur," always finding the least expensive,
cleverest way of doing things. See
[DC — contributions and stories](/people/dc.md#contributions-and-stories).

A Paradox is judged unlikely to plane on a larger outboard given its hull
shape; see

## Author of *A Speck on the Sea*

Longyard authored ***A Speck on the Sea*** (McGraw-Hill, 375 pages, 155
photographs), a book of small-boat ocean-crossing stories spanning over 70
voyagers and boats from Roman times to the present, written for readers of
small craft rather than the yachting or naval-history audience most such
books target, and covering voyagers' equipment, successes, and mistakes. It
is of likely interest to fans of both Matt Layden's Paradox and Sven
Yrvind's Bris boats. [Dave and Mindy Bolduc](/people/bolduc/dave.md) helped
along the way, including with donated photographs, and David Beard also
lent photographs used in the book; signed copies were available directly by
mail for $22. The book sold well enough to prompt hopes of a sequel,
which Longyard hoped could become, with its predecessor, "THE bible of small
craft voyaging" — though a planned Paradox voyage for that next edition
never made it into the first book. Readers who finished it, including
[Glen Maxwell](/people/glen-maxwell.md), praised it as a book they
"couldn't put it down," and it drew unprompted recommendations citing the
amphibious-jeep circumnavigation story among its highlights; Kjell Karlsson
called it "fantastic for us microcruisers in spe." See
[the "BRIS Paradox" — why Yrvind specified a flexible, not rigid,
sandwich](/boats/bris-paradox.md#why-yrvind-specified-a-flexible-not-rigid-sandwich).

Among its most notable episodes: the **City of Ragusa** story, a research
trail that took Longyard to Liverpool to investigate on location and drew
continuing interest in Croatia, New Zealand, and from a UK television
company, its mysteries not yet fully solved. Small-boat voyaging is framed
as a neglected field even though most boaters today sail small boats poorly
served by the "glossy" yachting magazines, and Paradox is placed in a
lineage of small "capsule" voyaging boats running back through the 1965-85
golden age of Gerry Spiess and Serge Testa to **Bill Verity's Nonoalca** —
Matt Layden's designs judged predicated on Verity's own work. A **Swedish
microcruiser builder known as "Sebastian,"** an acolyte of Sven Yrvind's, is
noted as deserving fuller treatment in any future edition beyond the brief
mention given him in the first edition (page 341).

The book's fullest account of capsule-boat provisioning holds that a voyager
stows food the way a grocery stockboy racks a case of 24, fitting 10-12 such
cases without using much room; a 75-day passage realistically needs only
about 30 gallons of water, since most voyagers are too busy underway to eat
much and tend to stuff their boats' interiors to bursting, eating and
drinking their way to more space as the voyage goes on. Robert Manry's book
*Tinkerbelle* has excellent interior-cutaway drawings showing how such a
boat is packed. Cooking aboard is typically a primus stove on a homemade
gimbal system; alcohol fuel is dangerous aboard a small boat specifically
because its flame is nearly invisible if it catches something alight.
Paradox is judged not necessarily an ocean crosser, but with ample space to
provision for a long passage regardless.
