# Derek Clark

**Derek Clark** is a UK Paradox builder from Wolverhampton whose boat, **["Enuf"](/boats/enuf.md)**, was renamed from an earlier working name, **"So-So,"** on his wife's suggestion. A diabetic, he weighed that condition directly against relying on sculling alone for auxiliary power, choosing to keep an outboard as well as build a yuloh.[1] He built "Enuf" over roughly two years starting in September 2004, launched her in August 2006, and sold her the following month once the physical exertion of sailing proved too risky for his condition.

Full build narrative — ballast layout, hull turning and a void repair, the tack-strut/boom-axle pivot joint, and the "three-tone green with a yellow bottom" paint scheme — lives on [boats/enuf.md](/boats/enuf.md).

## Community role

Derek was an active, good-humored participant in the list's technical threads and its running banter alike. See [boats/enuf.md](/boats/enuf.md#the-enuf-photo-folder-mystery-and-a-thank-you-to-the-list) for a photo-folder mix-up that briefly affected his own build photos before they reappeared.

He flagged Jim Michalak's "Robbsboat" as a possible Paradox alternative worth watching, then questioned whether Michalak's own designs were proven for the kind of long-distance, single-handed coastal cruising Paradox is built for — conceding a skilled sailor can do a great deal in any boat, but holding it is better to start with one suited to the purpose. See [similar-boat-designs/comparisons.md](/similar-boat-designs/comparisons.md#the-lake-boats-not-sea-boats-verdict-and-a-frolic2-counterexample).[18]

## Other contributions

Derek's most durable contribution to the group was a free, copyright-free **"Beginners guide to building a Paradox,"** posted to the files section once his own build was largely finished. He condensed his build's practical lessons into about four pages, invited other builders to amend it, and issued a revision within a day of readers' first suggestions. See [references/building-plans.md](/references/building-plans.md#a-community-written-beginners-guide).[12][13] He separately rated a commercially sold construction manual as poorly written, recommending Jim Michalak's book instead.[16]

He devised a fast, jig-free tiller: a one-inch galvanized pipe bent to shape by working it through a hole drilled in a block of wood, then cut to length and given a final touch-up bend in a vice so it would clear the under-deck foam at the extremes of its swing — used directly as the finished tiller rather than laminating plywood or bending ash. See [components/rudder.md](/components/rudder.md#tiller-construction-laminated-wood-versus-bent-metal-tube).[20][21]

Answering a masthead-wiring question, he pointed to a recessed below-deck plug as a model and described building his own masthead light inside a translucent plastic ball, rigged only when needed rather than wired permanently. See [operations/running-lights.md](/construction/electrical-wiring.md#clearing-a-snug-deck-hole-as-the-mast-is-stepped-and-unstepped). For a newcomer converting the plans' materials estimate to metric units, he supplied the gallon-to-litre and litre-to-kilogram conversions for epoxy. See [construction/epoxy-brands-and-chemistry.md](/construction/epoxy-brands-and-chemistry.md#converting-a-us-epoxy-quantity-to-litres-or-kilograms).[2] After an epoxy supplier warned of serious health risk from skin contact, he used his own tank compartments as lift-up-lid food storage instead, carrying drinking water in roll-up carriers under the seat. See [construction/water-tank-lining.md](/construction/water-tank-lining.md#epoxy-mixed-opinions).[3]

On plywood grade, he used exterior-grade ply throughout on the grounds that it would be covered in epoxy and fibreglass regardless, noting neither "Enuf"'s stablemate "Swamp Thing" nor "Little Cruiser" was originally glassed.[9] He favored Jim Michalak's cup-and-marked-stick epoxy-mixing method over a dispensing pump after crystals forming in his own pump once cost him a redo of a hull bottom, and recommended a UK epoxy supplier along with Michalak's book for its epoxy-chemistry commentary. See [construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#long-term-plywood-durability-resale-value-and-why-to-expect-neither).[10][11]

Weighing in on a 12-volt wiring-gauge debate, Derek judged sizing the conductor to actual current draw the practical approach, but held that using the largest cross-section of copper that will physically fit remains the real ideal; he used salvaged multi-strand cable himself. See [operations/radio-and-electronics.md](/construction/electrical-wiring.md#12-volt-wiring-gauge-choosing-conductor-size-for-a-low-draw-boat).[15]

In the recurring "max pilot weight, height" thread, Derek clarified that a stability caution given to a large prospective builder was really about cabin room rather than hull stability, and gave the practical fix for taller sailors: dropping the floor between bulkheads 1 and 2. See [operations/crew-size-and-fit.md](/operations/crew-size-and-fit.md#pilot-weight-height-and-stability-at-stock-length).[7] Answering a later bulkhead-3 modification proposal for sleeping two, he suggested doubling or trebling the bulkhead's plywood thickness rather than adding steel reinforcement. See [components/bulkheads.md](/components/bulkheads.md#modifying-bulkhead-3-to-gain-room-for-sleeping-two).[17]

## Resuming the build after a break

In mid-December 2005, Derek reported restarting work on "Enuf" after a pause, with a new boom taking shape and a propane fan heater warming his unheated garage.[4] By early 2006 he had finished the boom and mast, resolved a recurring bamboo-versus-wood yard question in favor of wood, settled on a wedge-and-rowlock socket at the transom for sculling, and confirmed his 2 hp Honda outboard was the correct short-shaft version.[5][6] Full build narrative at [boats/enuf.md](/boats/enuf.md#resuming-work-a-new-boom-and-a-propane-heater) and [boats/enuf.md](/boats/enuf.md#the-yard-revisited-a-strop-and-sheave-halyard-and-finishing-the-boom).

Derek is based in Wolverhampton, not far from "Little Jim" in Yeovil, and launched "Enuf" at Wareham, Dorset.[8] A carpal tunnel operation in April 2006 halted his build entirely for five weeks.[14]

"Enuf" was finally launched in early August 2006 and sailed for the first time over three days in Poole Harbour alongside "Little Jim" — an eventful maiden outing (a mudbank and three moored boats struck, an outboard that fouled the mainsheet and made the steering wander) that left Derek planning a relocated motor, a new boom, and a different mainsheet lead. See [boats/enuf.md](/boats/enuf.md#launched-three-days-in-poole-harbour-and-lessons-from-the-outboard).[19] Following up on that outboard trouble, he settled on a transom-mounted 2.3 hp Honda petrol motor, chosen over an electric motor specifically to avoid depending on recharging, with the throttle extended by a PVC pipe and the kill switch worked by a cord, both reached through a removable rear window. See [operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-transom-mounted-petrol-outboard-fuel-stowage-and-trim).[22]

## Selling "Enuf"

A diabetic, Derek found the exertion of sailing brought on hypoglycemic episodes at a level he judged too risky to continue, and put "Enuf" up for sale in September 2006 rather than keep sailing through it. See [boats/enuf.md](/boats/enuf.md#enuf-offered-for-sale).[23] In the thread that followed, he gave a fuller account of the diabetes itself, reported his wife had bought him a small motor cruiser to restore in Enuf's place, and reflected that the build and time spent on Poole Harbour had been worthwhile regardless. See [boats/enuf.md](/boats/enuf.md#a-near-miss-paradox-type-motor-cruiser-and-enuf-as-a-talking-point-for-motorboating).[24]

"Enuf" sold within days, setting off by road toward a new owner in Norway; in the same reply, Derek floated a fresh, purpose-built Honda-outboard "power microcruiser" design idea of his own, pitching it to designers Jim Michalak, Paul Fisher, and John Welsford. See [boats/enuf.md](/boats/enuf.md#the-sale-completes-and-a-honda-outboard-power-microcruiser-idea).[25][26]

He then announced his departure from the group after roughly 30 months, timed to the boat's departure and prompted in part by a scheduled operation he hoped might cure his diabetes outright.[27] The announcement drew immediate, unanimous pushback rather than farewells, with several members arguing the design itself, and the security of always having a sail to fall back on, was reason enough not to give it up.[28] Derek subsequently confirmed he was not, in fact, leaving. Full account at [boats/enuf.md](/boats/enuf.md#a-departure-an-outpouring-of-support-and-second-thoughts).[29]

Derek's original construction was later assessed by "Enuf"'s third owner, whose 2013–14 refit found economical hardware throughout — corroding window and steering-pulley bolts, a hatch not built to the plans, and rudder sheathing that did not fully encapsulate the leading-edge brass — alongside the polystyrene buoyancy Derek had built in; see [boats/enuf.md](/boats/enuf.md#what-the-boat-looked-like-on-inspection-and-the-original-builders-economies) for the fuller account.[30]
