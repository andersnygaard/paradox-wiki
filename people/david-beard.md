# David Beard

**David Beard** (Tupelo, MS — 90 miles south of Memphis) is a first-time boatbuilder who began building his own [Paradox](/paradox.md) around 2000, completing the bulkheads before a divorce interrupted the work. In late 2001 he bought a secondhand Paradox built and originally sold out of Steinhatchee, FL, intending to sail her while finishing his own build (see [secondhand Paradox market](/operations/secondhand-market.md)). He refitted that boat through 2002, naming her **"[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)"**, and became one of the `paradoxbuilders` list's most prolific and enthusiastic contributors.

## What he built and did

David owned and refitted Whisper, the secondhand Paradox, spending 2001–2003 bringing her from a rough, less-finished condition up toward the standard of the reference boat Zoë. He was also building his own Paradox from scratch — his intended keeper boat — but paused that build to sail and study Whisper first, settling on two changes for his own boat along the way: omitting the water tanks in favor of small water containers plus permanent ballast, and using a plywood other than checking-prone fir. Owning an already-finished boat answered many construction questions simply by allowing him to study a completed Paradox in person. The full build, launch, refit, and cruise narrative for Whisper lives on [Whisper](/boats/whisper.md).

## Contributions and stories

David's most durable contributions cluster around a handful of themes; full detail lives on the linked topic and boat pages.

**Refit, rot repair, and robust construction.** Whisper's refit turned up a sequence of problems that became reference cases for later builders: a discolored-but-solid spot, then damp (not rotten) plywood, then genuine rot in a void at bulkhead #3, treated with injected antifreeze and epoxy. Whether it was then safe to epoxy-seal over the treated area, and later whether epoxy might trap residual moisture rather than release it, was settled by Matt Layden's own recommendation of epoxy inside and out, topped with paint. Measuring Whisper against the plans turned up sides built roughly 1/4 in thick rather than the plans' 1/2 in; a second 1/4 in layer was added during a later refit, leaving her sides a sandwich of ply/glass/epoxy, ply/glass/epoxy. Tearing out her original glued-in foam flotation for inspection, after buying her neglected and rain-damp, found sound wood wherever the glue bond had been good and real rot at the corners wherever it had been poor, leading him to prefer screwed-in foam over glued-in for future work. See [checking a used Paradox for rot](/construction/rot-inspection-ice-pick.md), [epoxy pre-coating technique](/construction/epoxy-coating-technique.md#sealing-a-hull-with-prior-waterrot-damage-epoxy-vs-paint), [hull planking — is a second layer of cloth worth it?](/construction/hull-planking.md#the-case-for-a-second-layer-of-cloth), and [plywood grade and foam flotation debate](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#a-real-world-data-point-glued-foam-gone-wrong-on-a-neglected-boat).

**Chine runners and rudder fittings.** Built his own chine runners using templates, epoxy fill for the low spots, and a hand plane for the high, with good results; years later, a rebuild of Whisper's own runners — found far out of shape and location from her original build — brought them within 1-2 mm of correct and transformed her performance. He found no upward curve at the runner's outer edge made any difference, and reported a Paradox pointing as high under chine-runner power as a Seapearl. He also cast his own glassed-epoxy lower rudder fitting, confirming the plans' 6-layer, 24 oz roving lay-up and working out how to get the heavy roving to lie down in the mold, and separately asked whether a rudder's pivot point belongs on the transom's centerline. See [chine runner construction](/chine-runners/construction.md#method-4-templates-epoxy-fill-and-a-hand-plane), [Whisper — chine runners rebuilt after the shakedown cruise](/boats/whisper.md#chine-runners-rebuilt-after-the-shakedown-cruise), [chine runners — how they work](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md#the-chine-runner-curve-cosmetic-fabrication-choice-or-hydrodynamic-feature), [lower rudder fitting — casting technique](/construction/rudder-bearing-materials.md#casting-a-glass-lower-rudder-gudgeon-technique), and [rudder pivot point on the transom centerline](/components/rudder.md#rudder-pivot-point-on-the-transom-centerline).

**Sailing characteristics, weight, and the rudder-up discovery.** Whisper generated some of the archive's richest sailing-characteristics material: real difficulty completing tacks despite decent pointing, later a lee-helm symptom, and a full loaded-weight breakdown (roughly 750 lb all told) that seeded a debate on weight distribution and helm balance. His most notable single finding was that Whisper seemed to sail better with her rudder left up, prompting further testing and a proposal to cut the rudder down by half. A drunk jet-skier's collision with Whisper at anchor, which left only cosmetic damage, and very little pounding even under power, both stood as further evidence of a soundly built hull. See [sailing characteristics and rig balance](/operations/sailing-characteristics.md), [pounding: rare at anchor, occasional underway](/operations/sailing-characteristics.md#pounding-rare-at-anchor-occasional-underway), [weight and ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md), and [rudder experiments](/components/rudder.md).

**Outboards, furling, and sea trials.** Confirmed a topping lift is essential to make the furling system work. Found that a 65 lb outboard could not be balanced out on a Paradox while a 27 lb 2 hp Honda pushed Whisper along at 4.5 kn; a 35 lb thrust electric outboard, paired with gel-cell ballast batteries, also performed well; and a 4 hp four-stroke Yamaha trial, reaching 6.5 kn but pushing too much water to plane, became one more data point behind the settled conclusion that Paradox's hull shape rules out planing regardless of engine size. He settled on the short-shaft version of his Honda 2 hp over a long-shaft alternative, since it keeps the motor's lowest point above the hull's so the hull grounds first, and later ran that same Honda as sole power for a roughly 300-mile winter delivery cruise down the Tennessee-Tombigbee Waterway to the Gulf of Mexico; for years he simply stored the outboard's fuel on deck without incident, including river runs of up to 150 miles at a stretch on outboard power alone.[267] Crediting Matt Layden's philosophy that motors are a luxury, he also took a deliberate motor-free yuloh trip, and later built his own yuloh, comparing its curved blade to Matt Layden's own straight, harder-on-the-wrist design. Following a week of sea trials at Pirates Cove, Alabama, he fixed a mainsheet-fouling problem with a built brass rod fairlead bar across the transom and showed Whisper at the Josephine, AL Wooden Boat Festival that spring. He also solved two recurring hardware failures on his own boat: a tack tie-down strut that kept failing under compression, ended for good by switching to bronze Schedule 40 pipe, and a stock mainsheet/topping-lift tang that bent under load, stopped by bolting three tangs together in place of the single stock part. See [auxiliary outboard power](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#weighing-a-2-hp-four-stroke-against-a-bigger-motor-and-whether-paradox-can-plane), [sculling and yuloh technique](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md#matt-laydens-own-yuloh-speed-and-the-straight-vs-curved-blade-trade-off), [yuloh and stowed-rig retention](/components/yuloh.md#building-the-yuloh-itself-blade-curvature-and-the-pivot-boltsocket), [furling system](/components/rig/furling.md), [Whisper — a Wooden Boat Festival appearance, and sea trials at Pirates Cove](/boats/whisper.md#a-wooden-boat-festival-appearance-and-sea-trials-at-pirates-cove), [Whisper — a winter Tenn-Tom Waterway delivery cruise](/boats/whisper.md#a-winter-tenn-tom-waterway-delivery-cruise), [tack strop — the tack strut also carries compression, not just tension](/components/rig/tack-strop.md#the-tack-strut-also-carries-compression-not-just-tension), and [Faith's rudder — boom-tang strength](/boats/faith.md#faiths-rudder-a-sheet-fairlead-fix-and-boom-tang-strength).

**Sail and rig choices.** Endorsed Dabbler Sails after hearing a sailmaker used Dabbler-built sails on his own sailing canoes, and had Dabbler build his own next sail. He floated experimentally fitting a small jib salvaged from a dinghy to his own Paradox purely to see what it would do — the only recorded proposal to add a headsail to the design. See [sail — sail pricing](/components/rig/sail.md#sail-pricing) and [rig and sail — a speculative small jib](/components/rig/index.md#a-speculative-small-jib).

**Community role.** A self-taught first boatbuilder who credited [Don Elliott](/people/don-elliott.md)'s ["Building the Paradox" articles](/references/building-the-paradox-articles.md), David actively encouraged novices that Paradox was a workable first boat and welcomed new members to the list. He was also the first list member to reply to designer [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)'s own first-ever list post, addressing him as a fellow member rather than in the third person, and repeatedly opened his home waters near Tupelo, Mississippi to visiting builders over the years. When the group-written Build Manual was opened for contributions, he volunteered to write the yuloh section, and, having missed a *Messing About In Boats* article on Zoë's shakedown cruise in print, prompted it to be scanned into the group's files section. He urged builders to request a Material Safety Data Sheet for every product used on a boat, consistent with his own respirator-safety habits, and opened a recurring design discussion comparing Paradox's coastal-cruiser classification against Sven Yrvind's blue-water Bris designs. See [is Paradox a suitable first boat?](/operations/first-boat-suitability.md), [Whisper — a 2007 Cedar Key visit-planning exchange](/boats/whisper.md#a-2007-cedar-key-visit-planning-exchange), [Zoë — press coverage](/boats/zoe.md#press-coverage-two-2003-magazine-articles), [topside and hull painting](/construction/topside-painting.md#glen-maxwells-full-awlgrip-roll-and-tip-recipe-and-why-not-to-spray-it), and [blue-water suitability](/design/blue-water-suitability.md).

**Other durable technique notes.** He warned against mounting a locking hatch hasp's hinge on the hatch itself, which can trap a sailor inside; routed masthead-light wiring through a shallow groove cut into the mast rather than running it proud outside or fishing it through the interior; sealed Whisper's tiller thru-hull with a cut wetsuit-neoprene gasket, later plugging the resulting ventilation for cold-weather sailing; fitted a toe rail that made moving forward on deck much easier; found a discarded wheelchair cushion eliminated helm-seat fatigue entirely; modified a stock trailer to sit lower for low-tide launching; found small paint blisters formed wherever a tarp touched Whisper's fresh paint during a six-week cover, a residual-moisture effect; and left Whisper's own vent-trunk side panel removable — only its top half — with no gasket needed and no leakage, the halyard-turnaround block mounted on the panel itself and every line worked through the vent opening on deck, valuing the access chiefly for inspection and repainting.[268] See [hatch hardware and security](/construction/hatch-hardware.md#orient-the-hinge-on-the-aft-window-not-the-hatch-itself), [running lights](/construction/electrical-wiring.md#routing-masthead-light-wiring-outside-the-mast-below-the-sheave), [transom deck plate](/construction/transom-deck-plate.md#the-vent-baffle-why-the-tiller-rope-opening-exists-and-whether-its-necessary), [boats/enuf.md](/boats/enuf.md#toe-rail-is-it-needed-and-where-to-source-one-in-the-uk), [crew size and fit](/operations/crew-size-and-fit.md#long-passage-seat-comfort-cushion-practice), [trailering](/operations/trailering.md#a-stock-power-boat-trailer-adapted-for-tongue-weight), [topside and hull painting choices](/construction/topside-painting.md#paint-blisters-from-a-tarp-left-on-too-long), and [vent box — access to the vent box: a removable side panel versus gluing it in place](/components/vent-box.md#access-to-the-vent-box-a-removable-side-panel-versus-gluing-it-in-place).

**Philosophy and later years.** Some of his most memorable sailing came on small, out-of-the-way lakes and rivers, since Paradox's shallow draft can reach water no larger sailboat could — he encouraged new builders not to fear exploring, to drop the mast and go, and simply to push off if grounded. Having also partially built a second Paradox of his own, he twice mused, years apart, about converting Whisper into a motor-only "power sharpie" rather than selling her, and briefly considered trading her for a used Peep Hen before deciding against it — unwilling to sell her outright, though open to a future sale. See [the "BRIS Paradox" — sailing grounds](/boats/bris-paradox.md#sailing-grounds-the-stockholm-archipelago-and-a-baltic-states-dream), [Power Paradox (motor-only conversion, speculative)](/design/ideas/power-paradox.md), and [Whisper — a 2007 trade offer declined](/boats/whisper.md#a-2007-trade-offer-declined).
