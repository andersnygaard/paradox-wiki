# Dave Skaife

**Dave Skaife** is an American small-boat sailor who built a [Paradox](/paradox.md) in Hawaii and sailed it, motorless, over four seasons between 2014 and 2018 — across Hawaii, the Gulf of Mexico, the Bahamas twice, and Cuba. He documented the whole of it on a personal blog, `daveskaife.wordpress.com`, which is the archive's longest continuous first-person record of a single Paradox after [Bill Serjeant](/people/bill-serjeant.md)'s. His blog is preserved as a first-class source at [references/dave-skaife-blog.md](/references/dave-skaife-blog.md); this page draws the person and the voyage out of it.

His boat carried **no name** anywhere in the blog — he called it only "my Paradox" or "my microcruiser" — and none should be invented for it.

## Why he built a Paradox

Dave came to the design moving *down*, not up. He had a 36 ft monohull that he almost always sailed solo, and found himself maintaining and pushing around far more boat than he needed; he wanted something small, simple, and trailerable. Looking through many designs, he found a [Don Elliott](/people/don-elliott.md) sketch of a Paradox and was, in his words, "pretty much hooked" — it was the kind of sailing he wanted, which he learned was called microcruising. Not wanting to build from scratch but finding it the only way to get one, he talked it over with his boatbuilding mentor and ordered the plans through [microcruising.com](/references/microcruising-website.md).[1]

He judged the plans "jam packed with details and information, almost a sensory overload," warned that a Paradox takes much longer to build than a normal fourteen-footer because of everything built into it, and called [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md) "an evil genius when it comes to boat designs — he thinks so far outside the box that you forget there was a box." The boat, he wrote soon after launch, had already exceeded his expectations.[1]

## Building the boat in Hawaii

Dave built the boat from scratch in Hawaii, blogging the tail end of construction from December 2013. His first post is characteristic of how he built: a tiny bamboo drip-guide fitted under the cabin windows to catch condensation and lead it to a sponge in the corner — an easy upgrade to do before the windows and paint went on, and, he cheerfully admitted, "probably one of those times when I over think something."[2] Through the build and first season he documented his electrical system, fender and anchor storage, dual-purpose running lights, a hatch lock, and multipurpose water-storage buckets — the small, considered outfitting choices that carry through the rest of the log.

## Four seasons under sail and oar

The boat was **motorless** throughout — Dave sculled and sailed it, through locks and calms as well as open water — which makes the blog a sustained real-world account of engine-free Paradox cruising over an unusually wide range:

- **[Hawaii (2014)](/merits/skaife-hawaii-cruise.md).** A roughly 415-nautical-mile inter-island cruise of the main chain — Hilo on the Big Island up through Lanai, Molokai, and Oahu to Kauai, with two four-day unbroken passages — including reef-damage repair and the tiller-pilot he rated his most valuable single piece of gear.
- **The Gulf ICW (2015).** He shipped the boat from Hawaii to Texas and sailed on across the Gulf of Mexico's [Intracoastal Waterway](/merits/inland-waterway-and-canal-touring.md) from Texas to Florida, blogging it almost daily through the Louisiana and Mississippi marshes, the Chandeleur and Ship Islands, and Florida's Big Bend, and closing the season with a damage-assessment post recording what the long passage had actually broken.
- **The Bahamas, twice (2016 and 2017).** A Pensacola shakedown and Gulf Stream crossing to Bimini and the Exumas in 2016, then George Town, the Jumentos, and Ragged Island in 2017.
- **[Cuba (2017)](/merits/skaife-cuba-voyage.md), and the end.** A 17-hour passage from the southern Bahamas took him to Puerto de Vita on Cuba's north coast; he cruised west to Cayo Guillermo and inland to Morón — check-in, money, and internet all documented — then made an engine-free Gulf Stream return to Florida, closing out the four seasons through hurricane season and the Everglades.[3]

## "Comfortable microcruising"

Between seasons Dave wrote a deliberate twelve-post **"comfortable microcruising"** series — his own thinking on how to make a fourteen-foot Paradox genuinely liveable: ventilation, the padded "cell" interior, dressing for the conditions, window covers, an "ultimate personal environment management system," and the balance of sentiment against minimalism aboard a very small boat. Read as one owner's outfitting philosophy rather than as documentation of the plans, it is the densest original design thinking on the blog and its most useful material for topic pages; alongside it he ran a `gear-i-like` review series (Bebi anchor light, JetBoil, Streamlight spotlight).[4]

## The boat's later life

The single fullest description of the boat is Dave's 2017 for-sale post, written after two Bahamas seasons: a "14 foot motorless sailboat," registered in Hawaii with a hull identification number (he registered it there because proving ownership for the paperwork trail was simplest, and most states want residency), and equipped with an electronic autopilot, a personal locator beacon, a submersible handheld VHF, four anchors, cabin fans, and a full kit of cruising gear — "pretty much turn-key."[5] He offered it for sale in Pensacola, Florida from 2017 as he moved on to overland travel in Southeast Asia, and it sold in March 2018 — to "a new excited owner," with the proceeds earmarked for further adventures.[6][7]
