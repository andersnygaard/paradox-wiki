# Nels Arvidson

**Nels Arvidson** — who usually signed simply "Nels" — was a Canadian prospective Paradox builder and active list contributor, posting from `arvent@...` (Yahoo profile `recree8`) from September 2002 onward. He first appeared on the list seeking a copy of Don Elliott's paid "Building the Paradox" articles and was pointed instead to [Charles Wilson](/people/charles-wilson.md)'s free plans CD. He never built a Paradox: his planned build was a simplified, cold-climate version with no water tanks, and, having limited big-water sailing experience of his own, he intended to prove it first on Lake Michigan's sheltered water before cruising **British Columbia** — drawing on firsthand experience servicing weather equipment and training radio operators by plane across the western Hudson Bay coast. He instead sailed a Bolger Micro of his own, named "Micro," and by 2008 was weighing Matt Layden's smaller Enigma as a solo-sailing alternative (below). He is best remembered as the archive's most active real-time chronicler of Matt Layden's 2003 Everglades Challenge win.

**Disambiguation.** One message carries a mailer "From" line reading "~Edwin~" while the body is signed "Nels" — apparently a nickname or middle name for a poster whose account name was Edwin. All messages share the same `arvent@` address and `recree8` Yahoo profile, confirming a single person.[6]

## Chronicling the 2003 Everglades Challenge

Over 11-12 March 2003, Nels became the archive's principal relayer of **Everglades Challenge** news, watching the WaterTribe forum and forwarding it to the list with his own analysis. He relayed checkpoint standings and finishing order, crediting Matt's roller-launching design as decisive: it let Matt sail and sleep underway while paddled competitors had to land to rest. This is the archive's only mention that Matt **stopped and rescued an overturned kayaker and towed him ashore** mid-race. The official WaterTribe results were posted under the subject "MATT WINS!" — Matt finishing Key Largo (304 miles) at 17:51 on 11 March with no rival yet past Checkpoint 4, a result Nels summed up as Matt having created a class of his own, the microcruiser class. The full race narrative and results table live on [original-paradox.md](/boats/original-paradox.md).

## Design and construction contributions

- **A "motorsailer" Paradox proposal.** A lengthened, substantially modified Paradox was proposed for inland-lake use — connected by small rivers, bridges, and weirs — illustrating the tension between customizing the boat and preserving the designer's original intent. See [Motorsailer Paradox (speculative)](/design/variants/motorsailer-idea.md).[3]
- **Advocacy for an enlarged Paradox.** Repeatedly raised the case for a larger Paradox over several years, citing a dockside visitor left standing in the rain for lack of cabin room and reports that humid weather made two-up cruising uncomfortable, while judging the stock design well suited to its original purpose; while standing in as moderator, disclosed that the group itself was considering developing plans for an enlarged version. In 2008 confirmed that a 20% scale-up had been approved in principle by Matt Layden, though of the roughly 40 members who had floated the idea over the years, nobody had actually built one and reported results back. Separately cautioned that a folding, tabernacle-stepped mast on an enlarged Paradox is not easy to design and brace against sail loads, since the mast structure is what drives the boat. See [stretched Paradox — enlarging for a tall sailor, and three named stretch methods](/design/variants/stretched-paradox.md#enlarging-for-a-tall-sailor-and-three-named-stretch-methods) and [Building Paradox larger — Matt Layden's approval](/design/larger-paradox.md#matt-laydens-approval-and-why-no-scaled-up-paradox-has-yet-been-built).[16][26][41][43]
- **Located Matt Layden's unfinished "Big Cruiser" cartoons.** Pointed to the precise location, in the `microcruising` Yahoo group's files section, of Matt Layden's own unfinished stretched/enlarged Paradox cartoons, which never reached final plans — the first confirmation of where the "Big Cruiser" material was archived since its only prior mention ten months earlier. See [Matt Layden (biography) — asides and footnotes](/matt-layden/biography.md#asides-and-footnotes).
- **Enigma as a solo-sailing alternative.** By 2008 was weighing Matt Layden's smaller Enigma instead of Paradox for himself, since he sailed with a crew at times and had judged the stock Paradox too small for two — his own build had settled on a Bolger Micro (with two 7 ft berths) for that reason — while Enigma looked far easier to handle ashore and lighter to tow for solo use. Confirmed Enigma's empty weight at 180 lb from a photo posted to the EnigmaBuilders-as-Designed-by-Matt_Layden Yahoo group, and relayed that group's news of a bigger Enigma 460 in the works, with plans to follow if its prototype succeeded — restating its dimensions, displacement, and ballast, and adding a maximum-payload estimate of about 450 kg (1,000 lb). See [Enigma — Paradox and Enigma compared](/matt-layden/enigma.md#paradox-and-enigma-compared-overbuilt-strength-versus-light-displacement-simplicity) and [Enigma — the Enigma family](/matt-layden/enigma.md#the-enigma-family-the-360-and-the-plywood-cabin-460).[44][45][47][48][49]
- **A bow-anchor heaving-to aid, and a canoeing bucket-drogue parallel.** Streaming an anchor off the bow was suggested to help a boat heave to, drawing on Phil Bolger's view that the rode's drag in calmer water below the surface can rival a chute near the surface, with the side benefit of slowing a boat blown toward a lee shore; a parallel was drawn to a rock-filled bucket dragged on a bow-to-bow bridle in canoeing practice, the same rig traditionally used for lining a canoe downstream or tracking it upstream through rapids. See [heavy-weather tactics — why a keel-less, dory-like hull resists tripping in steep waves](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#why-a-keel-less-dory-like-hull-resists-tripping-in-steep-waves).[23]
- **How chine runners work.** Gave the archive's fullest account of why the chine-runner concept depends on a box-hull's own resistance to sideways slippage plus Paradox's deep, heavily rockered midships section — a dory hull, by contrast, loses most of that resistance to its flared topsides and grows dangerously tender once ballasted deep enough to compensate — illustrated with Phil Bolger's redesigned St. Pierre Dory set against his box-hulled "Jesse Cooper." Explained why runners work better as a boat moves faster (a heeled hull's downward-forced water is deflected aft on meeting the runner, adding forward push rather than pure drag) and argued that having no keel below the hull is itself a safety feature off a lee shore, letting the boat slide sideways rather than trip. Elsewhere explained chine-runner effectiveness via a shallow-fin-keel analogy, and noted that "Jubilee" and "Hogfish Maximus" both pair chine runners with a centerboard, a combination judged worth adding at around a 25 ft waterline. See [how chine runners work — a Bolger box-hull-versus-dory-hull design comparison](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md#a-bolger-box-hull-versus-dory-hull-design-comparison) and [why speed itself reduces leeway](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md#why-speed-itself-reduces-leeway-and-a-shallow-draft-safety-case-for-no-keel-at-all).[15][30][31][39]
- **The steel/aluminum Paradox debate.** A leading voice in a sustained debate over alternative hull materials: doubted Paradox was practically buildable in steel at her size, then argued her plywood/glass construction — comparable in principle to a foam-cored whitewater canoe — was likely already tough enough that steel added little, and that commercial steel boatbuilding scales to boats considerably larger than Paradox (a 25 ft practical minimum, consistent with Phil Bolger's own published figure). Also proposed combining stitch-and-glue construction with bilge keels and a Chinese lug rig on a carbon-fiber tabernacled mast, and floated a welded-aluminum-hull/inflatable-collar hybrid. See [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative)](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md).[18][19][20][21][22]
- **A Spanish-windlass technique for pulling in a hull's sides.** Offered a builder struggling to draw the bow panels together a method using a heavy rope loop closed gradually by twisting a dowel passed under it, with temporary uprights to stop the loop slipping forward and temporary scabs at the bow to support clamps once the ends draw together. See [hull assembly sequence — pulling the bow together with a Spanish windlass](/construction/hull-assembly-sequence.md#pulling-a-hulls-sides-together-with-a-spanish-windlass).[32]
- **Scarfing and joint techniques.** Explained how to fasten and reinforce a router-cut lap butt joint in bottom-panel plywood without ring nails so that clamping pressure would not starve the joint of epoxy, and how a strip of glass tape over the joint can leave it stronger than the surrounding plywood; later described the lighter-sanding scarfing method, often called a "Payson joint" after Harold "Dynamite" Payson, that removes only about 1/8 in of material before taping over the join. See [scarf joints vs. butt blocks](/construction/scarf-joints.md#a-lap-butt-joint-as-a-scarf-alternative) and [scarfing](/components/scarfing.md).[33][42]
- **Sheer-strake and chine-log material guidance.** Explained that these parts carry real structural load as well as fastener purchase, recommended good-quality soft/flexible species (spruce, fir, cedar) over cheap wood upgraded with epoxy, warned that ripped plywood strips hold fasteners poorly compared to laminated solid-wood strips, and relayed Matt Layden's list of species to avoid (oak, teak, maple, birch, poplar). See [chine log vs. taped-seam construction](/construction/chine-log-vs-taped-seam.md#what-sheer-strakes-and-chine-logs-actually-do-and-why-plywood-is-a-poor-substitute).[29]
- **Ballast-and-buoyancy placement doctrine.** Gave the fullest statement in the archive of why ballast belongs low and amidships — Paradox's bottom curves like a banana, so amidships is the hull's low point — while flotation belongs high and at the ends, for self-righting. See [plywood grade and foam flotation debate — ballast and buoyancy placement, and why the Paradox's rocker matters](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#ballast-and-buoyancy-placement-and-why-the-paradoxs-rocker-matters). A practical trim sequence was also recommended: launch with an empty hull, load her with all cruising gear, then add lead until she balances to her waterline before mounting the ballast permanently — usefully showing the chine runners' true performance before the boat reaches final trim. See [weight, ballast, and displacement — ballast placement: outboard vs. centered](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#ballast-placement-outboard-vs-centered).[38]
- **Sailing characteristics: momentum and rudder stall.** Drawing on Phil Bolger's writing on sharpies, argued lighter displacement reduces a hull's weathercocking tendency when tacking in light air, and separately warned that over-applying rudder to force the bow through the wind before way is lost turns the rudder into a brake — recommending minimal rudder, falling off to regather speed, or an oar stroke to kick the stern over. See [sailing characteristics and rig balance](/operations/sailing-characteristics.md#a-second-round-weathercocking-ballast-and-rudder-stall).[28]
- **Seating position and a "mini pilothouse" idea.** Flagged a reclined interior seating position as a likely source of lower-back pain if held for any length of time, sitting upright being the fix, and floated raising the hatch sides for more headroom — with a forward transparent window and perhaps the overhead too — as a way to sit more upright with the hatch closed in rain, in effect a small pilothouse, particularly for a taller sailor. See [interior bin panels and hinged floor — seating position, back comfort, and a "mini pilothouse" idea](/components/interior.md#seating-position-back-comfort-and-a-mini-pilothouse-idea).[46]
- **Finishing and materials practices.** Argued for filling the fiberglass weave before painting rather than leaving it exposed to save sanding time, since an open weave traps moisture and dirt and won't hold paint as well, and recommended a practical finishing sequence of thin coats with fairing compound applied afterward. Recommended MDO (medium-density overlay) plywood for its smooth surface and better paint-holding, and explained that a Paradox could in principle be built with no fiberglass cloth at all — epoxy used only to seal joints — and still last a long time. Drew on twelve years' experience with a differently built small boat of his own, "Micro," kept outdoors year-round, comparing the wear patterns of Cetol, varnish, house paint, and marine enamel across her various surfaces, and endorsed tinted Lexan for the UV protection it gives paint and interior cushions. See [epoxy pre-coating technique — filling the fiberglass weave before painting](/construction/epoxy-coating-technique.md#filling-the-fiberglass-weave-before-painting-fairing-compound-and-the-case-against-leaving-it-exposed) and [plywood grades and foam flotation](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#a-july-2006-recurrence-and-a-recommendation-for-mdo-plywood).[36][40][50]
- **Proposing a group translation of Sven Yrvind's book.** Proposed a crowd-translated English edition of Yrvind's book — either a pre-order-funded print run or a PayPal e-book — and worked out a concrete chapter-by-chapter volunteer-translation pipeline with several willing translators lined up; later welcomed and encouraged the translated chapter once posted. See [Sven Yrvind's book — a proposed group project to translate Sven Yrvind's full book into English](/references/yrvind-book.md#a-proposed-group-project-to-translate-sven-yrvinds-full-book-into-english).[17][24][25][27]
- **Reading-list contributions.** Recommended Harold "Dynamite" Payson's books, recalling an unannounced visit to buy a set of "Cartopper" plans that turned into an entire afternoon spent with Payson, his wife, and their son; also nominated Sven Yrvind's and the Bolducs' own writing as reading-list material, and separately recommended Lin and Larry Pardey. See ["Beach Cruising and Coastal Camping" — a wider Paradox-sailor reading list](/references/beachcruising-book.md#a-wider-paradox-sailor-reading-list).[14][35][37]
