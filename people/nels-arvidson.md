# Nels Arvidson

**Nels Arvidson** — who usually signed simply "Nels" — was a Canadian prospective Paradox builder and active list contributor, posting from `arvent@...` (Yahoo profile `recree8`) from September 2002 onward. He first appeared on the list seeking a copy of Don Elliott's paid "Building the Paradox" articles and was pointed instead to [Charles Wilson](/people/charles-wilson.md)'s free plans CD. He never built a Paradox: his planned build was a simplified, cold-climate version with no water tanks, and, having limited big-water sailing experience of his own, he intended to prove it first on Lake Michigan's sheltered water before cruising **British Columbia** — drawing on firsthand experience servicing weather equipment and training radio operators by plane across the western Hudson Bay coast. He instead sailed a Bolger Micro of his own, named "Micro," and by 2008 was weighing Matt Layden's smaller Enigma as a solo-sailing alternative (below). He is best remembered as the archive's most active real-time chronicler of Matt Layden's 2003 Everglades Challenge win.

**Disambiguation.** One message carries a mailer "From" line reading "~Edwin~" while the body is signed "Nels" — apparently a nickname or middle name for a poster whose account name was Edwin. All messages share the same `arvent@` address and `recree8` Yahoo profile, confirming a single person.[6]

## Chronicling the 2003 Everglades Challenge

Over 11-12 March 2003, Nels became the archive's principal relayer of **Everglades Challenge** news, watching the WaterTribe forum and forwarding it to the list with his own analysis. He relayed checkpoint standings and finishing order, crediting Matt's roller-launching design as decisive: it let Matt sail and sleep underway while paddled competitors had to land to rest. This is the archive's only mention that Matt **stopped and rescued an overturned kayaker and towed him ashore** mid-race. The official WaterTribe results were posted under the subject "MATT WINS!" — Matt finishing Key Largo (304 miles) at 17:51 on 11 March with no rival yet past Checkpoint 4, a result Nels summed up as Matt having created a class of his own, the microcruiser class. The full race narrative and results table live on [original-paradox.md](/boats/original-paradox.md).

## Design and construction contributions

- **A "motorsailer" Paradox proposal.** A lengthened, substantially modified Paradox was proposed for inland-lake use — connected by small rivers, bridges, and weirs — illustrating the tension between customizing the boat and preserving the designer's original intent. See [Motorsailer Paradox (speculative)](/design/ideas/motorsailer-idea.md).[3]
- **Advocacy for an enlarged Paradox.** Repeatedly raised the case for a larger Paradox over several years, citing a dockside visitor left standing in the rain for lack of cabin room and reports that humid weather made two-up cruising uncomfortable, while judging the stock design well suited to its original purpose; while standing in as moderator, disclosed that the group itself was considering developing plans for an enlarged version. In 2008 confirmed that a 20% scale-up had been approved in principle by Matt Layden, though of the roughly 40 members who had floated the idea over the years, nobody had actually built one and reported results back. Separately cautioned that a folding, tabernacle-stepped mast on an enlarged Paradox is not easy to design and brace against sail loads, since the mast structure is what drives the boat. See [stretched Paradox — enlarging for a tall sailor, and three named stretch methods](/design/ideas/stretched-paradox.md#enlarging-for-a-tall-sailor-and-three-named-stretch-methods) and [Building Paradox larger — Matt Layden's approval](/design/larger-paradox.md#matt-laydens-approval-and-why-no-scaled-up-paradox-has-yet-been-built).[16][26][41][43]
- **Located Matt Layden's unfinished "Big Cruiser" cartoons.** Pointed to the precise location, in the `microcruising` Yahoo group's files section, of Matt Layden's own unfinished stretched/enlarged Paradox cartoons, which never reached final plans — the first confirmation of where the "Big Cruiser" material was archived since its only prior mention ten months earlier. See [Matt Layden (biography) — asides and footnotes](/matt-layden/biography.md#asides-and-footnotes).
- **Enigma as a solo-sailing alternative.** By 2008 was weighing Matt Layden's smaller Enigma instead of Paradox for himself, since he sailed with a crew at times and had judged the stock Paradox too small for two — his own build had settled on a Bolger Micro (with two 7 ft berths) for that reason — while Enigma looked far easier to handle ashore and lighter to tow for solo use. Confirmed Enigma's empty weight at 180 lb from a photo posted to the EnigmaBuilders-as-Designed-by-Matt_Layden Yahoo group, and relayed that group's news of a bigger Enigma 460 in the works, with plans to follow if its prototype succeeded — restating its dimensions, displacement, and ballast, and adding a maximum-payload estimate of about 450 kg (1,000 lb). See [Enigma — Paradox and Enigma compared](/matt-layden/enigma.md#paradox-and-enigma-compared-overbuilt-strength-versus-light-displacement-simplicity) and [Enigma — the Enigma family](/matt-layden/enigma.md#the-enigma-family-the-360-and-the-plywood-cabin-460).[44][45][47][48][49]
- **A bow-anchor heaving-to aid, and a canoeing bucket-drogue parallel.** Streaming an anchor off the bow was suggested to help a boat heave to, drawing on Phil Bolger's view that the rode's drag in calmer water below the surface can rival a chute near the surface, with the side benefit of slowing a boat blown toward a lee shore; a parallel was drawn to a rock-filled bucket dragged on a bow-to-bow bridle in canoeing practice, the same rig traditionally used for lining a canoe downstream or tracking it upstream through rapids. See [heavy-weather tactics — why a keel-less, dory-like hull resists tripping in steep waves](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#why-a-keel-less-dory-like-hull-resists-tripping-in-steep-waves).[23]
- **How chine runners work.** Gave the archive's fullest account of why the chine-runner concept depends on a box-hull's own resistance to sideways slippage plus Paradox's deep, heavily rockered midships section — a dory hull, by contrast, loses most of that resistance to its flared topsides and grows dangerously tender once ballasted deep enough to compensate — illustrated with Phil Bolger's redesigned St. Pierre Dory set against his box-hulled "Jesse Cooper." Explained why runners work better as a boat moves faster (a heeled hull's downward-forced water is deflected aft on meeting the runner, adding forward push rather than pure drag) and argued that having no keel below the hull is itself a safety feature off a lee shore, letting the boat slide sideways rather than trip. Elsewhere explained chine-runner effectiveness via a shallow-fin-keel analogy, and noted that "Jubilee" and "Hogfish Maximus" both pair chine runners with a centerboard, a combination judged worth adding at around a 25 ft waterline. See [how chine runners work — a Bolger box-hull-versus-dory-hull design comparison](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md#a-bolger-box-hull-versus-dory-hull-design-comparison) and [why speed itself reduces leeway](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md#why-speed-itself-reduces-leeway-and-a-shallow-draft-safety-case-for-no-keel-at-all).[15][30][31][39]
- **The steel/aluminum Paradox debate.** A leading voice in a sustained debate over alternative hull materials: doubted Paradox was practically buildable in steel at her size, then argued her plywood/glass construction — comparable in principle to a foam-cored whitewater canoe — was likely already tough enough that steel added little, and that commercial steel boatbuilding scales to boats considerably larger than Paradox (a 25 ft practical minimum, consistent with Phil Bolger's own published figure). Also proposed combining stitch-and-glue construction with bilge keels and a Chinese lug rig on a carbon-fiber tabernacled mast, and floated a welded-aluminum-hull/inflatable-collar hybrid. See [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative)](/design/ideas/aluminum-construction-idea.md).[18][19][20][21][22]
- **A Spanish-windlass technique for pulling in a hull's sides.** Offered a builder struggling to draw the bow panels together a method using a heavy rope loop closed gradually by twisting a dowel passed under it, with temporary uprights to stop the loop slipping forward and temporary scabs at the bow to support clamps once the ends draw together. See [hull assembly sequence — pulling the bow together with a Spanish windlass](/construction/hull-assembly-sequence.md#pulling-a-hulls-sides-together-with-a-spanish-windlass).[32]
- **Scarfing and joint techniques.** Explained how to fasten and reinforce a router-cut lap butt joint in bottom-panel plywood without ring nails so that clamping pressure would not starve the joint of epoxy, and how a strip of glass tape over the joint can leave it stronger than the surrounding plywood; later described the lighter-sanding scarfing method, often called a "Payson joint" after Harold "Dynamite" Payson, that removes only about 1/8 in of material before taping over the join. See [scarf joints vs. butt blocks](/construction/scarf-joints.md#a-lap-butt-joint-as-a-scarf-alternative) and [scarfing](/components/scarfing.md).[33][42]
- **Sheer-strake and chine-log material guidance.** Explained that these parts carry real structural load as well as fastener purchase, recommended good-quality soft/flexible species (spruce, fir, cedar) over cheap wood upgraded with epoxy, warned that ripped plywood strips hold fasteners poorly compared to laminated solid-wood strips, and relayed Matt Layden's list of species to avoid (oak, teak, maple, birch, poplar). See [chine log vs. taped-seam construction](/construction/chine-log-vs-taped-seam.md#what-sheer-strakes-and-chine-logs-actually-do-and-why-plywood-is-a-poor-substitute).[29]
- **Ballast-and-buoyancy placement doctrine.** Gave the fullest statement in the archive of why ballast belongs low and amidships — Paradox's bottom curves like a banana, so amidships is the hull's low point — while flotation belongs high and at the ends, for self-righting. See [plywood grade and foam flotation debate — ballast and buoyancy placement, and why the Paradox's rocker matters](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#ballast-and-buoyancy-placement-and-why-the-paradoxs-rocker-matters). A practical trim sequence was also recommended: launch with an empty hull, load her with all cruising gear, then add lead until she balances to her waterline before mounting the ballast permanently — usefully showing the chine runners' true performance before the boat reaches final trim. See [weight, ballast, and displacement — ballast placement: outboard vs. centered](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#ballast-placement-outboard-vs-centered).[38]
- **Sailing characteristics: momentum and rudder stall.** Drawing on Phil Bolger's writing on sharpies, argued lighter displacement reduces a hull's weathercocking tendency when tacking in light air, and separately warned that over-applying rudder to force the bow through the wind before way is lost turns the rudder into a brake — recommending minimal rudder, falling off to regather speed, or an oar stroke to kick the stern over. See [sailing characteristics and rig balance](/operations/sailing-characteristics.md#a-second-round-weathercocking-ballast-and-rudder-stall).[28]
- **Seating position and a "mini pilothouse" idea.** Flagged a reclined interior seating position as a likely source of lower-back pain if held for any length of time, sitting upright being the fix, and floated raising the hatch sides for more headroom — with a forward transparent window and perhaps the overhead too — as a way to sit more upright with the hatch closed in rain, in effect a small pilothouse, particularly for a taller sailor. See [interior bin panels and hinged floor — seating position, back comfort, and a "mini pilothouse" idea](/components/interior.md#seating-position-back-comfort-and-a-mini-pilothouse-idea).[46]
- **Finishing and materials practices.** Argued for filling the fiberglass weave before painting rather than leaving it exposed to save sanding time, since an open weave traps moisture and dirt and won't hold paint as well, and recommended a practical finishing sequence of thin coats with fairing compound applied afterward. Recommended MDO (medium-density overlay) plywood for its smooth surface and better paint-holding, and explained that a Paradox could in principle be built with no fiberglass cloth at all — epoxy used only to seal joints — and still last a long time. Drew on twelve years' experience with a differently built small boat of his own, "Micro," kept outdoors year-round, comparing the wear patterns of Cetol, varnish, house paint, and marine enamel across her various surfaces, and endorsed tinted Lexan for the UV protection it gives paint and interior cushions. See [epoxy pre-coating technique — filling the fiberglass weave before painting](/construction/epoxy-coating-technique.md#filling-the-fiberglass-weave-before-painting-fairing-compound-and-the-case-against-leaving-it-exposed) and [plywood grades and foam flotation](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#a-july-2006-recurrence-and-a-recommendation-for-mdo-plywood).[36][40][50]
- **Proposing a group translation of Sven Yrvind's book.** Proposed a crowd-translated English edition of Yrvind's book — either a pre-order-funded print run or a PayPal e-book — and worked out a concrete chapter-by-chapter volunteer-translation pipeline with several willing translators lined up; later welcomed and encouraged the translated chapter once posted. See [Sven Yrvind's book — a proposed group project to translate Sven Yrvind's full book into English](/references/yrvind-book.md#a-proposed-group-project-to-translate-sven-yrvinds-full-book-into-english).[17][24][25][27]
- **Reading-list contributions.** Recommended Harold "Dynamite" Payson's books, recalling an unannounced visit to buy a set of "Cartopper" plans that turned into an entire afternoon spent with Payson, his wife, and their son; also nominated Sven Yrvind's and the Bolducs' own writing as reading-list material, and separately recommended Lin and Larry Pardey. See ["Beach Cruising and Coastal Camping" — a wider Paradox-sailor reading list](/references/beachcruising-book.md#a-wider-paradox-sailor-reading-list).[14][35][37]
- **A metabolic-overhead rebuttal to a calorie-based case for human power, and his own hybrid power setup.** Countered a fellow correspondent's baked-beans-calories argument for human-powered propulsion by pointing to the energy cost of digestion and of simply staying alive, judged a small gas outboard's energy density and simplicity hard to beat against a full electric setup's combined weight and expense, and described his own hybrid rig — a 1,250-watt Kawasaki genset, two deep-cycle batteries, four solar panels, and a controller/regulator and smart charger, lacking only a trolling motor — while cautioning that shore-power slip fees can exceed whatever solar charging saves. Continuing the same thread, framed a motor strictly as backup for specific situations (bad weather, a dying wind, current or traffic, a schedule, injury or gear failure), recalled his own years of wilderness canoeing (20-25 miles a day, occasionally under a tarp sail), noted "Little Jim"'s owner had himself accepted a tow under a companion's outboard on their latest joint trip, and, once a correspondent floated bolting a motor onto an old outboard's leg, judged any genuinely useful electric setup would need a 24-volt system with four golf-cart batteries and a sizeable solar array, dismissed ordinary trolling-motor props as close to useless, and floated a dream motor-sailor built around a tiny biodiesel engine and a 90-amp alternator instead. See [auxiliary outboard power — a calorie-based case for human power, and its real-world limits](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-calorie-based-case-for-human-power-and-its-real-world-limits).[51][52][53][54][55][56]
- **Sail-tuning follow-up questions on the 2008 New Sail thread.** Asked whether a sleeve gap as long as 8-10 in at the sail's head would hurt performance and whether it needs a half-round shape, reasoned that the boom-to-mast tack distance is fixed by the tack strop pipe and swivel-rod curve rather than independently adjustable, and asked after sailmaker recommendations and boom-shaping technique for the newer sail. See [Mk2 sail — locating the sail's forward top corner](/components/rig/mk2-sail.md#locating-the-sails-forward-top-corner-and-tuning-balance-through-the-sleeve-gap).[64]
- **Comparing the two 2008 sail plans.** Set out the trade-offs between the original and newer Paradox sail plans in detail — the newer sail's shorter boom clearing the self-steering vane and a raised outboard motor, and reducing the chance of the boom striking the water downwind in a broach — while noting Michael Storer and John Welsford both favor a higher aspect ratio in a lug sail, and wondering whether a lighter cloth would help the higher-aspect sail's loose upper section "fall" more smoothly when reefed. See [Sail — choosing between the two 2008 Sailrite listings](/components/rig/sail.md#choosing-between-the-two-2008-sailrite-listings).[65][66]
- **The furling axle's field record, and a Sven Yrvind parallel.** Raised the reliability of the roller-furling gear on a genuinely long ocean passage, noting Sven Yrvind was said to be tackling the same problem on his own "Yrvind ½," and asked whether wood or carbon-fiber spars and sealed or plain bearings would serve better at the boom's ends. See [heavy-weather tactics — clarifying the offshore spares list](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#clarifying-the-offshore-spares-list-the-tack-arrangement-spacer-and-the-furling-axles-field-record).[67]
- **A trailering-mast pushback: no need for full enclosure.** Pushed back on newer builders' worry that a mast must stow fully inside the hull for the road, recalling having trailered boats thousands of miles without a thought for the mast beyond a red flag on the protruding end and keeping it clear of abrasion. See [mast interior sealing and anchor-light fit — further anchor-light and lightning-rod length fixes](/construction/mast-sealing-and-fit.md#further-anchor-light-and-lightning-rod-length-fixes).[63]
- **Follow-up questions on the tack-strop pipe and the boom's mainsheet tang.** On the "Force 8 gale" thread, asked what weight and material pipe a fellow builder had switched his tack-arrangement spacer to after an earlier failure, and separately asked about refinements to the boom-end tang assembly for the main sheet, including rounding the boom end for less friction and the bolt attachment — both answered in detail. See [tack strop — the tack strut also carries compression, not just tension](/components/rig/tack-strop.md#the-tack-strut-also-carries-compression-not-just-tension) and [mainsheet, outboard, and rudder fouling — boom-end splitting at the mainsheet-tang bolt](/components/mainsheet-outboard-fouling.md#boom-end-splitting-at-the-mainsheet-tang-bolt-and-a-construction-fix).[68]
- **Sailrite-calculator questions, and further furling-technique ideas for the newer sail.** Treated the Sailrite calculator's own tack-angle reading with scepticism given its asterisked caveat, asked what values belonged in its dimension boxes, and floated moving the boom further forward or lengthening the swivel rod's tail as untried fixes for the newer sail's tendency to climb the furling spool; separately described stowing the furled sail with Velcro or bungee straps and a possible second support tube, and suggested the last unfurled feet of sail could work like a storm trysail. See [Mk2 sail — tack geometry and the furling drum](/components/rig/mk2-sail.md#tack-geometry-and-the-furling-drum) and [Furling system — stowing a taller sail once furled](/components/rig/furling.md#stowing-a-taller-sail-once-furled-a-storm-trysail-like-partial-reef).[69]
- **A topping-lift fix for the newer sail's climbing tendency.** Followed up the calculator concern above with a simpler practical cure: raise the boom's aft end on the topping lift until the furled sail rolls evenly, then mark the topping-lift line at that setting, pointing to the group's own "Topping Lift" files-section folder for the fuller picture. See [Furling system — a topping-lift fix for the sail's forward creep onto the drum](/components/rig/furling.md#a-topping-lift-fix-for-the-sails-forward-creep-onto-the-drum).[72]
- **An epoxy-over-polyester compatibility answer.** Explained why coating wood with diluted polyester resin first does not help a later epoxy laminate: polyester leaves a surface wax that blocks epoxy adhesion, and dilution does not impregnate the wood, only its solvent doing so; recommended polyester alone for glassing a bottom up to the waterline where epoxy is unaffordable, with good paint over the rest. See [epoxy brands, chemistry, and cure behavior — epoxy over polyester](/construction/epoxy-brands-and-chemistry.md#epoxy-over-polyester-wax-impregnation-and-a-professionals-caution).[73]
- **A topping lift as a sunshade or awning support, in port.** Floated using a topped-up topping lift to rig a sunshade over the open hatch, or to sit out on the aft deck, pointing to a Duckworks Magazine article on the Bolger Micro for the idea's origin, and wondering whether it would work on Paradox. See [Furling system — a second use for the topping lift](/components/rig/furling.md#a-second-use-for-the-topping-lift-readiness-yuloh-clearance-and-a-rain-awning).[74]
- **Closing the 2008 sail-plan debate: cost, a Michalak-calculator caution, and a final recommendation.** Priced a full custom-cut sail kit at over double a Sailrite kit's cost once added to the sail itself; reported that inputting other designs' (Michalak) dimensions into Sailrite's own calculator did not always reproduce those plans' stated sail area, and their tack angles sometimes ran high too, a further reason to treat the calculator's output with caution; recommended the smaller, original Paradox sail plan over the newer one, fitted with a topping lift built exactly as already documented for "Zoë." See [Sail — choosing between the two 2008 Sailrite listings](/components/rig/sail.md#choosing-between-the-two-2008-sailrite-listings).[75]
- **Closing a build-hardship joke with a song, and a wondering about epoxy and glucosamine.** Answered a fellow builder's wry complaint about the physical toll of a first build by quoting Leonard Cohen's "Tower of Song" ("I ache in the places I used to play") and wondering, in the same spirit, whether powdered glucosamine mixed into epoxy might do double duty strengthening joints and joints alike.[70]
- **A field account of a hull's way after masting down, and a Sven Yrvind connection.** Struck by a report of "Little Jim" still making 2 knots after her mast was dropped entirely with no sail set, reasoned this confirmed the hull working as a "lifting body," per Sven Yrvind's own claims for the type, combined with the momentum of a relatively heavy displacement — and noted how much praise Yrvind, with his own Atlantic-crossing experience, gives Matt Layden's design work. See [Little Jim — a partial-reef account in a Force 5-7](/boats/little-jim.md#a-partial-reef-account-in-a-force-5-7-and-the-hulls-way-after-masting-down-september-2008) and [how chine runners work — the designer's own account: a lifting-body system](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md#the-designers-own-account-2007-a-lifting-body-system).[71]
- **Closing the thread: a motor doesn't cut a sailor off from nature, and a song for the one-lunger.** Argued that carrying a small motor does not automatically mean losing connection to the water — a small engine still leaves plenty to contend with — illustrated by his own home cruising ground, about 12 miles upstream through some rapids to reach open water, too far to paddle against by canoe and further still to scull, so most of his own paddling trips are planned to run downstream instead. Closed the exchange quoting Stan Rogers's "Make and Break Harbour" on a small engine's place aboard an old family working boat. A joking aside about ocean-front property (his own river/lake home being nowhere near the coast), followed by a Stan Rogers "Barrett's Privateers" singalong invitation, closed out the social side of the thread.[57][58][59] That home water is the **South Thompson River**, near the town of Chase in British Columbia's interior: it runs some 4-5 mph at flood stage, with a further stretch of grade 1-2 rapids upstream near Little Shuswap Lake and whitewater below Kamloops where the North Thompson joins — current swift enough to rule out paddling against it by canoe, let alone sculling, so trips there run downstream rather than up. The same stretch of river hosts one of the largest Pacific salmon runs anywhere, peaking roughly every four years in the nearby Adams River. See [auxiliary outboard power — a qualifier: when local current itself calls for an engine](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-qualifier-when-local-current-itself-calls-for-an-engine).[60][61][62]
