# Alberto Callejon

**Alberto Callejon**, who posted to the mailing list as **`laciudad2004`** and later administered the Paradox builders' Facebook group,[15] is a builder from **Rawson, Chubut**, on Argentina's Patagonian coast — a broad river mouth opening onto the open South Atlantic (43°21′S, 65°03′W), a region of strong wind and sea with almost no local sailing tradition beyond rowing and occasional windsurfing. Writing in Spanish and relying on machine translation for the mailing list, he began building a Paradox in 2008 and named her **"Cachivache"** — Spanish slang for a piece of junk or a knick-knack — styled on the list as "el Paradox mas austral," the most southerly Paradox on record. Full build account: [boats/cachivache.md](/boats/cachivache.md).

## Weighing the design before building

Before committing to a build, Alberto asked whether Paradox could handle conditions on his home coast, given the region's strong wind and sea and the lack of a local small-boat tradition to compare against. The reassurance he received — that Paradox is, for its size, stronger and safer than most other boats — together with the local sea and wind detail he gave in reply, is recorded in full at [blue-water suitability — a high-latitude query from Argentine Patagonia](/design/blue-water-suitability.md#a-high-latitude-query-from-argentine-patagonia).[1][2][3]

## A steel-hull question, and reaching other Spanish-speaking builders

Starting construction, Alberto asked whether "Cachivache"'s bottom and sides could be built from sheet steel instead of plywood — a question that, unusually among the archive's many recurrences of the same idea, drew a direct answer: a minimum 3.5 mm low-carbon steel plate, sandblasted and epoxy-coated to stop rust, with a warning to expect skepticism toward steel from other builders regardless. See [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative) — a 2008 recurrence, from a builder in Patagonia](/design/ideas/aluminum-construction-idea.md#a-2008-recurrence-from-a-builder-in-patagonia) for the full exchange, including confirmation that the advice came from general steel-boatbuilding knowledge rather than from an actual steel-built Paradox.[4][5][6]

Separately, he offered his own instant-messenger contact to any other Spanish-speaking builders wanting to share the construction experience.[7]

## A polyester-and-epoxy compatibility question

Weighing how to impregnate Cachivache's wood, Alberto asked whether a diluted coat of polyester resin, applied first, would still let epoxy be laminated over it — answered that it would not reliably, since polyester resin typically leaves a surface wax that blocks epoxy adhesion, and dilution does not impregnate wood in any case. Full exchange, including a professional's caution against the combination and the fallback advice for a tight budget: [epoxy brands, chemistry, and cure behavior — epoxy over polyester](/construction/epoxy-brands-and-chemistry.md#epoxy-over-polyester-wax-impregnation-and-a-professionals-caution).[12] He settled on laminating just above the waterline with epoxy resin, painting the rest.[13] A further reply arrived after he had already settled the question, offering epoxy diluted with acetone as an alternative to a polyester pre-coat.[14]

## Seeking Don Elliott's construction manual

Unable to reach Don Elliott's own `tomah.com/delliott` site, referenced in messages from as far back as 2001, Alberto asked whether anyone held copies of the construction articles once posted there.[8] Directed to Don Elliott's own companion group as the likely source — with a warning about how strictly its moderator kept discussion to Matt Layden's stock design — he subscribed and e-mailed Don Elliott directly, but never received a reply. See [people/don-elliott.md](/people/don-elliott.md#contributions-and-technical-positions) for the moderation practice itself.[9][10] Weeks later, still without the manual, he asked the list for an Argentine contact with connections in the United States who might help him obtain a copy.[11]
