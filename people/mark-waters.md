# Mark Waters

**Mark Waters** (Yahoo profile `mwatersworld`) is a Los Angeles, California Paradox builder who introduced himself to the list in February 2006 while registering his boat with a UK build registry. At that point his Paradox — built strictly to the plans, with no name yet chosen — was **90% complete**, with completion expected by summer 2006. He had also built a Selway-Fisher 50/50 sailing canoe before turning to the Paradox.

Waters asked to correspond with any other Paradox builders or owners in the Los Angeles area; a query followed asking him to clarify whether "L.A." meant the city proper or the wider county, hoping to see the boat on a future visit. Waters had no objection to having his e-mail address listed alongside his registry entry when the idea was raised shortly afterward. The e-mail address on file for Waters was subsequently flagged as apparently incorrect, with a request for him to get in touch to fix it.[5] Full account of the registry episode at [references/paradox-uk-website.md](/references/paradox-uk-website.md#a-2006-database-renewal-and-a-new-los-angeles-builder).

Photographs of Waters' finished Paradox were later called "superb," with plans to add them to the UK Paradox site. See [boats/faith.md](/boats/faith.md#chine-runner-and-gudgeon-photos-and-mark-waters-paradox).[6]

The gallery link was then posted to the group, and Waters' finished boat drew unprompted praise from three quarters: "incredibly beautiful" job; the interior finish singled out as looking especially well done from what could be seen in the photos; and the standard of finish admired along with a joke about lacking the patience for that quality of work.[7][8][9]

Waters was one of only two respondents to an August 2006 group poll on ballast weight, voting for **300 lb** — see [weight, ballast, and displacement — ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#ballast).[11]

His interior work continued to draw notice two years later, when a newer California builder, having admired it in photographs, e-mailed Mark directly to ask what wood he had used — mahogany — and adopted the same wood for his own interior joinery. See [boats/wizard-spawn.md](/boats/wizard-spawn.md#mahogany-for-the-interior-inspired-by-another-builders-finish).[14]

His boat was named **"Raffles."** A 2014 restatement of the wider community's praise for her workmanship called Waters' Paradox "one of the best built," a verdict echoing the "incredibly beautiful" and "superb finish" comments recorded above.[12]

**An unresolved location discrepancy.** A 2014 census lists Waters' location as **Portland, Oregon**, against **Los Angeles, California** everywhere else in the archive, including his own 2006 self-identification. Neither figure is corrected here; the conflict is left standing for lack of a way to adjudicate it.[13]

## Weighing a self-steering wind vane, and the book behind it

With "Raffles" otherwise finished, Waters' one remaining project was a self-steering wind vane, and he asked after good design sources, whether his own thinking resembled the vane built for "[Zoë](/boats/zoe.md)," and whether a sheet-to-tiller arrangement might be worth trying — prompted partly by finding "Raffles" tracked so well under sail that a simpler, old-fashioned direct vane, without a servo-pendulum linkage, seemed like it might suffice. See [self-steering — a further owner's tracking observation, and interest in a simpler vane](/operations/self-steering.md#a-further-owners-tracking-observation-and-interest-in-a-simpler-vane) for the general answer this drew.[15] He later passed along a free PDF copy of *Self-Steering Under Sail*, judging it strong on theory and useful for understanding commercial self-steering systems from a "reverse engineering" perspective, though offering no step-by-step building instructions of its own.[16]

Separately, Waters asked after the dimensions behind "Zoë"'s own vane gear, drawing detailed servo-blade proportions in reply and a recommendation to study the commercially built Cape Horn gear; a follow-up question about whether the Cape Horn oar swings freely, and about a Rudder-Head-Mount (RHM) design combining a trim tab's and a pendulum's advantages, drew a fuller explanation of how a rudder-head-mount gear differs in principle from a conventional servo-pendulum. See [self-steering — quantified servo-blade proportions, and the rudder-head-mount principle explained](/operations/self-steering.md#quantified-servo-blade-proportions-and-the-rudder-head-mount-principle-explained) for the full technical exchange.[17]

## Answering detailed vane-building questions, and an epoxy recommendation

Having built a model of an RHM gear over a weekend and found "the penny finally dropped," Waters asked four detailed practical questions about putting one into practice — whether the rudder tiller is left to swing completely free, whether the pendulum swings out if not secured, whether the securing line around the pendulum shaft causes noticeable friction or would call for a bearing, and where the vane setting lines are best secured, including whether they can be reached safely from the cockpit in a seaway. See [self-steering — practical rudder-head-mount building questions, answered](/operations/self-steering.md#practical-rudder-head-mount-building-questions-answered) for the answers this drew.[18]

Separately, answering a newer California builder weighing epoxy brands, Waters recommended **System Three**, having built the whole of "Raffles" with it for its price, easy mixing, and customer service, and pointed to **Aircraft Spruce** in Corona as a Southern California supplier stocking both generic structural epoxies and System Three itself. See [epoxy brands, chemistry, and cure behavior — MAS epoxy validated for general use, and a California supplier](/construction/epoxy-brands-and-chemistry.md#mas-epoxy-validated-for-general-use-and-a-california-supplier) for the fuller brand comparison this contributed to.[19]

Three weeks later, with two rival models under way at once — an upside-down design and a conventional one, to compare which performed best — Waters asked specifically about the Walt Murray-inspired system's own **5 in x 32 in x 9 in** vane-blade dimensions, having read of light-air trouble with a vane built to that size, and floated switching between a larger and a smaller vane blade to suit conditions rather than settling on one size. See [self-steering — progress on two competing vane builds, and a question of blade size](/operations/self-steering.md#progress-on-two-competing-vane-builds-and-a-question-of-blade-size) for the cross-linked technique.[20]
