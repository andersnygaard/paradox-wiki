# Jeff UK ("roverticket")

**Jeff**, a UK boatbuilder and designer on the Isle of Wight, posted to the `paradoxbuilders` list from 2001 to 2008 under two successive e-mail handles — **doryman_uk** (2001–2004, signing "Jeff Please" then "Jeff UK") and **apcf14** (from 2003, signing "Jeff UK" and "Jeffery Please") — both tied to the same Yahoo profile, **`roverticket`**. He had owned as many as five boats at one point (since reduced to two), worked professionally on design projects (12 years in a large engineering company's drawing office, then contract work for boat and hovercraft firms), and later became a director of VectaVane Ltd., a UK wind-vane self-steering gear manufacturer. He ordered materials for an early Paradox derivative and, in 2002, drew a from-scratch 16 ft redesign, "Paramour," which was never built; after several quiet years he returned in September 2006 with a new 17 ft Paradox/Enigma hybrid, "PARANIGMA," renamed **NOLI** in January 2007 and built through 2007–2008 toward a launch. His surname is not recorded.

## What he built and designed

Jeff ordered material for a Paradox derivative but, with only a lean-to behind his house to build in, planned to start the following spring. His build plan called for **3/8 in marine plywood sides** and a **two-layer 3/8 in ply bottom** with a heavy glass-and-Kevlar layup — reasoning that few builders cruise as hard as Matt Layden or the Bolducs, that 3/8 in ply matches 1/2 in exterior ply for strength while lasting longer and bending fairer, and that on a wider (4 ft) stitch-and-glue bottom the [chine runner](/chine-runners/construction.md) would be fixed outside, flush, then glassed over. See [hull planking](/construction/hull-planking.md) for the full thread.

His larger project was **"Paramour,"** a from-scratch 16 ft stitch-and-glue microcruiser sharing Paradox's rig and rudder but with a flatter hull run — 1,507 lb displacement and a .566 prismatic coefficient (via PLYBOATS) against Paradox's 1,410 lb and .580. It grew from a speculative "design a new 16 ft hull" idea — floated because the UK has no US-style 14 ft trailering rule — into a named design drawn over several months and estimated ~0.5 kt faster than stock. See [Paramour](/design/variants/paramour-design.md) for the full specification and [stretched Paradox](/design/variants/stretched-paradox.md) for the enlargement debate.

## Construction contributions

- **Chine-runner construction, the kerf-and-bend method:** rather than pre-manufacturing runners from small blocks, cut a single timber to the drawing's shape and saw kerfs (4–6 in apart, ~12 mm of wood left, cut deeper toward the sharper sweep aft) through the back and bottom edges so it bends to the curve, then fill the kerfs with epoxy — "in glue and dust we put our trust." See [chine runner construction](/chine-runners/construction.md).
- **Against metal-edging the runners:** the runners' shape governs the boat's **windward ability**, so refacing them in metal risks the hydrodynamics unless the builder has real metalworking skill.
- **How chine runners work:** they act as a **fence** stopping water from breaking off the hull sides and running underneath — the main cause of leeway on a flat-bottomed hull — rather than resisting leeway like a centerboard, and so should work at any angle of heel. A head-to-head reach between [Paradox](/paradox.md) and [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) (centerboard raised) was proposed to settle the point; no such test is recorded. See [stretched Paradox](/design/variants/stretched-paradox.md).
- **Thin-ply precedent:** **Shane Acton's "SHRIMPY,"** an 18 ft 6 in Caprice-class sloop built near Cowes that circumnavigated on a 5/16 in plywood skin with no glass, was cited repeatedly — both to justify light scantlings on his own build and to reassure a builder whose secondhand Paradox turned out to have ~6 mm sides rather than the specified 12 mm that 6 mm plus glass was fine on a 13 ft 10 in boat. See [Help! — undersize side panels](/construction/undersize-side-panel-ply.md).
- **Materials sourcing:** a Kevlar bottom never meant paying retail — a Paradox needs only ~5 sq m, best scrounged as offcuts from a race-boat yard. See [epoxy and fiberglass materials sourcing](/references/materials-sourcing-links.md). A UK epoxy/copper antifouling coating, **"Copperbot,"** claimed to last ~10 years, is also on record from him.

## Design arguments

Jeff made the case **against** modifying Paradox toward bilge keels: they would add drag, need extra internal structure, increase leeway and draft when heeled, suit the flat hull poorly, and make trailer-launching much harder. The underlying principle — illustrated by a 16 ft rowing skiff of his own design that a later owner cut open for an outboard it was never meant to take — is that Paradox is unique and already proven at sea, and anyone needing more capability should choose a different design rather than compromise this one. See [stretched Paradox](/design/variants/stretched-paradox.md).

## Reactions and community exchanges

A complete, sailing Paradox once offered for **$500** in Florida, passed up at the time, struck Jeff as startling value from the UK side — about **£340**, barely a Paradox sail's worth, though only enough for "a crappy Mirror dinghy" in the UK — prompting him to wonder what transatlantic shipping would cost. See [secondhand Paradox market](/operations/secondhand-market.md).

The Paramour reveal drew the design's first recorded buyer interest, with two list members asking to buy a plan set. Jeff, flattered, held off until he could check the drawings for errors, citing a crowded workload (drum fittings, a 53 ft motor-cruiser layout for a friend) and a struggle to get his Mustek 1200 scanner working under Windows XP to post a TURBOCAD sketch. No completed plan set, price, or sale is recorded; a later query about "his 16' Paradox plans" went unanswered. See [Paramour](/design/variants/paramour-design.md) and [Building Paradox larger](/design/larger-paradox.md).

His recurring sign-off motto: *"May the strong wind behind you never be your own."*

### Background and his own build plans

"Jeff UK" had a professional design background — 12 years in a large engineering company's drawing office, then contract work for boat and hovercraft firms, with a professional-welder brother — and by early 2004 was a **director of VectaVane Ltd.**, a UK manufacturer of what he described as the lightest servo-pendulum wind-vane self-steering gear available.

Asked, ten months after an earlier unanswered query, whether anyone had heard from the designer of "the 16' stitch and glue version of Paradox," "Jeff UK" confirmed he was still lurking on the list and still meant to build a Paradox, though work commitments had kept him from starting. His drawings for a 16 ft Paradox-type design were by then finished with enough detail to build from, though he described the design more modestly than the earlier "Paramour" specification — as simply **stretching Paradox by 8 in** to gain extra living space, rather than a from-scratch redesign — and his building area remained committed to two other boats. He had met a fellow builder in person at Cowes, aboard "[little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)," during that builder's Poole-to-Cowes crossing, and remarked on how much interior room Matt Layden had designed into so small a boat. In the "Steel?" thread that December he laid out a further concrete build plan — **3/8 in sides, a two-layer 1/2 in + 3/8 in bottom, solid-shaped screwed-in-place chine runners, and egg-box main frames sharing the water tank's longerons** — three years delayed by family circumstances but about to begin, using epoxy kept at a constant low temperature for those three years.

His first appearances welcomed Matt Layden to the list — a transatlantic congratulation on the Everglades Challenge win (joking Matt might have become "a sort of boating version of Howard Hughs") and a request for detailed Paradox photos.

### The "Steel?" design-philosophy objection

The strongest design-philosophy case on record against building a Paradox in steel argued: her whole point is that she can be built by anyone with low woodworking skills, and steel adds fabrication problems (faithfully shaping the chine runners, fairing a round-tubed steel chine edge, fitting a wooden deck to a steel hull) for no real gain, when Matt Layden has already sailed the plywood original thousands of miles. The case was answered directly — see [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative) — a builder's case against steel, on design-philosophy grounds](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md#a-builders-case-against-steel-on-design-philosophy-grounds).

The exchange escalated into a direct, personal challenge over seamanship ("take up golf or buy a white stick"), drawing a pointed reply and prompting efforts to defuse the argument. Once the personal edge cooled, the technical questions were answered in more detail — distinguishing sailing in fog from navigating near it, disputing that a Bruce Roberts steel design validates a steel Paradox, and arguing that if steel were really better, Matt Layden would have built his own Paradox in it — the design-philosophy objection was restated point by point once more, and a list of steel-specific build questions was posed. The thread's fullest technical rebuttal of a steel Paradox, when it arrived, was welcomed as hitting "the nail right on the head," and the moment was used to lay out a concrete build plan (above); the steel question was not raised again. See [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative) — the design-philosophy objection escalates into a personal dispute](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md#the-design-philosophy-objection-escalates-into-a-personal-dispute) and [rebuttal: why a steel Paradox would be "a horrible mistake"](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md#smiths-rebuttal-why-a-steel-paradox-would-be-a-horrible-mistake).

### Seamanship and gear opinions

- **Anchoring:** Matt Layden's own view on anchoring was sought out "after all he has sailed a few miles now in his boat"; a bow-ring/entry-pipe/bridle scheme was sketched for retrieving the anchor rope from the afterdeck without going forward, with anchoring from the stern judged simpler still; a detachable anchor stowed under the floor was floated so a single rope could serve both a working anchor and a sea anchor; and the question of whether the Paradox afterdeck could simply be flat, as a work surface and short-term anchor stowage spot, was raised directly — no answer is recorded. See [ground tackle and anchoring — a bridle scheme, and a flat-afterdeck counterproposal](/operations/ground-tackle.md#jeff-uks-bridle-scheme-and-a-flat-afterdeck-counterproposal).
- **Wind vanes:** despite a commercial stake in selling more VectaVane units, fitting any wind vane to Paradox was judged "a waste of time" — the boat sails at too shallow an angle of heel (10-20 degrees) for such gear to earn its keep, stowing spars inside the hull would foul a centerline-mounted rudder linkage, and Matt Layden's own Everglades Challenge win, sailed largely hands-off, is proof enough that Paradox already self-steers adequately without it. An untried alternative seen on the James Wharram catamaran-building website was separately floated — a paddle-less wind vane, steered purely by wind pressure and mounted forward of the mast with lines led aft to the tiller — while the whole question was judged "purely academic" for a boat described as a coastal cruiser that only thinks it's an offshore one, explicitly excepting Matt, Dave, and Mindy, in whose hands she already is one. See [self-steering — a VectaVane director's skeptical outside view](/operations/self-steering.md#a-vectavane-directors-skeptical-outside-view).
- **Planing under power:** the definitive answer on record on whether a Paradox can plane under power — full text and citation at [auxiliary outboard power — weighing a 2 hp four-stroke against a bigger motor and whether Paradox can plane](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#weighing-a-2-hp-four-stroke-against-a-bigger-motor-and-whether-paradox-can-plane).
- **A full motor-only "Power Paradox" conversion scheme:** answering a query about a sail-free, motor-only Paradox, laid out a complete conversion — reduced draught and rocker, a small 2-2.5 hp four-stroke with the throttle led into the cabin, trim adjusted by tilting the engine rather than shifting ballast (the hull will not plane regardless of engine size), no chine runners needed, a forward deck hatch and solar vent in place of the vent trunk, a fold-down single-burner galley in the same area, wash boards as on Little Cruiser, and 2 in of extra transom rake with a small outboard well and skeg. See [auxiliary outboard power — ballasting for power-only use, and a full "Power Paradox" conversion scheme](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#ballasting-for-power-only-use-and-a-full-power-paradox-conversion-scheme).[51]
- **Boom taper and a plastic-pipe boom.** On the sail-furling drum question, agreed the boom's taper — not any drum fence — controls where the sail sits when reefed, tunable with a plane/spokeshave or built up with wrapped draught excluder to clear persistent creases, and floated a boom laminated from three or four lengths of 1 in plastic water pipe on plywood spacers (hidden by the sail's sleeve). See [rig and sail — the reefing triangle, and why reshaping the boom taper is the wrong fix](/components/rig/index.md#the-reefing-triangle-and-why-reshaping-the-boom-taper-is-the-wrong-fix).[52]
- **Enigma-style vee for an enlarged hull.** Suggested, for an 18-19 ft enlarged Paradox under construction, that veeing the bottom panel forward of midships (as on Enigma) would save a few hundred pounds of displacement. See [stretched Paradox — an 18-19 ft "maxi paradox," under way](/design/variants/stretched-paradox.md#a-builders-18-19-ft-maxi-paradox-under-way).[53]
- **A 7'6" Paradox/Enigma rig, mentioned then dropped.** On seeing a carbon-tape boom-core idea, declared it the answer for the rig of a "7' 6" version of PARADOX / ENIGMA" discussed off-list, and said the drawings would follow "right away" — but no drawings or further mention of this scaled-down rig followed on record, beyond a closing exchange of jokes (a Venetian-blind sail, and whether it would be "controlled by electric motors"), closed out with a one-line quip blaming a keyboard glitch for a run of missing R's. See [a scaled-down "mini" Paradox (speculative)](/design/variants/scaled-down-idea.md#reception-a-comparison-to-the-bolger-brick-and-a-cheaper-and-quicker-alternative).[54][56][57]
- **Scaling a mast and sail together for an enlarged hull.** For the 18-19 ft "maxi paradox," gave a worked method for sizing a new rig to an enlarged hull: design the mast to fit inside the boat first, then scale the Mk2 sail up to match (expecting to reduce it slightly once drawn), using the stock Paradox sail plan as a guide for the sail's lead — recalling a past calculation that put the lead at just under **10%**. See [stretched Paradox — an 18-19 ft "maxi paradox," under way](/design/variants/stretched-paradox.md#a-builders-18-19-ft-maxi-paradox-under-way).[55]
- **A near-built 17 ft Paradox-type motor cruiser, and "PARANIGMA."** Disclosed, in September 2006, a motor-cruiser project that had never previously appeared on the list: a **17 ft, Paradox-type hull** with the same accommodation as Paradox but a multi-chine bottom, sized for a 2 hp Honda outboard, for which the hull parts and bulkheads had already been cut a year before — derailed only by scaffolding erected next door. The still-pending next design was named **"PARANIGMA,"** a portmanteau for a Paradox/Enigma hybrid, with the motor cruiser to follow once PARANIGMA was finished. Full thread context at [boats/enuf.md — a near-miss Paradox-type motor cruiser, and "Enuf" as a talking point for motorboating](/boats/enuf.md#a-near-miss-paradox-type-motor-cruiser-and-enuf-as-a-talking-point-for-motorboating).[58]
- **Objecting to a full-skeg, barn-door-rudder redesign.** Answering a query about a drawn Paradox interpretation, explained why moving the rudder onto a long skeg is not a simple hardware swap: it shifts the center of lateral resistance aft, forcing the sail's center of effort and the mast aft with it, and alters the sail's lead — concluding the change is not worth making without fully understanding the consequences, since it only adds draught and risks a worse-sailing boat. Full text at [design/rudder.md — a proposed full skeg and barn-door rudder, and why it shifts the whole design](/components/rudder.md#a-proposed-full-skeg-and-barn-door-rudder-and-why-it-shifts-the-whole-design).[59]

By late January 2007 "Jeff UK" had renamed PARANIGMA to **"NOLI,"** reporting he had begun installing foam camping-mat insulation (about 3/8 in thick, roughly £3 a roll, held with spray adhesive), estimating around £15 to insulate the whole boat this way.[75] Asked directly for photographs of the project, "Jeff UK" pointed to an article he had posted in the Microcruisingpics forum's files section (titled "MY NEW BOAT"), with further pictures in that forum's photo section, and reported the boat had progressed somewhat further than shown there before weather stopped outside work, leaving only the mast and spars still under way.[76][77]

A follow-up asked whether the PDF plan/photo link for PARANIGMA/NOLI was dead.[78] The pictures and construction sketch were found in the photo album, drawing a comment that a couple of extra feet of length seemed to offer noticeably more room.[79] "Jeff UK" gave NOLI's dimensions directly: just under **14 ft**, the same length as stock PARADOX but with only **7 in of draught**, and about **500 lb lighter**.[80] Asked how the weight was cut and whether the construction still looked robust rather than relying on high-tech ultralight components, given the photos suggested a solidly built boat, "Jeff UK" explained NOLI's construction is far lighter than PARADOX's throughout, her sail area is smaller at **86 sq ft**, and she carries about the same amount of ballast despite the weight saved elsewhere.[81][82][83]

"Jeff UK" then gave NOLI's full construction schedule: a hull of **1/4 in and 3/8 in Gaboon marine plywood**, with **three layers of glass on the bottom** back to just past the halfway mark, tapering to two layers to the transom and a single layer on the sides and deck; bulkheads all **3/8 in Gaboon marine ply**; deck **1/4 in Gaboon marine ply**. He budgeted **350 lb for crew and stores** (against his own 196 lb weight), targeting a **558 lb hull weight** with ballast to bring her down to her marks — judged doable — and noted NOLI's fairer rocker compared with stock PARADOX, though he still rated PARADOX the better sea boat for his intended coastal Solent sailing, where half an hour should reach shelter. He deliberately would **not publish plans** for NOLI so as not to "step on Matt's toe's," though a successful design might eventually yield enough information — alongside Matt Layden's own PARADOX drawings, which would still have to be bought — plus his articles, a couple of expanded panel drawings, and a free CAD program, for an experienced dinghy builder to build a copy.[84] Thanking him for the figures, George looked forward to hearing how the lighter build worked out.[85] "Jeff UK" reassured George he would not be NOLI's only builder — "There's me for a start."[86]

By mid-March 2007 "Jeff UK" was again hoping to have NOLI in the water by June — cabin top and hatch under way alongside cut rudder blanks and a mast and spars in progress — though a rush work commitment had limited him to only about two hours on the boat during a rare stretch of fine weather; he came down with the flu shortly afterward. Full thread, including his exchange with Bill Serjeant and Al on sail comparisons and a proposed three-boat UK meet-up, at [Faith — fibreglassing the hull, and fairing woven roving before painting](/boats/faith.md#fibreglassing-the-hull-and-fairing-woven-roving-before-painting).[87][88]

Answering [Pete Martin](/people/pete-martin.md)'s materials-pricing question later the same March, "Jeff UK" confirmed NOLI's own hull was built from the cheaper "Far Eastern" marine ply, judging the price saving over Robbins' basic grade enough to more than pay for a sail, with only one small deck-edge delamination near the mast in the whole build, fixed with a glue squirt, tape, a cap strip, and glass; recommended MAS Low Viscosity Epoxy Resin (`wilsonswebsolutions.com`), noting UK epoxy prices run well above US prices for the same product. Full text and citation at [plywood grade and foam flotation debate — a 2007 UK shopping-list recurrence](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#a-2007-uk-shopping-list-recurrence-far-eastern-ply-and-epoxy-quantity).[89]

## A beamless, laminated cabin top for NOLI

In April 2007, replying to Bill Serjeant's cabin-top-beam count question (see [deck and cabin beam lamination — cabin-top beam count: three versus four](/construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md#cabin-top-beam-count-three-versus-four-and-whether-the-roof-takes-a-standing-load)), "Jeff UK" disclosed NOLI's cabin top dispensed with the plans' roof beams altogether: a roof laminated from **two layers of 3.5 mm plywood**, resting on the 1/2 in ply cabin sides and stitch-and-glued into place, with an **external beam** at the forward hatch coaming and a **truss made from a bent aluminium tube**, running from the cockpit coaming, fastened in the middle under the hatch coaming, to the opposite side — carrying the roof's downward load and side thrust without internal beams. The forward window frame was similarly built from two laminated layers of plywood, stitched and glued into position, judged as strong as the beamed original but lighter. Dry-fitting the first of NOLI's **chine runners** the same day, the **20 mm Scottish Douglas Fir** proved tough enough to break a drill bit, requiring saw cuts along two edges of each laminated layer to bend it around the hull; NOLI's chine runners measure **54 in long by 2 5/8 in wide**, cut at 35 degrees with a planned hollow added to the top face. With the boat's construction otherwise in hand, "Jeff UK" reported plans to visit his sailmaker that week to order NOLI's sail.[90]

Answering Glen Maxwell's satisfaction with his own re-cut Dabbler Mark II sail (see [rig and sail — the Dabbler re-cut](/components/rig/index.md#the-dabbler-re-cut-mark-ii-sail-a-decisive-improvement)), "Jeff UK" opted to stay with his own local sailmaker, **Graham of CAWS Sails** in Cowes on the Isle of Wight — a fellow one-man loft who had already cut him several sets of sprit, lug, and Bermudan sails, always setting well, and who tries a finished sail on the boat itself and makes further alterations if not satisfied.[91]

Replying to Dave Bolduc's repost of Matt Layden's dimensioned sailmaking recipe (see [sailmaking — Matt Layden's sailmaking recipe for a more weatherly Paradox sail](/construction/sailmaking.md#matt-laydens-sailmaking-recipe-for-a-more-weatherly-paradox-sail)), "Jeff UK" confirmed he would take the article to CAWS Sails when ordering NOLI's sail, recalling that the same loft had cut him a set of sails roughly 25 years earlier for a Dart 18 catamaran that set without a wrinkle — sails good enough that he began winning club races until told they were not one-design, and, forced back onto slower class-mandated sails "colourful" enough to please the fleet, placed second, sold the boat, and gave up racing rather than being told what to sail. That afternoon he had glued the port chine runner and filleted around the deck, with the starboard chine runner to follow once a day away from the boat had passed.[92]

The same message asked after ["Swamp Thing"](/people/bolduc/dave.md), Dave and Mindy Bolduc's own Paradox under construction, and whether she would be afloat that year.[92]

Bill Serjeant, seeing photographs of NOLI's progress, judged her lines "sweet" — likely to prove a slippery Paradox type with a good turn of speed, her extra length and lighter construction expected to leave stock Paradox behind in most conditions — and looked forward to meeting "Jeff UK" and NOLI in person that summer.[93]

Thanking Bill for the compliment, "Jeff UK" reported the build "giving me the greatest buzz" despite having designed and built about a dozen boats before it, while joking about the mounting cost of finishing her — a stolen door mirror, a car-insurance bill, and utility bills all arriving the same week — and confirmed the sail would not be needed for about two months yet, with further construction photographs to follow.[94]

## NOLI's chine runners finished, and a trailer purchase

By late April 2007 "Jeff UK" had finished NOLI's chine runners and glassed their underside, using PeelPly over the glass to get a smoother finish and reduce later fairing work, with the hatch next on the list. A new combi trailer, rated for about 500 lb and suited to both NOLI and a dory rowing skiff, was expected within the week; he still hoped to be sailing NOLI by the end of July, despite extra paid drawing work cutting into build time.[95] Trimming the surplus glass cloth from around the chine runners' edge is best done while the glass is still "green" (not fully cured), running a knife around the edge using the underside as a guide — combined with the PeelPly finish, this leaves a smooth, rounded edge at comparatively little extra effort.[96]

The new trailer itself was a **RAPIDE COMBI**, bought at £470 against an £800 list price after asking for the usual discount — chosen specifically because NOLI's lighter construction let a lighter, cheaper trailer suffice, unlike the roughly £1,800 quote a combi trailer for the stock-built "[Faith](/boats/faith.md)" had drawn. "Jeff UK" proposed the UK builders then racing toward a launch — himself with NOLI, Bill Serjeant with Faith, and [Alastair](/people/alastair-law.md) with [Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md) — might eventually meet up and sail in company, suggesting Poole Harbour or the Solent, with each photographing or filming the others' boats alongside his own.[96]

Around the same time, "Jeff UK" reported buying a cut-price portable toilet from LIDL — a "PortaCrapper" at £30, well made and comparable in appearance to a name-brand PortaPotti, bought alongside three other men doing the same and swapping jokes about the purchase — recommended as a good seat for looking out of the hatch.[97] Asked whether it matched an even cheaper £39.99 unit seen at a rival discount chain, Netto, and whether it carried any maker's name or brand, no answer is on record.[98]

## NOLI's bottom thickness, and a Potter comparison

Watching Pete Martin's newly dry-mocked-up JoHanna, "Jeff UK" suggested she could be built stitch-and-glue with a bottom panel of either two layers or a single thickness of 1/2 in ply, reasoning from NOLI's own bottom — built to only **3/8 in** — itself justified against the original wooden West Wight Potter's even wider bottom panel, built to only 5/16 in for broadly similar duty to Paradox.[99] Answering a West Wight Potter owner's account of a hull-holing grounding on gravel (see [hull planking — how thick does the bottom really need to be?](/construction/hull-planking.md#how-thick-does-the-bottom-really-need-to-be)), "Jeff UK" noted that boat's GRP construction and stepped cabin top still call for care over where to beach, and that a thick bottom under a thick layer of glass does not by itself license beaching anywhere — the bottom needs treating with respect whatever its thickness.[100]

## A removable hardtop concept, and NOLI restated as a "Paradox-sized Enigma"

Replying to a newcomer's proposal for a lighter, car-topping Paradox/Enigma hybrid with a bolt-off, invertible cabin top (see [car-topping conversion ideas](/design/variants/car-topping-conversion-idea.md)), "Jeff UK" set out a removable hardtop of his own devising for a car-topped Enigma-type boat: built to the same shape as Enigma's original folding soft top, bent from 1.5 mm plywood with the front windscreen stitch-and-glued, glassed inside and out, and window apertures cut and glazed with 1.5-2 mm clear acrylic — a weight about the same as the soft top and its internal tubing, workable enough to leave fitted to the boat on the roof rack.[103] Comparing his own build directly against Matt's Enigma — built from 1/4 in topsides and 3/8 in bottom — "Jeff UK" restated NOLI as effectively **"a PARADOX sized ENIGMA,"** built to the same-sized materials; reducing the draught to 7 in and tweaking the design on computer had shaved just over a third of stock PARADOX's weight off her, to the point that, empty, she might for a laugh even be cartoppable herself. He gave his location as the Isle of Wight.[104]

A 2008 outside listing of Layden-lineage micro-cruisers under construction or already sailing, answering a newcomer's question about designs comparable to Paradox, named NOLI a **"4.24 m length Enigma,"** alongside Little Cruiser and the Enigma 460 prototype, pointing to a "MY NEW BOAT.pdf" build update posted in the EnigmaBuilders-as-Designed-by-Matt_Layden Yahoo group's files section — likely referring to NOLI given Jeff's own "Paradox-sized Enigma" characterization above, though the two accounts were never directly reconciled on the list.[126]

## NOLI's own dimensions, and a frame-2/frame-3 modification for extra sleeping room

Answering a newcomer's basic-dimensions question, "Jeff UK" gave NOLI's own interior figures directly: a **1900 mm** sleeping-area length — matching the stock Paradox figure — and **920 mm** between the bins at frame 3 (see [crew size and cabin fit — a newcomer's basic-interior-dimensions FAQ, answered](/operations/crew-size-and-fit.md#a-newcomers-basic-interior-dimensions-faq-answered) for the fuller thread). Lowering frame 2's opening flush with the cabin sole, reducing its lower-corner radius to 50 mm, and trimming the opening back to the bin and mast box lets a sleeper's feet extend forward into the "stateroom"; narrowing frame 3 itself by 1 in and glassing it directly to the hull, with the side panel there thinned from 1/2 in to 3/8 in, was offered as a way to reduce the pressure the frame otherwise bears — even with the bins left out, frame 3 still stands in the way. NOLI's own sides are built from **1/4 in** marine-grade plywood, with frame 3 at **3/8 in**. Cruising two aboard a stock-sized cabin — a full-sized sailor and a son of unknown size — was judged more realistically solved by camping ashore than by both sleeping aboard, in contrast to Matt Layden, whose own slight build let him and a partner sleep aboard together.[105]

## A reversal on publishing NOLI's plans, and a design debt to Matt Layden and "Sven"

Having earlier decided not to publish NOLI's plans "so as not to step on Matt's toe's" (see [above](#nolis-chine-runners-finished-and-a-trailer-purchase)), "Jeff UK" reversed course once several members wrote asking directly: once NOLI was launched and performing to his liking, he would release his drawings free as a download to this and related groups. Using them would still mean separately buying Matt Layden's own PARADOX drawings from Dave and Mindy Bolduc, at $40-45 including shipping — "two boats for the price of one" — plus a basic CAD software package, some of it available free online. He framed NOLI's design as a collaboration with Matt Layden despite never having spoken to him directly, "it always felt like he was looking over my shoulder," and credited Matt's own years of research with models and prototypes with bringing his latest design to the drawing board together with **Sven** — a research partnership distinct from, and earlier than, Sven Yrvind's own later, separately documented visit to consult Matt about a project of Sven's own (see [Matt Layden biography](/matt-layden/biography.md)). No boat-design textbook, in his view, could have produced a boat like Paradox. Working to "the minimum of information" himself, as a hobby builder, he did not want the chore of producing sale-ready drawings or explaining them to a first-time builder; he would instead release his own dimensioned panel-layout and rig/general-arrangement drawings — essentially the same approach as Paradox's own — as a modification to Paradox, with community help and further files-section photos expected to see a builder through. He signed off intending to get "part 2 of my tome" under way.[106] A newly signed-up Paradox plans owner thanked him for the offer within days, calling future NOLI plans a welcome New Year's gift whenever they arrived.[107] A separate builder, approving of NOLI as an example of *informed* modification during a wider "dingos"/design-philosophy debate (see [paradox.md](/paradox.md#citations)), asked whether NOLI yet had a proposed launch date — the question went unanswered on the list.[108]

## Weather delays, a Japanese sculling-oar alternative, and NOLI's storage-space question

By late January 2008, with NOLI's own build halted by cold, rain, and wind, work was expected to resume once the weather improved, with a launch date still hoped for that June — an answer, at last, to the standing question of whether NOLI had a proposed launch date. For propulsion, "Jeff UK" remained committed to the yuloh over an outboard, but not built exactly to Matt Layden's own drawing: he had begun studying the more efficient Japanese **VE-SCULL** design, which achieves laminar flow, and had already ordered a length of spruce toward a simple improvement of his own. See [yuloh and stowed-rig retention](/components/yuloh.md) for the standard yuloh this would depart from.[109]

Restating the headroom technique already given for a UK build (see [above](#construction-and-plans-answers)), "Jeff UK" reported that on NOLI itself, lowering the cabin sole and leaving out the roof's 2 in of foam gained 3-4 in of extra headroom, and that laminating the cabin roof separately, off the boat, as he had done on NOLI, saves a beam; taking the bulkhead-2 opening flush with the floor, also done on NOLI, gains additional leg room. For water storage, the gap under NOLI's own cabin sole fore and aft of bulkhead 3 was sized to take 2-litre bottles, giving about 32 litres — roughly a week's supply — with room to store more if needed. He closed by putting a question to Paradox owners generally: how much of the design's storage space actually gets used, how much of what's carried actually gets used, and how much could be done without.[109]

## Paint advice for a fellow UK builder, and cabin-sole matting and small gear for NOLI

Answering Pete Martin's interior-paint question, "Jeff UK" recommended a clear primer sealer for both interior and exterior surfaces — two coats, rubbed down lightly between them, then the usual undercoat and topcoat — sourced cheaper in bulk tins from International or a general paint/DIY shop, and reported using ordinary floor paint for an interior finish himself, rating it hardwearing. Full text and citation at [epoxy pre-coating technique — interior finish](/construction/epoxy-coating-technique.md#interior-finish-bare-wood-epoxy-varnish-cetol-or-paint).[121]

For NOLI's own cabin sole, "Jeff UK" bought a Rolson Cushion Floor pack from Maplin — six 600 x 600 x 10 mm sheets of hard-wearing, water- and oil-resistant, no-slip foam meant for garage and workshop floors — for just under £10, more than enough for a Paradox or NOLI alike.[122] For NOLI's cabin-side lining, camping mats from Screwfix cost under £3 each, with a foil-backed version at about the same price fitted under the deck with the foil showing.[123] Rounding out NOLI's small gear: a cheap 3-LED press light for about £1, a Maplin AA-battery solar charger at £6.99 (taking about six hours to charge four flat 2,600 mAh AA cells), and rechargeable batteries from an online retailer at about £3.50 for four 2,500 mAh AA cells — alongside an open search for a solar garden light to fix "fit and forget" atop the mast.[123] The mast-light search drew two further suggestions: an LED light meant for a garden-umbrella pole, donut-shaped with LEDs around its underside, cheap and reasonably waterproof (readily sealed further with silicone), and able to take a solar charger's input; and a complete solar shed light, wired with enough cable to mount the light itself partway up the mast with its own solar charger nearer the deck.[124] A third suggestion came from a fellow UK builder shopping for his own boat's small gear at the same time: a set of four stainless-steel solar garden lights from Maplin, about £10 for the set, with the idea of keeping one for the masthead and reselling the rest on eBay. See [JoHanna — camping mats, battery-powered lighting, and a masthead solar light suggested](/boats/johanna.md#camping-mats-battery-powered-lighting-and-a-masthead-solar-light-suggested) for the fuller exchange this answer came from.[125]

## NOLI's glassing schedule, and a lighter-hull counter-argument

NOLI carries three layers of fiberglass cloth on the bottom just past bulkhead 3, tapering to two layers back to the transom — a schedule settled after a conversation establishing that damage on a comparable hull always happened forward of Little Cruiser's lowest point, a pattern other builders are expected to bear out too.[117] Set against Paradox's much heavier fixed bottom, NOLI's own 3/8 in bottom leaves her displacing just over 500 lb less for a comparable size — on one view, that lighter displacement could make her less, not more, susceptible to hull damage, though the point remains untested and was offered half in jest.[118] See [hull scantlings — the case for over-construction](/design/hull-scantlings.md#the-case-for-over-construction-field-evidence-hazards-and-a-counter-argument) for the case for Paradox's heavier alternative.

## NOLI's foredeck built in two halves, avoiding the tight-curve problem

Rather than fighting the stock foredeck panel's tight curve near the stem, "Jeff UK" built NOLI's foredeck from lighter, thinner 1/4 in marine ply, laid up in two halves to save material. The bulkheads were cut oversized before installation, then a **kingplank** was fitted to give a straight centerline through to the stem; a fair 1/4 in square batten, clamped to each bulkhead and resting on the kingplank and both sheer clamps, gave a line to cut and plane to, with the sheer clamps themselves pre-beveled to the deck edge's approximate angle. A layer of glass cloth plus the cockpit carlin (running the full length from transom to stem) made the resulting deck strong despite its lighter build; a few places where the deck did not quite meet a bulkhead were left as-is, since a fillet was going there regardless.[127]
