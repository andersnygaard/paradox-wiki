# Ian Lang

**Ian Lang** (Yahoo handle `royalscot`/`ian944443`, e-mail `royalscot@…`, u=236193356) is a UK builder in **North London, England**. He introduced himself to the list in June 2007 with plans already in hand, spent several weeks gathering plywood and timber quotes from UK suppliers, and started construction in late June 2007 on a boat he named **[Irene May](/boats/irene-may.md)**, after his late mother.

## Plywood and timber sourcing

Welcomed to the list by [Bill Serjeant](/people/bill-serjeant.md) as the nearest established UK builder, Ian gathered three separate UK marine-plywood quotes — Goldbergs of Uxbridge and Enfield Timber alongside his own further research — before clearing over 20 years of accumulated contents out of his garage into an 8 cubic-metre skip and ordering plywood and Douglas Fir from Robbins: the most expensive of his quotes, but judged the most dependable quality. Full sourcing detail and citations are at [plywood grade and foam flotation debate — a second North London builder's plywood and timber sourcing](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#a-2007-uk-shopping-list-recurrence-far-eastern-ply-and-epoxy-quantity) and [boats/faith.md](/boats/faith.md#a-second-north-london-builders-plywood-and-timber-sourcing).

## Building "Irene May"

Ian started construction on his Paradox, **"Irene May,"** named after his late mother, in late June 2007, following the standard advice to fit out the boat's smaller parts before finishing the hull. Full build narrative — including a shrinking-garage milestone echoed across the UK builder community and a bulkhead-2 measuring correction — lives on [boats/irene-may.md](/boats/irene-may.md).

## A skin reaction to West System epoxy

Laminating "Irene May"'s curved deck beams with **West System** epoxy, Ian developed a painful, itchy skin rash across his arms, hands, and face, despite taking the recommended precautions of gloves and protective clothing and working in a shed with the door wide open — he had not yet done any epoxy sanding, ruling that out as the cause. The rash eventually narrowed down to a likely latex-glove allergy, prompted by re-reading the glove box's own warning label, leading to a switch to vinyl gloves to test the theory. See [epoxy brands, chemistry, and cure behavior — responses to a skin sensitivity case](/construction/epoxy-brands-and-chemistry.md#responses-to-a-skin-sensitivity-case-alternative-brands-resin-types-and-glovemask-technique) for the fuller record, including the alternative-epoxy, barrier-cream, and Gorilla Glue suggestions it drew.[4][5]
