# Bill Serjeant

**Bill Serjeant** (also William Serjeant; Yahoo profiles `barnacleid`,
later `barnacletoo`, then `williamserjeant`; e-mail `w.serjeant@`/`william@`)
is a UK small-boat sailor and the builder of the community's principal UK
web presence, who spent nearly two years as the group's de facto UK
registrar and facilitator before starting his own Paradox from scratch.
Based in England, he already owned and sailed a boat named **"Bumper"**
throughout the Paradox-UK years, and kept a separate build/cruise blog at
`bills-log.blogspot.com`.

## The Small Sailboats / Paradox-UK web presence

After receiving his plans from Dave Bolduc in October 2003, Bill added a
Paradox section to his existing site, **`www.smallsailboats.co.uk`**, then
split it out into a dedicated **`www.paradox-uk.co.uk`**, giving the
design's UK builder community a home-country counterpart to the Bolducs'
microcruising.com. He also set up a companion **`smallsailboats`** Yahoo
Group and pointed builders to the third-party **irbs.com** Small Sailboats
Usergroup. Full detail, including the site's temporary 2004 outage, its
sailmaker recommendations, and its role relaying Dave Bolduc's contact
address, lives at
[references/paradox-uk-website.md](/references/paradox-uk-website.md).

Bill compiled and maintained the group's closest thing to a **build
registry** — a running list of completed and in-progress Paradoxes at
`www.smallsailboats.co.uk/paradox/parindex.htm` and `names.htm` — explicitly
dependent on builders reporting their own boats in, and periodically
reporting updated counts (at least 14 boats on or under construction by
late 2004). He also compiled the group's original **"Tools" list**, drawn
from what Don Elliott had used building his own Paradox — his own
compilation, not an endorsement from Don. See
[construction/build-effort.md](/construction/build-effort.md#tools) for
the list itself. He repeated the registry's open invitation once more in
January 2006, encouraging any builder not already listed on the site to get
in touch, "to have as comprehensive a list as possible" — replying, in
passing, to the two-person-capacity question answered in full at
[operations/first-boat-suitability.md](/operations/first-boat-suitability.md#the-two-person-question-answered-directly).[32]
He renewed the appeal again the following month, this time specifically
asking for photos of finished boats rather than further building-stage
pictures — drawing in a new Los Angeles builder, Mark Waters, and a
follow-up proposal from Derek Clark to add e-mail addresses to the
registry. Serjeant added the e-mail column himself, posted the registry's
direct link to the whole list, and flagged that Waters' own e-mail address
on file appeared to be incorrect; a new Phoenix, Arizona prospective builder
who inquired about using the registry to find nearby owners was told the
usual rule — his details would be added once he actually started
building.[38][39] See [references/paradox-uk-website.md — a 2006 database
renewal, and a new Los Angeles
builder](/references/paradox-uk-website.md#a-2006-database-renewal-and-a-new-los-angeles-builder).

## A kit Paradox that fell through, then a scratch build begins

In January 2005, Alec Jordan of Jordan Boats agreed to supply Bill his
first "kit" Paradox, working with Matt Layden on the project — but by July
2005 Jordan reported the kit would not be ready after all, the panel
design/cutting process needing more research than could be resolved soon.
Jordan supplied Bill with the plywood that would have gone into the kit
regardless, and Bill chose to start building from Matt Layden's original
plans instead, crediting his decision partly to trying out Alastair's
["Little Jim"](/boats/little-jim.md) firsthand at Plymouth. Jack Gardiner
cheered him on: "Have at it Bill!!!! Good luck on your adventure!!!" Full
account at [references/paradox-uk-website.md — a UK kit-built option:
Jordan Boats](/references/paradox-uk-website.md#a-uk-kit-built-option-jordan-boats).

Bill began cutting and laminating deck-beam strips within days, practicing
the lamination technique before committing to the actual beams and taking
care to leave each beam clamped a full 24 hours rather than rush the cure.
Working through his first beams, he asked whether the 4 ft maximum strip
length he'd read was too short given the deck beam's curvature; Derek
Clark confirmed all beam strips are cut a uniform 4 ft across a sheet of
plywood and trimmed to length once fitted, since the boat's widest point
(bulkhead #3) carries no deck beam at all. Full technique record at
[construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md](/construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md#why-4-ft-strips-are-long-enough-for-every-beam).

## Boom and yard curvature, and a persisting sag question

Shaping and sanding Faith's boom, Bill noticed the plans draw one side of
both the boom and yard straight and the other curved, without stating which
side should face up, and separately raised a longer-running concern about
the yard sagging under the sail's own weight (worsened to windward by sheet
load on the boom's end) — including whether Glen Maxwell's suggested fix of
recutting a dart-shaped strip from the sail itself was the best solution.
Alastair answered that the boom has no fixed top or bottom in practice, and
recommended working on the yard's own curvature rather than having the sail
recut, since a yard is easy to swap back if an experiment fails. Glen
Maxwell separately confirmed why the boom is curved at all: furling it for
reefing removes the sail's draft so it sets correctly reefed as well as
full — reassurance Bill took as a cue to press on, reporting the stempost
made and the frames and cleats next on his own build. Full technical
discussion at [design/rig.md — which side of the boom and yard faces up,
and curing persistent yard
sag](/design/rig.md#which-side-of-the-boom-and-yard-faces-up-and-curing-persistent-yard-sag).[43][44][48][49]

On the recurring plywood-grade and durability debate, Bill gave his own
specification for a properly finished Paradox: heavy fiberglass sheathing
regardless of ply grade beneath it (per the plans' sheet #5, 4 mm of
fiberglass with the sides and deck each getting one layer), sealed with
three coats of epoxy and a compatible top-coat paint — a combination his own
marine-plywood boats, including his epoxy-and-marine-paint-finished "50/50"
canoe, have shown gives a 20-year-plus life expectancy. See [plywood grade
and foam flotation debate — long-term plywood durability, resale value, and
why to expect
neither](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#long-term-plywood-durability-resale-value-and-why-to-expect-neither).[45]

Bill mixes UK Epoxies' resin at a 2:1 ratio using dispensing pumps he rates
excellent for guessing minute quantities accurately — his own Paradox being
the third boat built using the same pair — and, when a fellow builder
warned that hardener crystals had once fouled his own pumps, replied that
periodically clearing the nozzles with a bit of wire is all the maintenance
his have ever needed. See [plywood grade and foam flotation debate —
long-term plywood durability, resale value, and why to expect
neither](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#long-term-plywood-durability-resale-value-and-why-to-expect-neither).[46][47]

Turning to window glazing, Bill asked where to source Lexan in the UK and
whether Perspex is the same material — prompting sourcing tips and a
polycarbonate-versus-acrylic technical comparison from Larry, Glen Maxwell,
Alastair, and Paul Reppeto. Bill reported that the UK price of a 6 mm Lexan
sheet had actually fallen between 2003 and 2006, "not often prices get
cheaper." Full technical and sourcing detail at [window glazing — Lexan
versus Perspex/acrylic](/construction/window-glazing-materials.md).[50][51][52][53]

Working on his stern baffle, Bill raised a spar-and-sail road-stowage
question — how practical it is to pass the mast, boom, yard, and sail
through the transom hatch for trailering, and whether the hatch is worth
fitting at all — answered in full by Alastair. See [transom deck plate —
stowing the mast, boom, yard, and sail through the transom hatch for road
travel](/construction/transom-deck-plate.md#stowing-the-mast-boom-yard-and-sail-through-the-transom-hatch-for-road-travel).[54]
Confirmed his own `smallsailboats` site and photo album as his, thanking
Derek Clark for the "Enuf" photo update. See [boats/enuf.md — a
carpal-tunnel operation halts progress, and a five-week
pause](/boats/enuf.md#a-carpal-tunnel-operation-halts-progress-and-a-five-week-pause).[55]

## Build-cost comparison, and a hull-assembly milestone

Replying to a newcomer's "it's only 14 feet" quip about the scale of the
commitment, Bill offered his own comparative data point: having also built a
25 ft boat, he judged there was markedly less work in that bigger boat than
in the Paradox he had underway, at only marginally lower cost — having
started in August 2005, he did not expect to finish before summer 2007, and
judged even a full-time professional with power tools could not complete a
Paradox in under six months. See [operations/secondhand-market.md — build
vs. buy secondhand](/operations/secondhand-market.md#build-vs-buy-secondhand-a-uk-newcomers-cost-benefit-hesitation)
for the fuller build-versus-buy discussion this fed into.[56]

A week later, with fine weather arrived, Bill reported good progress and
pointed to fresh photographs of the latest parts at his gallery
(`www.smallsailboats.co.uk/paradox/con_gallery.htm`), expecting to begin
assembling the hull within days, with practically every other part already
built.[57] David Beard and Glen Maxwell each congratulated him on the
progress;[58][59] Bill thanked Beard, promised further updates, and
planned to attempt a dry fit on the first good day.[60] He separately
thanked Glen for the encouragement, admitting he was finding the build a
challenge but was excited to see the boat gradually taking shape.[61]

When Amsterdam builder Ronald de Hon announced he could not finish his own
Paradox, "RHXII," and offered her for sale, Bill offered to add de Hon's
e-mail address to the UK registry to help find a buyer — see
[operations/secondhand-market.md — an abandoned build for sale:
"RHXII"](/operations/secondhand-market.md#an-abandoned-build-for-sale-rhxii-april-2006).[62]
He also followed up with de Hon off-list, praised the "RHX11" photos already
posted to the group, and called it a sadness de Hon could not finish
her.[63]

## A dry assembly of the hull, named "Faith"

Days later, Bill posted photographs of Faith's first **dry assembly** — the
hull's panels and bulkheads fitted together unglued, to check the fit before
committing epoxy.[64] Alastair and Derek Clark both praised how well the
stem joint had come together; Derek admitted that if his own stem had fitted
as cleanly he would have left the foredeck off so everyone could see it.[65][66]
Bill explained the fit had cost him most of the wood he started with: he had
begun with three large, expensive blocks, and by the time the stem was fully
shaped there was very little of them left.[67][68] Jim Hall, who had been
following the build, remarked on the pace of the work — from only the small
parts shown in Bill's first photo album, before the cold weather set in, to
very nearly a complete hull within a few short weeks of the cold snap
breaking.[69]

## Gluing the frames and floors, and the vent trunk

Within days of the dry assembly, Bill glued the floors and the vent trunk
permanently in place, and cut in the frames — reporting he had begun the
frames as early as late February despite the outward appearance (from his
first photo album) of having only made small parts before the cold
weather set in; being retired, he could put in about four days a week on
the build.[71] He posted a photo of the completed vent trunk and, the
next day, planned to apply epoxy to permanently join the frames and side
panels.[72][73]

## Faith's rudder: a sheet-fairlead fix, and boom-tang strength

Back from Australia, Bill posted fresh photographs of Faith's completed
rudder to his `smallsailboats.co.uk` gallery.[100] The photos drew two
independent technical replies from builders who had already sailed the stock
design. Alastair and Derek Clark had both separately found that the rudder,
built to the plans' specified height, lets the mainsheet drop into the gap
between the rudder stock and the transom on a broad reach or run — starboard
tack in particular — forcing the sheet to slide up over the pintle/gudgeon on
every sail adjustment. Alastair's fix was a small wooden insert filling that
gap to act as a fairlead: not essential to sailing the boat, but a real
improvement to how easily the sheet runs. This is a second, independently
reported instance of the same rudder-height/mainsheet-fouling problem tracked
in full at [design/rudder.md — rudder-stock height and mainsheet fouling at
the transom notch](/design/rudder.md#rudder-stock-height-and-mainsheet-fouling-at-the-transom-notch).[101]

David Beard separately flagged a hardware-strength problem visible in the
same photos: the stock mainsheet/topping-lift tang, as fitted, is not strong
enough and will bend under load — on his own "Whisper," he had to fit three
tangs bolted together in place of the single stock part to stop the bending,
and understood [Glen Maxwell](/people/glen-maxwell.md) had met the same
problem on "Zoë," though without recording how Glen resolved it.[102]

Bill fitted Alastair's wooden fairlead insert the same day, reporting his
own steering line worked well once it was in.[103] On the tang question, he
had deliberately held off strengthening it, intending to load-test the stock
part first before modifying anything — but conceded the point once David
Beard weighed in, and resolved to reinforce it before Faith's planned launch
the following summer.[104] Glen Maxwell confirmed the same tang had bent on
his own boat, solved with a heavier West Marine part; he separately
described a second, unrelated rudder problem of his own — the raised blade
banging its trailing edge against the transom — fixed with a small
stainless bracket and a length of rubber hose acting as a stop, recorded in
full at [design/rudder.md](/design/rudder.md#rudder-stock-height-and-mainsheet-fouling-at-the-transom-notch).[105]

## Why he wanted a kit, and a possible plywood-bottom kit method

Asked by a grateful correspondent about the kit Paradox that fell through
(see [a kit Paradox that fell through, then a scratch build
begins](#a-kit-paradox-that-fell-through-then-a-scratch-build-begins) above),
Bill explained his reason for wanting one in the first place was purely
**speed of construction** — not a full kit, just the main components. He
still believed a kit could be produced, but judged the construction method
would need rethinking first: the bottom, for instance, could be built up
from three layers of thin plywood, with the first layer stitched to the
sides and the remaining layers applied over it, rather than the plans' own
single-thickness bottom.[95]

## Lower pintle: an alternative to drilling the rudder stock

Bill devised and photographed a method of installing the rudder's lower
pintle onto the rudder stock without drilling a hole for it — a technique he
judged simpler and more accurate than drilling, which he shared as a

## The "quintessential seat" proposal

Bill also raised the case for a purpose-built adjustable helm seat, citing
Hugh Horton's adjustable seat in his cruising paddling-and-sailing canoe as a
model — variable height and horizontal position within the cockpit, plus an
adjustable backrest, that Horton reports lets him sit for hours without
numbness. Bill's own specification called for a seat that rotates and locks
in any position, adjusts for height and inclination, can be fixed at
variable athwartships positions, supports the back well, stows clear of the
cabin for sleeping, and still allows ready access to the lazarette — floating
a bicycle saddle as a lighter-hearted alternative to Uffa Fox's horse's
saddle, once fitted as a helmsman's seat on one of his own boats. See [crew
size and fit — long-passage seat comfort: cushion
practice](/operations/crew-size-and-fit.md#long-passage-seat-comfort-cushion-practice).[22]

## A caution against departing from the stock design, and the Wharram Hina's knockdown

Responding to Derek Clark's slot-top cabin idea for "[Enuf](/boats/enuf.md)",
Bill cautioned against deviating significantly from Matt Layden's design even
for waters judged sheltered, drawing on his own experience: his 22 ft Wharram
Hina catamaran was once knocked down by a sudden, freak gust on an otherwise
calm afternoon. Full account, including his self-righting/water-ingress
reasoning and two comparable knockdown-sinkings he recalled, at
[boats/enuf.md](/boats/enuf.md#a-slot-top-cabin-idea-for-open-water-use).[33]

Asked for more detail on the Hina story — her sail area, whether she was built
to Wharram's stock plan, and whether she was salvaged or righted — Bill did
not retell the incident itself, instead pointing to two pages on his own
website covering "Twinstar" (his name for the Hina) where the capsize is
mentioned.[34]

## Ballast-access and tank-top questions

Once Faith's rudder was sorted, Bill raised two further late-build
questions: whether other builders had found the inner ballast bolts hard to
reach once the overlapping floors were in place, and, separately, how best
to fix down the tops of the water ballast tanks. Alastair answered the
tank-top question first, with a thickened-glue-and-hand-fillet technique
applied through the access hatches that has kept the tank tops themselves
leak-free; see [water tank construction and lining — fixing the tank tops in
place](/construction/water-tank-lining.md#fixing-the-tank-tops-in-place) for
the full technique.[106][107][108]

On ballast access, Alastair answered with his own tapped-lead-pig-and-plate
method; Bill then filled in the fuller picture of his own arrangement — top
wooden retaining pieces bolted into a floor block and the chine log, which
let the pigs shift fore and aft but hid the real difficulty at two bolts
under the ballast-tank top's flap — and proposed a removable plank in the
flap's place. Alastair's counter-suggestion of small access holes above the
bolt heads was in turn rejected, since fixed side pieces at that scale would
still be awkward to reach through and would also block off the jubilee-clip
joint between the ballast tank's copper and PVC plumbing. Bill settled on
building the water-ballast tank top's sides as separate, removable "planks"
instead — giving full, ongoing access to both the lead ballast and that
plumbing joint. See [weight, ballast, and displacement — reaching the
ballast fastenings after the floors are
in](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#reaching-the-ballast-fastenings-after-the-floors-are-in)
for the full exchange.[109][110][111]

## A removable-floorboard technique for full storage and ballast access

Bill also described his own broader solution to floor and locker access,
prompted by a "Haliyard and Reef lines" thread that had turned to Stephen
Yahn's plan to split his floor hatches ahead of the seat. Bill built his own
aft floorboards to the plans, but without hinges: instead, he supported them
on removable under-flaps taken from the sides of the storage bins, so that
either side of the floor can simply be lifted and slid forward, opening the
whole of the storage compartments beneath — a plan he judged with an eye to
posting photographs of it. He also fitted side flaps/planks either side of
the water tanks, giving good access to the forward ballast — the same
"planks" solution he had just settled on above. See
[design/interior-bins-and-floor.md — a removable-floorboard technique for
full storage and ballast
access](/design/interior-bins-and-floor.md#a-removable-floorboard-technique-for-full-storage-and-ballast-access)
for the cross-linked technique.[112]
