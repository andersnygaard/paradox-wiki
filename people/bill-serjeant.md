# Bill Serjeant

**Bill Serjeant** (also William Serjeant; Yahoo profiles `barnacleid`, later `barnacletoo`, then `williamserjeant`; e-mail `w.serjeant@`/`william@`) is a UK small-boat sailor and the builder of the community's principal UK web presence, who spent nearly two years as the group's de facto UK registrar and facilitator before starting his own Paradox from scratch. Based in **Essex, England** (consistent with cruising "Bumper" from the River Crouch, in the same county), he already owned and sailed that boat throughout the Paradox-UK years, and kept a separate build/cruise blog at `bills-log.blogspot.com`. Bill's Paradox, "Faith," and its build are documented at [Faith](/boats/faith.md).

## The Small Sailboats / Paradox-UK web presence

After receiving plans in October 2003, Bill added a Paradox section to his existing site, **`www.smallsailboats.co.uk`**, then split it out into a dedicated **`www.paradox-uk.co.uk`**, giving the design's UK builder community a home-country counterpart to microcruising.com. He also set up a companion **`smallsailboats`** Yahoo Group and pointed builders to the third-party **irbs.com** Small Sailboats Usergroup. Full detail, including the site's temporary 2004 outage, its sailmaker recommendations, and its role relaying the designer's contact address, lives at [references/paradox-uk-website.md](/references/paradox-uk-website.md).[1]

In October 2006 Bill added a "Why Build Paradox" page to the same site, answering a newcomer's question about the design's appeal; see [boats/little-jim.md](/boats/little-jim.md#a-scilly-isles-rendezvous-and-a-why-build-paradox-web-page), including his proposal to cruise the Scilly Isles jointly with Alastair once "Faith" was launched.[2]

Bill compiled and maintained the group's closest thing to a build registry — a running list of completed and in-progress Paradoxes at `www.smallsailboats.co.uk/paradox/parindex.htm` and `names.htm`, always dependent on builders reporting their own boats in. He also compiled the group's original "Tools" list, drawn from the tools used building an earlier Paradox; see [construction/build-effort.md](/construction/build-effort.md#tools).[3] Over the following years he renewed the registry's open invitation repeatedly, added an e-mail-address column, and folded in each new builder as they reported in — among them Mark Waters and a Los Angeles builder in 2006,[4][5][6][7][8] "JoHanna" in May 2007, by which point the great majority of Paradoxes on record were still US-built,[9] Jimmy Lu of Taiwan in July 2007,[10] and Ian Lang, whom Bill welcomed and later congratulated on starting his own Paradox, "Irene May."[11][12] See [references/paradox-uk-website.md](/references/paradox-uk-website.md#a-2006-database-renewal-and-a-new-los-angeles-builder), [boats/johanna.md](/boats/johanna.md#added-to-the-uk-ownersbuilders-registry), and [boats/irene-may.md](/boats/irene-may.md#a-shrinking-garage-and-encouragement-from-other-uk-and-us-builders). At the end of that year he also congratulated two nearly-finished UK and Taiwan builders on their progress, calling their boats new members of the Paradox family.[13]

Bill was still actively fielding "who's building" check-ins and registry updates as late as September 2008, more than two years into his own Paradox build: pointing a returning correspondent to the registry with the same long-standing caveat that it remains "far from comprehensive," and separately confirming a Puget Sound builder as the registry's most recently added entry while asking to be told once that boat launched. See [references/paradox-uk-website.md — a UK and worldwide build count](/references/paradox-uk-website.md#a-uk-and-worldwide-build-count) and [boats/ironsides.md](/boats/ironsides.md#nearing-completion-a-september-2008-launch-expected-and-registry-congratulations) for the full exchange.[115][116]

## A June 2006 site outage

The `smallsailboats.co.uk` domain went down again in June 2006, showing visitors only a domain registrar's holding page; Bill worked to sort out the registration, asked for patience, and had the site re-uploaded and running again within a day. Full account, alongside the 2004 outage, at [references/paradox-uk-website.md](/references/paradox-uk-website.md#a-second-outage-june-2006-and-an-apparent-domain-registrar-issue).[14][15][16][17][18]

## Building "Faith"

Having already bought the wood and fittings, Bill ordered the bulk of his build timber from Robbins Timber of Bristol just as a boom-knot dispute with that same supplier played out on the list; he said he would check his own delivery for knots while its guarantee held.[19] By August 2005 he had cut and fitted his deck, cabin, and hatch beams, laminated his tiller, and begun drawing the bulkheads out on plywood; his timber then arrived from Robbins in an unexpectedly large delivery he could not fully check before signing, so he noted the exception on the paperwork instead.[20][21][22] Gluing the mast's forward piece into place, he worked out with the group's help how to route the masthead light's wiring past the point where the mast is stepped and unstepped for trailering, settling on running the wire outside the mast below the sheave with a plug/socket arrangement below deck.[23][24][25] Around the same time, hunting for a name among roughly eighty candidates, he settled by March 2006 on "Faith."[26][27]

Passing through Yeovil in December 2005, Bill called on Alastair, was shown "Little Jim," and came away with renewed enthusiasm for finishing his own boat.[28][29] Answering a recurring tools question the following year, he reported his build had needed neither a table saw nor a bandsaw — a rip saw, hand planing, and an electric jigsaw did the job, with the clamp the one truly indispensable tool.[30]

By April 2007 he had fitted Faith's cabin trunk roof and built the hatch, drawing on tips from Alastair, and cut the polycarbonate windows once it trial-fitted successfully.[31][32][33] The front window went on and the decks were painted in early May, with wet weather delaying the next stage — tipping the boat on her side to fair the topsides and apply the antifouling.[34] With only the hull and bottom paint left before launch, Bill marked the approaching milestone in mid-May with a thank-you to the community for two years of building help, crediting Alastair's loan of "Little Jim" for a sail as the reason he had started building at all, and naming Matt Layden as the design's originator.[35][36] Faith left the garage for the front lawn in early June, essentially complete, while Bill waited on warm weather to cure the antifouling before launch.[37][38] She was launched in mid-June 2007, nearly two years after construction began; Bill marked the occasion with a new cruise/build blog at `bills-log.blogspot.com` and updated `smallsailboats.co.uk` with photographs from the launch.[39][40]

## A caution on saw-grooving the foredeck panel

Recalling his own build, Bill noted the forward foredeck panel can be bent using leverage from the "wings" left standing when shaping either side, though this can still open a small crack up the centerline, as happened on Faith's own deck; a saw-groove cut deliberately in the same area leaves a visible ridge once the panel takes its shape, since the pieces on either side straighten themselves out — harmless once glassed over, but less pleasing than the design's intended continuous curve. See [construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md](/construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md#fitting-the-forward-deck-skin-to-a-tight-curve).[41]

## Cruising Faith

Bill kept a running cruise blog, `bills-log.blogspot.com`, to keep family and friends updated during passages; a mobile data card and a laptop were enough to update it daily underway, with mobile coverage reliable within about 12 miles of the UK coast.[42] He firmed up a West Country cruise for early summer 2007 — Exmouth to Dartmouth, on to Plymouth and the Tamar, then Falmouth and, weather permitting, the Isles of Scilly.[43] A cruise account was later published in the Dinghy Cruising Association's bulletin; Bill judged it a very good account with fine colour photographs, and hoped for another chance at the Scilly Isles crossing the following year.[44] Underway, he deliberately beached Faith stern-first to angle the cabin sole so his head lay higher than his feet for a comfortable night's sleep — a technique that risks wear on the transom's corners and, on a pebbly beach, the rudder's base, learned the hard way; the better practice is finding a gently shelving beach and getting the hull as close to horizontal as circumstances allow. See [components/rudder.md](/components/rudder.md#beaching-stern-first-a-sleeping-comfort-technique-and-why-it-doesnt-damage-the-rudder).[45]

## A planned West Country cruise: the Transat and the Jester Challenge (May 2008)

Bill and Alastair settled on cruising plans for the following year: most of May 2008 spent in the West Country together, calling at Plymouth for the start of the Transat on 11 May and the Jester Challenge on 31 May, hoping to reach the Isles of Scilly somewhere between the two dates.[46]

## A man-overboard account, and a published appraisal of Faith

Falling overboard from Faith's foredeck during a 2007 Dinghy Cruising Association week at Cobnor, Bill reboarded via the rudder's built-in boarding step without a buoyancy aid; the primary, contemporaneous account is at [boats/faith.md](/boats/faith.md#the-cobnor-week-a-dinghy-cruising-association-meeting-and-falling-overboard).[47][48][49][50] The experience settled his later preference for a snug-fitting buoyancy aid over a lifejacket when working a boat: harder to right an unconscious wearer, but easier to swim in and, he judged, better odds of staying mobile enough to get back aboard.[50] He also linked an independent published appraisal of Faith from the smallsailboats.co.uk editorial page, for anyone considering building or buying a Paradox; full text at [boats/faith.md](/boats/faith.md#a-published-appraisal-of-faith).[51]

## A Force 8 gale crewed firsthand, and a bosun's chest for an ocean passage

Crewing a 40 ft yacht through a real force 8 gale roughly 130 miles offshore in the Bay of Biscay and the English Channel, Bill judged Faith would have given a rough but no worse ride, and a considerably drier and warmer one; the same voyage's sprung leak (stopped only by tacking plywood with mastic over the worst area) and a failed, underway-repaired tiller fitting became a general case for always putting to sea with a proper tool box and bosun's chest. Full worked comparison: [heavy-weather tactics — a Force 8 in the Bay of Biscay](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#a-force-8-in-the-bay-of-biscay-a-worked-comparison-and-a-tool-box-lesson).[112] Answering a follow-up on the same account, Bill judged nothing about Faith needed changing for genuine offshore use, credited Matt Layden with proving the boat could cope, and set out a bare-minimum ocean-passage kit — a spare tack-arrangement spacer, a second halyard on a separate external block, and a bosun's chest of epoxy, spare wood, fasteners, mastic, canvas, thread, and cordage — reasoning from the Fastnet Race disaster that a sound boat given sea room will usually outlast her crew. Full account, including his surprise at a reported tiller failure and his standing confidence in the rudder's single-bolt-through-stock fixing: [heavy-weather tactics — a Force 8 in the Bay of Biscay](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#a-force-8-in-the-bay-of-biscay-a-worked-comparison-and-a-tool-box-lesson).[113] Asked to clarify the spacer and the second halyard's purpose, Bill pointed to Drawing 9, described his own split "Y" spacer (wood-capped and epoxy-sheathed, proven over two seasons), and explained the second halyard as a spare against the original through-mast halyard failing. Full account: [heavy-weather tactics — clarifying the offshore spares list](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#clarifying-the-offshore-spares-list-the-tack-arrangement-spacer-and-the-furling-axles-field-record).[114]

## A later verdict on Faith

Bill sold Faith in 2009. In later retrospective blog posts he judged her perhaps the best boat he had owned — a trifle small, but very well thought out for ease of working — and, years afterward, still the only boat he ever regretted selling.[53][54] Her subsequent ownership and the modifications that followed are recorded at [boats/faith.md](/boats/faith.md#sold-in-2009-the-forepeak-reworked-a-higher-peaked-sail-and-the-yuloh-reshaped).

## Contributions to design discussion

Bill contributed steadily to design threads beyond his own build. He raised the rudder blade's pivot-bolt fixing and, over two years later, asked how to stop water ingress at that same fixing — answered with an epoxy-fill-and-rebore technique that he judged a very good solution and used on his own rudder.[55][56][57] He also raised a twin-rudder proposal for freeing up the transom centerline for an outboard, judged not worth the added complexity, and asked the question that produced the group's fullest auto-pilot-versus-wind-vane comparison, including the specific problem a folding rudder poses for a trim-tab wind vane.[58] He measured Paradox against the Dix-designed Mini Transat 650 racer and judged the two to have essentially nothing in common as sailing craft.[59]

Self-steering was a recurring interest of his over several years.[60] He asked about a fellow builder's new windvane gear trialed at the 2007 Cedar Key gathering and judged the solution ingenious, having spent years imagining Paradox would hold a course comparable to Joshua Slocum's "Spray."[61][62][63] On a separate thread he judged a proposed pendulum-weight rudderhead idea could matter to windward, though locking the steering line — as many sailors do — would negate it entirely, and recalled his own 1974 Hasler SP90 windvane, which worked well downwind apart from slight meandering.[64][65] He asked whether a gear built for a shorter-boom sailplan would suit a boat rigged to the original sailplan instead;[66] weighed a bow-mounted vane-driven auxiliary rudder against Robin Knox-Johnston's twin bow vanes on "Suhaili" as a complicating precedent;[67] recalled a QME vane once fitted to his own Hunter 19 — cheap and popular, but reliable only on an exceptionally well-balanced boat, and, on Paradox, liable to be snagged by the sail and sheet unless mounted well astern of it;[68] and eventually judged himself likelier to fit a simple electronic autopilot than a windvane, since he did not plan long ocean passages.[69][70] See [components/rudder.md](/components/rudder.md#the-pivot-bolt-fixing-and-its-record-under-grounding-loads) and [operations/self-steering.md](/operations/self-steering.md#auto-pilot-versus-wind-vane-self-steering-a-practical-comparison) for the fuller record.

Bill gave the fullest statement on record of why the stock design suits a sailor built like Matt Layden particularly well, and why a considerably taller, heavier, or less agile sailor is better served choosing a different design than modifying this one.[71] Replying at length to a fellow builder's defense of modification, he worked through the argument point by point: changing a boat's rig is a real change to the original design even when it produces a better arrangement for a particular owner; his view that Paradox does not suit a large sailor is a personal judgment, not a claim that larger sailors owe the design any deference; and, agreeing that boats — like cars and houses — are commonly personalized by their owners, held that doing so does not by itself show any lack of appreciation for the original design.[72] He agreed elsewhere that a sufficiently altered boat is no longer a Paradox but a Paradox derivative.[73] His own Wharram Hina catamaran was once knocked down by a sudden, freak gust on an otherwise calm afternoon, an experience he cited when cautioning against departing significantly from Matt Layden's stock design even for waters judged sheltered.[74][75] See [paradox.md](/paradox.md#citations), [design/variants/alternatives-for-a-larger-sailor.md](/similar-boat-designs/alternatives-for-a-larger-sailor.md#a-recommended-alternative-for-a-larger-sailor-michalaks-caroline-and-the-case-against-modifying-paradox-to-fit), and [boats/enuf.md](/boats/enuf.md#a-slot-top-cabin-idea-for-open-water-use).

Answering a fellow UK builder's question of whether Enigma is an equal boat to Paradox as a coastal cruiser, Bill gave the fullest comparison of the two designs on record: Paradox is exceptionally strong and heavy-displacement, Enigma light and simple; Paradox the more able, more comfortable coastal cruiser, Enigma the faster, quicker-built lightweight day-sailer. He revisited the comparison again the following year.[76][77][73]

Building his own yuloh, Bill raised the plans' ambiguous curvature figure, the "For'd" nomenclature on sheet 12, blade-camber orientation, and the pivot-socket dimensions — a thread that drew the fullest technical treatment of yuloh camber and gearing on record, including a relayed explanation of Worcester's classic yuloh drawing.[78][79][80] A follow-up thread on the pivot socket's own material drew a shortlist of candidates from across the list — lignum vitae, a stainless ball in a copper cup, Acetal/UHMW plastic, and a teflon-coated ball.[81] See [components/yuloh.md](/components/yuloh.md#blade-camber-the-ford-nomenclature-and-pivot-socket-dimensions) and [components/yuloh.md — ball-joint versus oarlock](/components/yuloh.md#ball-joint-versus-oarlock-and-pivot-socket-wear-over-time).

Bill also raised the case for a purpose-built adjustable helm seat — rotating and locking in any position, adjustable for height, inclination, and athwartships position, supporting the back well, and stowing clear of the cabin for sleeping — citing an adjustable seat on a cruising canoe as a model.[82] He asked why the chine runners on a designer's 40 ft sharpie "Jubilee" looked so narrow for a hull that size, wondering whether a scaled-up Paradox hull needs proportionally bigger runners.[83][84] He reaffirmed the plans' own ballast placement — keeping lead near the boat's longitudinal centre for a sea-kindly motion — noting Faith's ballast, fitted per Matt Layden's plan including the centred water ballast, had needed no adjustment.[85] He weighed in on gudgeon-casting materials for the rudder's lower bearing, and gave the fullest technique on record for attaching the vent trunk's top to bulkhead 2 and fitting its drain pipe.[86][87][88][89][90][91] Describing his own bin-and-floor technique — aluminium-angle-mounted, lift-out panels in place of the plans' hinges — he judged that, building again, he would skip built-in water tanks altogether in favor of loose, clasped floors, and, after a season's sailing, came round to endorsing a drop-in rear-cabin-trunk washboard provided its sides are cut vertical; asked for reference figures in the same thread, he gave his own — 5'8" and 149 lb.[92][93] He noted a side benefit of a wire-bridle mainsheet's indirect forward loading: it helps keep the boom seated on its tack bearing even without a retaining screw, as he found once the original screw worked loose on his own boat;[94] and, separately, adapted another builder's fix for mainsheet fouling by encasing the sheet's tail in plastic piping where it crosses the transom.[95] Weighing whether to fit an outboard to Faith, he gathered reference photographs of another builder's Honda 2.3 installation on "Whisper" and weighed which side of the transom to mount his own.[96][97] He pointed newcomers to his site's plans-distributor links and the group's Files section for recurring questions, and suggested builders practice Paradox's own methods before attempting an enlarged version;[98] and, when a fellow builder worked out a 20% Paradox scale-up, judged the resulting boat could suit a good many people, proposing names such as "ParadoxPlus" for it.[99] He also arranged for a Messing About In Boats sea-trials article to be reproduced on paradox-uk.co.uk as "Zoë's Sea Trials," with permission.[100] Answering a builder's question about widening the cabin top out to the hull's edge, Bill argued against it: the stepped-in side decks stow the lowered sail and the yuloh, a wider cabin makes it harder to move forward to the anchor or to fend off, it adds windage especially to windward, and interior space would not actually increase unless the side bins were also removed. See [flush cabin sides for Paradox (speculative)](/design/ideas/flush-cabin-sides-idea.md#a-2008-recurrence-answered).[102]

Confirming both a slot-and-locknut fix and a tapped-epoxy fix for a splitting boom-tang bolt hole had each worked, in his own case simply setting the bolt directly into epoxy filler with no thread tapped had likewise given no further trouble; drew from it a broader design view — every moving part in a boat is a built-in weakness, and a sailing boat cannot avoid articulation altogether, so Paradox's own lifting rudder, tack arrangement, roller-reefing system, running-rigging blocks, and boom tang fitting all simply have to be strongly made and checked regularly in use. See [mainsheet, outboard, and rudder fouling — boom-end splitting at the mainsheet-tang bolt](/components/mainsheet-outboard-fouling.md#boom-end-splitting-at-the-mainsheet-tang-bolt-and-a-construction-fix).[103]

Resolved his own long-dithered engine-mounting question for "Faith": a starboard-side Honda 2.3 mount, considered since spring, was ruled out as impracticable given the engine's vulnerability to submersion when heeled or motor-sailing, and a sponson-style fuel-and-motor extension raised its own stowage problem when not in use; settled on the transom instead, protected from swamping, the sheet fouling the motor only rarely, when raised to its highest position with the shaft near horizontal. Built a three-position bracket (high, medium, low); in the high position, with the engine vertical, only the skeg's tip touches the water, so there is no appreciable drag and the sheet runs clear. Petrol storage of two 5-litre cans at the forward end of the cabin has likewise given no trouble, the cooking stove never lit while refuelling. See [auxiliary outboard power — a side-mounted motor-and-fuel sponson considered and rejected](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-side-mounted-motor-and-fuel-sponson-considered-and-rejected-a-three-position-transom-bracket).[104] Answering the same thread's closing question about reaching a transom motor's throttle and fuel from inside the cabin, reported the throttle reachable with a bit of a stretch, and refuelling manageable from inside except for judging the fuel level, which still meant stepping onto the rear deck; weighed a rubber bulb pump and a fishing-float level gauge as refinements better suited to a long-distance cruise than to his own mostly short, occasional motoring. See [auxiliary outboard power — reaching a transom motor's throttle and fuel from inside the cabin](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-side-mounted-motor-and-fuel-sponson-considered-and-rejected-a-three-position-transom-bracket).[105] Building his own yuloh, later reported a further refinement beyond the plans: the pivot-pin hole should be bored with enough twist to bring the blade to about 50-60 degrees from horizontal by the end of each stroke, and relocating the hole 5.25 in further toward the handle end gives more power and a more upright working stance, a modification adopted with good results. See [Yuloh — pivot socket dimensions](/components/yuloh.md#blade-camber-the-ford-nomenclature-and-pivot-socket-dimensions).[106] Answering the same thread's closing exchange over the refuelling photographs, defended going without a safety harness as itself a considered safety choice — free movement over restriction — reserved only for conditions mild enough to be refuelling at all, and found the engine steadier to service left ticking over in gear. See [Faith — refuelling practice underway, and a name's double meaning](/boats/faith.md#refuelling-practice-underway-and-a-names-double-meaning).[107] Making the case for keeping the outboard from the opposite direction, set out a firsthand example of exactly when only the engine would do: two miles to windward against a force 4 wind with the flood tide running the same way, timed to reach a drying home mooring by high water — a passage he judged sail and yuloh alone could not have made in the time available. See [Faith — a tide-and-wind case for the outboard, on the Blackwater](/boats/faith.md#a-tide-and-wind-case-for-the-outboard-on-the-blackwater).[108] Answering the follow-up on how much fuel that outing took, gave a concrete consumption figure and restated a firm preference for petrol over an electric motor, framing a petrol-fitted Paradox as capable of touring the canals of Europe; judged the yuloh useful on a Paradox only in near-calm conditions, doubting it holds a course in a force 2 or more even with the rudder raised, and floated the idea that Sven Yrvind's own boats fare better under yuloh power thanks to a forward drop board. See [Faith — a tide-and-wind case for the outboard, on the Blackwater](/boats/faith.md#a-tide-and-wind-case-for-the-outboard-on-the-blackwater).[109]

Answering a fellow builder's drip-rail question, reported sailing Faith in cold April conditions that brought on real window condensation, and found the plans' own drip tray, built as drawn, worked well — backing the stock trim piece over an improvised alternative. See [window glazing — cabin condensation](/construction/window-glazing-materials.md#cabin-condensation-a-drip-rail-a-moulded-gutter-and-window-drain-holes).[110] Answering a fellow owner's anchor-attachment question the same week, described his own cabin-worked routine: laying the anchor standing in the cabin by feeding the rode from a bucket to starboard, hooking it over the starboard fore cleat, then securing it from inside to the aft starboard cleat, retrieving it the same way so the anchor and rode get cleaned as they come aboard — falling back on a fully cockpit-worked method only in rougher conditions. See [ground tackle and anchoring — a cabin-fed bucket routine, and treating any system as optional](/operations/ground-tackle.md#a-cabin-fed-bucket-routine-and-treating-any-system-as-optional).[111]

## One person across three handles

Bill Serjeant's Yahoo profile name changed twice over the archive's span — `barnacleid` (2002–2003) to `barnacletoo` ("for technical reasons," January 2004) to `williamserjeant` (mid-2004 onward) — with the underlying e-mail address shifting in step from `w.serjeant@` to `william@`.[101] All three identities are the same person; he signs "Bill" throughout, and other correspondents address him as Bill across all three eras. His Yahoo profile briefly reverted to display as `barnacleid` again in a February 2006 posting, still from the `william@` address — the underlying account, not a fourth identity. By September 2008 both the profile handle and the `From:` address itself had reverted fully to the original `barnacleid@` form;[113] the content (a firsthand account of crewing a yacht, "Bill" signature, and continuity with the ongoing Faith/heavy-weather threads) confirms the same person, not a distinct account — a caution for any future batch checking this handle against the archive's other, unrelated "Bill"s (Bill Longyard, William Crawford, William Nix, and the `bllfs6`/`bllfsh666` handles).
