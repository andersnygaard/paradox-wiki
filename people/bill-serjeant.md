# Bill Serjeant

**Bill Serjeant** (also William Serjeant; Yahoo profiles `barnacleid`, later `barnacletoo`, then `williamserjeant`; e-mail `w.serjeant@`/`william@`) is a UK small-boat sailor and the builder of the community's principal UK web presence, who spent nearly two years as the group's de facto UK registrar and facilitator before starting his own Paradox from scratch. Based in England, he already owned and sailed a boat named **"Bumper"** throughout the Paradox-UK years, and kept a separate build/cruise blog at `bills-log.blogspot.com`. Bill's Paradox, "Faith," and its build are documented at [Faith](/boats/faith.md).

## The Small Sailboats / Paradox-UK web presence

After receiving plans in October 2003, Bill added a Paradox section to his existing site, **`www.smallsailboats.co.uk`**, then split it out into a dedicated **`www.paradox-uk.co.uk`**, giving the design's UK builder community a home-country counterpart to microcruising.com. He also set up a companion **`smallsailboats`** Yahoo Group and pointed builders to the third-party **irbs.com** Small Sailboats Usergroup. Full detail, including the site's temporary 2004 outage, its sailmaker recommendations, and its role relaying the designer's contact address, lives at [references/paradox-uk-website.md](/references/paradox-uk-website.md).[1]

Bill compiled and maintained the group's closest thing to a **build registry** — a running list of completed and in-progress Paradoxes at `www.smallsailboats.co.uk/paradox/parindex.htm` and `names.htm` — explicitly dependent on builders reporting their own boats in, and periodically reporting updated counts (at least 14 boats on or under construction by late 2004). He also compiled the group's original **"Tools" list**, drawn from the tools used building an earlier Paradox — his own compilation, not an endorsement from that builder. See [construction/build-effort.md](/construction/build-effort.md#tools) for the list itself.[2] He repeated the registry's open invitation once more in January 2006, encouraging any builder not already listed on the site to get in touch, "to have as comprehensive a list as possible" — replying, in passing, to the two-person-capacity question answered in full at [operations/first-boat-suitability.md](/operations/first-boat-suitability.md#the-two-person-question-answered-directly).[3] He renewed the appeal again the following month, this time specifically asking for photos of finished boats rather than further building-stage pictures — drawing in a new Los Angeles builder and a follow-up proposal to add e-mail addresses to the registry.[4][5] Bill added the e-mail column himself, posted the registry's direct link to the whole list, and flagged that the new Los Angeles builder's own e-mail address on file appeared to be incorrect; a new Phoenix, Arizona prospective builder who inquired about using the registry to find nearby owners was told the usual rule — his details would be added once he actually started building.[6][7] See [references/paradox-uk-website.md — a 2006 database renewal, and a new Los Angeles builder](/references/paradox-uk-website.md#a-2006-database-renewal-and-a-new-los-angeles-builder).

## Contributions to design discussion

Beyond his own build, Bill contributed steadily to design threads: he raised the rudder blade's pivot-bolt fixing and, over two years later, asked how to stop water ingress at that same fixing — whether the stock and blade should be bushed around the pivot bolt, since the plans show only plain holes below the waterline with no stated waterproofing — answered with an epoxy-fill-and-rebore technique; he also raised a twin-rudder proposal for freeing up the transom centerline for an outboard (judged not worth the added complexity); asked the self-steering question that produced the group's fullest auto-pilot-versus-wind-vane comparison, including the specific problem a folding rudder poses for a trim-tab wind vane; and gave the archive's clearest one-line classification of Paradox against a genuine ocean racer — measured against the Dix-designed Mini Transat 650, the two are "as different as chalk and cheese." See [design/rudder.md](/design/rudder.md#the-pivot-bolt-fixing-and-its-record-under-grounding-loads) (including [the bushing fix](/design/rudder.md#bushing-the-pivot-bolt-against-water-ingress)), [operations/self-steering.md](/operations/self-steering.md#auto-pilot-versus-wind-vane-self-steering-a-practical-comparison), and [design/blue-water-suitability.md](/design/blue-water-suitability.md#a-racercruiser-comparison-and-bill-serjeants-verdict).[8][9][10][11] He judged the epoxy-fill/rebore fix "a very good solution" and reported he was about to bush his own rudder's pivot bolt the same way.[12]

He also arranged for a *Messing About In Boats* sea-trials article to be reproduced on paradox-uk.co.uk as "Zoë's Sea Trials," with permission — see [boats/zoe.md](/boats/zoe.md#press-coverage-two-2003-magazine-articles).[13]

Having just ordered the bulk of his own build timber from Robbins Timber of Bristol when a boom-knot dispute with that same supplier played out on the list, Bill noted he would be checking his own delivery for knots himself while its guarantee still held — see [boats/enuf.md](/boats/enuf.md#finishing-the-spars-epoxy-varnish-and-knots-in-the-boom).[14][15]

Days later, Bill posted his first actual build-progress photos: deck beams, cabin beams, and hatch beams, added to a freshly updated `paradox-uk.co.uk` and mirrored to the group's own `paradoxbuilders2` Yahoo Group photo section. He used the update to ask for other UK Paradox builders' details not already listed on the site's `names.htm` page, keeping the registry current — see [references/paradox-uk-website.md](/references/paradox-uk-website.md#a-uk-and-worldwide-build-count) for the registry's own standing dependence on builders reporting in. He repeated the same request to the list again the following day.[16][17]

His build timber arrived from Robbins Timber shortly after — an unexpectedly large delivery he could not fully check before signing for it, noting the exception on the delivery paperwork instead. By then he had completed all of his beams, including the cabin and hatch beams; laminated his tiller; and begun drawing the bulkheads out on plywood.[18]

Gluing his mast's front piece into place, Bill asked how to route the masthead light's wiring down through the mast and out to the battery given the mast is stepped and unstepped for trailering — answered by cross-linking the community's standing masthead-wiring options. See [running lights — routing masthead-light wiring outside the mast, below the sheave](/operations/running-lights.md#routing-masthead-light-wiring-outside-the-mast-below-the-sheave).[19] A follow-up question — how the flex avoids being trapped as a snugly-fitting mast is lowered through the deck — drew several answers between them, settling on the mast's own taper as the reason and converging on two different below-deck plug/socket arrangements; full detail at [running lights — clearing a snug deck hole as the mast is stepped and unstepped](/operations/running-lights.md#clearing-a-snug-deck-hole-as-the-mast-is-stepped-and-unstepped).[20][21]

Around the same time, still searching for a name for the boat, Bill put a shortlist of roughly 80 candidate names to the group for suggestions and reasons — among them *Braveheart*, *Crusader*, *Eureka*, *Mudlark*, *Paradoxical*, *Pilgrim*, *Scallywag*, and *Xanadu* — inviting the list to also propose names of their own if none of the eighty appealed.[22] One reply, only asked in passing, declined to offer a strong opinion beyond ruling out "Yoyo" as giving "an entirely untrue impression" — noting that boat's own name, "Little Jim," only settled 18 months after the build began.[20] By March 2006 he had settled the question: his Paradox is named **"Faith."**[23]

## A June 2006 site outage

The `smallsailboats.co.uk` domain went down again in June 2006, this time showing visitors only a domain registrar's holding page; Bill worked to sort out the registration and asked for patience while it might take hours or days to restore. Within a day he had the site re-uploaded and confirmed fully "up and running" again. Full account, alongside the 2004 outage, at [references/paradox-uk-website.md — a second outage, June 2006, and an apparent domain-registrar issue](/references/paradox-uk-website.md#a-second-outage-june-2006-and-an-apparent-domain-registrar-issue).[24][25][26][27][28]

## The "quintessential seat" proposal

Bill also raised the case for a purpose-built adjustable helm seat, citing an adjustable seat fitted to a cruising paddling-and-sailing canoe as a model — variable height and horizontal position within the cockpit, plus an adjustable backrest, reported to allow sitting for hours without numbness. Bill's own specification called for a seat that rotates and locks in any position, adjusts for height and inclination, can be fixed at variable athwartships positions, supports the back well, stows clear of the cabin for sleeping, and still allows ready access to the lazarette — floating a bicycle saddle as a lighter-hearted alternative to a horse's saddle once fitted as a helmsman's seat on a well-known boat. See [crew size and fit — long-passage seat comfort: cushion practice](/operations/crew-size-and-fit.md#long-passage-seat-comfort-cushion-practice).[29]

## A caution against departing from the stock design, and the Wharram Hina's knockdown

Responding to a slot-top cabin idea for "[Enuf](/boats/enuf.md)", Bill cautioned against deviating significantly from Matt Layden's design even for waters judged sheltered, drawing on his own experience: his 22 ft Wharram Hina catamaran was once knocked down by a sudden, freak gust on an otherwise calm afternoon. Full account, including his self-righting/water-ingress reasoning and two comparable knockdown-sinkings he recalled, at [boats/enuf.md](/boats/enuf.md#a-slot-top-cabin-idea-for-open-water-use).[30]

Asked for more detail on the Hina story — her sail area, whether she was built to Wharram's stock plan, and whether she was salvaged or righted — Bill did not retell the incident itself, instead pointing to two pages on his own website covering "Twinstar" (his name for the Hina) where the capsize is mentioned.[31]

## A chine-runner-width question, prompted by "Jubilee"

Bill also asked why the chine runners on a designer's 40 ft sharpie "Jubilee" — a photo of which had been posted in the group's Hogfish Maximus files folder — looked so narrow for a hull that size, wondering whether a Paradox-family hull scaled up needs proportionally bigger runners. See [how chine runners work — narrow "wing" runners, and a centerboard-plus-runner combination](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md#narrow-wing-runners-on-a-chris-morejohn-sharpie-and-a-centerboard-plus-runner-combination) for the answer.[32][33]

## A visit to Alastair's

Passing through Yeovil, Bill called on a fellow builder there, and was shown "Little Jim," spending nearly two hours talking through the build — an encounter Bill credited with renewing his own enthusiasm for finishing his Paradox.[34] He corrected himself the same week: the visit had actually run to nearly three hours, made memorable in part by coffee served in a DCA (Dinghy Cruising Association) mug.[35]

Drawing on running a comparable Yahoo group of his own (`smallsailboats`), Bill explained the mechanics behind a fellow builder's photo-upload "privileges" error: it is a moderator-controlled setting, not a fault on the member's own account — see [the mailing list — photo-album upload privileges are a moderator-controlled setting](/references/mailing-list.md#photo-album-upload-privileges-are-a-moderator-controlled-setting).[36]

## One person across three handles

Bill Serjeant's Yahoo profile name changed twice over the archive's span — `barnacleid` (2002–2003) to `barnacletoo` ("for technical reasons," January 2004) to `williamserjeant` (mid-2004 onward) — with the underlying e-mail address shifting in step from `w.serjeant@` to `william@`.[37] All three identities are the same person; he signs "Bill" throughout, and other correspondents address him as Bill across all three eras. His Yahoo profile briefly reverted to display as `barnacleid` again in a February 2006 posting, still from the `william@` address — the underlying account, not a fourth identity.
