# Bill Serjeant

**Bill Serjeant** (also William Serjeant; Yahoo profiles `barnacleid`, later `barnacletoo`, then `williamserjeant`; e-mail `w.serjeant@`/`william@`) is a UK small-boat sailor and the builder of the community's principal UK web presence, who spent nearly two years as the group's de facto UK registrar and facilitator before starting his own Paradox from scratch. Based in England, he already owned and sailed a boat named **"Bumper"** throughout the Paradox-UK years, and kept a separate build/cruise blog at `bills-log.blogspot.com`. Bill's Paradox, "Faith," and its build are documented at [Faith](/boats/faith.md).

## The Small Sailboats / Paradox-UK web presence

After receiving plans in October 2003, Bill added a Paradox section to his existing site, **`www.smallsailboats.co.uk`**, then split it out into a dedicated **`www.paradox-uk.co.uk`**, giving the design's UK builder community a home-country counterpart to microcruising.com. He also set up a companion **`smallsailboats`** Yahoo Group and pointed builders to the third-party **irbs.com** Small Sailboats Usergroup. Full detail, including the site's temporary 2004 outage, its sailmaker recommendations, and its role relaying the designer's contact address, lives at [references/paradox-uk-website.md](/references/paradox-uk-website.md).[1]

In October 2006 Bill added a "Why Build Paradox" page to the same site, `www.smallsailboats.co.uk/paradox/why.htm`, answering a newcomer's question about the design's appeal; see [boats/little-jim.md](/boats/little-jim.md#a-scilly-isles-rendezvous-and-a-why-build-paradox-web-page) for the full cross-linked account, including his proposal to cruise the Scilly Isles jointly with Alastair once "Faith" was launched.[38]

Bill compiled and maintained the group's closest thing to a **build registry** — a running list of completed and in-progress Paradoxes at `www.smallsailboats.co.uk/paradox/parindex.htm` and `names.htm` — explicitly dependent on builders reporting their own boats in, and periodically reporting updated counts (at least 14 boats on or under construction by late 2004). He also compiled the group's original **"Tools" list**, drawn from the tools used building an earlier Paradox — his own compilation, not an endorsement from that builder. See [construction/build-effort.md](/construction/build-effort.md#tools) for the list itself.[2] He repeated the registry's open invitation once more in January 2006, encouraging any builder not already listed on the site to get in touch, "to have as comprehensive a list as possible" — replying, in passing, to the two-person-capacity question answered in full at [operations/first-boat-suitability.md](/operations/first-boat-suitability.md#the-two-person-question-answered-directly).[3] He renewed the appeal again the following month, this time specifically asking for photos of finished boats rather than further building-stage pictures — drawing in a new Los Angeles builder and a follow-up proposal to add e-mail addresses to the registry.[4][5] Bill added the e-mail column himself, posted the registry's direct link to the whole list, and flagged that the new Los Angeles builder's own e-mail address on file appeared to be incorrect; a new Phoenix, Arizona prospective builder who inquired about using the registry to find nearby owners was told the usual rule — his details would be added once he actually started building.[6][7] See [references/paradox-uk-website.md — a 2006 database renewal, and a new Los Angeles builder](/references/paradox-uk-website.md#a-2006-database-renewal-and-a-new-los-angeles-builder). "JoHanna" was added to the registry's Owners/Builders page the same way in May 2007, with a note that the great majority of Paradoxes on record remain US-built. See [boats/johanna.md — added to the UK Owners/Builders registry](/boats/johanna.md#added-to-the-uk-ownersbuilders-registry).[60]

## Contributions to design discussion

Beyond his own build, Bill contributed steadily to design threads: he raised the rudder blade's pivot-bolt fixing and, over two years later, asked how to stop water ingress at that same fixing — whether the stock and blade should be bushed around the pivot bolt, since the plans show only plain holes below the waterline with no stated waterproofing — answered with an epoxy-fill-and-rebore technique; he also raised a twin-rudder proposal for freeing up the transom centerline for an outboard (judged not worth the added complexity); asked the self-steering question that produced the group's fullest auto-pilot-versus-wind-vane comparison, including the specific problem a folding rudder poses for a trim-tab wind vane; and gave the archive's clearest one-line classification of Paradox against a genuine ocean racer — measured against the Dix-designed Mini Transat 650, the two are "as different as chalk and cheese." See [design/rudder.md](/components/rudder.md#the-pivot-bolt-fixing-and-its-record-under-grounding-loads) (including [the bushing fix](/components/rudder.md#bushing-the-pivot-bolt-against-water-ingress)), [operations/self-steering.md](/operations/self-steering.md#auto-pilot-versus-wind-vane-self-steering-a-practical-comparison), and [design/blue-water-suitability.md](/design/blue-water-suitability.md#a-racercruiser-comparison-and-bill-serjeants-verdict).[8][9][10][11] He judged the epoxy-fill/rebore fix "a very good solution" and reported he was about to bush his own rudder's pivot bolt the same way.[12]

Replying at length to a fellow builder's defense of modification a day later, Bill worked through the argument point by point: changing a boat's rig is a real change to the original design even when it produces a better arrangement for that particular owner; his own view that Paradox does not suit a large sailor is a personal judgment, not a claim that larger sailors owe the design any deference; and, agreeing that boats — like cars and houses — are commonly personalized by their owners, held that doing so does not by itself show any lack of appreciation for the original design. Full text and citation also at [paradox.md](/paradox.md#citations).[94]

He also arranged for a *Messing About In Boats* sea-trials article to be reproduced on paradox-uk.co.uk as "Zoë's Sea Trials," with permission — see [boats/zoe.md](/boats/zoe.md#press-coverage-two-2003-magazine-articles).[13]

Building his own yuloh, Bill raised the plans' ambiguous curvature figure, the "For'd" nomenclature on sheet 12, blade-camber orientation, and the pivot-socket dimensions — a thread that drew the fullest technical treatment of yuloh camber and gearing on record, including a relayed explanation of Worcester's classic yuloh drawing. See [components/yuloh.md — blade camber, the "for'd" nomenclature, and pivot socket dimensions](/components/yuloh.md#blade-camber-the-ford-nomenclature-and-pivot-socket-dimensions).[40][41][42] A follow-up thread working out the pivot socket's own material drew a shortlist of candidates from across the list — lignum vitae, a stainless ball in a copper cup, Acetal/UHMW plastic, and a teflon-coated ball — each acknowledged and thanked in turn; see [components/yuloh.md — ball-joint versus oarlock, and pivot-socket wear over time](/components/yuloh.md#ball-joint-versus-oarlock-and-pivot-socket-wear-over-time).[48]

Having just ordered the bulk of his own build timber from Robbins Timber of Bristol when a boom-knot dispute with that same supplier played out on the list, Bill noted he would be checking his own delivery for knots himself while its guarantee still held — see [boats/enuf.md](/boats/enuf.md#finishing-the-spars-epoxy-varnish-and-knots-in-the-boom).[14][15]

Days later, Bill posted his first actual build-progress photos: deck beams, cabin beams, and hatch beams, added to a freshly updated `paradox-uk.co.uk` and mirrored to the group's own `paradoxbuilders2` Yahoo Group photo section. He used the update to ask for other UK Paradox builders' details not already listed on the site's `names.htm` page, keeping the registry current — see [references/paradox-uk-website.md](/references/paradox-uk-website.md#a-uk-and-worldwide-build-count) for the registry's own standing dependence on builders reporting in. He repeated the same request to the list again the following day.[16][17]

His build timber arrived from Robbins Timber shortly after — an unexpectedly large delivery he could not fully check before signing for it, noting the exception on the delivery paperwork instead. By then he had completed all of his beams, including the cabin and hatch beams; laminated his tiller; and begun drawing the bulkheads out on plywood.[18]

Gluing his mast's front piece into place, Bill asked how to route the masthead light's wiring down through the mast and out to the battery given the mast is stepped and unstepped for trailering — answered by cross-linking the community's standing masthead-wiring options. See [running lights — routing masthead-light wiring outside the mast, below the sheave](/operations/running-lights.md#routing-masthead-light-wiring-outside-the-mast-below-the-sheave).[19] A follow-up question — how the flex avoids being trapped as a snugly-fitting mast is lowered through the deck — drew several answers between them, settling on the mast's own taper as the reason and converging on two different below-deck plug/socket arrangements; full detail at [running lights — clearing a snug deck hole as the mast is stepped and unstepped](/operations/running-lights.md#clearing-a-snug-deck-hole-as-the-mast-is-stepped-and-unstepped).[20][21]

Around the same time, still searching for a name for the boat, Bill put a shortlist of roughly 80 candidate names to the group for suggestions and reasons — among them *Braveheart*, *Crusader*, *Eureka*, *Mudlark*, *Paradoxical*, *Pilgrim*, *Scallywag*, and *Xanadu* — inviting the list to also propose names of their own if none of the eighty appealed.[22] One reply, only asked in passing, declined to offer a strong opinion beyond ruling out "Yoyo" as giving "an entirely untrue impression" — noting that boat's own name, "Little Jim," only settled 18 months after the build began.[20] By March 2006 he had settled the question: his Paradox is named **"Faith."**[23]

## The "quintessential seat" proposal

Bill also raised the case for a purpose-built adjustable helm seat, citing an adjustable seat fitted to a cruising paddling-and-sailing canoe as a model — variable height and horizontal position within the cockpit, plus an adjustable backrest, reported to allow sitting for hours without numbness. Bill's own specification called for a seat that rotates and locks in any position, adjusts for height and inclination, can be fixed at variable athwartships positions, supports the back well, stows clear of the cabin for sleeping, and still allows ready access to the lazarette — floating a bicycle saddle as a lighter-hearted alternative to a horse's saddle once fitted as a helmsman's seat on a well-known boat. See [crew size and fit — long-passage seat comfort: cushion practice](/operations/crew-size-and-fit.md#long-passage-seat-comfort-cushion-practice).[29]

## A caution against departing from the stock design, and the Wharram Hina's knockdown

Responding to a slot-top cabin idea for "[Enuf](/boats/enuf.md)", Bill cautioned against deviating significantly from Matt Layden's design even for waters judged sheltered, drawing on his own experience: his 22 ft Wharram Hina catamaran was once knocked down by a sudden, freak gust on an otherwise calm afternoon. Full account, including his self-righting/water-ingress reasoning and two comparable knockdown-sinkings he recalled, at [boats/enuf.md](/boats/enuf.md#a-slot-top-cabin-idea-for-open-water-use).[30]

Asked for more detail on the Hina story — her sail area, whether she was built to Wharram's stock plan, and whether she was salvaged or righted — Bill did not retell the incident itself, instead pointing to two pages on his own website covering "Twinstar" (his name for the Hina) where the capsize is mentioned.[31]

## Trailer braking, a firmed cruise schedule, and a surplus bronze-nail offer

Answering questions on his own trailer, sourced secondhand from Derek Clark's "Enuf," Bill explained Paradox's weight ideally calls for auto brakes, which that trailer lacks and may need converting for later, complicated by the risk of brakes fouling once immersed — a purpose-built trailer sidesteps the problem entirely by never submerging its axles, at the cost of the added strength (and expense) a heavier trailer then needs. He also firmed up his West Country cruise plan for early summer 2007 (Exmouth to Dartmouth, on to Plymouth and the Tamar, then Falmouth and, weather permitting, the Isles of Scilly), and offered a fellow builder his own surplus of bronze ring nails, left over from building "Faith," at a lower price than his original supplier, Classic Marine. ## Reference photographs for a Honda outboard, considered for Faith

Weighing whether to fit an outboard to Faith, Bill gathered close-up reference photographs of David Beard's Honda outboard (given as a 2.3 hp model) on "[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)," to help decide whether to copy the installation, and published them on the smallsailboats.co.uk Editorial page.[81]

A follow-up message the next week asked whether Jack Gardiner's own outboard sat in the same position as David Beard's — by then confirmed mounted on Whisper's port-side transom, per the same photographs — while weighing whether Faith's own motor, if fitted, should instead go to starboard, the side left free of the sail, boom, and yard when stowed; the chief worry was the sheet fouling the motor, especially when gybing. ## Ballast placement, and the published cruise account

Asked about a proposed steel-bottomed alternative to loose lead ballast, Bill reaffirmed the plans' own ballast placement: keeping lead near the boat's longitudinal centre preserves buoyancy at bow and stern for a sea-kindly motion, and Faith's ballast — fitted per Matt Layden's plan, including the water ballast at the centreline — has needed no adjustment. See [weight, ballast, and displacement — ballast placement: outboard vs. centered](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#ballast-placement-outboard-vs-centered).[83] When Little Jim's owner's promised write-up of their joint West Country cruise finally appeared that October, published in the Dinghy Cruising Association's bulletin, Bill called it a very good account with fine colour photographs, and hoped for another chance at the Scilly Isles crossing the following year. ## A November 2007 windvane catch-up

Reopening the vane-gear question after a summer's quiet, Bill asked whether Glen was still around and satisfied with his own gear, and what electronic self-steering equipment he used and how it connected to the tiller.[85] Once a bow-mounted, vane-driven auxiliary rudder came up as an idea, Bill judged a bow mount risked sail interference and cited Robin Knox-Johnston's twin bow-mounted vanes on "Suhaili" as a precedent that complicates rather than simplifies the arrangement.[86] Answering a link to a simple horizontal-axis vane design, Bill recalled a similar commercial unit — the QME, made by Quantock Marine Enterprises of Highbridge in England's West Country — once fitted to his own Hunter 19: cheap and popular, but reliable only on an exceptionally well-balanced boat, and on Paradox liable to be snagged by the sail and sheet unless mounted well astern of it.[87] Thanking Glen for his fuller reply and sympathizing over his wife's illness, Bill judged himself edging toward an electronic self-steering system instead, for its simplicity and light weight, given he does not plan long ocean passages — though a perfected windvane gear would still tempt him to copy it. See [self-steering — boom interference, vane-blade materials, and a commercial precedent: the QME](/operations/self-steering.md#boom-interference-vane-blade-materials-and-a-commercial-precedent-the-qme) for the full November 2007 exchange.[88] Once Glen promised photographs of his own autopilot-to-tiller-line setup, Bill said he'd look forward to them;[89] when the photographs went up in a new files folder, Bill thanked Glen and noted he would need to mount a similar system to port rather than starboard, since his own compass already occupied the starboard side — a placement question Glen agreed might if anything make the port side the easier of the two to use. See [self-steering — photographs of the autopilot coupling, and further tiller-lock variants](/operations/self-steering.md#photographs-of-the-autopilot-coupling-and-further-tiller-lock-variants) for the fuller exchange, including a further tiller-lock design shown in the photos.[90]

## Beaching stern-first for a comfortable sleeping angle

Beaching stern-first, deliberately chosen to angle the cabin sole so the head lies higher than the feet for a comfortable night, carries a real wear risk: the transom's corners are vulnerable to that approach, and grounding stern-first on a pebbly beach specifically risks thumping the rudder's base — a lesson learned the hard way the previous year. The better practice is to find a gently shelving beach and get the hull as close to horizontal as circumstances allow. See [rudder and chine experiments — beaching stern-first: a sleeping-comfort technique, and why it doesn't damage the rudder](/components/rudder.md#beaching-stern-first-a-sleeping-comfort-technique-and-why-it-doesnt-damage-the-rudder) for the full account, including why the rudder's own fittings tolerate the contact without damage.[100]

## A mainsheet benefit noticed: keeping the boom seated on its tack bearing

Answering a wire-bridle mainsheet-routing proposal (see [rig and sail — mainsheet fouling on the rudder and rear deck with an outboard fitted](/components/rig/index.md#mainsheet-fouling-on-the-rudder-and-rear-deck-with-an-outboard-fitted)), Bill Serjeant pointed out a side benefit of the sheet's own indirect forward loading: it helps hold the boom in place on the tack bearing, handy if the small retaining screw securing the hooked "gooseneck" fitting works loose, as happened on his own boat — since then, he has done without a retaining screw altogether, relying on the sheet's constant forward thrust to keep the boom seated.[102]

## A caution on saw-grooving the foredeck panel

Recalling his own build, Bill Serjeant noted the forward part of the foredeck can be bent using leverage from the "wings" left standing when shaping either side, but that this can still open a small crack roughly 9 in long up the centerline, as happened with "Faith"'s own deck; a saw-groove cut deliberately in the same area leaves a ridge clearly visible once the panel takes its shape, since the pieces on either side straighten themselves out — harmless once glassed over, but less pleasing than the continuous curve the design intends. See [deck and cabin beam lamination — fitting the forward deck skin to a tight curve](/construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md#fitting-the-forward-deck-skin-to-a-tight-curve).[103]

## A mainsheet-fouling fix adapted from another builder, and a planned outboard test

Encasing the tail of the mainsheet in plastic piping where it crosses the transom, cutting down on snagging, worked well for a similar fouling problem on another Paradox — see [Enuf](/boats/enuf.md) — and was planned as an experiment aboard "Faith" too; the engine and bracket can be removed easily if the arrangement does not suit. Despite an initial reluctance to fit the boat's Honda 2.3 outboard, the choice matched other owners' generally happy experience with the same engine, with a thorough test planned before setting out on the West Country cruise (see above).[104]
