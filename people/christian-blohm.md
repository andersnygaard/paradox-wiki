# Christian Blohm

**Christian Blohm** is an amateur builder in **north-east Ohio**, USA, who had "been dreaming of building a Paradox for years" — he had ordered a set of plans about seven years earlier and lost the files, weighed a Wharram Tiki 21 against a [Paradox](/paradox.md) as more tractable for a father of four daughters, and reordered plans before clearing out his garage for a build start in January 2023.[1] He documented nearly the whole hull-construction phase in careful, methodical posts to the [Facebook group](/references/paradox-facebook-group.md), making him a useful representative of the modern American home-builder working the design from first principles and the group's how-to threads.

## Materials and getting started

He ordered **six gallons of Raka epoxy** and picked up marine plywood to the plans' materials list, a trip he priced out in full: $1,261 for the plywood, $41 for the U-Haul rental, and $65 in gas, plus five hours of his time.[2] Weighing sail cloth, he got SailRite quotes for both the kit-standard **4oz Dacron** and a heavier **5.8oz** option, about $300 apart, and asked the group whether the extra strength was worth it given his plans to sail the Great Lakes and, eventually, the Florida Keys and Bahamas around 40 days a season.[3]

## Deck beams and bulkheads

He made his **first official cuts on 26 February 2023**, after three months of quiet preparation — gathering materials, setting up a workshop, and reading through old io.groups postings and general wooden-boat-building references.[4] To rip the strips for the six laminated deck beams he built a jig and cut all 42 strips to length, buying his first tablesaw, a SawStop CTS, for the job.[4] He laminated the first beam with the whole sequence **rehearsed in advance per a Don Casey book** and the strips marked in order beforehand, a level of preparation that held even when an old box of nitrile gloves fell apart on him mid-glue-up.[5] Within two weeks all six beams were complete and bulkheads #1 and #2 were cut; the beams fit both bulkhead curves "spot on," and he noted this was his first time transferring plans to plywood — "a real swift learning curve."[6] The pace was set by his work schedule: seven days on, seven off, with the off-weeks away from home, which by late April had given him roughly 24 days of building at five hours a session.[7]

## Side panels: layout and scarfing

When it came to cutting the side panels, he questioned the build manual's instruction to lay out the opposite side from scratch before cutting, asking the group whether one already-cut panel could simply be traced as a template for its mate; a fellow builder, Anders Nygaard, confirmed the shortcut, describing how he had screwed his first cut side to the next sheet of plywood and used a router to copy it, with a small jig to transfer the bulkhead locations to the opposite side.[8] For the scarf joints he practiced first rather than committing straight to the real panels, trying his new Makita planer on waste stock.[9] After a couple more practice runs he judged that cutting the scarfs **freehand would not be accurate enough**, so he made two side-jigs from one of his best practice pieces and used them to guide the planer before scarfing the port forward side panel.[10]

## Method questions and assembly prep

Blohm reasoned through several build-order questions in public rather than guessing. On priming, he concluded from [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/index.md)'s own guidance in "Building Paradox" that as much finish work as possible should be done while the panels were still flat on the bench, leaving only the wood-on-wood bonding areas — bulkhead edges, chine and shear logs — bare for later epoxy and fillets.[11] He later put the same question to the group directly, asking whether to paint the bulkheads before or after assembly; the replies split, with one builder warning that painted surfaces would be the first to break free of an epoxy bond and another arguing that a first epoxy coat on bare wood makes the strongest bond, so pre-painting was only safe if no further gluing was planned on those surfaces.[12] Once the panels were done he moved on to the **sleepers and cradle** needed to support the hull through 3D assembly, asking the group about adding castering wheels and building one side to support a curved fixture for rolling the hull over.[13]

## Status

Progress ran in fits and starts after that. He resumed the build in August 2024 after roughly a year's hiatus, finishing the stem and aligning the panels for glue-up, and gave the boat her name — **"Rebel,"** as in resisting generally accepted convention.[14] As of an April 2026 comment on an unrelated post, the hull was still **half-built in his garage**, and he described himself as looking for the motivation to finish it.[15]
