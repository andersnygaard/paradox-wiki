# Jack Gardiner

**Jack Gardiner** (`alex29@...`, Yahoo profile `tidybowlmann`) is a Rhode Island carpenter and tradesman — a self-described "mr fix it type" who had already designed and built his own diving boat in six weeks before buying a set of [Paradox](/paradox.md) plans in December 2003. He wanted the boat for coastal cruising rather than ocean crossing, joining the list at a point when six Paradoxes had already been launched. He went on to build a stitch-and-glue Paradox of his own, "Jill," launched in September 2004, and remained an active correspondent on construction and rig questions for years afterward.[1][2]

## Contributions

Jack's distinctive early contribution was opening and driving the archive's fullest debate on building the whole Paradox hull in steel rather than plywood, over several days in late December 2003 — first floating a narrower steel-hull-*bottom* idea for combined ballast and impact protection, then pressing the case for an all-steel build. He proposed adjustable, bolt-on or sliding chine runners built from lead-filled steel tube or angle iron; argued that since ballast is unneeded at the boat's ends, a steel floor need only run under the cabin; and floated using that floor to support the mast step, open up the mast bulkhead for more room, or enlarge the cockpit and cabin top. He brought practical fabrication credibility to the idea — he owned a Lincoln 110 wire-feed welder and had prior ornamental-ironwork experience — citing a finding that steel runs roughly 30-40% stronger than wood for its weight, alongside a case for fast priming/painting and spray-foam condensation control. He flagged remote repair as steel's real weak point, proposing epoxy as a stopgap and spare bolts and washers for a mechanical patch. See [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative) — whole-hull steel construction](/design/ideas/aluminum-construction-idea.md#whole-hull-steel-construction-gauge-framing-and-damage-tolerance).

Continuing the exchange over the following days, Jack settled on roughly 3/16 in plate as a target-weight compromise, framing his own priority as matching wood's *loaded* weight and center of gravity rather than bare hull weight, and proposed a fully weldable, gasketed water-tank system. He described his own welding-stress technique — tacking small sections and building up incrementally rather than heating a large area at once, since thin steel dissipates heat fast enough to avoid the stress thick-plate welding develops. He later suggested round thin-wall tubing as an alternative to square tubing for bending the compound-curved chine logs and sheer clamps, and floated building a steel-hulled Paradox's cabin top and deck as a stitch-and-glue plywood/foam/epoxy composite. See [gauge, weight, and the case for steel over wood generally](/design/ideas/aluminum-construction-idea.md#gauge-weight-and-the-case-for-steel-over-wood-generally), [composite wood/steel hulls, and steel's fabrication practicalities](/design/ideas/aluminum-construction-idea.md#composite-woodsteel-hulls-and-steels-fabrication-practicalities), and [fabricating steel chine logs and sheer clamps](/design/ideas/aluminum-construction-idea.md#fabricating-steel-chine-logs-and-sheer-clamps-two-curves-at-once).

The debate grew heated, drawing pushback that questioned Jack's seamanship along with his construction choices; he answered in kind at first, then held to a stated intent to keep the exchange civil, defended his fabrication answers point by point, and framed his own draw to unconventional construction as consistent with Matt Layden's own rule-breaking approach to designing Paradox in the first place. The thread closed after the archive's fullest rebuttal of the whole steel-Paradox idea, which Jack answered point by point before taking the subject private. See [the design-philosophy objection escalates into a personal dispute](/design/ideas/aluminum-construction-idea.md#the-design-philosophy-objection-escalates-into-a-personal-dispute) and [Smith's rebuttal: why a steel Paradox would be "a horrible mistake"](/design/ideas/aluminum-construction-idea.md#smiths-rebuttal-why-a-steel-paradox-would-be-a-horrible-mistake).

Jack settled his own resolution to the whole question: build a stock, plans-as-drawn Paradox in wood first and sail it, continuing toward steel only if he liked the boat enough to justify it — reasoning that the stock hull suits steel less well than a stretched hull would, and that his winter workshop ruled out steel fabrication for the time being despite having welder friends and shop access. No steel-hulled Paradox, by Jack or anyone else, was ever built; the idea remains speculative. See [Jack Gardiner's own resolution: a stock wood Paradox first, steel later](/design/ideas/aluminum-construction-idea.md#jack-gardiners-own-resolution-a-stock-wood-paradox-first-steel-later).

## Construction begins

Jack began building his stock wood Paradox in early January 2004, having picked up 80% of the plywood he needed, and reached "keel laying" some two weeks later — going fully three-dimensional with the hull using stitch-and-glue construction throughout, with no nails or screws — installing both chine logs and sheer clamps, laminated from plywood and epoxy rather than filleted-and-taped, with 50 in-wide 12 oz biaxial cloth on the bottom. His stitch-and-glue method joined panels without wire stitches — clamping or pinning the joint with sheetrock screws instead — using West System epoxy and silica with added wood flour to slow epoxy runs, 4 oz 2 in-wide tape for inside joints, and 12 oz biaxial cloth to tab the bow glue-ups. The milestone drew congratulations from the list.[3][4][5][6][7][8][9][10][11]

With the bottom about to go on, Jack considered several departures from the stock cabin layout — extending the cabin forward, raising the aft hatch area, or fitting a raised dome over the forward stateroom — before a design-tradeoff discussion concluded that lowering the floor, rather than raising the roof, better preserves the mast's ability to stow low for trailering, at some cost to water-tank capacity.[12][13] He ultimately settled on fitting a secure, weathertight hatch over the forward stateroom, reachable from outside the hull, to stow items not often needed.[14]

Jack also opened a "chinerunners" thread prompted by his own rocky-water diving experience, asking whether the chine runners should be added as a sacrificial rub rail over an already-sealed hull bottom rather than built integral with it; the standard build-integral answer is at [chine runner construction — build order](/chine-runners/construction.md#build-order-runners-integral-with-the-bottom-not-a-sacrificial-add-on). Fitting his own vent trunk, water tanks, and bin sides, he found the plans vague on dimensions and asked for detail, confirming he would use West System epoxy in the tanks and flush them with fresh water before relying on the water for drinking on longer trips (see [mast partners and vent trunk](/components/rig/mast.md#vent-trunk-top-dimensions-and-why-exact-measurements-dont-matter-much) and [water tank construction and lining](/construction/water-tank-lining.md)).

By mid-February 2004 Jack had rolled his hull twice — once to apply the bottom, once to bring it back over — with one further roll expected for finishing. On cabin window construction, he favored traditional framed rubber-channel windows over thicker lexan for ease of replacement, judging the stock plan adequate for coastal sailing but wanting a sturdier system for open-ocean use.[15] He began sewing his own sail in 4.5 oz Dacron on a Sailrite LS-1 machine, building Matt Layden's new, taller sail plan (see [rig and sail — building a lugsail at home](/components/rig/index.md#building-a-lugsail-at-home-measuring-yard-deflection-before-cutting)).

Answering a newcomer's build-time and cost questions, Jack judged Paradox not an ideal first build for someone without prior woodworking or building experience, though workable for an experienced amateur, and reported his own build — begun around New Year's — progressing part-time toward an expected launch.[16]

## A late-build status report, and stowing dive gear aboard

By mid-August 2004, Jack reported his build in its finishing stages — cabin on, hatch under way, mast half complete, lower rudder leaded and finished, upper rudder still building. A recreational diver, he collected lost lead sinkers and weight belts while diving locally and used the recovered lead as his own boat's ballast, judging sand a workable but bulkier alternative. He restated his wood-first resolution on the steel question, holding that the stock 14 ft hull suits steel less well than a larger, stretched one would, though he judged steel fabrication itself would go faster than wood.[17]

Answering a fellow diver's question about stowing gear aboard on longer trips, Jack judged a stretched, roughly 19 ft Paradox would suit tank storage better, and discussed the tradeoffs of towing a small inflatable as a dive tender — useful inshore, but one he would not want under tow in rough conditions.[18]

## Build complete, sail hoisted

In early September 2004, Jack reported his Paradox essentially finished — the sail hoisted and photographed at sunset, with only window installation and final ballasting left before launching. He passed the news to a UK Paradox-site maintainer keeping a running worldwide build count (see [references/paradox-uk-website.md](/references/paradox-uk-website.md#a-uk-and-worldwide-build-count)), drawing further congratulations from the list.[19][20]

## The "chinerunners" hydrodynamic-curve thread

Jack took the lead in answering a correspondent's question about whether the chine runners' upward-curving outer edge serves a real hydrodynamic function, suggesting fabrication approaches for a metal bottom and recalling that the runner's outward dimension matters more than its exact shape. See [chine runners — how they work](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md#the-chine-runner-curve-cosmetic-fabrication-choice-or-hydrodynamic-feature).[21]

When the thread turned to whether a steel-hulled Paradox's chine runners could be fabricated in steel, Jack — drawing on a professional fabrication background that included shipyard work near Groton, CT and Quonset Point, RI — argued the job posed no real difficulty at any hull size, and credited modern, affordable tools with putting steel fabrication within a backyard builder's reach. He countered a corrosion/pinhole concern directly: epoxy coating over a weld, a known backyard steel-hull practice, fills pinholes before priming and painting, with ordinary maintenance preventing the feared corrosion. See [chine runner construction — fabricating chine runners in steel](/chine-runners/construction.md#fabricating-chine-runners-in-steel-if-the-whole-hull-is-steel).[22][23]

When the "Steel Paradox?" question recurred in August 2006, Jack summed up his standing view: the topic had been well beaten already, the stock 13'10" length is questionable for a steel build, but a larger, stretched hull would work out better. See [a second 2006 recurrence, briefly answered](/design/ideas/aluminum-construction-idea.md#a-second-2006-recurrence-briefly-answered).[24]

## Launch: "Jill"

Jack launched his stitch-and-glue Paradox on 11 September 2004 at Gaspee Point, Narragansett Bay, Rhode Island, naming her **"Jill"** in his wife's honor — a deliberately chosen 9/11 anniversary date at the historic site where the Providence sloop *Hannah* lured and grounded HMS *Gaspee* in 1772.[25] He was welcomed to Paradox sailing with advice to ballast her up to her full cruising displacement (a 9 in design draft), since the boat "loves the weight."[26] He reported sitting on the waterline short of that full draft on his maiden sail, with the boat feeling a little tender under light ballast.[27] The boat became known on the list as **"Jill Baby,"** was sailed hard in a small-craft advisory the following week — a wedged rudder blade famously freed a stuck trailer tire — and featured in the archive's fullest firsthand account of a Paradox under sail: two days in a steady 20-35 mph wind, loaded with two adults and tool ballast.[28] Full launch narrative and sailing record: [boats/jill.md](/boats/jill.md).

Jack filmed Jill under sail the following summer and circulated the footage to the group; see [boats/jill.md — the "paradox mpeg movie" thread](/boats/jill.md#the-paradox-mpeg-movie-thread-two-days-in-20-35-mph-wind) for the full sailing account the footage documented.[29]

Jill's construction became a frequently cited reference point for later builders: stitch-and-glue with fir chine logs, tabbed and filleted rather than nailed, doubled 2 oz glass over exterior-grade fir plywood, and 12 oz biaxial cloth (rather than the plans' specified woven roving) on the bottom and chine runners — a build that took repeated beach and dock-piling groundings with no damage. See [chine log vs. taped-seam construction — chine-log thickness](/construction/chine-log-vs-taped-seam.md#chine-log-thickness-why-38-mm-rather-than-19-mm), [chine runner construction — Method 5](/chine-runners/construction.md#method-5-cardboard-patterns-and-a-block-built-wall), and [epoxy pre-coating technique](/construction/epoxy-coating-technique.md#a-builders-own-polyester-to-epoxy-conversion-and-the-case-against-poly-on-a-strip-planked-deck).[30][31][32][33]

Over the following years Jack answered fellow builders on rudder pivot-bolt wear (none, after heavy motoring, with dragging over rocks rather than sand judged the real risk to the blade), plywood grades and epoxy's advantage over aging polyester, and bronze fittings as a viable upgrade over the plans' wood-and-epoxy rudder gudgeon wherever skill and budget allow. See [rudder — the pivot-bolt fixing](/components/rudder.md#the-pivot-bolt-fixing-and-its-record-under-grounding-loads), [plywood grade and foam flotation debate — a 2008 recurrence](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#a-2008-recurrence-the-cost-gap-between-a-top-job-and-an-economy-build), and [rudder-bearing-materials.md](/construction/rudder-bearing-materials.md#casting-a-glass-lower-rudder-gudgeon-technique).[34][35][36]

He also developed his own tack-strut/boom-axle pivot-joint fabrication method, drawn on later for the "Enuf" rig. See [boats/enuf.md — the tack strut and boom axle's pivot joint](/boats/enuf.md#the-tack-strut-and-boom-axles-pivot-joint).[37]

## Urging Derek Clark not to leave the list

When a fellow builder announced he was leaving the group after selling his own Paradox ("Enuf"), Jack made his own case for staying: he valued his own Paradox over his 19 ft, 75 hp motorboat, since a dead outboard still leaves the sail to get him home, and judged Paradox worth taking into rough offshore weather over a powerboat for the same reason, with beaching as a further fallback. He judged Paradox's design the product of a great deal of deep thought, whose survivability-in-a-small-space genius only grows more apparent with familiarity, and urged against selling short or burning bridges regardless of which way the decision went.[38] He closed by recounting his own experience of losing his boat's rudder to misfortune and rebuilding past it — having once backed his truck's drive wheels over the rudder while hauling the boat free of a beach, crushing it, and resolving simply to make the replacement better. Full account at [boats/enuf.md](/boats/enuf.md#a-departure-an-outpouring-of-support-and-second-thoughts).[39]
