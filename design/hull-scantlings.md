# Hull scantlings

The [Paradox](/paradox.md) hull is built from marine plywood in the following thicknesses, taken from the building plans:

| Panel | Ply thickness |
|-------|---------------|
| Bottom | **18 mm** |
| Sides | **12 mm** |

These are single panels of the stated thickness, not laminated from thinner sheets: the single-thickness ply is bent onto the hull directly, using screws to pull it down (see [hull planking](/construction/hull-planking.md)).

The plans (Sheet 1) tie fastener spacing to ply thickness: *"Space the nails 40–70 mm according to the ply thickness."* See [fastening and gluing](/construction/fastening-and-gluing.md).

# Plywood bill of materials

A complete Paradox build uses **four thicknesses** of plywood — not just the 18 mm bottom and 12 mm sides. The reported sheet count is:

| Ply thickness | Sheets |
|---------------|--------|
| 6 mm  | 1 |
| 9 mm  | 3 |
| 12 mm | 4 |
| 18 mm | 2 |

That is **10 sheets of plywood in total**, plus "quite a lot of real wood" (solid timber) for framing, the [mast partners](/design/mast-partners.md), and similar members. The plans' own drawing 6 parts layout confirms the same sheet count, read directly off the plan sheet: 3 sheets of 3/8 in (9 mm), 4 sheets of 1/2 in (12 mm), 2 sheets of 3/4 in (18 mm), and 1 sheet of 1/4 in (6 mm).

# Substituting laminated ply for the single-sheet 3/4 in bottom

Instead of the plans' single **3/4 in (≈18 mm)** bottom sheet, a builder can laminate the bottom from **one 1/2 in sheet plus one 1/4 in sheet** if that is the stock on hand. This makes **no real difference** to the boat: the laminate is in fact **stronger** than the specified single 3/4 in sheet. The only recommendation is to orient the panels with their **bad (rougher) faces glued together**, so the finished bottom presents **two good faces** — one inside, one outside — simplifying finishing.

This is a useful precedent for other builders substituting on-hand plywood stock for a single specified thickness (compare the ready availability of various ply thicknesses discussed under the [plywood bill of materials](#plywood-bill-of-materials) above).

# Why hem-fir, and why 1/2 in sides

**Hem-fir** is the specified plywood species, chosen for cost and because it is a renewable, fast-growing farm resource. Being fairly low-density, a thicker panel can be used without a weight penalty, which is why the worst grade — 3-ply 3/8 in hem-fir — is avoided below the waterline in favor of 1/2 in sides. The original Paradox's 10-year service record, built of the specified plywood, stands as evidence the choice holds up in practice. See [hull planking — Matt Layden's own account of the scantling choice](/construction/hull-planking.md#matt-laydens-own-account-of-the-scantling-choice) for the full reasoning, including the case for lighter/heavier alternatives and non-plywood materials.

# Lumber and plywood sourcing

A source for good grades of lumber and plywood at reasonable prices: `hardwoodint.com`.

## A piece of Cornish oak for the stem, and the warping question

Solid stem wood need not be built up from plywood lamination alone: a **2 in thick, 3 ft long piece of green Cornish oak**, curving from 12 in down to nothing, obtained from shipwrights restoring a 200-year-old fishing boat, is a nautically fitting alternative to laminating the stem from plywood finished with an old cabinet oak offcut. Whether green oak of that kind can be cut to the stem's shape and counted on not to warp as it dries remained an open question on record, with using it only for the outer lamination of the stem raised as a fallback if warping proved unavoidable.

## Alder and hemlock for structural and joiner work

Neither **alder** nor **hemlock** suits solid-timber structural or joiner work inside a Paradox. Alder has poor rot resistance, and hemlock is notorious for warping with humidity changes; epoxy saturation can make either usable in a pinch, but a more appropriate species is the better choice. **Fir** and **larch** both perform better — lumberyard stock sold as "hem-fir" in practice contains **little true hemlock**, since the better hemlock grade is sold separately for trim, where it stains well. The Paradox's plywood scantlings are sized specifically to allow "hem-fir" lumberyard stock, chosen as a fast-growing, renewable, replanted farm-forest resource, with no rot found aboard the original Paradox after years' use, built in AC exterior plywood — [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)'s own stated rationale. For solid joinery wood specifically, **Alaska yellow cedar** (soft but pleasant to work) and old-growth **Douglas fir** are recommended, shaped mostly with edge tools rather than sandpaper since its soft summer-growth wood sands away faster than the harder winter growth — with **Edensaw Hardwoods**, whose owners have direct boatbuilding, maintenance, and sailing experience, a good source.
