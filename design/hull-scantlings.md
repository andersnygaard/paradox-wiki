# Hull scantlings

The [Paradox](/paradox.md) hull is built from marine plywood in the following thicknesses, taken from the building plans:

| Panel | Ply thickness |
|-------|---------------|
| Bottom | **18 mm** |
| Sides | **12 mm** |

These are single panels of the stated thickness, not laminated from thinner sheets: the single-thickness ply is bent onto the hull directly, using screws to pull it down (see [hull planking](/construction/hull-planking.md)).

The plans (Sheet 1) tie fastener spacing to ply thickness: *"Space the nails 40–70 mm according to the ply thickness."* See [fastening and gluing](/construction/fastening-and-gluing.md).

# Published design particulars

Paradox's own specifications box — titled "Paradox / 4.2 m Coastal Cruiser / Matt Layden · 7/94 · Drawing #1," first printed in July 1995 and reprinted with the 1996–97 construction series — gives two figures not otherwise in this record: a **prismatic coefficient of 0.58** and a **displacement/length ratio of 270**. The same box confirms LOA 4.2 m (13'10"), LWL 4.1 m (13'5"), beam 1.29 m (4'0½"), draft 23 cm (9"), sail area 9.3 m² (100 ft²), and displacement 640 kg (1,410 lb) to DWL in sailing trim, consistent with the plywood scantlings above and with the [weight and ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md) figures recorded elsewhere.[16]

The stock **13'10"** length has also been attributed in part to a US registration threshold of the era: a small, engine-free craft under 14 ft needed no registration numbers in some states, a margin the design was built to stay under.[17]

# Plywood bill of materials

A complete Paradox build uses **four thicknesses** of plywood — not just the 18 mm bottom and 12 mm sides. The reported sheet count is:

| Ply thickness | Sheets |
|---------------|--------|
| 6 mm  | 1 |
| 9 mm  | 3 |
| 12 mm | 4 |
| 18 mm | 2 |

That is **10 sheets of plywood in total**, plus "quite a lot of real wood" (solid timber) for framing, the [mast partners](/components/rig/mast.md), and similar members. The plans' own drawing 6 parts layout confirms the same sheet count, read directly off the plan sheet: 3 sheets of 3/8 in (9 mm), 4 sheets of 1/2 in (12 mm), 2 sheets of 3/4 in (18 mm), and 1 sheet of 1/4 in (6 mm).

# Substituting laminated ply for the single-sheet 3/4 in bottom

Instead of the plans' single **3/4 in (≈18 mm)** bottom sheet, a builder can laminate the bottom from **one 1/2 in sheet plus one 1/4 in sheet** if that is the stock on hand. This makes **no real difference** to the boat: the laminate is in fact **stronger** than the specified single 3/4 in sheet. The only recommendation is to orient the panels with their **bad (rougher) faces glued together**, so the finished bottom presents **two good faces** — one inside, one outside — simplifying finishing.

This is a useful precedent for other builders substituting on-hand plywood stock for a single specified thickness (compare the ready availability of various ply thicknesses discussed under the [plywood bill of materials](#plywood-bill-of-materials) above).

# Why the bottom is built heavy

The plans' hull bottom is deliberately over-built well beyond a bare structural minimum: **3/4 in plywood** under **three layers of 24 oz fiberglass cloth**, for a total hull-bottom thickness of about **1 in** — heavier than the lighter, Enigma-style car-topping builds discussed under [car-topping conversion ideas](/design/variants/car-topping-conversion-idea.md#a-stripped-paradoxs-own-car-topping-weight-and-roof-rack-loading-technique). Three reasons are given for the extra weight, rather than treating it as pure conservatism: it adds to the boat's **self-righting ability** even without counting the internal lead and water ballast; it gives the bottom enough inherent **stiffness** to resist "oil canning" (the bottom panel flexing in and out) without needing extra internal stiffening structure; and it serves as a straightforward **safety** margin.[9] The same heavy-bottom, self-righting philosophy is shared by twenty or more other shoal-draft, flat-bottomed designs built with higher topsides for added flotation and shelter; several have been field-tested for self-righting behavior, with testers consistently surprised at how well the hulls perform.[10]

# The case for over-construction: field evidence, hazards, and a counter-argument

A hull built light, to a bare structural minimum, is not automatically the better choice: light weight also means a less robust structure, and while modern composite technique can build a strong, light structure, it is more critical to get right during construction, and beaching on a rocky shore plus years of ordinary use show up faster on a light structure than on a stouter one. Other things being equal, a heavier structure is usually the more seaworthy one; a boat that is strong, light, long-lasting, and seaworthy all together is achievable, but generally only at real expense — genuinely light weight is worth paying for when a boat must go on a car roof, tow easily, or race, less so for a cruiser, beach-cruiser, or day-boat.[11]

A heavily-built bottom pays off directly at anchor: not needing to survey a drying-out spot ahead of time for rocks that could hole a lighter hull is itself a form of comfort, over and above outright capability. [Faith](/boats/faith.md) once dried out squarely on top of a submerged concrete mooring sinker with no damage found once she refloated — see [Faith — drying out on a sunken mooring block during the West Country cruise](/boats/faith.md#drying-out-on-a-sunken-mooring-block-during-the-west-country-cruise) for the full account.[12] Less heavily built boats have fared worse under similar circumstances: within a single season, two other boats sustained damage severe enough to be taking on water, one from drying out on a stone and one from striking a just-submerged post, and a separate grounding elsewhere was serious enough that the cruise was abandoned and the boat beached immediately.[13] Old piers, bridge pilings, and rip-rap deposited to control bank erosion are worse hazards than rocks underfoot, especially with waves running, and are more often met along rivers; a boat's own shallow draft can tempt a sailor into places boats have never been before just to see what is there, raising the odds of meeting submerged junk capable of damaging the hull.[14]

Not every builder agrees the extra weight is worth it. A lighter, Paradox-derived design built to a 3/8 in bottom rather than Paradox's 3/4 in displaces over 500 lb less than Paradox for a comparable size — on one view, that lighter displacement could make the lighter hull less, not more, susceptible to damage. See [NOLI's glassing schedule, and a lighter-hull counter-argument](/boats/noli.md#nolis-glassing-schedule-and-a-lighter-hull-counter-argument) for the fuller construction detail and discussion.[15]

# Why hem-fir, and why 1/2 in sides

**Hem-fir** is the specified plywood species, chosen for cost and because it is a renewable, fast-growing farm resource. Being fairly low-density, a thicker panel can be used without a weight penalty, which is why the worst grade — 3-ply 3/8 in hem-fir — is avoided below the waterline in favor of 1/2 in sides. The original Paradox's 10-year service record, built of the specified plywood, stands as evidence the choice holds up in practice. See [hull planking — Matt Layden's own account of the scantling choice](/construction/hull-planking.md#matt-laydens-own-account-of-the-scantling-choice) for the full reasoning, including the case for lighter/heavier alternatives and non-plywood materials, and [hull planking — lumber and plywood sourcing](/construction/hull-planking.md#lumber-and-plywood-sourcing) for solid-wood species alternatives and where to source lumber and plywood.
