# Self-righting and capsize recovery

[Paradox](/paradox.md)'s narrow beam and low, secured ballast (see [beam, freeboard, and stability](/design/beam-and-stability.md) and [weight, ballast, and displacement](/design/weight-and-ballast.md)) combine to give her an unusually high angle of vanishing stability: multiple independent sources put her self-righting point at 160° to 165° of heel, and the design is intended to recover from a full knockdown without any action from the crew.

## Angle of vanishing stability: 160 and 165 degrees, from independent sources

An early figure, dated 1996, put Paradox's self-righting point at about **120 degrees** of heel — a rough estimate, not a settled value.[1] Later figures, expressed in proper naval-architecture terms, run substantially higher. One source gives an **Angle of Vanishing Stability (AVS)** — the heel angle at which the upthrust of the centre of buoyancy and the downthrust of the centre of gravity come into line — of **160 degrees**, alongside an "above average" ballast ratio; on that account the boat self-rights via buoyancy from the coachroof combined with ballast strapped to the floor and the convex curve of both fore-and-aft decks, with wave motion itself helping to flip a capsized hull back upright, and the short, stout mast and low-aspect sail not expected to greatly impede the process.[2]

The designer's own figure, given in a published interview, is **165°** — "better than many modern offshore keel boats" — with the boat's heavily crowned deck making her unlikely to stay inverted (she could, if needed, be righted by hand like a capsized dinghy), and 2 inches of foam insulation providing positive buoyancy if she is ever holed.[3] The same 165° figure is independently corroborated years before that interview and from a different angle: a builder's recollection of having received "a graph, either with the plans or with Don [Elliott]'s articles" shows a correctly ballasted Paradox self-rights from 165 degrees of heel — the earliest record of the figure appearing on a builder's own paperwork (the plans themselves, or the construction articles) rather than only in the later interview, strengthening its provenance as the designer's own stated claim.[4]

Independent naval-architecture software offers a third, technical confirmation: a Paradox modeled from the plans in "Nautilus" boat-design software, with the designer's own stability figures run through it, checks out at **100%** — on the assumption that the boat's Vertical Center of Gravity is as low as believed, roughly 120 mm above the design waterline. The designer likely did not use a computer for the full stability calculation himself; a complete hand calculation of this kind is "a monstrous task" rarely undertaken before computers became available.[5]

A basic stability question — how stable Paradox is, how much ballast she carries, whether she is self-righting, and whether her non-self-draining, enclosed cockpit/cabin causes a problem — has a full answer pointing to two independent sources for the same figure.[6] A stability curve published separately (in the smallboat group) showed Paradox's **range of positive stability is about 160 degrees**, consistent with the 165° figure from the plans/construction articles and the later interview.[7]

## Firsthand knockdown accounts

The fullest firsthand answer comes from "[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)": the stability curve suggests she self-rights from 160°, and in practice she **pops back up quickly from 90°**, while standing on the rail and leaning out only pulled her over about **30°**. Her ballast at the time was 80 kg water plus 100 kg lead, plus anchor and heavy stores, all stowed under the floor. On the non-self-draining question: during normal sailing, most water aboard comes from wet feet; the only time significant water came aboard was when she was **knocked down to 90°** with the hatch open a crack, letting in about 2-3 litres. A Paradox's ability to be bailed from inside without opening the hatch is one of the design's "cunning" features — see [bailing and bilge pumps](/operations/bailing-and-bilge-pumps.md) for the pump installation and how much real use it gets.[8] A photograph on Little Jim's own website documents the boat's stability further.[9]

A plotted righting-moment-versus-heel-angle curve for Paradox (or possibly Little Cruiser) was independently recalled as being on file in the group's photo archive, in a folder labeled "Longyard" — the same stability data discussed above via the published curve and Little Jim's own field measurements.[10][11][12]

The archive's only **quantified field measurement** of a Paradox's righting moment also comes from Little Jim: at a boat show, friends hauled the masthead down while a crew member sat aboard in full sailing trim, reading the load on a 25 lb spring balance, which ran straight off its scale — the pull was "well over 25 pounds," implying a righting moment of **well over 350 ft-lb**, "more than required to undo the wheel nuts on my car." Releasing the masthead also showed how strongly the hull damps its own roll.[13] See [Little Jim — a masthead righting-moment test](/boats/little-jim.md#a-2020-retrospective-the-ayrs-talk) for the fuller firsthand account, including how the same boat's ballast evolved over nearly two decades of ownership. Little Jim's owner also recorded twice being knocked flat with the sail in the water and recovering unaided both times, including once being knocked to 90° in broken surf and coming upright the moment the surf had passed (see [Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md) for that account in full).

## When self-righting could fail

Properly secured ballast makes the odds of a Paradox turning turtle and staying that way effectively nil — though that should not be overstated as an absolute guarantee.[14][15] Plenty of real-world conditions can keep any boat from righting itself again after a capsize: a Paradox's ability to sail in very thin water means an owner may take her into shallows where strong wind and breaking waves, combined with a soft, muddy bottom, could let the mast stick into the bottom and hold the hull inverted — the same "stick in the bottom" failure mode seen on capsized multihulls, normally recoverable only with a powerboat's help or a lucky wave, freed only by a boat's own crew doing something clever. A knockdown with halyards or sheets tangled in a bottom obstruction, combined with a leaking hatch and the resulting hydraulic pressure from a filling hull, is another scenario that could defeat an otherwise strongly self-righting design. In short: a design built to be markedly unstable upside-down and to self-right automatically still cannot fully rule out every combination of circumstances a sailor might eventually encounter.[16]

On the multihull-turtling and thin-water scenarios specifically, the risk is small in practice: a Paradox spills wind and settles rather than staying pinned down the way a multihull's mast can, and rigging tangled on an obstruction is something to cut free rather than fear, after which the hull would most likely come back up, swamped but bailable. Matt Layden's own boat has needed help from a crew member before — Matt himself was dislodged from the hull while helping her right after a knockdown, by grabbing the cabin-top rail — and the mast is built to slide out of its step under load in any case, so entanglement is less likely to hold the boat down than it might first appear. Fitting the foam flotation as designed is the real safeguard, so that if a capsize does happen the hull works with the crew toward self-righting rather than against them; a 72-hour overboard bag with a flare gun is further personal insurance. As a reminder that groundings and mishaps are not unique to a small, shoal-draft boat like Paradox: a grounded 30 ft powerboat once needed a full tide cycle and outside help to get off a sandbar it had gone hard aground on while surrounded by curious onlookers.[17]

## Stability under crew load: standing at the stern

Stability at the extreme stern also came up directly, in the context of a raised hoop cage proposed to keep the mainsheet clear of a mounted outboard (see [rig and sail — a horse fitted to clear the outboard](/components/mainsheet-outboard-fouling.md#a-horse-fitted-to-clear-the-outboard-and-the-mainsheet-angle-physics-behind-it)). A safety concern about the cage design was raised: reentry over the stern past it would force a sailor to hop rather than step down, raising the body's center of gravity at an awkward moment — while a Paradox seemed unlikely to capsize as a result, the sailor doing the hopping might go over.[18] The direct answer: **stability is not a practical problem for a Paradox**, even standing at the stern — Little Jim had carried **three adults standing on the aft deck at once** without any trouble.[19] Pressed on whether that held true **underway**, and not simply at rest,[20] the three-adults test was confirmed to have been conducted with the boat under sail, though without being tied on and without the sail fully hoisted at the time — a lapse in safety practice worth correcting in future.[21]

## Design intent: the deck arch, and a stability chart on file

Matt Layden designed Paradox to be markedly unstable fully upside-down, not merely self-righting from a heel — the deck's pronounced arch follows directly from that design goal, so that the same weather capable of producing a full knockdown is also expected to supply enough wave and swell action to turn the ballasted hull back upright quickly, before more than a trivial amount of water gets below.[22] A note on drawing sheet 9 states plainly that the vents are intended to prevent significant water intrusion in any conditions, including a full 180-degree knockdown.[23] There are no turnbuckles anywhere in the design — the mast is freestanding — and the righting force comes from the ballast called out on the plans together with the buoyancy of the hull's foam insulation; no full 180-degree knockdown has been reported on the list.[23] A plotted stability curve across a range of heel angles, filed as `parastab.jpg`, is held in the group's Files section under "Study Plans."[24]

Whether a closed stern vent could form an airlock through the mast-drain path during a full inversion — and whether that would force water in through the cabin hatch instead, if the hatch were by then underwater — along with how the tiller-opening's position relative to the waterline bears on the outcome, is an open question with no answer on record.[22]
