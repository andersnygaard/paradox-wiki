# Car-topping conversion ideas (speculative)

[Enigma](/matt-layden/enigma.md) already established that a light-enough Paradox-family hull can be carried on a car's roof rack — Matt Layden's own first-ever car-topping trip, in his own words, is recorded at [Cedar Key trials and the first car-topping test](/matt-layden/enigma.md#cedar-key-trials-and-the-first-car-topping-test-in-matt-laydens-own-words).[2] A photograph of the same car-topped boat later circulated on the list as direct proof.[2]

## A bolt-off, invertible cabin-top proposal

A lighter Paradox/Enigma hybrid was proposed with the cabin sides and roof bolting off the hull and inverting to stow inside it for towing — built light enough, with thinner plywood for the sides and roof, to sit on a car's roof rack rather than needing a trailer.[1]

## Refining the fastening and glazing

Follow-up refinements considered how such a bolt-off top might actually be built and sealed. One approach: 6 mm or thinner plywood for the cabin top — scavenged 4 mm ply from eBay auctions was suggested as a ready source — provided the cabin's depth and taper both stayed inside the hull's own, so the roof's spring would let it collapse down inside the hull for stowage; fastening with a dozen 1/4 in bolts and wing nuts, resealed with silicone sealant before each sail. An alternative avoiding bolts altogether: perspex side windows hinging down into the hull, under a birdwatcher-style canvas top.[4]

## A UK builder's own removable hardtop, and a Paradox-sized Enigma

Answering the same proposal, a UK builder (see [Jeff (UK, "doryman_uk")](/people/jeff-doryman.md#a-removable-hardtop-concept-and-noli-restated-as-a-paradox-sized-enigma)) sketched a removable hardtop of his own devising for a car-topped Enigma-type boat — 1.5 mm plywood bent to the shape of Enigma's original folding soft top, glassed inside and out, glazed with 1.5-2 mm clear acrylic — and restated his own lighter Paradox-derivative build as effectively "a PARADOX sized ENIGMA," built to Enigma's own scantlings and, once trimmed down and reduced in draught, close enough to that boat's own weight to be cartoppable herself.[3][5]

## A competing view: a small trailer over car-topping, and sleeping aboard en route

A different practical answer to the same "how do I get this boat to the water" question favored a small trailer over car-topping outright: rather than add the windage of a boat lashed to a small car's roof over a long highway drive, buying a cheap trailer (under $200 from a discount tool chain) or renting a U-Haul-type trailer for the outbound and return legs was judged the better tradeoff — with the further benefit, on a long trip, of a place to sleep in the boat itself, down to a half-joking image of an overnight stay in a Walmart parking lot.[6] See [trailering — tow weight and vehicle capacity](/operations/trailering.md) for the fuller case for a purpose-built trailer.

A fuller practical case against car-topping a stock Paradox specifically followed later: every item of gear — ballast, water, life jackets, ground tackle, and any motor and fuel — would have to come out of the boat and ride inside the car instead, since the hull has to be completely empty to load at all; getting the boat off the roof and back on again at an average launching ramp, without a wheeled dolly or roller set, could prove awkward at many locations; and unloading gear at trip's end, or driving home with the boat still lashed to the roof, both compare poorly with simply sliding a boat off a trailer and winching her back on, gear and all, at the water's edge. The [chine runners](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md#why-heavy-displacement-and-heel-are-essential) sharpen the point for a stock Paradox specifically: they need the hull loaded down enough to sit on her lines to work at all, whereas Enigma's smaller, differently shaped runners need less weight to do the same job — water, gear, and maybe a couple of sandbags are enough.[9]

A car-topped Michalak "Piccup Pram" (about 112 lb), built as a light, go-anywhere boat needing no ramp, reached the same conclusion from separate real-world experience: in practice, getting the small boat underway took just as long as launching a larger, more comfortable boat off a trailer, leaving the car-topper little used — car-topping, on this account, is "overrated," for all the reasons already given above.[10]

## A stripped Paradox's own car-topping weight, and roof-rack loading technique

A stripped Paradox — without ballast or water — was estimated at **550 to 650 lb**, itself too heavy for a car roof; even Enigma was judged a little heavy for the purpose, with a practical car-topping limit put at around **75-80 lb**, or a little more for two smaller boats carried together, by analogy with car-topping a pair of kayaks (each around 40 lb apiece, easily lifted) rather than one boat of 80 lb or more (a real job on its own) — the harder a boat is to load and unload, the less likely a sailor is to actually go out.[11] Most roof racks carry load limits of only around **120-150 lb**, especially on vehicles without rain gutters. A long, narrow hull loads far more easily than a short, wide one for the same weight: demonstrated with a 17 ft, 72 lb royalex canoe fitted with a portage yoke amidships — carried up to the vehicle with the bow rested on the forward rack, then lifted clear of the yoke and the stern swung onto the rear rack, letting one person load a boat considerably longer than the car without ever lifting the whole weight at once. A shorter, lighter 16 ft kevlar canoe (45 lb) saw roughly a hundred times more actual use than the longer, heavier one, precisely because it demanded so much less effort to load — though the heavier canoe's own advantage was that it could run rapids the lighter one would have to be portaged around.[12]

## A flexible-window pop-up roof, tested on an existing boat first

A further variant proposed thick, flexible polythene sheeting for the cabin windows, letting the whole cabin top pop up and collapse flat again for stowage while car-topping — sized to fit a specific compact MPV, built of 1/4 in plywood, and ballasted with plastic water containers (80 L), reusing an existing gaff rig. The idea was floated for trial first on a smaller boat already owned, before being adapted to a car-topped Paradox/Enigma hybrid.[7]

## A later revision: reduced hull dimensions, and what would be dropped to save weight

A further revision cut weight more aggressively: five 1/4 in plywood sheets for the hull, four 20-litre containers for ballast, and a cloth top rather than a hard one — raising the practical question of how often a sailor would actually need to shut the hatch and sail from inside anyway. Hull depth was set at 2 ft, length increased to 15 ft, and beam widened slightly to 5 ft; the sail became a dinghy mainsail with its head cut off to make a standing lug, set on an 11 ft mast of 3 in diameter softwood. Reaching that weight meant giving up several of Paradox's own features to start with: the inside-steered rudder, the inside sail-furling arrangement, and the watertight snorkel-tube vent.[8]
