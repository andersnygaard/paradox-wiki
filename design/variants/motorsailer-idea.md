# Motorsailer Paradox (speculative)

A substantially modified **"motorsailer"** version of the
[Paradox](/paradox.md) has been proposed for inland lakes connected by small
rivers, bridges, and weirs — terrain where motorboats can move freely between
lakes but sailboats are normally stuck in whichever lake their home marina
sits on. **[Nels](/people/nels.md)**, cruising in **Canada**, is the boat
owner behind the idea.

## Nels's proposed modifications

- A **"slipper" stern**, in the style of Bolger's *Sneakeasy*, adding about
  **three feet** to the stern to provide extra buoyancy aft and **enclose a
  small four-stroke outboard**.
- Relocating the **aftmost bulkhead** to where the last deck beam currently
  sits, and extending the **cabin** the same distance aft.
- Possibly raising the **aft cabin roof** about a foot, or fitting a **pram
  hood**, to keep rain out of the cockpit while motoring.
- The extra space freed up would hold a **porta potti** and an extra **house
  battery** in the forward area, intended to rebalance the weight added aft.
- A **pedestal-mounted mast** raised and lowered via a forestay, to clear
  bridges — which would also open the door to a **self-furling jib**, turning
  the boat into a genuine canal cruiser as well as a lake boat.

## Designer fidelity, not engineering, is the objection

The standing objection to the motorsailer proposal — **"why make a sows ear
out of a silk purse?"** — is that [Paradox](/paradox.md) is designed
specifically as a **coastal cruiser**, "a very sturdy and heavy one at that,"
and that a river or canal boat should instead **start from a design built for
that environment**.

The weight-rebalancing plan (porta potti and battery moved forward to offset
the added stern weight) runs into a real engineering subtlety: **static
balance is easy to achieve, but getting the moment of inertia right is much
harder** — simply moving equal weights fore and aft does not guarantee the
boat will behave the same way underway.

CAD renderings are of limited use for previewing a modified Paradox: CAD can
show **any shape** requested, but "that will tell you nothing about how it
will perform" — echoing advice attributed to **Sir Alec Issigonis**, designer
of the original Mini: **"First do the design, then when it is finished put it
on paper."**

The lakes Nels hoped to reach are in fact connected by small rivers with
bridges and weirs "somewhat similar to canals," rather than true canals, and
Paradox was understood, from prior list discussion, to be capable of being
**scaled upward** without becoming a "sow's ear."

## A boat is more than the sum of its parts

**[Paul V.](/people/paul-v.md)** — himself an independent small-boat designer
(see [blue-water suitability](/design/blue-water-suitability.md)) — frames the
broader design-philosophy case against modifying a Paradox to taste:

- **"A boat is far more than the sum of its parts.** When you start changing
  parts of a boat design, you affect the total system." Paradox's appeal is
  precisely that it was **refined over years by a designer who worked out the
  bugs** — a builder who simply follows the plans gets a boat that does what
  it should.
- Paradox is **unique**: an enclosed cabin combined with bilge keels ([chine
  runners](/design/chine-runners-and-windward-performance.md)) in a small
  boat, in a field with very few sharpie-cruiser designs to begin with — so
  anyone wanting a small sharpie cruiser naturally starts from Paradox and
  wants to change it "a little." That a design can simply be altered to taste
  is a **general misunderstanding**: real changes require balancing many
  considerations to reach an "adequate compromise."
- The **pram hood** idea is reasonable on its own, but adding three feet or
  changing the rig would need to be part of a **complete,
  independently-thought-out new design based on Paradox** — a design-process
  objection alongside the designer-fidelity one above.

### Paul's own planned modifications

Smaller, more modest changes than Nels's motorsailer proposal are on record
as planned for an individual Paradox, "hesitate[d]" over even at that scale:

- A **hinged hatch** in place of the stock **sliding hatch**, so it can be
  **dogged down watertight** — for **cold-climate** use.
- **Lead ballast** in place of the **water tank**, given the risk of built-in
  water tanks surviving **freeze/thaw cycles** in the hull, and a preference
  for **bottled water** over tank water — the same underlying
  tanks-vs-ballast tradeoff [David Beard](/people/david-beard.md) worked
  through independently, from a different motivation (see
  [plywood grade and foam flotation debate — David Beard answers Paul's
  water-tank question](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md)).
- Additional **insulation** and attention to **bug protection**, both
  cold/wet-climate details.

The goal was a short trip up to a remote Canadian location (`mandow.ca`),
where most people making the trip use **freighter canoes with big
outboards**, and the accepted bad-weather strategy is to **haul the boat up
on shore, wherever you may be, and wait out the weather** — "perfect for a
Paradox." Designing a boat from scratch for that trip was considered and set
aside: an original design would need **at least three boats** built to work
the bugs out, more time than was available. The verdict on the motorsailer
idea itself: the changes proposed were substantial enough to need a **full,
independent redesign** based on Paradox, not a modification of it.

## A shared cold-climate outlook, and Hudson Bay warnings

A calmer variant of the motorsailer idea settles for enlarging the interior
by adding length **at the centre** of the boat rather than at the stern,
which should actually **improve performance**. [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md)
is ruled out as an alternative (no plans generally available), and Matt
Layden's newer designs are "actually bigger than [needed]" for this
purpose — an oblique, secondhand reference to designs later documented in
full on [microcruising.com](/references/microcruising-website.md) (see
[Matt Layden — designs](/matt-layden/index.md)).

Cold-climate cruising plans (in this case, for **British Columbia**) line up
with the hinged-hatch, insulation, and no-built-in-water-tanks plan above.
An extended, firsthand account of **northern travel experience** —
servicing weather equipment and training radio operators by plane across
Canada's western Hudson Bay coast (Fort Churchill, Chesterfield Inlet,
Repulse Bay — these settlements now use Inuktitut names, as do the
Quebec-side James Bay settlements) — warns that **Hudson Bay is incredibly
dangerous**: extremely shallow, rocky shorelines and significant tides mean a
boat can be **10-20 miles offshore and still not have enough water to float a
canoe**, compounded by wind and a topography so uniformly ice-or-water that
the shoreline itself can be hard to locate. **James Bay**, by comparison,
"may not be too bad."

## Sanitation: "bucket and chuck it"

The stock Paradox has no room for a built-in head, which raises a practical
objection: dumping raw sewage overboard is "not a part of ... being a
responsible person," not to mention illegal.

A **portapotti** aboard a separate motor boat is, in practice, as often as
not emptied overboard clear of the coast for lack of shore emptying
facilities — no different, on this view, from the seals and seabirds, or
from "99% of the UK boating population"; the practice is not illegal in the
UK, and even crowded estuaries such as Salcombe show no sign of pollution
despite regular water-quality checks. The counter-question: what does one do
when there is **no room for a portapotti at all**, as in Paradox itself, and
what about a child who needs the toilet "Now"? The seals-and-seabirds
argument is "interesting," but doesn't obviously justify "pooping on your
front lawn" the way a dog would.

The settled answer, in three words, is **"Bucket & chuck it!"** — matching
the informal practice already attributed to Dave Bolduc and, secondhand, to
Matt Layden himself (see
[head and sanitation arrangements](/operations/head-and-sanitation.md)). The
dog analogy doesn't hold up either way: "You would find it hard to convince
[anyone] that you should let your dog do it." No dedicated Paradox head
fitting is on record; the sanitation question rests in these "bucket and
chuck it" terms.

## A pointer to a longer/lighter Paradox derivative

The lakes in question are connected only by small rivers with bridges and
weirs "somewhat similar to canals," not true canals, and Paradox is
understood, from prior list discussion, to be capable of being **scaled
upward without becoming a sow's ear**. A relevant precedent was already
underway: fellow [Micro Cruising group](/references/micro-cruising-group.md)
member **[Jeff (UK)](/people/jeff-doryman.md)** was, at that time, designing
his own boat **based on Paradox, but longer and lighter** — a from-scratch
16 ft redesign, "[Paramour](/design/variants/paramour-design.md)," rather
than a stock Paradox modification.

## Significance

This is the most detailed statement on record of a **design-fidelity**
objection to modifying Paradox, and the place where one owner's own planned
modifications to a personal Paradox are recorded alongside them. Compare
[aluminum Paradox](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md) for an earlier
speculative-modification thread that drew a similar designer-fidelity
objection, and
[stretched Paradox](/design/variants/stretched-paradox.md) /
[Paramour](/design/variants/paramour-design.md) for the other recorded
enlarged/modified-Paradox proposals — the latter confirmed to be an active
project by Jeff (UK) at the same time as this one.
