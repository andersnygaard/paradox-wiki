# "The Best Open Beachcruiser" (a 1996 design request to Matt Layden)

In November 1996 a design brief was published as an open request addressed to Matt Layden, asking him to draw a second, open companion boat to complement [Paradox](/paradox.md)[1]. Given the completeness of Paradox's features, Matt Layden was judged the best choice for the task[1]. No such design is recorded as ever having been drawn, which makes the brief a documented but unbuilt Layden-directed proposal, and its lasting value is an argument carried out loud about lateral resistance, including a frank case against chine runners for this particular boat[1].

## The premise: a second, open boat alongside Paradox

Paradox, in this reasoning, is a closed coastal cruiser, and a second boat was wanted for onshore use — sailing mostly within a mile of shore, with a few fair-weather distant crossings[1]. The wanted boat did not need to be dry or comfortable, but it did need to be safe, quickly built, light and handy, free of unnecessary complexity, low in freeboard for stepping in and out while carrying gear, fitted with ample dry stowage, and able to sleep two — "it must be stunning to look at"[1].

## A classic-sharpie starting point

The proposed starting point is the "A Sharpie" chapter of John Gardner's *Building Classic Small Craft*, whose profile "seems ideal," though the brief holds that the classic hull would need extensive change to become the "Best"[1]. The classic sharpie's shape, proportions, and cat-ketch rig were to be retained; everything else was to be rethought, with Matt Layden left to complete the design from that point[1]. A companion essay on the classic sharpie's history, printed two issues earlier, frames the same request: it describes the type's three mast steps, which let a mizzen be unshipped for summer sailing and the main be re-stepped amidships for fall and spring, and it proposes "Chine Runner Sharpie" as a name for Paradox itself, on the argument that a sharpie's bottom shape, not its era or rig, is what still qualifies a boat as a sharpie[2].

## The feature list

Several features are set out, each with its own stated reasoning:

- **Easily built** — within a week, a month at the outside; built the classic way, two side panels wrapped around and joined at the ends, but in plywood, epoxy, ring nails, and fibreglass cloth rather than Gardner's own materials; narrow enough in beam that a standard four-foot-wide sheet of plywood does the job; side panels about 18 in high amidships; "not at all fancy, a workboat appearance"[1].
- **Light** — 300 lb all up or less, with weight controlling length; 18 ft might be too large to be handy[1].
- **A "backpack type boat"** — ample water-resistant compartments sized for weekend beachcruises and, occasionally, a week-long one[1].
- **Sleeping area** — 36 in wide, or two 18 in areas[1].
- **Maximum simplicity** — few manufactured parts: a mainsheet tied straight to the sprit boom needing no fasteners, a hole drilled through the deck with a backing block and a stopper knot taking the line's load, a post sufficient to bend a line on; "these things are not new but somehow have been forgotten"[1].
- **Stability provided by the captain** — by skill and judgement, and only a fraction by weight, with all heavy equipment stowed along the sides and movable lead bricks fitted with hand ropes, dropping into strongly built deep pockets so they can be shifted side to side[1].

## Three harder decisions: rig, rudder, lateral resistance

From there the brief allows the decisions become harder, naming the rig, the rudder, and the device for lateral resistance as the three points where Gardner's own book itself struggled[1].

### The rig

A sprit boom is specified in place of Gardner's own boom, sloped up at the foot so the helmsman can see under the sail[1]. Sail track is rejected as expensive and prone to jamming at the wrong moment, and lacing is rejected because it spoils the luff, leaving a sleeved or partly sleeved luff — easily made onto a sail and suited to the boat's overall simplicity — as the remaining choice[1]. An 18 ft spar is set as around the largest size worth dealing with, and the spars are to carry some device letting them be unshipped even with the boat lying on its side[1]. Light air draws a deliberate refusal to enlarge the sail plan: heavier gear aloft, reef points, and added expense are all judged not worth the trouble, and the stated answer to light wind is to strike sail and either wait for it to return or scull ashore[1].

### The rudder

Nothing but a transom-hung rudder is proposed, possibly backed by a sturdy skeg, kept simple enough to unship at the shore, in the spirit of the nicely shaped rudders fitted to some of the smaller 1870s sharpies[1].

### Lateral resistance

The most fully argued section weighs four devices against each other and eliminates three of them. A **centerboard** is set aside as complex to build, prone to jamming, heavy, weakening to the hull because of its long slot, and prone to leaks[1]. **Leeboards** are set aside as needing two built rather than one, noisy, cumbersome, a nuisance to operate, and less effective than the alternatives because they are surface-piercing foils[1]. **[Chine runners](/chine-runners/index.md)** — the feature that would go on to define Paradox itself — are weighed and set aside for this particular boat: their stated disadvantage is that they add some fixed weight to an otherwise lightweight hull, "although it's in the right places," and that they are [time-consuming to build](/chine-runners/construction.md)[1]. That leaves a **weighted daggerboard** as the only remaining choice, its weight there mainly to neutralise the board's own buoyancy, built strong enough to stand on to right the boat after a knockdown, and — like the rudder — unshippable, so the boat can be dragged ashore lighter[1].

## Balancing the rig instead of relocating the board

Gardner conceded there is no foolproof rule for locating a boat's lateral resistance, holding that trial and error works best, and suggested making the board over-long and cutting it back until the balance is right[1]. The brief takes a different approach: fix everything underwater at a good guess, then work out the balance above the water by moving the rig instead[1]. The scheme calls for a fixed step at the bow, several mizzen steps or a way to move the mizzen mast fore and aft, and a thwart set at deck level rather than at Gardner's own height, sliding fore and aft along the deck and pinned or tied off at the coamings[1]. Because the hull has no case to stiffen the bottom, a big keel is fitted on the centreline, on the inside, drilled with a series of holes so the mizzen step can be repositioned[1]. A further step is provided for fresh winds: exactly as on the classic sharpie described in the companion essay, the mizzen is left ashore and the main is re-stepped amidships, with sail trim doing the rest of the work of taming the helm[2][1].

## The daggerboard case

The daggerboard case is specified with a large bolt at each corner and multiple layers of woven roving built into the corners inside the case, so that striking mud or sand either stops the boat outright, if the board is built extra strong, or shears the board itself without damaging the surrounding hull structure[1]. The case is sited off-centre and outboard, for two stated reasons: to preserve the sleeping room, and so it can be switched from port to starboard without tripping over it and going overboard[1]. An off-centre board is favoured on a further argument — struck while sailing with the board on the windward side, the boat should pivot, luff up, and stop — though the brief concedes, in the same breath, that in reality the board would likely stay wherever it was put, moved only when the mood suited[1].

## Status: unbuilt, unanswered

The brief closes on a wager rather than a drawing: "It's my bet that I'm not alone in waiting for plans" for "The Best Open Beachcruiser"[1]. No such plans are recorded as ever having been produced, and no open beachcruiser answering this brief is documented in the [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/index.md) record; it stands as an unbuilt, unanswered proposal, distinct from the built and speculative hull ideas gathered under [design variants](/design/variants/index.md). The fuller series in which it appeared is indexed at [the "Building Paradox" MAIB series](/references/building-paradox-series.md).
