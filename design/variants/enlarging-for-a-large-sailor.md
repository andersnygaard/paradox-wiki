# Enlarging Paradox for a tall/heavy sailor ("Paradox size")

A stock [Paradox](/paradox.md) can be a tight fit for a large sailor, and builders above about 6 ft and 200 lb have had to decide how much to enlarge the design to fit their frame. Where the earlier ["Stretch Paradox"/"Wearing the Boat"](/design/variants/stretched-paradox.md) discussions were mostly driven by wanting a **faster, larger** boat or a few extra inches of headroom, sizing the boat to a large body is a distinct question, revisiting the same scaling-rule territory from a different angle: how far can the design be enlarged, and by what method, before the build becomes impractical?

## Scale length only, not beam or freeboard

One approach is to **increase length only** and leave beam and freeboard alone. Under this approach mass grows **linearly** with length rather than as the **cube** of the overall dimensions (as it would under uniform 3-D scaling), so a length-only stretch is far less punishing on weight — at the cost of producing what would likely be a **faster, straighter-tracking** boat rather than a true enlargement in every dimension.

This differs from the standing advice recorded in [stretched Paradox](/design/variants/stretched-paradox.md), that scaling length **without** beam "would alter the overall qualities of the design and come out with something new."

## The 10% enlargement, and why it's a practical ceiling

A 6'3", 210 lb build (partway through its own 10%-upsized "Paradox 1.1" — see [stretched Paradox](/design/variants/stretched-paradox.md)) settled on a **10% enlargement**, judging anything larger problematic for two concrete reasons:

- Beyond 10%, the **side panels** need an **additional scarf** to reach the required length.
- The **bottom panel's width** (already a tight fit at 100% and 110% scale — see [stretched Paradox](/design/variants/stretched-paradox.md)) would **exceed 4 ft** once the chines are added, at any enlargement beyond 10%, forcing more scarf joints and more wasted plywood.

Short of those caveats, a 10% enlargement is straightforward — a practical ceiling drawn from a build already partway through execution, rather than a theoretical estimate.

## Fitting a couple aboard a stock or slightly enlarged Paradox

A recurring question from prospective builders planning to sail two-up: how a Paradox handles a couple of noticeably different sizes, both underway and sleeping aboard, given that most photos and reports on record show the boat sailed solo. A concrete case involved a 5'11", ~200 lb sailor and a wife just under 5 ft tall — see [double-layer plywood for an enlarged Paradox and testing an unconventional mast wood](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#a-double-layer-plywood-technique-for-an-enlarged-paradox-and-testing-an-unconventional-mast-wood) for the same build's plywood and mast-wood questions. See also [modifying bulkhead 3 to gain room for sleeping two](/operations/crew-size-and-fit.md#modifying-bulkhead-3-to-gain-room-for-sleeping-two) for a structural approach to the same two-up sleeping problem, and [pilot weight, height, and stability at stock length](/operations/crew-size-and-fit.md#pilot-weight-height-and-stability-at-stock-length) for Matt Layden's own two-up sailing and the load-splitting/counterbalancing technique that made it work in practice.

## Paramour: a ground-up redesign for a bigger sailor

Rather than scaling stock Paradox further, a builder in the UK drew a **new design, "Paramour,"** as a bigger boat for a bigger sailor. See [Paramour (a stretched-Paradox redesign)](/design/variants/paramour-design.md) for the full specification — overall length 16 ft, a **flatter fore-and-aft hull run** than stock Paradox, the same rig and rudder, computed via the PLYBOATS design program. A practical method for checking whether a hull will fit a given sailor: sit on the floor and have someone measure from the floor to the top of your head.

## Flush cabin sides, as an unexplored alternative

A separate idea approaches interior room from a different angle than lengthening: fairing the cabin sides flush with the hull sides rather than stepping them in from the sheer, on the reasoning that it would open up the interior and increase the righting effect of a knockdown by carrying more buoyancy further outboard. No build has followed this approach, and no reply has weighed its structural or stability tradeoffs against the stock stepped-in cabin.

The practical case for keeping the stock stepped-in side decks: the recessed side decks are useful stowage, giving the **yuloh** and the **lowered mast/spars** a place to lie on deck when not in use (see [yuloh and stowed-rig retention](/components/yuloh.md)) — flushing the cabin sides to the hull would remove that space. One further, untested thought followed: whether a **sprit rig**, needing no boom or yard to stow, would free a flush-sided design from needing on-deck spar storage at all, or whether brackets could instead be fitted to the cabin roof for the purpose.

Paradox's stock 13'10" length has been attributed in part to a US "14 ft rule": a small craft **under 14 ft** that is **not powered by an engine** (oars, paddles, or a Chinese yuloh/"yuhlo" are fine) does **not need to carry registration numbers and letters** on its hull. Registration requirements otherwise vary by US state regardless of the 14 ft threshold. No specific US statute or state is cited for the rule in the source discussion, so it should be read as a builder's understanding rather than a verified legal citation — and, lacking a UK equivalent, it is what let Paramour's designer size her up to 16 ft.

## A September 2007 recurrence: routed to the FAQ, then to Enigma

A prospective builder at 6 ft, 225 lb asked whether Paradox had ever been stretched a few feet longer and wider to fit a bigger sailor, worried about crossing his legs in the stock 15-footer; the reply pointed him to the group's Files section and discussion archive rather than repeating the scaling rules above, and a second reply suggested Matt Layden's own [Enigma](/matt-layden/enigma.md) instead, already built as a larger, 15 ft version by another prototyping builder. See [building plans — a second September 2007 recurrence](/references/building-plans.md#a-second-september-2007-recurrence-and-a-pointer-to-enigma-for-a-larger-sailor) for the full thread.[15]

## A recommended alternative for a larger sailor: Michalak's Caroline, and the case against modifying Paradox to fit

Rather than reworking Paradox's cabin, the standing recommendation for a sailor who simply does not fit the stock boat — considerably taller, heavier, or less agile than the design's own reference build — is to choose a different, more spacious existing design instead of modifying this one. Jim Michalak's **"Caroline,"** his first design aimed at off-shore coastal cruising, has been suggested for exactly this case, and already has at least one Paradox-list member's plans on order.[16]

The physical case for treating fit as a hard limit rather than something to design around: the stock boat suits a sailor of the designer's own build well, fitting "like a comfortable slipper," while a taller, fatter, or less agile sailor will find her "like a bunion in an iron shoe" — such a sailor is better served looking for a boat with Paradox's own strengths but more habitable cabin space, with Michalak's Caroline offered as one candidate, rather than by modifying Paradox itself.[17] The same reasoning extends to auxiliary propulsion: Paradox's minimal, largely engine-free approach to close-quarters maneuvering (see [auxiliary outboard power](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-port-quarter-honda-23-mounting-and-a-port-side-electric-outboards-setup-time) and [sculling and yuloh technique](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md)) suits the sailor she was drawn for but is a real practical shortcoming for anyone who needs reliable powered maneuvering in a mooring or narrow waterway.[17]

A different critique of the same recommendation held that Caroline was not in Paradox's class for seaworthiness and handiness in windy conditions, and pressed the underlying question harder: does any design at all offer Paradox's specific combination of sail-from-inside steering, self-righting stability, and shoal-draft cruising in a small package — a want sharpened by an anticipated need for a small auxiliary motor, given limited available cruising time (see [auxiliary outboard power — whether fitting a motor is worth it at all](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#whether-fitting-a-motor-is-worth-it-at-all)).[29] The closest match on record turned out not to be a rival design at all but Matt Layden's own other small cruisers: [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md), built in 1988 and still sailed by her owners two decades on, and the newer, lighter [Enigma](/matt-layden/enigma.md) family, all sharing the same chine-runner, self-righting approach in what was described as a developmental progression — alongside a UK builder's own Paradox-sized Enigma derivative then under construction (see [Jeff (UK)](/people/jeff-doryman.md#a-removable-hardtop-concept-and-noli-restated-as-a-paradox-sized-enigma)).[30]

## A tall sailor's hesitation, and reassurance that fit-driven scaling is welcome

A large sailor who had held stock Paradox plans for several years, put off starting a build by negative reaction on the list to modifying the original design, asked what would be wrong with a Paradox built 10-20% larger while keeping every internal detail of the original — having found no other design with Paradox's own combination of features.[18] The concern is misplaced: scaling a design to fit a sailor's own size is a different matter from customizing its interior, sail, or propulsion system, and Paradox's own designer found what worked for his own body and sailing style — a builder who plans to sail and live aboard the way the designer did is well served following the plans as closely as possible, but adapting the boat to a different body is not itself a departure from that design intent.[19]

## A worked 20% scale-up: dimensions, sail area, and displacement

A ready-worked scale-up already existed in the group's Files section by 2008: multiplying every major dimension by 1.2 (a 20% increase) gives an LOA of 16'6.4" (5.04 m), LWL of 16'1.7" (4.92 m), beam of 4'11.3" (1.48 m), and draft of 10.8" (0.276 m), for a sail area of 168 sq ft, a displacement of 2,321 lb, a prismatic coefficient of .56, and a displacement/length ratio of 261. The cabin's living/sleeping area grows by only 15 in in length and 10 in in width at this scale — enough to matter but not enough to force a new interior layout — while the stock scantlings remain usable throughout except for the spars, which scale by the same 1.2 factor; leaving the water supply and stores lockers at their stock size yields a 7.5 in gain in headroom under the hatch. Bulkhead-to-bulkhead spacing grows by only 5.5-7.5 in at each bay, too small an increase on its own to justify a full redesign. The hardest part of the calculation was working out the new sail area (a 68% increase) and displacement (a 60% increase) needed to put the boat on her new lines while preserving the stock sail-area-to-displacement ratio. A smaller step is worked out the same way: at a 1.16 scale factor (a 16 ft boat), displacement comes to 2,075 lb and sail area to 148 sq ft.[20]

## Matt Layden's approval, and why no scaled-up Paradox has yet been built

Matt Layden has given his approval in principle to a 20% scale-up of this kind, on the reasoning that no new set of drawings is needed — every dimension required is already on the stock plans as drawn.[20][21] Despite the idea recurring on the mailing list roughly forty times over the years, no one has yet actually built a scaled-up Paradox to these figures and reported the results back to the group.[21]

## Naming an enlarged Paradox, and speculation about the designer's own workload

A larger Paradox built to this scale would need a name distinct from the stock boat to avoid confusion — "ParadoxPlus," "ParadoxToo," and "ParadoxForTwo" have all been floated,[22] as has "Paradox Max,"[23] though none has been adopted for an actual build. One guess, offered as speculation rather than established fact, was that Matt Layden had not himself drawn up and marketed an official larger set of plans — for this scale-up or for Enigma — because he was fully occupied building and sea-trialing his own next boat for a race he called the "Around in Ten Race," with its start still roughly a year away; an alternative guess was that he judged the market too small to be worth his time.[22]

## Correcting the 20% figures for plywood curvature, and a beam-widening alternative

The worked 20% figures above measure length along the plywood panel's straight-line dimension; accounting for the panel's actual curvature once bent into the hull brings the true overall length down to roughly 15'9" rather than a flat 16'6".[23] A further alternative to scaling beam uniformly with length was floated for two-up comfort: 4 ft at the chine and 5 ft at the deck, reasoned to make the boat noticeably more comfortable for two users without otherwise changing the 20% scale-up.[23] The same reasoning was applied to Matt Layden's own [Enigma 460](/matt-layden/enigma.md#the-enigma-family-the-360-and-the-plywood-cabin-460): judged close to the right overall size already but too narrow in the beam, widening her to 5 ft and taking the deckhouse to full width was suggested as a way to improve her interior in the same spirit.[23]

## Reference sailor heights and weights, and a two-up alternative: the Bolger Micro

A large sailor comparing a 10% and a 20% enlargement judged the 10% step would build a boat about [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md)'s own size, and asked for reference height-and-weight figures from other Paradox owners, giving his own as 6'4" and 270 lb.[24] Photos in the group's Interior files album were pointed to as the visual answer, alongside a concrete figure: [Glen Maxwell](/people/glen-maxwell.md), who sails Paradox solo only, is 5'9" and 185 lb.[25] A sailor of about the same size as Glen Maxwell, but who sails with a crew rather than solo, judged the stock boat too small for that purpose and chose a **Bolger Micro** instead, which has two 7 ft berths.[25] Weighing the same tradeoff, the sailor who raised the original height-and-weight question was separately working out buying a completed secondhand Micro nearby — if that purchase went through, the plan was to keep a Paradox for sailing solo and use the Micro whenever taking others along.[26] Two more owners gave their own figures for comparison: one at 5'8" and 149 lb, for whom the stock boat is "absolutely right"; another, sailing [Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md), at 5'6" and 180 lb, for whom Paradox "fits...like a glove," though squeezing right to the fit's limit is best done in quiet conditions.[27][28]

## A bowsprit, and how state registration rules treat it

A gaff-sloop rig with a bowsprit was floated purely speculatively, illustrated with a photo of an unrelated Brewer-designed sloop's bowsprit, without further development or a build. The idea it prompted with more substance was a hinged bow anchor roller, to keep the anchor from scratching the bow when brought back aboard — but a roller of that kind pushes the boat's effective length-overall out beyond 14 ft, which matters directly for the 14 ft registration threshold above: at her stock **13'10"** and with no engine, Paradox needs no registration in Florida, Alabama, or Texas, a margin an anchor roller or bowsprit could easily erase. Texas specifically excludes bowsprits and bumkins from how a hull's registered length is measured, so a Texas-registered Paradox could gain a bowsprit or anchor roller without crossing into the 14 ft threshold — a state-specific answer to the general rule stated above, rather than a universal one.

## A counterpoint: Paradox as arguably too seaworthy for two-up sailing, and a further design pointer

A further design pointed to as possibly meeting the shoal-draft requirement, though not evaluated against Paradox's other criteria, was a small beachable dinghy able to be left leaning on its own edge as the tide goes out without damage, and anchored with the crew able to wade ashore.[31]

A different answer to the same question held that Paradox's real problem may be being too seaworthy rather than not seaworthy enough for two-up cruising: a hull that stays safe and comfortable in wind and rough water can tempt a sailor into conditions that would otherwise be given a wide berth, a risk that changes character once a companion who did not choose to take it is also aboard. Twenty years running a small motorboat as a family day-boat — tolerant of rough water but prone to rolling badly past about a force 4 — produced the opposite habit, since discomfort rather than danger kept the boat off open water in poor conditions; sailing solo, several outings have instead gone ahead specifically because the boat was trusted to handle worse than it in fact met, trips that would not have been risked sailing two-up. A sailor choosing a design for two should weigh the weather actually expected as a criterion of its own — for ordinary coastal cruising with the forecast properly read, Paradox's blue-water capability can be overkill, and being genuinely caught out by a sudden change in conditions after five years of coastal sailing is rare enough to suggest inadequate homework rather than bad luck.[32]

A concrete illustration recalled from a Solent crossing: two 40 ft yachts that had crossed from Lymington in storm-force winds and lumpy seas — one of three such yachts having turned back partway, judging it too rough — arrived at Cowes only about a quarter of an hour ahead of a Paradox that had come from Poole the same day, to the visible surprise of their crews. Sailing that particular crossing solo made it an acceptable risk; sailing it with someone else aboard, it would not have been attempted.[33][34]
