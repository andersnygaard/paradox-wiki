# Beam, freeboard, and stability

Compared to most boats, sharpies are narrow for their length, and [Paradox](/paradox.md) follows the type: her beam is just under 48 inches, not counting the chine runners.[1] The first, intuitive assumption anyone makes about a hull this narrow is that a wider boat would be a better boat overall, and Paradox's whole beam-and-freeboard question is really an argument against that assumption — written out from first principles, checked against a named stability text, and afterward confirmed by an actual stability curve obtained from the boat's own designer.[1]

## The case against added beam

Some hulls add beam specifically to buy upright stability, but that beam is not free: it increases wetted surface, which in turn demands more sail power to drive the hull through the water, and once heeled over a wide hull presents more of its bottom area to wind and wave than a narrow one does.[1] The real cost shows up at the extreme end of the scale: a wide, low-freeboard hull, once knocked onto her beam ends, goes over quickly, and the same beam that resisted heeling in the first place then makes her very hard to right.[1] A narrow, high-sided boat may have to reduce sail earlier than a beamier boat of the same length, but she is both harder to put on her beam ends to begin with, and more likely to right herself, and sooner, if she does go over.[1]

## A design text on slim hulls and high freeboard

John Teale's *How to Design a Boat* is cited directly on both points. On beam: "in the last analysis and a total knock-down the slimmer vessel is likely to right herself, where as the beamier boat may remain capsized and upside down."[1] On freeboard: "high freeboard, however undesirable for windage and whether caused by high top sides or high deck work, does marvels for maximum righting moments and range of stability."[1]

## Checked against the designer's own stability curve

The beam-and-freeboard analysis was worked out on paper first, and only afterward checked against the boat itself: a stability curve for Paradox was subsequently obtained from the designer, and it confirmed the analysis — comparing the two diagrams showed the boat to be actually more stable than expected going in.[1] The accompanying beam-comparison diagram survives only as a badly OCR-garbled archive scan; its own caption text cannot be transcribed reliably, so none of the diagram's figures are quotable here.[1]

## Two consequences already built into the design

Beam and freeboard are not the only place this stability logic shows up. Because Paradox is sailed from a closed cabin rather than an open cockpit, a knockdown on the beam ends does not throw the crew into the water the way a knockdown can in a boat with an open cockpit.[2] And because Paradox is a heavy boat for her length, she sits deep in the water, so the hull side itself counts as lateral resistance alongside the chine runners — daggerboards, centerboards, and fin keels are not the only surfaces that provide it.[3] See [chine runners — how they work](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md) for the fuller lateral-resistance picture, [hull scantlings](/design/hull-scantlings.md) for the plywood thicknesses behind that weight, and [heavy-weather tactics](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md) for a real-world heel-angle account from inside the closed cabin.

## Size, handling, and the coastal-cruiser definition

Beam and freeboard sit inside a larger argument about size generally. Size matters mainly for handling: small enough to be trailered easily, launched and retrieved without fuss, and squeezed into a crowded anchorage; small enough to tie up at a dinghy dock rather than swim ashore or inflate and deflate a tender; small enough to be beachable and to go up narrow rivers and inlets; and small enough that the mast can be lowered by one person.[4]

A coastal cruiser is defined against an offshore cruiser on exactly this size question. An offshore boat crosses oceans and spends many days or weeks at sea; it cannot estimate the conditions it will meet, and once it is a few days out it cannot exit those conditions, so it has to be built to deal with the worst, and provisioned without any chance of restocking for long stretches.[4] A coastal cruiser sails along or within reach of a near shore; passages of 24 to 48 hours allow predictable weather forecasts, so the chance of being caught out in extreme weather can be safely reduced.[4] A coastal cruiser therefore does not need to be large — "in fact it's better to be small."[4] See [blue-water suitability](/design/blue-water-suitability.md) for the fuller coastal-vs-offshore classification debate this definition feeds into.

## Against "size determines seaworthiness"

A separate opinion holds that only large boats are safe, and that size alone determines seaworthiness — a claim disproven many times over by small boats that have demonstrated real seaworthiness. Yankee Girl, Tinkerbelle, Mermaid, and Chidiock Tichborne are named as proof, and the most extreme case on record is Sergio Testa's Acrohc Australis.[4] The honest concession runs the other way: a small boat is without question more uncomfortable, slower, and less weatherly than a larger one — but not, on that evidence, unsafe.[4]

## The 1996 self-righting estimate

An early figure put Paradox's self-righting point at about 120 degrees of heel.[4] That figure is a dated 1996 estimate, not a settled value: later community figures for the same boat run higher — see [weight, ballast, and displacement](/design/weight-and-ballast.md) for the fuller, later record on self-righting angle.
