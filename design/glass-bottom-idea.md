# Glass-bottom Paradox (speculative)

Sailing over shallow water full of coral and tropical fish raises the idea of a "glass-bottom boat": making part of the Paradox's **flat plywood bottom transparent**, so the crew could view marine life from inside the enclosed cabin.

## Obstacles to a transparent bottom

- The Paradox's built-in **storage compartments and water tanks** (see [interior bins and hinged floor](/components/interior.md) and [water tank construction and lining](/construction/water-tank-lining.md)) sit over most of the bottom and cannot be relocated without redesigning the whole boat.
- A partial solution: make only the bottom **under the storage compartments** transparent, moving stowed items and floorboards aside when viewing — or, more ambitiously, make the compartment covers and tank tops transparent too, giving visibility even with the floorboards in place (at the cost of a collision risk from watching fish instead of watching where the boat is going).
- A window also needs to survive a boat that is deliberately **beached and dried out** (see [chine runner construction](/chine-runners/construction.md) and [chine runners](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md) for how hard the bottom and chine runners work in shallow water), raising the question of whether plexiglass or thick plastic is structurally strong enough, and whether it scratches badly when the boat is pulled up on a beach.
- An alternative is a separate glass-bottom **dinghy**, towed instead of modifying the Paradox itself.

## Prior art: bottom windows in other small boats

Bottom windows are "nothing new" in small-boat building. Two boats were fitted with **thick Lexan** bottom windows: the 32 ft cruising sharpie **"Hog Fish Lips"** and a power sharpie called **"Tropicstar."** Both windows scratched over time but remained easy to see through — likely because water fills in fine scratches optically.

A **Chesapeake Light Craft** shop-tips article, *"The Glass Bottomed Mill Creek,"* is a useful how-to starting point. Building a similar window into a skiff (positioned beside a central skeg for scratch protection) can leave the window outlasting the rest of the boat, which eventually rots out around it.

## Practical recommendations for a Paradox window

- Keep the window **under 1 square foot** and add **fasteners**, not just adhesive/bedding — large enough to be useful without giving up much storage space, and small enough to stay structurally modest.
- A **1 sq ft Lexan window** would also double as **interior lighting** whenever the boat sits in shallow water.
- **3/8 to 1/2 in thick plastic** is probably plenty strong; **polycarbonate** (drillable and tappable fairly easily) is probably stronger than the plywood bottom it would replace.
- **Rub strips** — protective strips around the window edge, as used on the Mill Creek — are a good approach to scratch resistance.
- Scratches that do get bad can simply be **sanded out**.
- An open skiff may need a light-blocking cone-shaped box built over the window to see through it in daylight, but this problem likely would not arise **inside the enclosed cabin** of a Paradox, where ambient light is already controlled.
- For further reading on window-glazing prior art generally, a Google Groups search of `rec.boats.building` for "Glass bottom" turns up other discussion of glass-bottom boats.

This remains a speculative idea rather than a build report — no builder is recorded as having actually built a glass-bottom Paradox — but between the named prior-art boats, the referenced how-to article, and concrete material/thickness recommendations, a future builder has a genuine starting point. For the Lexan-versus-Perspex/acrylic tradeoff itself (toughness, scratch resistance, working properties, and sourcing), see [window glazing materials](/construction/window-glazing-materials.md).
