# Junk rig

A junk rig is an alternative to the Paradox's stock standing-lug rig, occasionally discussed as an option for the boat. On the list the question stayed theoretical — sail plans were drawn and argued over, but no conversion was carried through. **Two** junk-rigged Paradoxes were built and sailed outside the list era — a stock-lug conversion and a purpose-designed cambered rig on a scaled-up hull — and their verdicts are recorded at [junk rigs actually built and sailed](#junk-rigs-actually-built-and-sailed) below.

## Why the stock rig is judged hard to beat with a Chinese lug

The Paradox's stock rig already reefs easily, is controlled through a gybe, and points well to windward, so a Chinese lug rig would need compelling advantages to be worth the change.[1] Several practical objections to fitting one stand on the record: a Chinese lug typically carries proportionally more sail forward of the mast, which would likely require moving the mast aft to keep the boat in balance; it needs sheets running aft from the battens, and since the stock sail already extends behind the transom, a lug conversion would need either a bumkin or a further-forward center of effort, upsetting the design's already near-perfect balance; and a Chinese lug's boom, yard, and sail would either stow awkwardly on the side deck (as the stock sail does) or swing loose overhead on the topping lift, in the way when standing.[1] A Chinese lug's luff typically sits anywhere from a few percent to about 20% forward of the mast, and more elaborate versions use a yard parrel and a luff parrel to shift the sail fore-and-aft for balance; the usual sheeting arrangement uses two mainsheets (one to windward, one to leeward) attached to the boom about 20% in from its aft end, without needing to move the sail's center of effort or the mast.[2]

## Lazy jacks are incompatible with the standing lug's rotating boom

A Hasler-type junk rig's lazy jacks run through eyes on the underside of the boom — an arrangement that cannot work on Matt Layden's standing lug, since that boom rotates for reefing.[3] The standing lug's center of effort also sits much lower than a Hasler-type junk's, and a junk rig is considerably heavier aloft, inducing heel more readily than the lug sail; the rolling-reefing lug's core advantage over a junk conversion is its simplicity.[3] After roughly 15 years of trial and error refining the Paradox, further design improvement is considered difficult; the only changes one longtime builder made were internal — reworking the under-floor storage so removable, slidable floor panels give clear access to the ballast areas from a raised working seat without needing hinges, whether the main hatch is open or closed.[3]

## A worked Hasler/McLeod junk-rig sail plan (April 2007), and why higher aspect ratio doesn't work

A second, independently developed Hasler/McLeod junk-rig sail plan for the Paradox — 105 sq ft, drawn up by a sailor with prior experience designing and building junk rigs for other small boats — was uploaded to the group's files section for comment.[4][5] The design choice was framed as a genuine test of the "sailing system" idea behind the stock rig: any boat with any rig is in some sense a sailing system, so a rig substitution is a legitimate variation to explore rather than a departure from the design's essential character, in the same way established yacht designers have long accommodated personalization of their own stock designs.[5]

Converting to a junk rig would remove the sail's dependence on the mainsail's own specific rolling-boom furling geometry, in principle simplifying some of the stock rig's construction — though a higher-aspect-ratio version was tried first and rejected: raising the aspect ratio moved the sail's center of effort forward of the hull's center of lateral resistance, and the rig could not be adjusted back from the lower-aspect-ratio version actually built without inducing substantial weather helm. How far aft Matt Layden placed the stock sail's own center of effort, by comparison, was noted as a striking design choice once seen up close.[7][8]

A junk rig on Paradox should carry a higher aspect ratio than usual specifically to keep the battens' after ends inboard of the transom.[6] Firsthand experience converting an Albin Vega to junk rig and cruising it for years found only a small loss of windward performance against the fore-and-aft rig it replaced, with a best noon-to-noon run of 173 nautical miles ahead of a 25-knot nor'easter; despite that, a lug-to-junk conversion was judged not worth making on a Paradox specifically, given the extra spar weight aloft, the extra line needed to manage a junk's sail, and the lug's own advantage of easy, tidy stowage on the side deck.[6]

Sheeting a junk sail this far aft on a Paradox would need a bumpkin pole, since the sail already extends well behind the transom because of the chine runners and the rudder's large role in balance; an experimental forward extension of the chine runners was once tried on Matt Layden's own original boat, but any windward-performance improvement from it proved too small to measure.[9][11] A centerboard added well forward, in the manner of Little Cruiser, or a leeboard, was floated as a way to make a junk rig easier to balance on Paradox, at the cost of moving further from the design's essential simplicity.[9] A straight-sided, square-headed junk sail — close to the shape of the stock lug but with more area at the top rear corner — was also floated as a way to let the mast keep its forward rake while holding the center of effort in roughly the same place; such sails have long been used on Chinese junks, including tall rectangular versions illustrated by G. Worcester from Yangtze-river junks, built for a river where wind often does not reach the water's surface.[10][13]

Attaching a boomkin to the rudder, as done on other boats, was one option considered for sheeting a Paradox junk rig, though a satisfactory solution was not settled on; fitting anything to the transom itself risks fouling the yuloh.[12] A junk rig's familiarity and ease of tuning, learned from cruising a junk-rigged boat for years, can outweigh an unfamiliar standard rig's own performance for a builder already comfortable with the Chinese lug.[12]

A light, purpose-built **arch** spanning the transom is a further boomkin alternative: with a junk rig's low sheet loads, even a tubing arch should be strong enough, drawing on a mast-gallows installation of stainless pipe set into stainless lifeline base receivers that had held up well on an unrelated boat.[14][15] The arch idea also keeps the sheeting clear of the raised rudder, an advantage over mounting a boomkin on the rudder itself.[15]

## A daggerboard-forward CLR experiment, and why Paradox's rudder was built large

A 10 ft sailing dinghy built with its daggerboard moved forward, to the bow, to lengthen the center of lateral resistance and let the mast move forward for a cat rig, tracked as straight as an arrow under that arrangement — with an unplanned side effect: thrown hard over, the forward daggerboard acted as a pivot point, stalling the bow while the stern swung around.[17] The same builder expected the junk rig's individual sail panels would need some camber built in to sail efficiently, beyond the flat panels of his own first attempt.[17]

Separately, Matt Layden's own reasoning for building Paradox's rudder as large as it is was relayed firsthand: sailing a Little Cruiser at Cedar Key and discussing the pros and cons of rudder size directly with Matt Layden, the rudder was placed well aft as part of the boat's lateral-resistance equation, and Matt held the Paradox hull specifically needed the larger rudder for increased lateral resistance.[16] This is a second, independent account of the same rudder-size rationale documented at [rudder and chine experiments — why the rudder was built large, and the trade-offs of its size](/components/rudder.md#why-the-rudder-was-built-large-and-the-trade-offs-of-its-size). The same exchange noted how far aft Matt Layden set the stock sail's own center of effort, cautioning that raising a junk conversion's aspect ratio risks moving the sail's center of effort forward of the hull's center of lateral resistance and inducing weather helm the rig cannot then be adjusted back out of.[16]

## Junk rigs actually built and sailed

Everything above is argument in advance of a boat. **Two** junk-rigged Paradoxes were in fact built and sailed — both outside the list era, and by two opposite routes. [Planktos](/boats/planktos.md) took the stock lug and **converted** it; [THU](/boats/thu.md) was given a junk rig **designed from scratch** on a scaled-up hull. Between them they turn the page's long theoretical argument into two data points — but only the second records how the balance and sheeting problems were actually handled.

### Planktos: the stock lug converted (outcome, not method)

One Paradox was **built to the stock lug rig with roller furling and then changed to a junk rig** — a Western Australian build of 2013–2016, cruised on a shakedown passage to Shark Bay and presented to a home-boatbuilding club in April 2021.[18] It is the only junk-rig Paradox on record that was a **conversion** of the stock rig, and the only one **cruised**; the full build is at [Planktos](/boats/planktos.md).

The verdict after cruising is favourable and specific: the junk rig performed well overall, proving **easy and quick to reef and unreef**, with **good sail twist control** and **cheap, lightweight fittings**.[18] The reefing speed and the low cost of fittings answer, in practice, two of the qualities the stock rig was defended on above.

The report does **not** settle the objections that dominate the rest of this page. How the sheeting was arranged, whether a bumkin or arch was needed, whether the mast was moved, and how the centre of effort was kept behind the hull's centre of lateral resistance are all unrecorded.[18] The boat's stated limit — **around 20 knots** of wind, beyond which conditions become survival sailing — is given as a whole-boat figure, not attributed to the rig.[18] So the outcome is documented while the method is not: a junk-rigged Paradox works, but this source does not show how the balance and sheeting problems were solved.

### THU: a purpose-designed cambered rig (method, not yet fully solved)

Where Planktos gives the outcome, [Eerik Mee](/people/eerik-mee.md)'s **"[THU](/boats/thu.md)"** — a [20% scaled-up](/design/larger-paradox.md) Paradox built from 2015 and launched in 2017 — records the **method**. Rather than convert the stock lug, Mee designed a **six-panel cambered junk sail** from scratch to **Arne Kverneland**'s Junk Rig Association method, with **8% camber** built into the panels.[19] This directly follows the intuition recorded above — that a junk rig on Paradox needs [built-in camber, not flat panels](#a-daggerboard-forward-clr-experiment-and-why-paradoxs-rudder-was-built-large) — and Mee reached the same conclusion by trial: he rejected a flat-cut sample plan because "The HML is actually flat cut sail with not so good winward performance. It's the AK's camber that improves it."[20]

On the specific objections the rest of this page raises, THU is the first built boat to answer several:

- **Mast position.** The page predicts a junk's extra area forward of the mast would force the mast **aft** for balance. THU went the other way — the mast moved **forward of bulkhead 2**, driven by the taller 6 m spar (Mee wanted more mast below deck and clearance between the transom and the upper sheet).[20] Balance was then fiddly rather than automatic: one tack showed noticeable **lee helm**, which he cured by adding **water ballast forward** and tightening the **tack parrel** to move the centre of effort back.[21]
- **Sheeting and the transom overhang.** Mee needed **no bumkin or arch**; raising the mast opened ~10° of clearance between the transom and the upper sheet, and he sheeted to the boom directly. But this is exactly where THU is **not** finished — the **boom twisted** too much under sheet, and he moved block positions around without settling it.[21]
- **Extra line to manage.** Confirmed, and quantified: "We have 5 camcleats in total, because of the junk sail. Most Paradoxes have 2 or 3," the lines led in two levels with a three-sheave deflector on the mast to keep them clear of the lower sail downwind.[21]
- **Tacking.** The one clear win: THU **tacked without tricks** on her first sail, where a loaded lug Paradox usually needs the sail backed.[22]

So the two boats are complementary. Planktos shows a junk-rigged Paradox **cruises well**; THU shows **how one such rig was actually laid out and balanced** — with camber built in, the mast moved, and the line count higher — while leaving the sheeting-and-twist problem visibly unsolved and windward pointing explicitly unconcluded.[21][22]
