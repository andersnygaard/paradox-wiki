# Flush cabin sides for Paradox (speculative)

Fairing the cabin sides flush with the hull sides, rather than stepping them in from the sheer, has been proposed as an unbuilt alternative to Paradox's stock cabin. No build has followed the idea; the practical objections raised against it — lost side-deck stowage, harder forward access, added windage, and no real interior gain unless the side bins go too — have never been weighed against the claimed stability benefit (more buoyancy carried further outboard) in a full structural or stability analysis.

## Flush cabin sides, as an unexplored alternative

A separate idea approaches interior room from a different angle than lengthening: fairing the cabin sides flush with the hull sides rather than stepping them in from the sheer, on the reasoning that it would open up the interior and increase the righting effect of a knockdown by carrying more buoyancy further outboard.[1] No build has followed this approach, and no reply has weighed its structural or stability tradeoffs against the stock stepped-in cabin.

The practical case for keeping the stock stepped-in side decks: the recessed side decks are useful stowage, giving the **yuloh** and the **lowered mast/spars** a place to lie on deck when not in use (see [yuloh and stowed-rig retention](/components/yuloh.md)) — flushing the cabin sides to the hull would remove that space.[2] One further, untested thought followed: whether a **sprit rig**, needing no boom or yard to stow, would free a flush-sided design from needing on-deck spar storage at all, or whether brackets could instead be fitted to the cabin roof for the purpose.[3]

## A 2008 recurrence, answered

The same question recurred a few years later — a builder beginning to lay out bulkheads, before cutting, considered enlarging the cabin out to the edge of the hull, and asked whether doing so would affect any of the boat's other parameters.[4] This time it drew a direct answer, restating the stock stepped-in side deck's own purpose and adding fresh objections of its own: the recessed side decks are where the lowered sail and the yuloh are stowed, so widening the cabin over them would remove that space; a wider cabin top also makes it harder to move forward along the deck to reach the anchor or fend off; it adds windage, felt especially going to windward; and, unless the internal side bins are removed as well, the cabin's actual usable interior space would not even increase. On balance, little advantage was seen in widening the cabin top this way.[5]
