# Building Paradox larger: the designer-sanctioned scale-up

Enlarging [Paradox](/paradox.md) up to 20% is sanctioned by the designer, via a document bundled separately with the stock plans — not an annotation on the drawing sheets themselves — on the reasoning that no new drawings are needed because every enlarged dimension is simply the stock plan's own dimension multiplied by the same scale factor. Because the sanction is for a uniform scale-up rather than a change of proportions, this is a *property of the stock design*, not a variant departing from it; builders above about 6 ft and 200 lb have used it to size the boat to their own frame.

## Scale length only, not beam or freeboard

One approach is to **increase length only** and leave beam and freeboard alone. Under this approach mass grows **linearly** with length rather than as the **cube** of the overall dimensions (as it would under uniform 3-D scaling), so a length-only stretch is far less punishing on weight — at the cost of producing what would likely be a **faster, straighter-tracking** boat rather than a true enlargement in every dimension.[1]

This differs from the standing advice recorded in [stretched Paradox](/design/ideas/stretched-paradox.md), that scaling length **without** beam "would alter the overall qualities of the design and come out with something new."

## The 10% enlargement, and why it's a practical ceiling

A 6'3", 210 lb build (partway through its own 10%-upsized "Paradox 1.1" — see [stretched Paradox](/design/ideas/stretched-paradox.md)) settled on a **10% enlargement**, judging anything larger problematic for two concrete reasons:[2][3]

- Beyond 10%, the **side panels** need an **additional scarf** to reach the required length.
- The **bottom panel's width** (already a tight fit at 100% and 110% scale — see [stretched Paradox](/design/ideas/stretched-paradox.md)) would **exceed 4 ft** once the chines are added, at any enlargement beyond 10%, forcing more scarf joints and more wasted plywood.

Short of those caveats, a 10% enlargement is straightforward — a practical ceiling drawn from a build already partway through execution, rather than a theoretical estimate.

## A tall sailor's hesitation, and reassurance that fit-driven scaling is welcome

A large sailor who had held stock Paradox plans for several years, put off starting a build by negative reaction on the list to modifying the original design, asked what would be wrong with a Paradox built 10-20% larger while keeping every internal detail of the original — having found no other design with Paradox's own combination of features.[4] The concern is misplaced: scaling a design to fit a sailor's own size is a different matter from customizing its interior, sail, or propulsion system, and Paradox's own designer found what worked for his own body and sailing style — a builder who plans to sail and live aboard the way the designer did is well served following the plans as closely as possible, but adapting the boat to a different body is not itself a departure from that design intent.[5]

## A worked 20% scale-up: dimensions, sail area, and displacement

A ready-worked scale-up already existed in the group's Files section by 2008: multiplying every major dimension by 1.2 (a 20% increase) gives an LOA of 16'6.4" (5.04 m), LWL of 16'1.7" (4.92 m), beam of 4'11.3" (1.48 m), and draft of 10.8" (0.276 m), for a sail area of 168 sq ft, a displacement of 2,321 lb, a prismatic coefficient of .56, and a displacement/length ratio of 261. The cabin's living/sleeping area grows by only 15 in in length and 10 in in width at this scale — enough to matter but not enough to force a new interior layout — while the stock scantlings remain usable throughout except for the spars, which scale by the same 1.2 factor; leaving the water supply and stores lockers at their stock size yields a 7.5 in gain in headroom under the hatch. Bulkhead-to-bulkhead spacing grows by only 5.5-7.5 in at each bay, too small an increase on its own to justify a full redesign. The hardest part of the calculation was working out the new sail area (a 68% increase) and displacement (a 60% increase) needed to put the boat on her new lines while preserving the stock sail-area-to-displacement ratio. A smaller step is worked out the same way: at a 1.16 scale factor (a 16 ft boat), displacement comes to 2,075 lb and sail area to 148 sq ft.[6]

## How far the designer's sanction actually reaches, and what the record does not settle

Most accounts converge on a **20% scale-up approved in principle**: the scale-up document bundled with the stock plans and posted to the group's Files section as "larger paradox" claims Matt Layden's blessing directly[6], an independent member confirmed the same 20% figure the same day in 2008[7], and the earliest report on record — from 2004 — already had Matt Layden giving the "all clear" to scale the boat 20% on the same scantlings[11], with a separate 2004 account of support for enlarging the design following shortly after[12]. One account, from a builder who put the question to Matt Layden directly, caps his approval at **10%**, reasoning that anything larger would amount to designing a new boat[8] — matching the practical ceiling reached independently above from side-panel and bottom-panel scarfing constraints. A single builder has claimed that **33-50%** is achievable without difficulty, but this is his own stated opinion, not something Matt Layden endorsed: it followed only a partial reply about an unrelated 21 ft "ultimate cruiser" concept, with no answer to a follow-up offering to pay for engineering time[13].

**A 10% enlargement is uncontroversial under every account on record.** A 20% enlargement rests on the plans document and two independent confirmations, weighed against the one account capping the designer's own approval at 10%. **Nothing in the record supports designer sanction beyond 20%** — the 33-50% figure is one builder's own opinion, not designer-sanctioned. The fuller case for stopping at 10% — including the practical cost of going bigger at all — is worked out at [stretched Paradox — carrying company aboard, and the practical cost of going bigger](/design/ideas/stretched-paradox.md#carrying-company-aboard-and-the-practical-cost-of-going-bigger).

## Matt Layden's approval, and why no scaled-up Paradox has yet been built

Matt Layden has given his approval in principle to a 20% scale-up of this kind, on the reasoning that no new set of drawings is needed — every dimension required is already on the stock plans as drawn.[6][7] Despite the idea recurring on the mailing list roughly forty times over the years, no one has yet actually built a scaled-up Paradox to these figures and reported the results back to the group.[7] Scaling up is more involved than redrawing dimensions at a fixed factor: sail area and displacement each have to grow well beyond the linear scale factor to preserve the stock sail-area-to-displacement ratio — a 68% and 60% increase respectively, in the worked 20% example above[6] — and the side and bottom panels need additional scarf joints once the increase passes 10%,[2][3] which plausibly accounts for the gap between recurring interest and an actual completed build.

## Naming an enlarged Paradox, and speculation about the designer's own workload

A larger Paradox built to this scale would need a name distinct from the stock boat to avoid confusion — "ParadoxPlus," "ParadoxToo," and "ParadoxForTwo" have all been floated,[9] as has "Paradox Max,"[10] though none has been adopted for an actual build. One guess, offered as speculation rather than established fact, was that Matt Layden had not himself drawn up and marketed an official larger set of plans — for this scale-up or for Enigma — because he was fully occupied building and sea-trialing his own next boat for a race he called the "Around in Ten Race," with its start still roughly a year away; an alternative guess was that he judged the market too small to be worth his time.[9]

## Correcting the 20% figures for plywood curvature, and a beam-widening alternative

The worked 20% figures above measure length along the plywood panel's straight-line dimension; accounting for the panel's actual curvature once bent into the hull brings the true overall length down to roughly 15'9" rather than a flat 16'6".[10] A further alternative to scaling beam uniformly with length was floated for two-up comfort: 4 ft at the chine and 5 ft at the deck, reasoned to make the boat noticeably more comfortable for two users without otherwise changing the 20% scale-up.[10] The same reasoning was applied to Matt Layden's own [Enigma 460](/matt-layden/enigma.md#the-enigma-family-the-360-and-the-plywood-cabin-460): judged close to the right overall size already but too narrow in the beam, widening her to 5 ft and taking the deckhouse to full width was suggested as a way to improve her interior in the same spirit.[10]
