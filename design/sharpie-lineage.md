# Sharpie lineage: is Paradox a sharpie?

On the label itself, the record is essentially uncontested: [Paradox](/paradox.md) is a hard-chine, flat-bottomed sharpie, and a 1996 construction series produced with the designer's own cooperation goes so far as to propose a name for the type outright.[1] What is genuinely divided in the record is narrower and more interesting than "sharpie or not" — which historical sharpie lineage the hull actually descends from, whether the larger hulls built on the same chine-runner idea still count as sharpies at all, and whether sharpie-typical hull behavior explains some of Paradox's own handling quirks.

## The label is settled

A 1996 magazine series documenting the boat's construction surveys the sharpie's history as a type — "an enlarged open sailing skiff, retaining the skiff's flat bottom, stretched in length and keeping it's beam as narrow as possible" — and records that builders and sailors of sharpies in their day were practical people who "rejected more complex boats, seeing no advantage in the rounded chines": "a sharpie was good enough with hard chines."[1] The same passage proposes a name for Paradox specifically: "For Paradox, I suggest using 'Chine Runner Sharpie', to differentiate from others. Then if someone would ask you what kind of boat Paradox is you could say; 'Its a chine runner sharpie'."[1] It also records, in passing, that the word itself had by then fallen out of general use — the 1996 editions of both the Webster and Random House Dictionaries had removed the definition of "sharpies" entirely[1] — and makes the definitional case explicit: sharpies today do not follow any one classic definition, and the feature that still qualifies a hull as a sharpie is the shape of its bottom, not its era or its rig.[1] That argument was made in service of an unbuilt companion-boat design brief; see ["The Best Open Beachcruiser"](/design/variants/best-open-beachcruiser.md) for the fuller proposal it fed into.

A community-written build manual's own introduction, independent of the magazine series, calls the boat outright "a heavy displacement sharpie type," proven through "empirical data through testing of scale models and preceeding vessels of the same type."[2] [Beam, freeboard, and stability](/design/beam-and-stability.md) states the same thing flatly: sharpies are narrow for their length, and Paradox follows the type, her beam running just under 48 inches, not counting the chine runners.[3] Builders use the word the same unselfconscious way throughout the record — nobody argues the label itself, only what lies behind it.

## Which sharpie lineage: the popular assumption, and the correction

The popular assumption, raised more than once in the record, is that Paradox and [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) carry a lot of Phil Bolger's sharpie philosophy.[4] The correction offered in the same exchange runs the other way, twice over. One reply judges it is Munroe's sharpie work, more than Bolger's, that actually inspired Paradox.[5] A second, fuller reply names the boats directly: Ralph "the Commodore" Munroe built one sharpie resembling Paradox's hull shape, "Kingfisher," to a local design delivered to Florida by steamer, and a later, more successful Munroe design, "Egret," was Kingfisher's more flared, dory-like, double-ended offspring — nothing like a Phil Bolger sharpie at all. Paradox's own shape is judged closer to "the original sharpies of over 100 years ago but with more freeboard for her length."[6]

## Whether the scaled-up family still counts as sharpies

The chine-runner idea has been carried onto hulls far larger than Paradox — see [chine runners: origin and evolution](/chine-runners/origin-and-evolution.md) for that family in full. The strongest explicit pushback on record concerns exactly those larger hulls: a roughly 38 ft chine-runner sharpie resembles what an enlarged Paradox would look like, but it is not a scaled-up Paradox at all — it and its sister hull are a separate design, arrived at through a separate design process — and, more to the point, they are not, strictly, sharpies at all: traditional sharpies have low sheers and low centers of gravity and sail differently, hence the coined term "Modified Sharpie" for these particular boats.[7] The distinction sits awkwardly next to Paradox's own, much smaller hull, which stays upright the opposite way — see "Where she departs," below.

## Whether sharpie theory explains her handling

A hull's weathercocking tendency under sail has been argued from sharpie theory directly: the lighter a boat's displacement, the less pronounced the effect, since low momentum lets a light hull stop dead when tacking rather than carry through — sharpie literature is cited directly for the point.[8] The counter-argument holds the comparison overstated: at roughly 1,400 lb and 14 ft, Paradox is "hardly a lightweight Sharpie," and the behavior in question reads more as an ordinary trim/balance issue, the same one a sailing canoe shows if trimmed carelessly.[9] See [sailing characteristics and rig balance](/operations/sailing-characteristics.md#a-second-round-weathercocking-ballast-and-rudder-stall) for the fuller tacking debate this theory was offered into.

## Traits shared with the type

Set against that unsettled family tree, the traits Paradox actually shares with the historical type are not in dispute. A sharpie is, at root, an enlarged open skiff: flat bottom, narrow beam, hard chines kept for practicality rather than "the beauty of the curved hulls."[1] Paradox's own beam runs just under 48 inches, not counting the chine runners — narrow for her length, in keeping with the type.[3] Her draft runs shallow the same way, 9 inches upright, deepening only under heel.[10] She is planked simply, in the classic sharpie's own manner — "two side panels wrapped around and joined at the ends" — in Paradox's case built up in plywood, epoxy, and ring nails rather than the type's older materials, but the same basic panel-and-chine geometry.[11] She inherits the type's practice of counting her own stores as ballast rather than relying on fixed weight alone: "in Matt's sharpies most of the ship's stores make up the ballast," so a cruising load has to be re-shifted and topped up as stores are consumed — see [weight, ballast, and displacement](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#the-designers-layered-ballast-water-tanks-stores-and-lead-together) for the fuller practice.[12] And she inherits the type's habit of beating its own theoretical hull speed: "it is not unusual for sharpies to sail much faster" than the classic displacement-hull formula predicts, a pattern Paradox's own logged GPS figures confirm — see [hull speed](/operations/hull-speed.md) for the fuller record.[13]

A builder's own firsthand comparison, sizing up an unrelated keelboat sharpie against Paradox on the same trailer, confirms the same self-description from the outside: Paradox is **"a single chine"** hull with **upright sides**, and her mast is remembered as **"stout"** and **"short."**[16]

## Where she departs from the type

**No leeboard or centreboard.** The clearest departure is also her signature feature. A working sharpie of any era carries a leeboard or a centreboard; Paradox carries neither, substituting the chine runner instead — see [chine runners: origin and evolution](/chine-runners/origin-and-evolution.md) for the runner's own history in full, not repeated here. The substitution is itself derived from other sharpies, by the designer's own account: comments about a couple of Phil Bolger's own sharpies fitted with external chine battens got Matt Layden thinking about an aerodynamic "fence," to separate high from low pressure and minimize flow across the chine — "and that evolved into my current chine runners."[14]

**An enclosed cabin, not an open working deck.** A working sharpie is sailed from an open cockpit or deck. Paradox eliminates the cockpit outright — "a small boat cannot spare the precious room required for it," and an open cockpit is vulnerable to swamping and exposes the crew to the elements — and is sailed entirely from within a closed cabin instead, never needing the crew to go forward or sit exposed on deck.[15]

**Heavy ballast for self-righting, not low freeboard.** Where a traditional sharpie stays upright through low sheer and a low center of gravity, Paradox does the opposite: she carries heavy internal ballast and comparatively high freeboard, and is built to self-right from a genuine knockdown rather than avoid one in the first place — precisely the distinction drawn against the larger "Modified Sharpie" family above.[7] See [beam, freeboard, and stability](/design/beam-and-stability.md) and [weight, ballast, and displacement](/design/weight-and-ballast.md) for the fuller case.

**A rig worked from inside, not a cat-ketch.** A classic sharpie typically carried a cat-ketch rig worked from an open deck, with three mast steps so a removable mizzen could be shipped for summer and the mainmast re-stepped amidships for the stronger fall and spring winds.[11] Paradox instead carries a single sprit/lug sail, reefed, raised, and lowered entirely from within the closed cabin with no need to go forward — see [rig and sail](/components/rig/index.md) for the rig itself.

## Type context and literature

The community's own build-manual bibliography places Paradox squarely inside the sharpie literature rather than treating her as a one-off design with no ancestry: Reuel Parker's *The Sharpie Book* and Howard Chapelle's *American Small Sailing Craft* are both named directly in the same 1996 magazine series,[1] and John Gardner's *Building Classic Small Craft* — specifically its "A Sharpie" chapter — was used as the literal starting profile for the unbuilt companion-boat brief mentioned above, its shape judged "ideal" as a starting point even though the classic hull would need extensive change to become the "Best."[11] See [the small-boat library behind the Paradox](/references/small-boat-library.md) for the fuller bibliography these citations belong to.

The bottom-shape argument above is also the standing answer to a related, practical question: [a sharpie hull suits chine runners far better than a dory hull does](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md#narrow-wing-runners-on-a-chris-morejohn-sharpie-and-a-centerboard-plus-runner-combination), because a box hull resists sideways slippage on its own, before the runners are even added — a property rooted in the same hard-chine, flat-bottom geometry that makes Paradox a sharpie in the first place, and not shared by a dory's flared topsides.
