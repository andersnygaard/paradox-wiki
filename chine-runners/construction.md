# Chine runner construction technique

This concept documents **how builders physically make and fit** the [Paradox](/paradox.md)'s chine runners — as distinct from [how chine runners work](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md) (the mechanism) and [windward performance](/chine-runners/windward-performance.md) (the sea-trial evidence). The runners' central construction challenge is that they **change shape in three dimensions** as the stations move aft, so most of the recorded methods are different ways to solve that same compound curve.

The runners are **not "fillets" in the traditional sense**: they are built from the plywood-and-glass/epoxy bottom plus **triangular fairing blocks** with two rolling bevels (one against the hull, one on the outboard face), shaped for a **hydrodynamic** reason rather than as a simple structural fillet — a curved fairing block, convex or concave, produces more drag than a straight one. As built, the **18 mm plywood** bottom is covered in roughly **6 mm of glass/epoxy** — enough that the runners need no bedding or electrolysis precautions at all, and any damage is easily repaired in place. This is why facing them in metal is discouraged (below).

## The pattern question

The "Building the Paradox" articles describe making a pattern from the **outside curve of the hull** before the bottom is fitted, for use later when constructing the chine runners. It leaves open whether the runner timber is cut **to that pattern first** — so it already carries the correct horizontal curve, leaving only a vertical bend to install — or whether an additional step is involved. Builders working without those articles in hand arrived at several independent alternatives, below.

## Method 1 — Pre-cut solid-timber blocks, shaped on a bandsaw

Built without the pattern articles in hand at this stage, one "[Zoë](/boats/zoe.md)" build made the chine runners out of **small pieces of fir 2×4, cut to shape and glued along the overhang** — solid timber rather than plywood, reasoning the runners take more abuse than plywood could comfortably absorb — then gave them "ample glass covering."

The runners were **pre-manufactured** rather than bent into place: each piece was roughly **10 in long**, small enough to cut out easily on a **band saw** — rated the single most valuable power tool for building a boat ("if I was only allowed one mounted power tool"), table saw second. Watching classified ads for secondhand tools pays off (a 50-year-old cast-iron Sears table saw, bought used for $50 some twenty years earlier, still does the job). Because the rolling bevels are a real challenge to install as drawn, this pre-cut-block method has become the **converging practice** among builders who work the problem independently.

## Method 2 — Kerf-and-bend a single shaped timber

A potentially easier method works from the drawings directly: since the plans give the chine's shape, angle, and size at each frame/station, cut timber to the drawing's shape and size, then **saw a series of kerfs** through the back and bottom edges — spaced roughly **4–6 in apart** — leaving about **12 mm of wood** parallel to the outside edge uncut. Because the chine sweeps up more sharply toward the stern, the kerfs there can be cut **deeper on the bottom edge**, letting that portion bend into a **tighter curve**. Once bent into place, **epoxy is squeezed into the saw kerfs** to fill and stiffen them, in keeping with an old UK boatbuilding saying that predates epoxy: "in glue and dust we put our trust." The chine member is heavy on the hull's inside and glass-sheathed outside, so the kerfing does not compromise the joint.

## Method 3 — Batten-and-fill

Another method lays a single **batten the length of the runner** and fills the gap between batten and hull with **scrap wood bits and epoxy** to build up the final shape, rather than pre-manufacturing separate blocks. Laying up **full-length, pre-shaped strakes** this way proved **almost impossible to bend to shape and hold** while the epoxy cured, so the practical fix is to cut the runners into **shorter sections**, glue each in as a "three-dimensional jigsaw," and — once set — grind them fair to templates with a **sanding disc on an angle grinder**.

## Method 4 — Templates, epoxy fill, and a hand plane

The runners' reputation for difficulty is overstated: a straightforward fit-and-fair sequence — **templates**, **epoxy** to fill any low spots, then a hand plane to bring down the high spots once cured — works with **no real problem** and "turned out great" on at least one build, a method seconded from another build. The recurring lesson across all four approaches is that builders **converge on whatever compound-curve solution suits their own tools and patience**; none of the methods is difficult, but each respects the runners' changing-in-three-dimensions curvature.

## Method 5 — Cardboard patterns and a block-built "wall"

A further method starts from the bottom already fitted, leaving a curved shelf along the chine to build the runner up from. **Cardboard patterns** cut to the hull's shape at several stations along the chine let a builder check how much material is needed before cutting anything permanent. The runner itself is then built up from **small blocks — roughly 3 in by 1 in, or larger where needed — glued in place one after another like courses in a wall**, rather than from a single length of timber, then shaped together with a hand plane, sanded fair, and sheathed in **2 oz glass cloth**. The reasoning for building in short blocks rather than one continuous length: since the runners are not structural, the wood grain does not need to run continuous — only the finished shape matters — so if the glass sheathing is ever scuffed through, any moisture that gets in is confined to a **single block** rather than tracking the runner's full length. A runner built this way has stood up to several years of use with no reported problems.

## Fitting order: blank before or after the bottom panel

All four Methods above assume the runner material goes on **after the bottom panel is fitted**, which the plans themselves suggest. An alternative sequence clamps a roughly shaped blank directly to the hull side **before the bottom goes on** at all: without the bottom in the way, a rough piece of fir is easy to clamp in position, and its exact shape at the bottom edge is not yet critical, since it can simply be **planed down to fair in with the chine logs** once the bottom panel is fitted over it, with only the extra width filled in at that point; the plan-view shape can be marked and cut out in the same step. **Steaming the blank first** makes it easier still to bend into position, whichever order it goes on in. Bending a full-length blank into position in one piece has proven difficult regardless of fitting order — the practical fix, as in the batten-and-fill method (Method 3) above, is to cut the runner into roughly **foot-long sections** and glue each one in separately, at some cost in extra epoxy and a less elegant look than a single unbroken strip.

## A closed-cell foam alternative, and a 2007 durability exchange

Because the runners are not structural and are sheathed in glass cloth regardless of the core material underneath, **closed-cell foam** — blue insulation foam or PVC foam — is workable in place of solid timber blocks: it bends easily into shape even after the hull has already been faired, without needing steaming or block-by-block building up. A builder wanting extra confidence in the bottom edge's strength can cut the bottom panel **slightly wide of the hull sides** before fitting it, leaving a "shelf" running a fair curve the length of the hull to form the runner's bottom surface, with the foam core simply laminated onto the top of that shelf.

The bottom panel's overhang past the hull sides is not itself a builder-added modification: the plans already call for the bottom sheet to be wider than the sides as standard.[63] A composite-hull build's own photographs, filed under "ParaPlastic" in the group's files section, show a worked example of a foam-fibre chine runner built this way — see [BRIS Paradox](/boats/bris-paradox.md) for the fuller build record.[64]

A concern was raised over exactly this kind of lighter build: because the chine rails project past the hull's sides, they take the first impact when the boat grounds, and ought to be built **moderately substantial** regardless of core material. Weighed against that, firsthand experience running a solid-timber-and-glass runner repeatedly onto beaches, dock pilings, and into surf recorded **no damage at all**, using a heavier **12 oz biaxial glass weave** over the runner rather than the plans' specified woven roving — with the runner's bottom, upswept surface, the part most likely to strike something, sheathed in that same heavier weave — and treating ordinary scuffs simply by repainting. The reasoning offered against over-building: anything capable of striking hard enough to break a chine runner outright would risk piercing the hull's **1/2 in plywood sides** just as easily, and actually striking the lighter-glassed, heeled-over part of the runner hard enough to matter would be a rare feat in the first place — a prudent skipper avoiding hazards altogether matters more than adding strength the runner is unlikely to ever need.

## Sven Yrvind's composite method (YRVIND ½)

The runners of the composite YRVIND ½ were built by a wholly different, moldless technique suited to a glassfibre hull:

1. **Attach plywood pieces** to the hull, insulated against the epoxy with **packing tape** (so they release later).
2. **Clamp a glassfibre sheet** to them, fastening it to the hull with a **fillet** — forming a shelf standing out from the chine.
3. Once that shelf is set, it **receives several layers of glassfibre**. Because the runners are exposed to grounding, they are built **strong — about 20 mm (¾ in) thick.**
4. **Grind to a fair shape** outdoors once the boat is moved outside.

The same source documented **how Matt Layden built Enigma's chine runner** in composite — a fit-up, glue-up, and rough-shaping sequence — noting that on a plywood boat like Paradox the solid-timber methods above apply, while a composite boat calls for the layup approach instead.

## Grounding wear, and why the runners aren't metal-edged

The chine runners, along with the **rudder**, are the **first parts to hit anything** in shallow water — though when the boat dries out fully, the **whole bottom** takes the brunt rather than the runners alone.

Fitting a **metal edge** for protection is discouraged unless the builder has genuine metalworking skills: the runners have been **shaped for a hydrodynamic reason**, and changing their shape (as a metal edge or shoe might require) risks **changing the boat's windward ability**. The glass/epoxy sheathing already makes them tough and easily repaired, so metal buys little and risks much. (This is consistent with the parallel argument against bilge keels and metal chine shoes — see [stretched Paradox](/design/variants/stretched-paradox.md).)

A boat this light and slow also grounds gently in the first place: without much mass or speed, striking a rock tends to let the hull simply move wherever it has to go rather than holing the bottom or causing heavy damage — genuine danger instead comes from being struck by something itself carrying speed and mass, a risk to the sides and topsides rather than the bottom. The same reasoning underpins the case for heavier fiberglass over metal facing; see [hull planking — woven roving versus biaxial cloth](/construction/hull-planking.md#woven-roving-versus-biaxial-cloth-what-the-laminating-schedule-is-for) for a professional lamination account of why, and a durability data point from a much heavier, faster power boat built the same way.[65]

### Bronze versus steel, if a builder insists on a shoe

For a builder set on a metal shoe anyway, **¼ in steel plates** bolted and bedded under the hull in the runner's own profile trade lead's low weight for edge-protection toughness, but **steel plate bedded to wood** carries a real corrosion risk once the finish is damaged, and trust in bedding compounds between dissimilar materials is not well founded. **Bronze plate** with bronze fasteners is the more durable, if costlier, alternative: electrolytic action becomes much less of a worry, and a scraped bronze edge stays sound under ordinary antifouling even after striking rocks. One compromise is an **epoxy coating on the steel plus 3M 5200** as the bedding — bedding epoxy-coated steel to an epoxy/glass coating rather than bare wood — though whether the coating would survive scraping without exposing the steel to rapid corrosion remains untested.

A composite-hull build reached the same bronze-over-steel conclusion from a different angle: fitting a metal runner base to the ["BRIS Paradox"](/boats/bris-paradox.md), the plan first called for a **stainless-steel sheet** for the bottom (doing double duty as ballast) before switching to **bronze** — costlier, but bondable and useful as a **lightning-protection ground plate** as well as a runner base, with the sheet protruding sideways to form the base the epoxy/fibreglass-filler runner body is built up on. A related, standing piece of advice for any Paradox owner launching from a beach: **keep your toes clear**, since the runners and rudder are the first parts to touch bottom.

The adhesive question for a metal runner base is settled: bonded on with an **NM (Nils Malmgren)** elastic epoxy filler, with **no mechanical fasteners** at all, rather than through-bolted or riveted. Epoxy essentially will not adhere to stainless steel, but a durable bond onto stainless is achievable anyway by heavily grinding the sheet and applying an elastic filler within seconds of grinding — necessary because the filler is markedly thixotropic and begins to set almost immediately; that bond outlasted the polyester laminate beneath it when tested years later by attempted removal (the laminate tore first), on a build of the related "Sofia" design. Bronze poses no comparable gluing problem, but the same elastic-epoxy practice between polyester and metal applies regardless — an elastic bondline is the right choice for a metal-to-composite joint either way.

A separate corrosion-control technique carries over from aluminum to bronze bonding (below): coat the surface with epoxy *first*, then sand through the still-wet epoxy, so the freshly abraded metal never meets open air. On the "Zoë" rivet-hole reinforcement technique (drilling and countersinking holes along a bonded edge so cured epoxy acts like a rivet, below), a manufacturer's specification promising no gluing problem with bronze made that extra step unnecessary in the BRIS case.

On bending the bronze sheet to the runner's curve, three fallback plans were tried in sequence as each proved harder than expected: **Plan A**, hammering the 2 mm bronze sheet round a curved pattern by hand or with a mechanical consultant's help, ran into a work-hardening risk (bronze beaten or hammered work-hardens and becomes brittle — below); **Plan B**, grinding away material and bonding on a separately curved strip, "sounds impossible"; a related alternative — laminating thickness onto the edge from offcut strips at bow and stern, glued, riveted, brazed, or welded up to sufficient thickness and then ground to the curve — was ruled out for welding specifically, since the divinycell foam core cannot take welding heat, leaving gluing as the only practical build-up method; **Plan C**, leaving the curve unresolved and finding out the hard way whether it has any hydrodynamic effect at all, was "seemingly the most probable" outcome. Without knowing a given sheet's exact tin/copper/phosphorus composition, its workability can't be predicted from general principles — a supplier specification and a blacksmithing reference (anvilfire.com) are the way to check, and testing two scale models (one with the curve's radius, one without) in a small stream would show whether the radius carries any real hydrodynamic effect. Bronze is an excellent choice for the bottom regardless of that outcome: even if the curve can't be reproduced and only a straight edge results, bronze-epoxy-divinycell construction leaves no wood to rot out at the chine, unlike a timber runner. In practice, bending tests on bronze scraps suggest 2 mm sheet is not brittle and can be bent considerably — the real problem is simply that 2 mm is a thick gauge to bend by hand.

### Fabricating chine runners in steel, if the whole hull is steel

The bronze/stainless case above assumes a **plywood-and-glass** hull with only the runner's own base plated in metal. A related, more radical question is fabricating the chine runners themselves in **steel**, on a hull built entirely in that material (see [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative)](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md) for the wider steel-hull debate this assumes as background). A simpler alternative to fabricating the runner in steel at all is a **hardwood chine runner bolted onto a steel hull**: shape the runner in oak or teak per the plans' frame-by-frame profile, drill through from inside and braze the bolts in position from outside, counter-bore the back face for nuts and washers, dry-fit and mark the bolts for length, seal the back face with wood sealer, bed it in mastic, and plug and seal the bolt holes with the plugs' grain running fore-and-aft. Fabricating the runner in steel itself, including its radiused outer edge, is "quite an undertaking," though the radius could simply be omitted on a steel build to ease the job.

Set against that caution, a professional fabricator's view is that steel runners pose no real difficulty regardless of hull size — "not a difficult undertaking... period" next to far more complex fabrication work seen done in commercial shipyards (submarine steam-loop piping, boiler-tube welding in confined spaces, spiral staircase handrails). The main caution is against mixing too many dissimilar materials on one hull; modern, affordable, even rentable tools (plasma cutters, angle grinders, small wire-feed welders) put steel fabrication within reach of a backyard builder. The runner's curved outer edge specifically can be formed by **welding a length of pipe along the edge** to get the radius, or by plasma-cutting and working the profile out directly.

The "not difficult" view has a caveat: aimed at a professional fabricator, it understates the risk for an ordinary backyard builder. Producing the chine runners in steel requires at least two full-length welds, and even a skilled welder can leave a pinhole or two in a weld that long; any resulting water intrusion inside the runner reaches unprotected steel and starts corrosion from the inside out — a failure mode seen firsthand in local boatyards littered with steel boats whose once-sound runners had rusted through. If a steel hull must have steel chine runners, hiring a genuinely skilled welder for that specific joint is worth the cost, given how much rides on weld quality alone.

The pinhole concern has a direct answer, though: coating the welded steel in **epoxy** — a known practice among backyard steel-hull builders — fills any pinholes before priming and painting, and ordinary maintenance thereafter prevents the water intrusion described above, just as neglect would eventually damage an unprotected wood boat too. Welding correctly is "not rocket science"; small welds on a boat this size can be tacked and moved incrementally to manage heat distortion (a routine, "common textbook" practice, not the difficulty sometimes implied), and backyard builders successfully cruising 40-foot-plus steel boats show the approach scales down fine to a Paradox-sized hull. Separately: **working bronze by beating or hammering it can work-harden the metal**, making it brittle — a hazard for whichever builder ends up hand-forming a bronze chine-runner curve (above).

On the flat-hull-and-metal-bottom question generally, a lamination alternative avoids bending sheet metal to the runner's curve at all: build up the required thickness at the outer edge from **offcut strips laminated in layers** — glued, riveted, brazed, or welded on in succession until enough thickness accumulates to grind the final curve into. This is a metal-specific analogue of the plywood block-and-fill methods above (Methods 1 and 3), trading a single bent sheet for a stack of flat strips that only need to be shaped, not bent, at the end.

On a **fully composite/metal-adjacent** hull rather than steel specifically, a corrosion-control technique for bonding epoxy to **aluminum** — which, unlike stainless steel, begins oxidizing the instant it is sanded — carries over: coat the surface with epoxy *first*, then sand through the still-wet epoxy, which keeps the freshly abraded metal from ever being exposed to open air. The same approach works equally well for bonding epoxy to **bronze**. A mechanical reinforcement for a bonded ground-strip or sheet edge, from the "[Zoë](/boats/zoe.md)" build: drilling holes along the plate's edge and countersinking the outside face before bonding, so the cured epoxy filling each hole acts like a rivet, resisting the peeling forces that are otherwise the weakest failure mode for a glued metal-to-hull joint of this kind.

## Milling solid chine logs from a dense hardwood

A separate internal member — the **chine log** itself (see [chine log vs. taped-seam construction](/construction/chine-log-vs-taped-seam.md) for its structural role) — poses its own milling challenge in a dense hardwood. Milling chine logs from **Honduras Mahogany** proved far harder to bend than the more common **white oak**, since mahogany is much less flexible; a builder may need to **resaw and laminate** the logs in thinner layers instead of bending them solid. As with the runners, the difficulty is inherent to the design's shape rather than a sign of anything wrong: the hull's narrowness means the required bend, while severe, should still be achievable.

## Casting lead ballast into the runners

The runners' already-solved compound-curve shape invites a second use: **casting lead ballast into them**, moving weight out of the bilge and further **outboard on the lever arm** for greater stiffening per pound. As the windward chine begins to surface under heel, ballast sited that far outboard sits exactly where it is most wanted, functioning something like extreme outboard water ballast. One approach reuses a **chine-runner-section mold** to cast lead **hardware-bonded** to the chine log; a fuller version uses a **plaster mold per side** (built from a bench-made doorskin/hot-glue/modeler's-clay plug), the lead **epoxied to the plywood and fastened from below**, fully encapsulated in the glass that already covers the runners — motivated as much by freeing bilge stowage as by stiffness, with an estimate of no more than ~**50 lb of lead per side** this way.

### The movable-vs-fixed-ballast tradeoff

Fixing ballast to the chine log has a cost: unlike loose bilge ballast, it can no longer be **shifted fore-and-aft** — weight forward to punch through head seas, weight aft when running. For Paradox specifically the objection is mostly moot: at **~1,400 lb displacement** the boat is small enough that **shifting crew weight** — an option the inside-steering layout uniquely allows — is the practical way to trim her, so lead ingots in the **30–40 lb** range are commonly secured in place and not moved under sail. One builder's **~25 lb lead bricks** (cast in an old bread tin, painted with Hammerite while warm) would be impractical to unbolt and re-secure against a knockdown at sea, so if weight forward were ever needed a crew member would sooner **sit further forward**; that build needed **250 lb** of ballast total, shifted the bricks to trim before fixing them, and removes some as stores are added on longer trips — using them as exactly the movable ballast that casting into the runners would foreclose.

### Lower-tech casting: lead birdshot in epoxy

As an alternative to a full mold, mixing **lead birdshot into epoxy** thickened with silica to a heavy, puttylike consistency, applied directly to the runner and worked to a rounded radius by hand, is a workable lower-tech route — a mix that could equally go **under the glass on the hull bottom** as a ballast-lowering base coat. An estimated **100–200 lb** of lead can be worked in this way, with the rest made up of interior lead pigs or sandbags (sandbags preferred by some builders to avoid skin contact with lead in the cabin).

A key **safety correction** on the mold method: a used, already-beveled runner section **cannot itself serve as the casting plug** (the plug must be flat and fair on the bottom face, without a rolling bevel), and whatever plug is used, the resulting **plaster mold must be fully dried/baked before pouring lead**, or trapped moisture can cause dangerous steam-driven splatter.

### A 2007 revisiting, and the case against a bolt-on steel "wing"

A later exchange took up the same casting-lead-into-the-runners idea from scratch, alongside a more radical alternative: a bolted-on **steel slab "wing,"** roughly 2 ft by 6 ft and 3/4 in thick, shaped with a grinder and mounted under the hull like a shallow-draft wing keel, sized deliberately oversize so material could be trimmed off after test-fitting.[48][49]

The case against both ideas, made in detail, rests on what internal removable ballast already does well: it makes the boat **lighter to move on land**, easier to **trim once afloat**, and — being genuinely removable — never becomes dead weight the builder is stuck with. Casting a pair of chine runners in lead was judged **beyond most amateur boatbuilders**, complicated further by the runners tapering to nothing toward the bow, and would put extra weight at the runners' bigger aft ends — the wrong end for trim, since the plans place internal lead ballast forward. A bolted-on steel wing fares no better: fitted under the hull it needs a gap to clear the hull's curve, which **adds draft** — cutting into the shallow-draft, dry-out-and-sleep-aboard usefulness that is much of the design's point, since a boat sitting that much deeper risks toppling onto her transom, bow in the air, when she dries out — and adds **wetted surface and drag** regardless of where it sits. Fitted instead at the sides, in place of the existing chine runners, the same wing shape would widen the boat's beam to **8 ft**. Shallow-draft wing keels of this kind are already a known, published option on production cruising yachts, but properly engineered ones are smaller and cast, not bolted-on slab steel; either version of the proposal was judged to reduce the design's usefulness rather than improve it.[50]

A one-line reaction to the steel-wing idea — "he said the S word" — was the only other response on record; the runners' hydrodynamic role (see [how chine runners work](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md)) is different enough from a true ballast keel that even raising the word "keel" in this context draws a laugh.[49a]

The steel-wing idea's proposer clarified it as a shorter, higher-aspect-ratio ballasted foil bolted flush under the hull rather than a shallow-draft cruising wing keel — comparing it to Enigma's own narrower, higher-aspect chine "wings" — reasoning it would add no extra draft if fitted correctly, put ballast low and centered, and let a builder "tune" the foil's length empirically by trimming it shorter through testing rather than guessing a final size up front.[51] A one-line reply dismissed the whole exchange without addressing the technical point.[52]

The detailed technical rebuttal followed point by point: bolting a plate to the underside is external ballast, not a lift-generating wing, since a genuine wing would need shaping to help the boat go to windward; the proposed 3/4 in steel slab would itself add 3/4 in of draft; bending such a thick plate to the hull's curve would need a roller mill, well beyond a hand-tool job; and if the "wing" instead extended out from the sides, it would have to be centered on the center of lateral resistance (just forward of frame 3) rather than wherever the trimming ballast happens to sit, would widen the hull to 6 ft locally underwater, would be hazardous to wade past, and would increase heeled draft over the stock design. Existing internal ballast was defended as easier to trim for different loading conditions (sailing solo and light versus cruising with stores), avoids new through-hull bolt holes below the waterline, and stays compatible with fitting an outboard, which itself calls for internal ballast anyway; the closing recommendation was to stick with chine runners as "a lot less hassle" and "far more practical."[53]

The reply conceded the specific dimensions had been picked arbitrarily and agreed a builder would need to flatten or fair the hull locally to fit any bolted plate, agreed the "wing" would assist to windward as a supplement to internal ballast rather than a replacement for it, and floated grinding a foil section into the steel — while noting a steel foil could not match the semi-symmetrical shape of the stock plywood chine runner and that the extent to which that symmetry actually matters was unclear. The underlying motivation was reframed as less a build proposal than an open question of whether different chine-runner configurations could ever be tested against each other scientifically, since no such controlled comparison has ever been run.[54]

A further technical response argued that adding anything to the bottom without increasing draft is only possible near the bow or stern, within the boat's existing upright draft envelope, since doing so elsewhere gives no improvement to the center of gravity while adding drag and losing ballast value to the added buoyancy of whatever is bolted on. Paradox's shallow draft and low center of gravity already work together with the chine rails' low aspect ratio, which lets the hull act efficiently as a lateral plane much like a pair of end plates. Casting lead onto the chine rails specifically was judged likely counterproductive: spreading ballast weight outboard slows the boat's roll rate, which can help damping in some sea states but risks the roll synchronizing dangerously with wave period in others. A preferred alternative was raised — running lead in pieces along the centerline outside the hull, as on Nathanael Herreshoff's sharpie **Meadowlark**, in roughly 15 in x 6 in x 1 in pieces laid end to end, adding about an inch of draft while also protecting the bottom — though the effect on roll characteristics was not calculated. The overall verdict stood against departing from the stock ballast arrangement in any of these ways.[55]

## Sheer-clamp and chine-log installation order

The **sheer clamps** are fitted **before the chine logs**; the sheer clamps themselves are little concern, being small compared to the chine logs. Whether Matt Layden's own prototype used **green or air-dried lumber** for the chine logs is not established. (For the related cabin-insulation and GPS-shielding discussion that arose in the same thread, see [plywood grade and foam flotation — foil-faced foam and radio/GPS shielding](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#foil-faced-foam-and-radiogps-shielding).)

## Internal chine logs as a rot risk, and runners as a possible substitute

During the "Stitch and Glue Paradox" thread (see [hull planking](/construction/hull-planking.md)), a related structural idea was raised: since the runners already run along the chine line, a heavy **inside fillet** plus the runners themselves could potentially substitute for a conventional **internal chine log**, making a separate log a "belt and suspenders" redundancy. The reasoning: internal chine logs are commonly where **rot starts** in a plywood hull — a risk considered reduced on Paradox specifically because the **enclosed cabin** keeps standing water out of that area even when the boat sits outdoors. This remains a speculative builder observation rather than a tested technique.

## Build order: runners integral with the bottom, not a sacrificial add-on

Building the runners **integral with the bottom**, per the methods above, is the right order — not fully glassing and sealing the hull bottom first and applying the chine runners afterward as a kind of sacrificial rub rail. That alternative sequence might seem to isolate the sealed bottom from any leak path if the runners were ever damaged (fitting them on afterward with just a simple epoxy bond and a light fiberglass taping), but it misreads the design: an integral runner built up as drawn is "built like a tank," and anything capable of doing serious damage to the chines would already be causing much bigger problems than a bit of wet plywood. Matt Layden's design intent was never "make the runners easy to repair if damaged," but rather "make them strong enough that they don't get damaged" in the first place — an argument for building them integral and robust rather than as a deliberately separable, replaceable part.

This matters even for a hull that expects routine abuse: a Paradox used for diving rocky waters, for instance, can expect the same rubs and bumps against rocks that a plywood-bottomed dive boat already takes elsewhere, with any deep gouges simply inspected and repaired as they appear rather than avoided altogether. The plywood bottom itself aids in fairing the runners to a smooth curve during construction, and repairs of this kind carry no haul-out cost on a trailerable boat this size.

[59] Archive message page_4667 (Jim Baltaxe, `paradoxbuilders`, msgnum 4705, 2007-09-06) — "Re: Chine runners": new correspondent, Island Bay, Wellington, New Zealand; proposes closed-cell foam (blue foam or PVC) as a runner core in place of solid timber, since the runners aren't structural and will be glass-sheathed regardless — foam bends easily into shape even after the hull is already faired; suggests cutting the bottom panel slightly wide of the hull sides to leave a fair-curved "shelf" the length of the hull, giving the runner a solid bottom surface, with foam laminated onto the shelf's top.

[62] Archive message page_4670 (alex29 / "tidybowlmann" / Jack Gardiner, `paradoxbuilders`, msgnum 4708, 2007-09-06) — "Re: Chine runners": disagrees with the grounding-vulnerability concern from firsthand experience — having repeatedly grounded his own boat on beaches, dock pilings, and sat in surf, using 12 oz biaxial glass (heavier than the plans' specified roving) over the runners, with the bottom, upswept part of the runners glassed in that same heavier weave, reports no damage, only ordinary scuffs repainted as needed; anything hitting hard enough to break a chine runner would risk piercing the hull's 1/2 in plywood sides regardless, and actually striking the lighter-glassed, heeled-over part of the runner hard enough to matter would be quite a feat; a prudent captain avoiding hazards matters more than over-building the part. Signed "Jack."
