# Chine runner construction technique

This concept documents **how builders physically make and fit** the
[Paradox](/paradox.md)'s chine runners — as distinct from
[how chine runners work](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md) (the mechanism) and
[windward performance](/chine-runners/windward-performance.md) (the sea-trial
evidence). The runners' central construction challenge is that they **change
shape in three dimensions** as the stations move aft, so most of the recorded
methods are different ways to solve that same compound curve.

The runners are **not "fillets" in the traditional sense**: they are built from
the plywood-and-glass/epoxy bottom plus **triangular fairing blocks** with two
rolling bevels (one against the hull, one on the outboard face), shaped for a
**hydrodynamic** reason rather than as a simple structural fillet — a curved
fairing block, convex or concave, produces more drag than a straight one. As
built, the **18 mm plywood** bottom is covered in roughly **6 mm of glass/epoxy**
— enough that the runners need no bedding or electrolysis precautions at all, and
any damage is easily repaired in place. This is why builders caution against
facing them in metal (below).

## The pattern question

[Don Elliott](/people/don-elliott.md)'s "Building the Paradox" articles describe
making a pattern from the **outside curve of the hull** before the bottom is
fitted, for use later when constructing the chine runners. It leaves open
whether the runner timber is cut **to that pattern first** — so it already
carries the correct horizontal curve, leaving only a vertical bend to install —
or whether an additional step is involved. Builders working without Don's
articles in hand arrived at several independent alternatives, below.

## Method 1 — Pre-cut solid-timber blocks, shaped on a bandsaw

Building "[Zoë](/boats/zoe.md)" without Don's articles in hand at this stage,
[Glen C. Maxwell](/people/glen-maxwell.md) built his chine runners out of
**small pieces of fir 2×4, cut to shape and glued along the overhang** — solid
timber rather than plywood, reasoning the runners take more abuse than plywood
could comfortably absorb — then gave them "ample glass covering."

The runners were **pre-manufactured** rather than bent into place: each piece
was roughly **10 in long**, small enough to cut out easily on a **band saw** —
rated the single most valuable power tool for building a boat ("if I was only
allowed one mounted power tool"), table saw second. Watching classified ads for
secondhand tools pays off (a 50-year-old cast-iron Sears table saw, bought used
for $50 some twenty years earlier, still does the job). Because the rolling
bevels are a real challenge to install as drawn, this pre-cut-block method has
become the **converging practice** among builders who work the problem
independently.

## Method 2 — Kerf-and-bend a single shaped timber

A potentially easier method works from the drawings directly: since the plans
give the chine's shape, angle, and size at each frame/station, cut timber to the
drawing's shape and size, then **saw a series of kerfs** through the back and
bottom edges — spaced roughly **4–6 in apart** — leaving about **12 mm of wood**
parallel to the outside edge uncut. Because the chine sweeps up more sharply
toward the stern, the kerfs there can be cut **deeper on the bottom edge**,
letting that portion bend into a **tighter curve**. Once bent into place,
**epoxy is squeezed into the saw kerfs** to fill and stiffen them, in keeping
with an old UK boatbuilding saying that predates epoxy: "in glue and dust we put
our trust." The chine member is heavy on the hull's inside and glass-sheathed
outside, so the kerfing does not compromise the joint.

## Method 3 — Batten-and-fill

[Alastair](/people/alastair.md) laid a single **batten the length of the runner**
and filled the gap between batten and hull with **scrap wood bits and epoxy** to
build up the final shape, rather than pre-manufacturing separate blocks. Laying
up **full-length, pre-shaped strakes** this way proved **almost impossible to
bend to shape and hold** while the epoxy cured, so the practical fix is to cut
the runners into **shorter sections**, glue each in as a "three-dimensional
jigsaw," and — once set — grind them fair to templates with a **sanding disc on
an angle grinder**.

## Method 4 — Templates, epoxy fill, and a hand plane

The runners' reputation for difficulty is overstated: a straightforward
fit-and-fair sequence — **templates**, **epoxy** to fill any low spots, then a
hand plane to bring down the high spots once cured — works with **no real
problem** and "turned out great" for at least one builder ([David
Beard](/people/david-beard.md)), a method [Alastair](/people/alastair.md)
seconded from his own build. The recurring lesson across all four approaches is
that builders **converge on whatever compound-curve solution suits their own
tools and patience**; none of the methods is difficult, but each respects the
runners' changing-in-three-dimensions curvature.

## Sven Yrvind's composite method (YRVIND ½)

[Sven Yrvind](/references/yrvind-website.md) built the runners of his composite
YRVIND ½ by a wholly different, moldless technique suited to a glassfibre hull:

1. **Attach plywood pieces** to the hull, insulated against the epoxy with
   **packing tape** (so they release later).
2. **Clamp a glassfibre sheet** to them, fastening it to the hull with a
   **fillet** — forming a shelf standing out from the chine.
3. Once that shelf is set, it **receives several layers of glassfibre**. Because
   the runners are exposed to grounding, Yrvind builds them **strong — about
   20 mm (¾ in) thick.**
4. **Grind to a fair shape** outdoors once the boat is moved outside.

Yrvind also documented **how Matt Layden built Enigma's chine runner** in
composite — a fit-up, glue-up, and rough-shaping sequence — noting that on a
plywood boat like Paradox the solid-timber methods above apply, while a composite
boat calls for his layup approach instead.

## Grounding wear, and why the runners aren't metal-edged

The chine runners, along with the **rudder**, are the **first parts to hit
anything** in shallow water — though when the boat dries out fully, the
**whole bottom** takes the brunt rather than the runners alone.

Fitting a **metal edge** for protection is discouraged unless the builder has
genuine metalworking skills: the runners have been **shaped for a hydrodynamic
reason**, and changing their shape (as a metal edge or shoe might require) risks
**changing the boat's windward ability**. The glass/epoxy sheathing already
makes them tough and easily repaired, so metal buys little and risks much. (This
is consistent with the parallel argument against bilge keels and metal chine
shoes — see [stretched Paradox](/design/variants/stretched-paradox.md).)

### Bronze versus steel, if a builder insists on a shoe

For a builder set on a metal shoe anyway, **¼ in steel plates** bolted and
bedded under the hull in the runner's own profile trade lead's low weight for
edge-protection toughness, but **steel plate bedded to wood** carries a real
corrosion risk once the finish is damaged, and trust in bedding compounds
between dissimilar materials is not well founded. **Bronze plate** with bronze
fasteners is the more durable, if costlier, alternative: electrolytic action
becomes much less of a worry, and a scraped bronze edge stays sound under
ordinary antifouling even after striking rocks. One compromise is an **epoxy
coating on the steel plus 3M 5200** as the bedding — bedding epoxy-coated steel
to an epoxy/glass coating rather than bare wood — though whether the coating
would survive scraping without exposing the steel to rapid corrosion remains
untested.

A composite-hull build reached the same bronze-over-steel conclusion from a
different angle: fitting a metal runner base to the ["BRIS
Paradox"](/boats/bris-paradox.md), [Kjell Karlsson](/people/kjell-karlsson.md)
first planned a **stainless-steel sheet** for the bottom (doing double duty as
ballast) before switching to **bronze** — costlier, but bondable and useful as a
**lightning-protection ground plate** as well as a runner base, with the sheet
protruding sideways to form the base the epoxy/fibreglass-filler runner body is
built up on. A related, standing piece of advice for any Paradox owner
launching from a beach: **keep your toes clear**, since the runners and rudder
are the first parts to touch bottom.

The adhesive question for a metal runner base is settled: bonded on with an
**NM (Nils Malmgren)** elastic epoxy filler, with **no mechanical fasteners**
at all, rather than through-bolted or riveted. Epoxy essentially will not
adhere to stainless steel, but a builder of the related "Sofia" design gets a
durable bond onto stainless anyway by heavily grinding the sheet and applying
an elastic filler within seconds of grinding — necessary because the filler is
markedly thixotropic and begins to set almost immediately; that bond outlasted
the polyester laminate beneath it when tested years later by attempted removal
(the laminate tore first). Bronze poses no comparable gluing problem, but the
same elastic-epoxy practice between polyester and metal applies regardless — an
elastic bondline is the right choice for a metal-to-composite joint either way.

A separate corrosion-control technique carries over from aluminum to bronze
bonding (below): coat the surface with epoxy *first*, then sand through the
still-wet epoxy, so the freshly abraded metal never meets open air. On the
"Zoë" rivet-hole reinforcement technique (drilling and countersinking holes
along a bonded edge so cured epoxy acts like a rivet, below), a manufacturer's
specification promising no gluing problem with bronze made that extra step
unnecessary in the BRIS case.

On bending the bronze sheet to the runner's curve, three fallback plans were
tried in sequence as each proved harder than expected: **Plan A**, hammering
the 2 mm bronze sheet round a curved pattern by hand or with a mechanical
consultant's help, ran into a work-hardening risk (bronze beaten or hammered
work-hardens and becomes brittle — below); **Plan B**, grinding away material
and bonding on a separately curved strip, "sounds impossible"; a related
alternative — laminating thickness onto the edge from offcut strips at bow and
stern, glued, riveted, brazed, or welded up to sufficient thickness and then
ground to the curve — was ruled out for welding specifically, since the
divinycell foam core cannot take welding heat, leaving gluing as the only
practical build-up method; **Plan C**, leaving the curve unresolved and finding
out the hard way whether it has any hydrodynamic effect at all, was "seemingly
the most probable" outcome. Without knowing a given sheet's exact
tin/copper/phosphorus composition, its workability can't be predicted from
general principles — a supplier specification and a blacksmithing reference
(anvilfire.com) are the way to check, and testing two scale models (one with
the curve's radius, one without) in a small stream would show whether the
radius carries any real hydrodynamic effect. Bronze is an excellent choice for
the bottom regardless of that outcome: even if the curve can't be reproduced
and only a straight edge results, bronze-epoxy-divinycell construction leaves
no wood to rot out at the chine, unlike a timber runner. In practice, bending
tests on bronze scraps suggest 2 mm sheet is not brittle and can be bent
considerably — the real problem is simply that 2 mm is a thick gauge to bend by
hand.

### Fabricating chine runners in steel, if the whole hull is steel

The bronze/stainless case above assumes a **plywood-and-glass** hull with
only the runner's own base plated in metal. A related, more radical question
is fabricating the chine runners themselves in **steel**, on a hull built
entirely in that material (see [aluminum and steel Paradox
(speculative)](/design/aluminum-construction-idea.md) for the wider steel-hull
debate this assumes as background). A simpler alternative to fabricating the
runner in steel at all is a **hardwood chine runner bolted onto a steel hull**:
shape the runner in oak or teak per the plans' frame-by-frame profile, drill
through from inside and braze the bolts in position from outside, counter-bore
the back face for nuts and washers, dry-fit and mark the bolts for length, seal
the back face with wood sealer, bed it in mastic, and plug and seal the bolt
holes with the plugs' grain running fore-and-aft. Fabricating the runner in
steel itself, including its radiused outer edge, is "quite an undertaking,"
though the radius could simply be omitted on a steel build to ease the job.

Set against that caution, a professional fabricator's view (see [his own
profile](/people/jack-gardiner.md)) is that steel runners pose no real
difficulty regardless of hull size — "not a difficult undertaking... period"
next to far more complex fabrication work seen done in commercial shipyards
(submarine steam-loop piping, boiler-tube welding in confined spaces, spiral
staircase handrails). The main caution is against mixing too many dissimilar
materials on one hull; modern, affordable, even rentable tools (plasma cutters,
angle grinders, small wire-feed welders) put steel fabrication within reach of
a backyard builder. The runner's curved outer edge specifically can be formed
by **welding a length of pipe along the edge** to get the radius, or by
plasma-cutting and working the profile out directly.

The "not difficult" view has a caveat: aimed at a professional fabricator, it
understates the risk for an ordinary backyard builder. Producing the chine
runners in steel requires at least two full-length welds, and even a skilled
welder can leave a pinhole or two in a weld that long; any resulting water
intrusion inside the runner reaches unprotected steel and starts corrosion
from the inside out — a failure mode seen firsthand in local boatyards
littered with steel boats whose once-sound runners had rusted through. If a
steel hull must have steel chine runners, hiring a genuinely skilled welder for
that specific joint is worth the cost, given how much rides on weld quality
alone.

The pinhole concern has a direct answer, though: coating the welded steel in
**epoxy** — a known practice among backyard steel-hull builders — fills any
pinholes before priming and painting, and ordinary maintenance thereafter
prevents the water intrusion described above, just as neglect would eventually
damage an unprotected wood boat too. Welding correctly is "not rocket
science"; small welds on a boat this size can be tacked and moved incrementally
to manage heat distortion (a routine, "common textbook" practice, not the
difficulty sometimes implied), and backyard builders successfully cruising
40-foot-plus steel boats show the approach scales down fine to a
Paradox-sized hull. Separately: **working bronze by beating or hammering it can
work-harden the metal**, making it brittle — a hazard for whichever builder
ends up hand-forming a bronze chine-runner curve (above).

On the flat-hull-and-metal-bottom question generally, a lamination alternative
avoids bending sheet metal to the runner's curve at all: build up the required
thickness at the outer edge from **offcut strips laminated in layers** —
glued, riveted, brazed, or welded on in succession until enough thickness
accumulates to grind the final curve into. This is a metal-specific analogue
of the plywood block-and-fill methods above (Methods 1 and 3), trading a
single bent sheet for a stack of flat strips that only need to be shaped, not
bent, at the end.

On a **fully composite/metal-adjacent** hull rather than steel specifically, a
corrosion-control technique for bonding epoxy to **aluminum** — which, unlike
stainless steel, begins oxidizing the instant it is sanded — carries over: coat
the surface with epoxy *first*, then sand through the still-wet epoxy, which
keeps the freshly abraded metal from ever being exposed to open air. The same
approach works equally well for bonding epoxy to **bronze**. A mechanical
reinforcement for a bonded ground-strip or sheet edge, from the
"[Zoë](/boats/zoe.md)" build: drilling holes along the plate's edge and
countersinking the outside face before bonding, so the cured epoxy filling
each hole acts like a rivet, resisting the peeling forces that are otherwise
the weakest failure mode for a glued metal-to-hull joint of this kind.

## Milling solid chine logs from a dense hardwood

A separate internal member — the **chine log** itself (see [chine log vs.
taped-seam construction](/construction/chine-log-vs-taped-seam.md) for its
structural role) — poses its own milling challenge in a dense hardwood. Milling
chine logs from **Honduras Mahogany** proved far harder to bend than the more
common **white oak**, since mahogany is much less flexible; a builder may need to
**resaw and laminate** the logs in thinner layers instead of bending them solid.
As with the runners, the difficulty is inherent to the design's shape rather than
a sign of anything wrong: the hull's narrowness means the required bend, while
severe, should still be achievable.

## Casting lead ballast into the runners

The runners' already-solved compound-curve shape invites a second use: **casting
lead ballast into them**, moving weight out of the bilge and further **outboard on
the lever arm** for greater stiffening per pound. As the windward chine begins to
surface under heel, ballast sited that far outboard sits exactly where it is most
wanted, functioning something like extreme outboard water ballast. One approach
reuses a **chine-runner-section mold** to cast lead **hardware-bonded** to the
chine log; a fuller version uses a **plaster mold per side** (built from a
bench-made doorskin/hot-glue/modeler's-clay plug), the lead **epoxied to the
plywood and fastened from below**, fully encapsulated in the glass that already
covers the runners — motivated as much by freeing bilge stowage as by stiffness,
with an estimate of no more than ~**50 lb of lead per side** this way.

### The movable-vs-fixed-ballast tradeoff

Fixing ballast to the chine log has a cost: unlike loose bilge ballast, it can no
longer be **shifted fore-and-aft** — weight forward to punch through head seas,
weight aft when running. For Paradox specifically the objection is mostly moot:
at **~1,400 lb displacement** the boat is small enough that **shifting crew
weight** — an option the inside-steering layout uniquely allows — is the
practical way to trim her, so lead ingots in the **30–40 lb** range are commonly
secured in place and not moved under sail. One builder's **~25 lb lead bricks**
(cast in an old bread tin, painted with Hammerite while warm) would be
impractical to unbolt and re-secure against a knockdown at sea, so if weight
forward were ever needed he would sooner have a crew member **sit further
forward**; he needed **250 lb** of ballast total, shifted the bricks to trim
before fixing them, and removes some as he adds stores on longer trips — using
them as exactly the movable ballast that casting into the runners would
foreclose.

### Lower-tech casting: lead birdshot in epoxy

As an alternative to a full mold, mixing **lead birdshot into epoxy** thickened
with silica to a heavy, puttylike consistency, applied directly to the runner
and worked to a rounded radius by hand, is a workable lower-tech route — a mix
that could equally go **under the glass on the hull bottom** as a
ballast-lowering base coat. An estimated **100–200 lb** of lead can be worked in
this way, with the rest made up of interior lead pigs or sandbags (sandbags
preferred by some builders to avoid skin contact with lead in the cabin).

A key **safety correction** on the mold method: a used, already-beveled runner
section **cannot itself serve as the casting plug** (the plug must be flat and
fair on the bottom face, without a rolling bevel), and whatever plug is used, the
resulting **plaster mold must be fully dried/baked before pouring lead**, or
trapped moisture can cause dangerous steam-driven splatter.

## Sheer-clamp and chine-log installation order

The **sheer clamps** are fitted **before the chine logs**; the sheer clamps
themselves are little concern, being small compared to the chine logs. Whether
Matt Layden's own prototype used **green or air-dried lumber** for the chine
logs is not established. (For the related cabin-insulation and
GPS-shielding discussion that arose in the same thread, see [plywood grade and
foam flotation — foil-faced foam and radio/GPS
shielding](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#foil-faced-foam-and-radiogps-shielding).)

## Internal chine logs as a rot risk, and runners as a possible substitute

During the "Stitch and Glue Paradox" thread (see [hull
planking](/construction/hull-planking.md)), a related structural idea was raised:
since the runners already run along the chine line, a heavy **inside fillet** plus
the runners themselves could potentially substitute for a conventional **internal
chine log**, making a separate log a "belt and suspenders" redundancy. The
reasoning: internal chine logs are commonly where **rot starts** in a plywood hull
— a risk considered reduced on Paradox specifically because the **enclosed cabin**
keeps standing water out of that area even when the boat sits outdoors. This
remains a speculative builder observation rather than a tested technique.

## Build order: runners integral with the bottom, not a sacrificial add-on

Building the runners **integral with the bottom**, per the methods above, is
the right order — not fully glassing and sealing the hull bottom first and
applying the chine runners afterward as a kind of sacrificial rub rail. That
alternative sequence might seem to isolate the sealed bottom from any leak
path if the runners were ever damaged (fitting them on afterward with just a
simple epoxy bond and a light fiberglass taping), but it misreads the design:
an integral runner built up as drawn is "built like a tank," and anything
capable of doing serious damage to the chines would already be causing much
bigger problems than a bit of wet plywood. Matt Layden's design intent was
never "make the runners easy to repair if damaged," but rather "make them
strong enough that they don't get damaged" in the first place — an argument
for building them integral and robust rather than as a deliberately separable,
replaceable part.

This matters even for a hull that expects routine abuse: a Paradox used for
diving rocky waters, for instance, can expect the same rubs and bumps against
rocks that a plywood-bottomed dive boat already takes elsewhere, with any deep
gouges simply inspected and repaired as they appear rather than avoided
altogether. The plywood bottom itself aids in fairing the runners to a smooth
curve during construction, and repairs of this kind carry no haul-out cost on
a trailerable boat this size.
