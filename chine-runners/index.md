# Chine runners

The **chine runner** is the [Paradox](/paradox.md)'s single most distinctive
feature — the tapered fins running along the hull's chine line that let a
flat-bottomed, shallow-draft boat sail to windward **without a centerboard or
keel**. It is the first thing most people notice about the boat, the feature
that most defines Matt Layden's whole family of designs, and the one Sven Yrvind
called "the greatest innovation for small cruising boats in the last century."
Because it is so central, it has its own top-level branch here rather than living
under [design](/design/index.md) or [construction](/construction/index.md).

# Concepts

* [How chine runners work](how-they-work.md) - The mechanism behind the Paradox's chine runners — the competing "deflector vs. foil" explanations (the Newtonian deflector hypothesis, the disc-vs-cup analogy, Matt Layden's hydrodynamic fence), Matt Layden's own 2005 and 2007 first-person accounts of the runner within a lifting-body system, why heavy displacement and heel are essential, and the shallow-fin-keel and waterline analogies.
* [Chine runners and windward performance](windward-performance.md) - The Paradox's chine runners deliver measurable windward performance, backed by a quantified VMG sea trial, an independent month-long test, a hard Cumberland Sound cruising confirmation, and consistent owner-reported pointing angles.
* [Chine runners — origin and evolution](origin-and-evolution.md) - Where the chine runner came from and how it changed across Matt Layden's boats — Terrapin's fouling leeboards giving way to Swamp Thing's first runners (1985, concept dated to 1982), which boats use runners versus a centerboard, the Enigma refinement, and the firsthand judgment that it is the greatest small-boat innovation of the century.
* [Chine runner construction technique](construction.md) - How builders shape, curve, and fit the Paradox's chine runners — pattern-making, pre-cut solid-timber blocks, kerf-and-bend, batten-and-fill, block-built-wall, and templates-and-plane methods; fitting order before or after the bottom panel; a closed-cell foam alternative to solid timber; Sven Yrvind's composite glassfibre technique; why builders avoid metal facing; the bronze-vs-steel-shoe debate; and casting lead ballast into the runners.

# Related branches

* [Sven Yrvind](/people/yrvind.md) - Swedish designer and ocean voyager celebrated for extremely small blue-water boats (the "Bris" series), who never posted to the Paradox list but is one of its most-mentioned outside figures — the benchmark against which Paradox's offshore capability is weighed, and the chine runner's most authoritative external champion, a personal friend of Matt Layden who sailed Paradox himself.
* [Rudder and chine experiments](/components/rudder.md) - The Paradox's oversized rudder, its design rationale and known handling trade-offs, the rejected forward-chine-extension experiment, a range of tried and untested wear-protection and rudder-resizing ideas, and technique advice for tacking problems and rudder-up sailing.
* [Weight, ballast, and displacement](/design/weight-and-ballast.md) - The Paradox's loaded-weight breakdown, lead ballast, and waterplane sensitivity — most of the weight sits low in the bilge for stability, and independent weight estimates for several boats converge on a design displacement of roughly 1,400 lb.
