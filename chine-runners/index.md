# Chine runners

The **chine runner** is the [Paradox](/paradox.md)'s single most distinctive
feature — the tapered fins running along the hull's chine line that let a
flat-bottomed, shallow-draft boat sail to windward **without a centerboard or
keel**. It is the first thing most people notice about the boat, the feature
that most defines Matt Layden's whole family of designs, and the one Sven Yrvind
called "the greatest innovation for small cruising boats in the last century."
Because it is so central, it has its own top-level branch here rather than living
under [design](/design/index.md) or [construction](/construction/index.md).

# Concepts

* [How chine runners work](how-they-work.md) - The mechanism and the competing explanations — Sven Yrvind's Newtonian "deflector, not hydrofoil" hypothesis, Alastair's disc-vs-cup analogy, and Matt Layden's hydrodynamic-fence account — plus why heavy displacement and heel are essential and the shallow-fin-keel and waterline analogies.
* [Chine runners and windward performance](windward-performance.md) - The evidence that they actually work — Matt Layden's quantified VMG sea trial, Sven Yrvind's month sailing Paradox in 1997, Glen Maxwell's five-hour Cumberland Sound confirmation, and the pointing angles owners report.
* [Chine runners — origin and evolution](origin-and-evolution.md) - Where the runner came from and how it changed across Matt Layden's boats — Terrapin's fouling leeboards giving way to Swamp Thing (1985, concept dated to 1982), which boats use runners versus a centerboard, the Enigma refinement, dimensions across the family, and Yrvind's firsthand endorsement.
* [Chine runner construction technique](construction.md) - How builders physically shape and fit the runners — pattern-making, pre-cut solid-timber blocks, kerf-and-bend, batten-and-fill, and templates-and-plane methods; Sven Yrvind's composite technique; why builders avoid metal facing; and casting lead ballast into the runners.
