# Chine runners — origin and evolution

The chine runner is [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)'s own invention, and the single feature that most defines the [Paradox](/paradox.md) and its sister designs. Its history is traced by three converging firsthand sources spanning four decades of sailing and studying the boats.

## From fouling leeboards to the first runners

After a severe knockdown off Maine, Matt abandoned the fouling leeboards of his first boat, [Terrapin](/matt-layden/terrapin.md), and on the 1985 [Swamp Thing](/matt-layden/swamp-thing.md) **first used the tapered external chine log he came to call the "chine runner."** The runners had "the added benefit of not picking up lobster-pot lines or seaweed" — the failing that had plagued the leeboards. Swamp Thing was "the **first boat to have chinerunners**," with "other boats based on the same concept" being **Paradox and Enigma**.

## The conceptual origin: Bolger's chine battens and the NASA lifting body

The runner's *physical* debut on Swamp Thing has a distinct *intellectual* origin, which Matt Layden set out in his own words in a 2007 *Small Craft Advisor* interview. Two unrelated sources fed into it. The hull form the runner serves — a "lifting body" that produces sideways lift from a pressure difference across the heeled hull — Layden traces to **1970s *Aviation Week* and *Popular Science* articles about experimental NASA aircraft**. The runner itself he traces to **Phil Bolger**: "some seemingly random comments by Phil Bolger about a couple of his sharpies with external chine battens also got me thinking about what aerodynamic engineers sometimes call a **'fence,'** to separate the high from the low pressure and minimize flow across the chine, **and that evolved into my current chine runners**." (See [sharpie lineage](/design/sharpie-lineage.md) for the fuller question of Paradox's own place in the sharpie type this Bolger-derived origin sits inside.) The fuller mechanism is described under [how they work](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md); the point here is that the feature grew from a Bolger chine-batten hint married to an aircraft lifting-body concept, not from foil practice in yacht design.

Layden is also candid about the feature's **narrow niche and uncertain prospects**. Its place, he says, "is in moderate to heavy cruisers that haul a lot of weight" — "not so great for the usual light beamy racer/cruiser or trailersailer" — and he is unassuming about how far it will spread: "Sven is kind, but I'm not sure chine runners will go far." He developed and validated the runners through **"rough sailing and towing model experiments,"** consistent with the scale-model tank-testing noted elsewhere in his design method. His own modesty is part of why the concept remained obscure (see below).

## An outside estimate of the development period

One owner's own estimate, offered without a cited source and not corroborated elsewhere in the record, puts the runners' development at **"a period of eleven years"** — read here as a rough impression rather than a dated fact, since it sits alongside, but does not resolve, the conception/first-build dating already established below.[8] The same owner's own definition of the feature, arrived at independently before he had sailed one himself, calls the runners **"wings"** that "act in some mysterious way to minimize leeway when the boat sails to windward," and reads their presence as evidence of the designer's own character: **"They indicate that he is a free-thinker; a person not caught up with restrictive traditional design. He is a pragmatic person very willing to try new ideas."**[9]

## Dating: concept 1982, first boat 1985

The two dates commonly attached to the chine runner **reconcile through a single witness**. The concept was **"created by Matt in 1982"** while **Swamp Thing, the first boat to carry it, was built in 1985**. The gap is therefore best read as **conception (1982) versus first build (1985)**, not a contradiction. (The same 1982 date recurs across sources; the first chine-runner *boat* is dated to 1985. All are consistent under the conception-vs-build reading.)

## Which boats use runners, and which use a centerboard

The split across Matt's designs is explicit: **Paradox, GJAC, and Swamp Thing use chine runners**, while [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) uses a **bow-mounted centerboard**; the later [Enigma](/matt-layden/enigma.md) and [Elusion](/matt-layden/elusion.md) **return to chine runners**. Matt went back to chine runners for Paradox after concluding Little Cruiser's centerboard "did not seem to improve windward performance sufficiently to merit its construction."

A firsthand comparison sharpens the practical verdict: sailing Paradox (chine runners) alongside Little Cruiser (centerboard), "we cannot tell which boat can point higher… both methods work equally well on Matt's designs." The chine runner's distinct edge is **really shallow water**, "where a centerboard can't be lowered sufficiently to provide adequate lateral resistance" — the runner keeps working where a board cannot.

## Dimensions across the chine-runner family

Dimensions for the family are useful for seeing how the concept scales (figures as reported; two accounts differ slightly on Swamp Thing, noted below):

| Boat | Year | Length | Beam | Draft | Notes |
|------|------|--------|------|-------|-------|
| [Swamp Thing](/matt-layden/swamp-thing.md) | 1985 | 4.0 m (13′2″) | 1.08–1.12 m (3′8″) | 18–19 cm (~7″) | first chine-runner boat; ~400 kg cruising weight |
| [Paradox](/paradox.md) | 1993 | 4.2 m (13′9″) | 1.23 m (4′) | 23 cm (9 1⁄16″) | draws ~5 cm / 2″ more than Swamp Thing |
| [Enigma](/matt-layden/enigma.md) | 2005 | 3.6 m (12′) | — | — | 80 kg (180 lb); runners "shorter and wider" |
| YRVIND ½ | — | — | 1.3 m | 22 cm | a later chine-runner boat built on the same concept |

The difference between Swamp Thing's and Paradox's draft — 5 cm — is one "most people would not notice, [but] Matt did," and reflects Matt's exacting standard for what counts as genuinely shallow draft (water "not above your knee," ~35 cm). Enigma's competitive credentials underline the concept: in **March 2006 Matt sailed the 80 kg Enigma 1,200 miles around Florida in the WaterTribe Ultimate Challenge, finishing first in her class and third overall.**

*(Discrepancy note: one 2008 account gives Swamp Thing as 1.08 m beam / 19 cm draft, while another gives 1.12 m beam / 18 cm draft. The difference is minor and left unreconciled.)*

## The Enigma refinement

The runner was **tuned, not frozen**. In 2008 Swamp Thing's runners were rebuilt **about ¼″ wider and ⅛″ thicker (~25% wider)** at Matt's suggestion to try to improve her windward performance, and Enigma's runners are described as **"shorter and wider"** than Paradox's. A second account instead describes the Enigma runners as **higher-aspect foils** of a "shorter and **deeper** shape especially created for this lightweight boat," which "Layden reports … seem to be **an improvement over Paradox's runners**." The two secondary accounts **disagree on the cross-section** ("wider" vs. "deeper") for the very same boat, so the exact shape change is left unreconciled — but both agree Enigma's runners were a deliberate, reportedly successful refinement.

## Sven Yrvind's verdict: the innovation of the century

The chine runner has its highest external endorsement from an ocean voyager and designer of radically small boats — offered from direct experience rather than theory. After sailing Paradox and studying it for a decade (see [windward performance](/chine-runners/windward-performance.md)), the conclusion was: **"I am now convinced that the chinerunner concept, created by Matt in 1982, is the greatest innovation for small cruising boats in the last century"** — restated elsewhere as "the most important innovation for small boats during the 20th century."

The reason **so few designers have adopted it** despite its being in plain sight for a quarter-century is equally clear: Matt "does not try to make his voice heard above the din from the egos in our conservative, self-centered society to promote his idea," and "the principles behind it are complex and subtle, and have been subject to little research and experiment."

## Yrvind's own boats, and where he parts ways

The concept was also adopted directly elsewhere. The **YRVIND.COM (2008)** was "a boat inspired by Matt's chinerunner series," and the later YRVIND ½ used a ballasted, near-vertical variant of the runners (see [how they work](/chine-runners/how-they-work.md)). There is **one place that adoption now diverges** from Matt: for long ocean passages a **variable lateral area** — something like a centerboard or leeboard — is preferred, because sailing downwind it gives one more way to control the boat, at the cost of added complication. The considered summary draws the boundary cleanly: **"Coastal sailing like Matt does, chinerunners is fine."** The feature is optimal for exactly the shallow-water coastal cruising the Paradox is built for.
