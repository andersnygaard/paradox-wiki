# Miramis

**"Miramis"** is the [Paradox](/paradox.md) that **Anders Nygaard** is building at home in **southern Norway**, a careful, heavily documented amateur build begun in 2020 and still under way. It is a **10% scaled-up** hull, built in **exterior-grade Baltic birch** plywood to the lighter scantlings [Simas Butavičius](/people/simas-butavicius.md) worked out, and it is the archive's clearest example of a modern builder folding **3D printing and digital fabrication** into an otherwise traditional plywood build. Nygaard was pointed at the Paradox and Matt Layden's boats a decade earlier by the Swedish designer-voyager **[Sven Yrvind](/people/yrvind.md)**, and modelled the concept in cardboard and glass fibre in a small Oslo apartment before he had the space to build.[1]

## The name

The boat is named **"Miramis"** after **Mio's white horse** in Astrid Lindgren's children's novel *Mio, min Mio* (*Mio, My Son*). Nygaard is fond of the small irony in the choice — naming a boat after a horse, a tradition already well worn by the sailing world's many boats called *Rosinante*, after Don Quixote's own nag.[22]

## The build: a 10% Paradox in Baltic birch

Nygaard is building the boat **10% larger than the plans** because he is **196 cm** tall and needed the extra cabin length to sleep and to pass through the bulkhead-2 doorway.[2] He is clear-eyed about the cost of scaling up: 10% on every dimension is about **a third more displacement**, and so more lead ballast, more trailer weight, more sheets of plywood, and three-length scarfs for the bottom and sides — the reward being roughly **20 cm more cabin length** and more storage, a change he judges to fit the design well.[2][3] It sits between the stock boat and [Eerik Mee](/people/eerik-mee.md)'s 20% "[THU](/boats/thu.md)" as one of only two documented scaled-up hulls (see [larger Paradox](/design/larger-paradox.md)). He settled on 10% as the largest scale-up that stays **trailerable**: 20% — Mee's format — is a dock boat, whereas at 10% the loaded hull still fits a road trailer, and the volume grows only about 1.33× (1.1³) against 20%'s roughly 1.72× (1.2³).[23] He weighed the more invasive alternative of building a stock-size hull and moving the saloon bulkheads to buy sleeping length, but rejected it as a change that would cost the boat other qualities, and scaled the whole boat instead.[23]

The hull is planked in **exterior-grade Baltic birch** — a plywood whose glue is the same waterproof bond used in marine ply, the "exterior" grade being the key qualifier — built to **[Simas Butavičius](/people/simas-butavicius.md)'s** lighter scantling scheme of **12 mm bottom, 9 mm sides, and 6 mm deck**, chosen to offset Baltic birch's extra weight; Nygaard credits Butavičius's own build for the inspiration.[4][5] He did **not** scale the plank thickness up with the rest of the boat — a straight 10% increase would land on unbuyable sheet thicknesses in any case — but kept Butavičius's dimensions and compensated for the proportionally thinner skin by **glassing the hull inside and out**, with the bottom and about 20 cm up the sides given an extra layer of 1708 and 600 g cloth in lieu of a keel well.[24] He works by the **screw-and-glue** method, filleting and glassing most of the joints, and cut matched side panels by routing one finished panel against the next.[6][7] He uses the screws only as temporary clamps, backing every one out and filling the hole, so **not a single nail or screw** remains in the finished hull, and every junction — bulkheads to sides and bottom, and around the chine log — is filleted and glassed; with hindsight, he says, he would have built a **stitch-and-glue** version instead.[24] The **bulkhead-2 opening is flush with the cabin floor** on this boat, extending the sleeping length forward.[3]

## Chine runners, ballast, and systems

Nygaard built the **chine runners** by laminating oak off-cuts and shaping them down with a power planer on the bench, then a final sanding once glued in place, and clad them in carbon fibre — an approach that spared him the difficulty other builders report bending a single batten around the double curve.[8][25] For **ballast** he cast his own lead in roughly 19 kg bars from more than 175 kg of scrap, planning four to six bars in the compartment just aft of bulkhead 3 and a further two to four low against the chines, positioned for trim.[9] By 2026 he had cast the lead as roughly ten ~17 kg pigs in bread tins, near **170 kg** in all — close to a stock boat's lead rather than the ~1.33× the added volume might imply, and not yet finalised.[26]

The boat is fitted out as a **systems boat**: battery management, electrical, a water pump, and a **flexible fresh-water bladder** set into the space between the water tanks (their dividing wall cut down to about a quarter to take the bladder), filled through a deck cap and drawn off by a galley tap; and a three-zone dimmable LED lighting scheme — forepeak, saloon, and galley — with a masthead-light switch, a USB-C socket, and a fuse box, mounted just aft of bulkhead 3.[10][11] The forepeak carries a dual-purpose compartment usable for stowage or for storing the mast inside the boat, with the rest of that space foam-filled for flotation and closed by a watertight hatch.[10] The deck itself is built as a **sandwich** — glass, then flotation/insulation foam, then glass — laid up from pre-produced 3 mm fibreglass panels cut to templates, with the deck beams glassed in.[24] He finishes with Hempel Light Primer under Epifanes two-part polyurethane.[12]

## 3D printing and digital fabrication

The distinguishing feature of the Miramis build is how much of it Nygaard **3D-prints**. He began by casting the lower rudder **gudgeon** in an MDF mould, then printed one instead for accuracy — a 50 mm-high part angled 7° to the transom, carrying a 22 mm bronze bushing for the pintle.[13] He printed **scarf-joint jigs** that clamp to the edge of a board (a router-bit collar stops the bit from hitting the jig), publishing a 9 mm and a 12 mm version.[14] Where ropes, hoses, and wiring pass through the bulkheads he prints **bushings and fairleads** to protect the sealed plywood end-grain and prevent chafe — most importantly where the steering ropes run through bulkheads 2 and 4 — and he prints **face plates** for the switch and fuse panels.[15][11]

## The rudder cassette — a deliberate departure

The one place Nygaard consciously departs from Matt Layden's design is the rudder. The blade is 3D-printed and clad in a heavy layup — two layers of 1708, one of 600 g carbon fibre, and three coats of epoxy worked wet-on-wet — and it is carried not on the stock hang but in a **cassette that braces the blade from both sides**, an arrangement he took from **John Welsford**'s boats to make the assembly stronger. The **tack strop** is made the same way, in **forged carbon fibre** from a 3D-printed mould. This is his single admitted break with the stock boat; the 10% scale-up itself he treats as a property of the design rather than a departure from it (see [larger Paradox](/design/larger-paradox.md)).[27]

## A home-sewn sail

Rather than buy a sail, Nygaard is **sewing his own** from 4 oz (about 160 g/m²) Dacron in 150 cm cloth aligned horizontally to the foot, with 25 mm seam overlaps, triple zig-zag seams, Sailrite Dyneema webbing for the corner rings, and Dacron tape edges.[16] He builds in a **moderate camber** — about 8–10% draft — through reduced seam overlap rather than trusting flat panels to the curved spars, following the shift Matt Layden himself made from a dead-flat first sail to shaped later ones.[17] He sews it to the **[Mk3](/components/rig/mk3-sail.md)** plan and took the sail's camber from measurements off the earlier **[Mk2](/components/rig/mk2-sail.md)** sail, working the shaping out with the help of ChatGPT.[29] His planned revision is drawn close to the mainsail **Michael Storer** designed for [Mark Brothers](/people/mark-brothers.md)'s "Pamela B."[18]

## Trailer and intended waters

Keeping the boat trailerable was a founding constraint, and Nygaard has cleared the result with Norway's road authority, **Statens vegvesen**: he has bought a trailer rated for **1250 kg and 18 feet**, which he plans to modify to suit a flat-bottomed hull.[28] Miramis is meant for the **Skagerrak** — though, he says with a straight face, officially she is bound for **Hardangervidda**, launched at the **Møsvatn** ramp to sail across to Mogen and Norway's largest tourist hut, a trip that (Møsvatn being famously shallow) needs exactly a boat like this one; he has also had standing invitations from the **Dinghy Cruising Association** to sail the **Limfjord** in Denmark and **Koster** in Sweden.[28]

## The builder, and a written guide

Nygaard has kept up a long correspondence with **[Sven Yrvind](/people/yrvind.md)**, sending him photographs of the build; into his eighties Yrvind has continued to praise the Paradox design and its chine runners.[19] Miramis is his **first boat**; a former sailboat owner and furniture-maker — "a maker at heart" — he reckons a build to a good standard runs to roughly **2,000 hours and about $10,000**, enjoys the slow work for its own sake, and steers first-time builders toward an **Oz Goose** instead.[30] In December 2023, marking the design's thirtieth year, Nygaard compiled **"A guide to the Paradox sailboat"** — a text-and-audiobook overview distilling the knowledge scattered through the old Yahoo builders' forums.[20] He posts progress on Instagram under `#paradoxboat`.[21]
