# Ironsides

**"Ironsides"** is [Stephen Yahn](/people/stephen-yahn.md)'s [Paradox](/paradox.md), started in Manchester, Washington, on Puget Sound, in July 2006. Stephen bought exterior fir plywood in mid-July 2006, reasoning the boat would spend 99% of its life on a trailer, and began construction shortly after.

## A widened, steel-reinforced bulkhead 3

Stephen opened the "Bulkhead 3" thread proposing to narrow that bulkhead's vertical plywood webs for extra sleeping width, reinforced with steel rather than more plywood (see [bulkheads — modifying bulkhead 3 to gain room for sleeping two](/components/bulkheads.md#modifying-bulkhead-3-to-gain-room-for-sleeping-two) for the full builder discussion this proposal drew). By late July 2006 he had begun construction in earnest, building the modification as steel, not plywood: a vertical web widened to 1.5 in, reinforced with steel weldments (4.8 lb each) sealed in epoxy, fastened with hot-dip galvanized bolts likewise assembled and sealed in epoxy.[1] He planned cloth, rather than plywood, bins in place of the standard fixed bin panels, did the trial assembly of the sides and bulkheads, and found fitting the stem "character building."[1] Built without bins at all, the finished modification yields a **41 in** cabin sole width.[2]

## A side-mounted outboard, fabricated to spare his hands

With his hands too old and sore for yuloing, and having already fitted a 2 hp Honda outboard, Stephen floated mounting it outside the hull near bulkhead #4 rather than on the transom or through a cabin panel, so it could be started, refueled, and throttled from the hatchway and unshipped for stowage when not needed.[3] He went ahead with the side-mount plan and started fitting blocks inside the hull for the motor mount.[4] Keeping the fuel supply in an outside plastic jerrycan rather than bottles stowed near the cooking area, he set the outboard's anticavitation plate flush with the hull bottom to keep the propeller from running deeper than, and damaging, the chine runners.[5] He posted photos of the finished side motor mount, showing its full construction — a sized-to-fit wooden pad for the Honda 2 hp motor, a hitch pin, a ball valve against flooding, a bracing shelf, and a copper (rather than plastic) grounding pipe doing double duty as a cable groove.[6] Prompted by a caution from another builder, he realized fitting the mount afloat risked shipping water as he leaned over the side to align the motor shaft, and planned to start the shaft in only as far as the ball valve before opening it, plus a lanyard since the motor would not float if dropped.[7]

## Building the vent trunk drain

Stephen chose a **one-inch copper pipe** for the vent trunk drain, doing double duty as the lightning-ground conductor down to his mast.[8] For the vent box itself, he glued together blocks of wood with a groove cut to accept the pipe liner, bonded in place with thickened epoxy, and used copper tubing for grounding his crystal radio, the pipe again doing double duty.[9] The groove was cut with two saw cuts, then a chisel to flatten the bottom, and filled around the pipe with epoxy thickened with sawdust or flour — plain kitchen wheat flour gave good long-term results — leaving the tubing proud and trimming it flush once cured.[10]

## Rudder gudgeons, deck keepers, and a first attempt at powder coating

Stephen machined a pair of rudder gudgeons from thick aluminum plate bought at Boeing surplus, and made the arched deck keepers from steel — flat discs cut from 1/4 in plate brazed to 1 in-diameter tubing bent from electric conduit fittings. To stop corrosion on the steel parts, he tried powder coating for the first time. On a suggestion, he changed the arched deck keepers' shape to a lower, stronger profile less prone to snagging rope, settling on 4 in high.[11]

## A pendulum self-steering idea

Stephen floated a pendulum-weight self-steering concept: a horizontal shaft fitted aft of the rudder axis, above the waterline, carrying a movable weight that shifts to leeward as the hull heels, offsetting the round-up tendency created as the sail's center of effort moves to leeward of the hull's center of resistance — an idea he traced to a century-old model-sailboat tactic, and one he thought fit naturally with the lead weight already cast into the plans' own rudder blade.[12] Recalling a casting pool at Lincoln Park in Santa Monica where he had sailed model boats, he asked whether the big iron rudder straps seen on some traditional full-size sailboats served a similar self-steering purpose; they turned out to be plank-reinforcement hardware unrelated to self-steering.[13][14] Locking the steering line down while sailing, the normal practice, was pointed out as already negating the pendulum idea's effect; Stephen had not yet fitted his own rudder's lead weight, leaving the idea untested on the water.[15] See [self-steering — a pendulum-weight self-steering idea, and its limits](/operations/self-steering.md#a-pendulum-weight-self-steering-idea-and-its-limits) for the full exchange.

## Building the mast: glued 2x4s and a router-groove wire conduit

Not following the plans precisely, Stephen built his mast from two ordinary construction-grade 2x4s glued together, rough-tapering it by eye with a handsaw to about 1½ in square at the top — slightly fatter than called for. Before gluing up, he router-cut a rounded groove (¼ in deep in each half) running the full length to hold a 0.5 in polyethylene tube, giving a conduit for his 6-gauge solid-copper lightning-ground wire and masthead-light wiring that can be pulled and replaced without reopening the mast. Judging an internal halyard sheave too weak-looking for a small solid mast, and awkward besides with wiring already inside, he fitted an external swivel pulley on a small shoulder near the top instead, with the conduit tube exiting the mast's side just above the foot; Gorilla Glue held the whole assembly together. Full construction detail and the wider technique discussion: [components/rig/mast.md — a glued-2x4 mast with an internal wiring and lightning-ground conduit](/components/rig/mast.md#a-glued-2x4-mast-with-an-internal-wiring-and-lightning-ground-conduit).[16]

Wondering whether a heavier mast would slow the boat's rolling motion, Stephen was assured it would not — long, narrow chine-runner hulls already roll very little regardless of mast weight, borne out both on "Zoë" and, at nearly 40 ft, on the "Hogfish Maximus" sister hull. See [components/rig/mast.md — whether a heavier mast reduces rolling](/components/rig/mast.md#whether-a-heavier-mast-reduces-rolling-no) for the fuller answer.[17]

## Deck framing, ballast, and cabin fit-out, June 2008

Asked for updated photographs,[18] Stephen reported the mast made and fitted into its home along with its own hardware, and the boom and its hardware likewise finished, with work turning next to the deck framing. The cabin's small stove still needed a guard fence to keep pots from sliding off, and the sole locker in the forward stateroom awaited installation, its pieces then being painted. Thin plywood was on hand to panel the cabin's interior sides from the seat frame forward to bulkhead 2, backed by 1-1/2 in insulation foam. For ballast, **375 lb of concrete bricks** — bought at **5 lb and $0.20 apiece**, cheaper than lead or iron and a good fit for the water-tank space — were set aside, not to be installed until launch time. Fiberglassing remained to be done, requiring the hull to be rolled over first; a mold for laminating the cabin and hatch beams had not yet been built, and the deck beams themselves were simply sawn from 2x4 stock, left at 1-1/2 in thick for buoyancy and less work. Stephen remarked that the cabin framing's many angles "look like a headache."[19]

## Fiberglassing before the cabin, a tire-cushioned roll-over, and the rudder blade's lead cast

Stephen settled the fiberglassing-versus-cabin sequencing question for his own build: doing all the glassing before building the cabin, reasoning there would then be no delicate structure bearing the hull's weight while it was rolled over. For the roll-over itself, he planned to cushion the hull on old tires, a technique already proven on an earlier boat of his own. The same update reported a dreaded job finally tackled and finished — melting lead into the rudder blade, found to be easy in practice.[20] The tire-cushioning technique drew independent confirmation from a fellow builder, who had done the same and judged it easy; that builder's own practice of fiberglassing the deck before fitting the cabin — leaving the glass's edge tucked under the cabin structure rather than butting against it, a whole sheet of cloth stretched over the deck and the openings cut out only once the epoxy had started to cure — is recorded in full at [Little Jim — glassing the deck before the cabin, recalled](/boats/little-jim.md#glassing-the-deck-before-the-cabin-recalled-june-2008).[21] Stephen credited [Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)'s own construction-photo website with directly shaping how he had sequenced this whole stage of the build.[22]

## Yuloh pivot sockets: machined Delrin, and the hull's fiberglass finished

Fitting the yuloh's pivot, Stephen machined two pockets for plastic sockets directly into the yuloh's shaft, turned from **Delrin** with a 0.750 in dia. ball end mill, each pocket 1 in in diameter and 0.625 in deep, held in place with a single #10 brass flathead screw — brass chosen so it would not scratch the steel ball — with about 0.125 in of plastic wear expected before the screw head is reached. The 0.750 in steel ball itself was drilled with a tungsten-carbide bit to take a 0.375 in dia. stainless steel lag bolt "like the plan," then brazed together and polished, in the hope the high polish would also help retard rusting. The yuloh got its first coat of varnish the same week, by which point the hull's fiberglassing was complete, ready to be turned rightside up the next day with the cabin next on the list; a coming trip to Seattle for Lexan polycarbonate for the windows was expected to test the budget.[23]

## Laminating the cabin and hatch beams, and salvaged hardwood trim

With the hull's fiberglassing finished, Stephen turned to the cabin: built a mold for laminating the cabin and hatch beams and completed a first test lamination, checking its springback the next day.[24] The result came in at only about 1/4 in per end — better than expected. Advised to build the plans' sheet-10 drip rail rather than improvise an alternative (see [window glazing — cabin condensation](/construction/window-glazing-materials.md#cabin-condensation-a-drip-rail-a-moulded-gutter-and-window-drain-holes) for the fuller condensation-management exchange this drew), Stephen settled on building it as drawn, and put reserved pieces of teak, mahogany, and an unidentified reddish wood — saved for something special — to use in the cabin's most visible trim around the hatch. The cabin framing's many odd angles proved manageable once taken one step at a time.[25]

## Painting the hull, and searching for a trailer (August 2008)

By August 2008 Stephen had moved on to painting the hull and was shopping for a good trailer, in the middle of reading a recommended self-steering reference (see [self-steering — a recommended self-steering reference, catalogued and freely available](/operations/self-steering.md#a-recommended-self-steering-reference-catalogued-and-freely-available)) but not yet finished with it.[26] The trailer question drew general advice not to skimp on the build, since a trailer that makes single-handed launching easy can be the difference between deciding to go sailing and staying home — see [trailering — single-handed launching as the real reason not to skimp](/operations/trailering.md#single-handed-launching-as-the-real-reason-not-to-skimp) for the fuller cross-link.

## Turn buttons for the sole hatches

Answering a question about the floor panels' turn buttons — the fasteners holding the sole hatches closed over stowed ballast cans — and whether they would survive a knockdown or full inversion,[27] Stephen reported having made two for his own boat, front and rear, and planned to fasten them with bolts rather than screws so a knockdown could not work them loose and spill the ballast stowed beneath.[28]

## Nearing completion: a September 2008 launch expected, and registry congratulations

Asked, in a general "who's still building" check-in from a longtime prospective builder,[29a] whether anyone was actively working on a Paradox, Stephen reported he was just finishing up and expected to launch **that month**, waiting only on a sail kit still to arrive from Sailrite, and pointed the newcomer to the photos section under "Ironsides."[29] The community registrar confirmed Stephen was the last builder added to the UK owners/builders registry and asked to be told once the boat was actually finished so her details could be amended and to hear how her sailing trials went — see [Small Sailboats / Paradox-UK websites — a UK and worldwide build count](/references/paradox-uk-website.md#a-uk-and-worldwide-build-count) for the fuller registry-keeping record this continues.[30] The announcement drew warm, unprompted congratulations: a longtime non-building well-wisher revealed his own life had since ruled out ever building a Paradox and that he had relocated to Nanaimo, BC — see [people/john-gilbert.md](/people/john-gilbert.md#life-takes-a-turn-settled-in-nanaimo-not-building-after-all) for the fuller update — while a newcomer then mid-build of his own admired Puget Sound as an ideal cruising ground for the design's enclosed cabin, given the region's frequent light drizzle and short sunny season, and predicted "Ironsides" would draw plenty of envious looks; see [people/rodney-cannon.md](/people/rodney-cannon.md#congratulating-stephen-yahn-and-praising-puget-sound) for the fuller exchange.[31][32][33] The original correspondent who had opened the thread, a longtime prospective builder curious about the boat's interior, asked to see photos and hear more about Stephen's own interior modifications once available, being himself a big man who could use the extra room.[34] A further reply from the same correspondent, having weathered Hurricane Ike's much-reduced remnants well inland in the Dallas area, checked in again on whether the boat's launch was still expected that year.[37]

## The sail kit arrives, and the interior nears completion

Days later, the sail kit arrived by UPS from Sailrite. Stephen spent that day making the tack-strop hardware and studying the enclosed sailmaking instructions — the panels themselves cut and marked out by computer and plotter, with a generous allowance of scrap cloth included to practice on first. Windows were next on the list, to be cut and fitted the following day, with the cabin sole to follow; with the weather holding fine, he weighed launching before the sail itself was finished, purely to test the trailer modifications and try out the yuloh.[35] A few days after that, with the interior very nearly together, Stephen gave his own height and weight — **5 ft 11 in and 205 lb** — finding the boat did not feel small to him at all, and promised new photographs once there was something worthwhile to show.[36]
