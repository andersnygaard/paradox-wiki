# Ironsides

**"Ironsides"** is [Stephen Yahn](/people/stephen-yahn.md)'s [Paradox](/paradox.md), started in Manchester, Washington, on Puget Sound, in July 2006. Stephen bought exterior fir plywood in mid-July 2006, reasoning the boat would spend 99% of its life on a trailer, and began construction shortly after.

## A widened, steel-reinforced bulkhead 3

Stephen opened the "Bulkhead 3" thread proposing to narrow that bulkhead's vertical plywood webs for extra sleeping width, reinforced with steel rather than more plywood (see [bulkheads — modifying bulkhead 3 to gain room for sleeping two](/components/bulkheads.md#modifying-bulkhead-3-to-gain-room-for-sleeping-two) for the full builder discussion this proposal drew). By late July 2006 he had begun construction in earnest, building the modification as steel, not plywood: a vertical web widened to 1.5 in, reinforced with steel weldments (4.8 lb each) sealed in epoxy, fastened with hot-dip galvanized bolts likewise assembled and sealed in epoxy.[1] He planned cloth, rather than plywood, bins in place of the standard fixed bin panels, did the trial assembly of the sides and bulkheads, and found fitting the stem "character building."[1] Built without bins at all, the finished modification yields a **41 in** cabin sole width.[2]

## A side-mounted outboard, fabricated to spare his hands

With his hands too old and sore for yuloing, and having already fitted a 2 hp Honda outboard, Stephen floated mounting it outside the hull near bulkhead #4 rather than on the transom or through a cabin panel, so it could be started, refueled, and throttled from the hatchway and unshipped for stowage when not needed.[3] He went ahead with the side-mount plan and started fitting blocks inside the hull for the motor mount.[4] Keeping the fuel supply in an outside plastic jerrycan rather than bottles stowed near the cooking area, he set the outboard's anticavitation plate flush with the hull bottom to keep the propeller from running deeper than, and damaging, the chine runners.[5] He posted photos of the finished side motor mount, showing its full construction — a sized-to-fit wooden pad for the Honda 2 hp motor, a hitch pin, a ball valve against flooding, a bracing shelf, and a copper (rather than plastic) grounding pipe doing double duty as a cable groove.[6] Prompted by a caution from another builder, he realized fitting the mount afloat risked shipping water as he leaned over the side to align the motor shaft, and planned to start the shaft in only as far as the ball valve before opening it, plus a lanyard since the motor would not float if dropped.[7]

## Building the vent trunk drain

Stephen chose a **one-inch copper pipe** for the vent trunk drain, doing double duty as the lightning-ground conductor down to his mast.[8] For the vent box itself, he glued together blocks of wood with a groove cut to accept the pipe liner, bonded in place with thickened epoxy, and used copper tubing for grounding his crystal radio, the pipe again doing double duty.[9] The groove was cut with two saw cuts, then a chisel to flatten the bottom, and filled around the pipe with epoxy thickened with sawdust or flour — plain kitchen wheat flour gave good long-term results — leaving the tubing proud and trimming it flush once cured.[10]

## Rudder gudgeons, deck keepers, and a first attempt at powder coating

Stephen machined a pair of rudder gudgeons from thick aluminum plate bought at Boeing surplus, and made the arched deck keepers from steel — flat discs cut from 1/4 in plate brazed to 1 in-diameter tubing bent from electric conduit fittings. To stop corrosion on the steel parts, he tried powder coating for the first time. On a suggestion, he changed the arched deck keepers' shape to a lower, stronger profile less prone to snagging rope, settling on 4 in high.[11]

## A pendulum self-steering idea

Stephen floated a pendulum-weight self-steering concept: a horizontal shaft fitted aft of the rudder axis, above the waterline, carrying a movable weight that shifts to leeward as the hull heels, offsetting the round-up tendency created as the sail's center of effort moves to leeward of the hull's center of resistance — an idea he traced to a century-old model-sailboat tactic, and one he thought fit naturally with the lead weight already cast into the plans' own rudder blade.[12] Recalling a casting pool at Lincoln Park in Santa Monica where he had sailed model boats, he asked whether the big iron rudder straps seen on some traditional full-size sailboats served a similar self-steering purpose; they turned out to be plank-reinforcement hardware unrelated to self-steering.[13][14] Locking the steering line down while sailing, the normal practice, was pointed out as already negating the pendulum idea's effect; Stephen had not yet fitted his own rudder's lead weight, leaving the idea untested on the water.[15] See [self-steering — a pendulum-weight self-steering idea, and its limits](/operations/self-steering.md#a-pendulum-weight-self-steering-idea-and-its-limits) for the full exchange.
