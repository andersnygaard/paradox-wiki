# Zoë

**"Zoë"** is the [Paradox](/paradox.md) built by professional boat builder [Glen C. Maxwell](/people/glen-maxwell.md), who founded the original `paradoxbuilders` Yahoo group. Her build hours, ballasting, interior fit-out, and a Cumberland Sound sea trial supply some of the best-documented real-world figures for the design.

# The build

- The build took **roughly 1,000 hours spread across about two years** of on-again, off-again work (as a fun project rather than a job, "as the spirit moves me"), with an intense final push in the **last three months** — a thousand hours works out to roughly six months of full-time (40 hr/week) labor, and the boat could have been finished in considerably less elapsed time had the work been continuous. The **vent trunk** stood out as a particularly labor-intensive single component. Finish quality is what drives total build time: an early, partway-through estimate of ~500 hours was superseded by the settled ~1,000-hour figure once the boat was complete. See [build effort](/construction/build-effort.md).
- Ahead of painting, the non-skid deck pattern (walnut shells in epoxy; see [non-skid deck](/construction/non-skid-deck.md)) was laid out after firming up all deck-hardware positions, with the bottom already painted (see [bottom painting](/construction/bottom-painting.md)).
- Construction photos of "Zoë" seeded the group's files area (the "construction" and "stove" folders).

Techniques used on "Zoë" are documented as general concepts elsewhere: [hull planking](/construction/hull-planking.md) (screwing the bottom on from the stern), a preference for removable screws over left-in nails ([fastening and gluing](/construction/fastening-and-gluing.md)), and [panel layout](/construction/panel-layout.md) choices. She was built to the plans on anchor stowage despite a builder's dislike of a below-deck anchor (see [ground tackle](/operations/ground-tackle.md)).

"Zoë" was built using a lower, **"ext A/B"** grade of plywood rather than Marine grade. The extra finishing labor needed to reach a fine finish with that grade proved unacceptably high, though it did give an acceptable **workboat finish** with less labor overall. See [plywood grade and foam flotation debate](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md).

## Launch and first sail

"Zoë" was **launched on Friday the 13th** [of November] 2001 and taken out for her first sail:

- Conditions were **light**: less than 5 kts of wind (measured by an anemometer).
- Boat speed **"ghosted" along between 2.2 and 3.3 kts** by GPS — respectable progress for such light air.
- "Zoë" was **very light** at this point: only about **75 lb of ballast** and **no water on board** (contrast the full loaded-ballast design figures in [weight and ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md), which call for 400 lb of lead alone).
- Even in this under-ballasted state, **stability was still quite good**: she could only be heeled to about **45 degrees** by standing on the dock and pulling the mast down by hand, and was expected to be "a treat when properly ballasted."
- She sailed for about **two hours** before hauling out for a trip to Philadelphia, and all went "very well."
- Her finish looked "as if she just left the ..factory."

## Trailer modifications for shallow launching

"Zoë"'s trailer was modified to allow launching in **less than two feet of water**, illustrated with photos posted to the group's albums (under "Zoe"):

- The **axle is lengthened** (visible from the extended fenders) and mounted **on top of the leaf spring** rather than under it, raising the hull.
- The **stub axle/spindle is welded to the top of the axle** tube.
- The **bunk boards are mounted directly on the trailer frame**, rather than raised on separate risers.

A same-base-size trailer needs only these axle, fender, and bunk-board modifications to reach a similarly low launch height (see [secondhand Paradox market](/operations/secondhand-market.md)).

See also mast fit lessons from finishing "Zoë"'s spar in [mast interior sealing and anchor-light fit](/construction/mast-sealing-and-fit.md) — the completed mast, with anchor light fitted, proved about 1.5 in too long to stow with the inspection port fully closed.

## Scale photos and first overnighter

Because a transom-port photo alone made "Zoë" look "enormous," a photo with **one or two people aboard** — including one of the builder sitting inside — was posted to give a true sense of her scale, alongside four more photos from "Zoë"'s **first overnighter**: the boat was loaded with a week's stores and full water tanks, and "all went well." She could still use **another ~100 lb of ballast** to stiffen her up further — as built and loaded for the overnighter she was still "a little too tender" when moving forward on deck to handle anchor or dock lines, though walking around would be easier once more weight went in. A week-long sea trial on Florida's west coast followed once further ballasted.

The photos also prompted a question about plywood **checking**, since "Zoë" was built of fir plywood; see [plywood grade and foam flotation debate](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md) for the fuller exchange on that question.

## Interior size and the chart table, shown photographically

How large a Paradox's interior really is, and how big a crew she can handle, is answered here visually rather than with more figures — a recurring question going back to the December 2001 ["wearing the boat"](/operations/crew-size-and-fit.md) discussion. Photos of a person **sitting inside "Zoë"** were posted to the group's files area (Interior folder) to give a real sense of interior scale, alongside separate photos of a **chart-table solution**, including one showing it in use while seated. The tight, single-hander-oriented interior is underscored by a wry aside that the images should let viewers "figure out how many close (intimate) friends" they would want to go sailing with. This is the first record of a **chart table** being fitted to a Paradox, though no further detail on its construction or location survives beyond the photos themselves. See [crew size and cabin fit](/operations/crew-size-and-fit.md) for the fuller "wearing the boat" discussion these photos speak to.

## The skylight and chart table in practical use: a Cumberland Sound cruise

Months after first showing the hatch skylight and chart table only in still photos (see [interior and chart-table photos](#interior-size-and-the-chart-table-shown-photographically) above), a weekend cruise of **Cumberland Sound** (St. Mary's River entrance, GA) put both to actual use — the fullest single trip report on record for "Zoë" since her original launch. On an overcast day spent helming from below with the hatch closed, the **skylight** allowed seeing the sail for trimming without going on deck, and, once heeled over, seeing to leeward without bending down to the leeward windows — an unanticipated bonus. The **wheelbarrow inner tube** seat cushion (an idea from *Sailing Just for Fun*, previously only proposed — see [Glen C. Maxwell — a seat-cushion comfort tip](/people/glen-maxwell/build-and-zoe.md)) "worked out better then hoped for" once actually used underway.

The same cruise supplied a real-world **windward-performance** data point for the [chine runners](/chine-runners/windward-performance.md) (nearly five hours short-tacking against 12 kt winds and a foul current, averaging 3.6 kt over 18 nm) and turned up a newly found **mast-heel play** problem possibly costing some of that performance (see [mast interior sealing and anchor-light fit](/construction/mast-sealing-and-fit.md)). It also yielded a **helm-balance fix** via halyard position (see [sailing characteristics and rig balance — Glen's CE/CLR diagnosis](/operations/sailing-characteristics.md) for the related "Sail balance" thread this experiment answered), a cold-night cabin-heat data point (see [cabin heating for cold-weather sailing](/operations/cabin-heating.md)), and two on-the-water mishaps recovered without outside help: a **trailer-tire blowout** before reaching the ramp (see [trailering — a blowout despite a new trailer](/operations/trailering.md)) and a **halyard-knot failure and furling-line jam** cleared underway (see [furling system — a halyard-knot failure and a furling-line jam](/components/rig/furling.md)). See [Glen C. Maxwell — "Building encouragement"](/people/glen-maxwell/build-and-zoe.md) for the full, single-message trip report all of these threads are drawn from.

## Condensation, CE/CLR, and a third weight estimate

"Zoë" carries **no insulation** and has not suffered for it on window-condensation drainage: a lip surrounding the cabin collects condensation running off the windows before it can pool, and through-ventilation carries off cabin moisture at the cost of a colder cabin (see [cabin heating — a window-condensation channel](/operations/cabin-heating.md)). Her CE/CLR relationship was never worked out numerically; the low-tech hand method for finding both is documented instead (see [sailing characteristics — a newcomer asks Glen to quantify CE/CLR](/operations/sailing-characteristics.md)). A **third, independent weight estimate** for "Zoë" put her at 650-750 lb empty with ballast, roughly 900 lb loaded for a month's cruise, against a 1,400 lb designed displacement — without ever actually scaling her (see [weight, ballast, and displacement — a third weight figure](/design/weight-and-ballast.md)). On the **hatch skylight**, some two and a half years later: it produces no condensation of its own.[41]

## Sailing with no water aboard, and a tiller stop

"Zoë" has sailed with **no water aboard**, and "did just fine" — though [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md) judged her to look "a little low in the water," suggesting either **removing some ballast** or simply day-sailing without filling the tanks, rather than always loading her to full cruising trim. This is a small but concrete real-world data point on how a Paradox handles lightly loaded, complementing the stability diagnosis for "[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)"'s own unballasted launch (see [weight, ballast, and displacement — an unloaded launch](/design/weight-and-ballast.md)).

A **tiller stop**, once fitted, "does help a lot" with the recurring difficulty of finding the right spot to tie off the tiller while working the yuloh — a problem that "does take some getting used to."

"Zoë" was also fitted with a purpose-built windvane self-steering gear, sea-tried for the first time at the 2007 Cedar Key gathering, where the mainsheet broke its oar carrier before a full trial could be made. See [self-steering — Glen Maxwell's horizontal-axis wind-vane concept for "Zoë"](/operations/self-steering.md#glen-maxwells-horizontal-axis-wind-vane-concept-for-zoë) and [self-steering — sea trial and damage at the 2007 Cedar Key gathering](/operations/self-steering.md#sea-trial-and-damage-at-the-2007-cedar-key-gathering) for the full design and trial account.

## The choice to leave "Zoë" without foam flotation

"Zoë" carries no foam flotation at all — a deliberate choice, restated and explained in full more than three years after launch. Across 45 years of sailing — including an Atlantic crossing aboard "[Hogfish Maximus](/people/chris-morejohn.md#contributions-and-stories)" — Glen never owned an unsinkable boat, and weighs flotation against a competing priority: being able to inspect behind it for dry rot or accumulated moisture. The plans' specified foam, concentrated in the deck head above the tiller-port opening, is suspected of doing more duty as **insulation** than as reserve buoyancy, and costs precious interior volume in so small a boat. The door-skin **[removable foam panel](/construction/removable-foam-panels.md)** technique — mounting foam on a paintable, unscrewable panel rather than gluing it to the hull — resolves the inspection problem in principle, but on "Zoë" herself the foam is left out altogether, with interior fittings mounted directly to blocks glued to the hull sides and deck head instead. The choice was considered unsafe elsewhere on the list; the settled verdict, after three years' sailing without it, is that the trade-off is worth making on this boat.[51] See [plywood grade and foam flotation debate](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md) for the fuller foam-vs-no-foam debate and a contrasting experience with glued-in foam on "[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)."

In July 2006 Glen put up a personal website with photographs of "Zoë" posted for the first time outside the group's own photo sections. ## A rudder up-haul safety lapse

A 50-mile round trip to try the wind-vane gear again turned up a rigging lapse: the rudder up-haul line had come loose, or had been left loose in the rush to get sailing, costing roughly **15%** of the rudder's area by the time the boat reached the ramp. The usual practice of running the mainsheet through the up-haul to safety it against exactly this loss was forgotten as well. Rather than abandon the outing, the exposed end-grain wood was coated with **Marine Goop** and the boat launched regardless — sailing in **16 mph gusting to 18 mph** with little if any noticeable difference in the helm despite the reduced rudder area. See [rudder — a lost rudder up-haul, and sailing on with reduced rudder area](/components/rudder.md#a-lost-rudder-up-haul-and-sailing-on-with-reduced-rudder-area) for the cross-linked rudder-hardware angle.[42]

## A full sea trial and course-holding test

The wind-vane gear (see [self-steering — Glen Maxwell's horizontal-axis wind-vane concept for "Zoë"](/operations/self-steering.md#glen-maxwells-horizontal-axis-wind-vane-concept-for-zoë) and [self-steering — sea trial and damage at the 2007 Cedar Key gathering](/operations/self-steering.md#sea-trial-and-damage-at-the-2007-cedar-key-gathering) for the design and its earlier, damage-cut-short Cedar Key outing) got a full, dedicated sea trial on 8 July 2007, once repaired. After sorting out minor teething problems underway — the oar tiller jumping out of its crank, the vane blade blowing out of alignment in strong gusts, and vibration-loosened screws — the gear held a steady 180° heading, plus or minus a couple of degrees, after a short period of tuning the course-setting lines, in 16 mph gusting to 19 mph wind. Full technical account of the trial and the vane's drive mechanism (explained afterward in response to reader questions) is at [self-steering — the 8 July 2007 sea trial: a full course-holding test](/operations/self-steering.md#the-8-july-2007-sea-trial-a-full-course-holding-test) and [self-steering — the vane's drive mechanism, explained](/operations/self-steering.md#the-vanes-drive-mechanism-explained).[43][44][45][46][47][48][49][50]

## Foam flotation on "Zoë" revisited, and window tint

A foam-flotation question drew Glen's reasoning on "Zoë" full circle: foam was left off her side walls and top deck specifically to keep the hull's inside visible for moisture inspection (see [the choice to leave "Zoë" without foam flotation](#the-choice-to-leave-zoë-without-foam-flotation) above), with the door-skin removable-panel method — worked out only after "Zoë" was finished — as the way to have both foam and inspectability on a later build; see [removable foam flotation panels](/construction/removable-foam-panels.md) for the technique in full.[54] A companion article on painting the hull, referenced from memory in the same reply, could not be relocated in the files section despite a search.[54]

Asked to confirm whether "Zoë"'s cabin windows were tinted, Glen reported barely being able to tell they were tinted from inside even at night, with very good visibility — the difference only becomes noticeable when the sun is shining directly on the glazing. See [window glazing — window tint color](/construction/window-glazing-materials.md#window-tint-color) for the fuller tint-color record this adds to.[55]

## Continued vane refinement: an undersized blade, and the autopilot coupling

Renewed light-air testing of "Zoë"'s wind-vane self-steering gear went poorly in November 2007, blamed on an undersized vane blade due for enlargement, with drawings deferred until the gear worked reliably; a run of minor assembly troubles was corrected as it turned up. See [self-steering — renewed testing: an undersized vane blade, and a rolling-hitch tillerpilot adapter](/operations/self-steering.md#renewed-testing-an-undersized-vane-blade-and-a-rolling-hitch-tillerpilot-adapter) for the fuller technical exchange, including the Autohelm 800's own tiller-line rolling-hitch coupling method.[56] Photographs of the Autohelm coupling followed, posted to a new "Zoë's Autopilot Setup" files folder;[57][58] mounting on the port side was judged the easier of the two, keeping the pilot clear of the compass.[59] See [self-steering — photographs of the autopilot coupling, and further tiller-lock variants](/operations/self-steering.md#photographs-of-the-autopilot-coupling-and-further-tiller-lock-variants) for the fuller exchange, including a further tiller-lock design shown in the photos.

## Press coverage: two 2003 magazine articles

"Zoë" and her 2001 shakedown cruise reached print twice in the same year. *Messing About In Boats*' August 15, 2003 issue carried an article with photographs from the six-day, west-Florida shakedown cruise (see [sea trials route](#sea-trials-route-cedar-key-to-clearwater) above), praising "Zoë" throughout, listing several in-progress improvements made along the way, and including tips on catching fish for dinner underway. A change of postal address had left the boat's builder roughly four issues behind on his own subscription, without a copy in hand until the editor arranged to send the missing copies. The article was later scanned and uploaded to the group's files section — after a few failed attempts, and with the photos stripped out to cut file size — as both a full-color version (nearly 2 MB, a long download over dial-up) and a smaller photo-free version, several months after the original print date.

The article's UK readership led to its reproduction on the **paradox-uk.co.uk** website (copyright remaining with the original author), posted as "Zoë's Sea Trials" at `www.btinternet.com/~w.serjeant/paradox/trials.htm`. See [Small Sailboats / Paradox-UK websites](/references/paradox-uk-website.md) for that site. Reading the article prompted a **topping-lift question** — full detail at [furling system — a UK builder's topping-lift question](/components/rig/furling.md#a-uk-builders-topping-lift-question) — along with an unanswered question about whether "Zoë" keeps a chain and warp permanently attached to her stowed main anchor.

The *Small Craft Advisor* Sept/Oct 2003 issue then carried "Cedar Key Small Boat Meet," which devoted substantial space to "Zoë" and "[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)" — the two Paradoxes present — praising both, and noting at one point that "Zoë appeared to be sailing to windward higher and faster than my Sea Pearl with its leeboard down." The article also mentioned seeing designer [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md), described as "the reclusive designer," at the same gathering, and ran four photographs of "Zoë" and "Whisper" alongside others from the meet. See [the Cedar Key small-boat meet](/operations/group-cruise-reports.md#the-2003-cedar-key-messabout-dates-and-logistics) for the gathering itself.
