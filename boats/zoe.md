# Zoë

**"Zoë"** is the [Paradox](/paradox.md) built by professional boat builder
[Glen C. Maxwell](/people/glen-maxwell.md), who founded the original
`paradoxbuilders` Yahoo group. Her build hours, ballasting, interior
fit-out, and a Cumberland Sound sea trial supply some of the
best-documented real-world figures for the design.

# The build

- The build took **roughly 1,000 hours spread across about two years** of
  on-again, off-again work (as a fun project rather than a job, "as the
  spirit moves me"), with an intense final push in the **last three
  months** — a thousand hours works out to roughly six months of
  full-time (40 hr/week) labor, and the boat could have been finished in
  considerably less elapsed time had the work been continuous. The
  **vent trunk** stood out as a particularly labor-intensive single
  component. Finish quality is what drives total build time: an early,
  partway-through estimate of ~500 hours was superseded by the settled
  ~1,000-hour figure once the boat was complete. See
  [build effort](/construction/build-effort.md).
- Ahead of painting, the non-skid deck pattern (walnut shells in epoxy; see
  [non-skid deck](/construction/non-skid-deck.md)) was laid out after
  firming up all deck-hardware positions, with the bottom already painted
  (see [bottom painting](/construction/bottom-painting.md)).
- Construction photos of "Zoë" seeded the group's files area (the
  "construction" and "stove" folders).

Techniques used on "Zoë" are documented as general concepts elsewhere:
[hull planking](/construction/hull-planking.md) (screwing the bottom on from
the stern), a preference for removable screws over left-in nails
([fastening and gluing](/construction/fastening-and-gluing.md)), and
[panel layout](/construction/panel-layout.md) choices. She was built to the
plans on anchor stowage despite a builder's dislike of a below-deck anchor
(see [ground tackle](/operations/ground-tackle.md)).

"Zoë" was built using a lower, **"ext A/B"** grade of plywood rather than
Marine grade. The extra finishing labor needed to reach a fine finish with
that grade proved unacceptably high, though it did give an acceptable
**workboat finish** with less labor overall. See
[plywood grade and foam flotation debate](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md).

## Launch and first sail

"Zoë" was **launched on Friday the 13th** [of November] 2001 and taken out
for her first sail:

- Conditions were **light**: less than 5 kts of wind (measured by a friend's
  anemometer).
- Boat speed **"ghosted" along between 2.2 and 3.3 kts** by GPS — respectable
  progress for such light air.
- "Zoë" was **very light** at this point: only about **75 lb of ballast** and
  **no water on board** (contrast the full loaded-ballast design figures in
  [weight and ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md), which call for 400 lb of
  lead alone).
- Even in this under-ballasted state, **stability was still quite good**: she
  could only be heeled to about **45 degrees** by standing on the dock and
  pulling the mast down by hand, and was expected to be "a treat when properly
  ballasted."
- She sailed for about **two hours** before hauling out for a trip to
  Philadelphia, and all went "very well."
- Her finish was praised as looking "as if she just left the ..factory."

## Trailer modifications for shallow launching

"Zoë"'s trailer was modified to allow launching in **less than two feet of
water**, illustrated with photos posted to the group's albums (under "Zoe"):

- The **axle is lengthened** (visible from the extended fenders) and mounted
  **on top of the leaf spring** rather than under it, raising the hull.
- The **stub axle/spindle is welded to the top of the axle** tube.
- The **bunk boards are mounted directly on the trailer frame**, rather than
  raised on separate risers.

A same-base-size trailer needs only these axle, fender, and bunk-board
modifications to reach a similarly low launch height (see
[secondhand Paradox market](/operations/secondhand-market.md)).

See also mast fit lessons from finishing "Zoë"'s spar in
[mast interior sealing and anchor-light fit](/construction/mast-sealing-and-fit.md) —
the completed mast, with anchor light fitted, proved about 1.5 in too long to
stow with the inspection port fully closed.

## Scale photos and first overnighter

Because a transom-port photo alone made "Zoë" look "enormous," a photo with
**one or two people aboard** — including one of the builder sitting
inside — was posted to give a true sense of her scale, alongside four more
photos from "Zoë"'s **first overnighter**: the boat was loaded with a week's
stores and full water tanks, and "all went well." She could still use
**another ~100 lb of ballast** to stiffen her up further — as built and
loaded for the overnighter she was still "a little too tender" when moving
forward on deck to handle anchor or dock lines, though walking around would
be easier once more weight went in. A week-long sea trial on Florida's west
coast followed once further ballasted.

The photos also prompted a question about plywood **checking**, since "Zoë"
was built of fir plywood; see
[plywood grade and foam flotation debate](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md)
for the fuller exchange on that question.

## Interior size and the chart table, shown photographically

How large a Paradox's interior really is, and how big a crew she can
handle, is answered here visually rather than with more figures — a
recurring question going back to the December 2001
["wearing the boat"](/operations/crew-size-and-fit.md) discussion. Photos of
a person **sitting inside "Zoë"** were posted to the group's files area
(Interior folder) to give a real sense of interior scale, alongside separate
photos of a **chart-table solution**, including one showing it in use while
seated. The tight, single-hander-oriented interior is underscored by a wry
aside that the images should let viewers "figure out how many close
(intimate) friends" they would want to go sailing with. This is the first
record of a **chart table** being fitted to a Paradox, though no further
detail on its construction or location survives beyond the photos
themselves. See [crew size and cabin fit](/operations/crew-size-and-fit.md)
for the fuller "wearing the boat" discussion these photos speak to.

## The skylight and chart table in practical use: a Cumberland Sound cruise

Months after first showing the hatch skylight and chart table only in still
photos (see
[interior and chart-table photos](#interior-size-and-the-chart-table-shown-photographically)
above), a weekend cruise of **Cumberland Sound** (St. Mary's River entrance,
GA) put both to actual use — the fullest single trip report on record for
"Zoë" since her original launch. On an overcast day spent helming from
below with the hatch closed, the **skylight** allowed seeing the sail for
trimming without going on deck, and, once heeled over, seeing to leeward
without bending down to the leeward windows — an unanticipated bonus. The
**wheelbarrow inner tube** seat cushion (an idea from Charles Stock's
*Sailing Just for Fun*, previously only proposed — see
[Glen C. Maxwell — a seat-cushion comfort tip](/people/glen-maxwell/build-and-zoe.md))
"worked out better then hoped for" once actually used underway.

The same cruise supplied a real-world **windward-performance** data point for
the [chine runners](/design/chine-runners-and-windward-performance.md)
(nearly five hours short-tacking against 12 kt winds and a foul current,
averaging 3.6 kt over 18 nm) and turned up a newly found **mast-heel play**
problem possibly costing some of that performance (see
[mast interior sealing and anchor-light fit](/construction/mast-sealing-and-fit.md)).
It also yielded a **helm-balance fix** via halyard position (see
[sailing characteristics and rig balance — Glen's CE/CLR diagnosis](/operations/sailing-characteristics.md)
for the related "Sail balance" thread this experiment answered), a cold-night
cabin-heat data point (see
[cabin heating for cold-weather sailing](/operations/cabin-heating.md)), and
two on-the-water mishaps recovered without outside help: a **trailer-tire
blowout** before reaching the ramp (see
[trailering — a blowout despite a new trailer](/operations/trailering.md))
and a **halyard-knot failure and furling-line jam** cleared underway (see
[furling system — a halyard-knot failure and a furling-line jam](/design/furling-system.md)).
See [Glen C. Maxwell — "Building encouragement"](/people/glen-maxwell/build-and-zoe.md)
for the full, single-message trip report all of these threads are drawn
from.

## Condensation, CE/CLR, and a third weight estimate

"Zoë" carries **no insulation** and has not suffered for it on
window-condensation drainage: a lip surrounding the cabin collects
condensation running off the windows before it can pool, and
through-ventilation carries off cabin moisture at the cost of a colder cabin
(see
[cabin heating — a window-condensation channel](/operations/cabin-heating.md)).
Her CE/CLR relationship was never worked out numerically; the low-tech hand
method for finding both is documented instead (see
[sailing characteristics — a newcomer asks Glen to quantify CE/CLR](/operations/sailing-characteristics.md)).
A **third, independent weight estimate** for "Zoë" put her at 650-750 lb
empty with ballast, roughly 900 lb loaded for a month's cruise, against a
1,400 lb designed displacement — without ever actually scaling her (see
[weight, ballast, and displacement — a third weight figure](/design/weight-and-ballast.md)).
On the **hatch skylight**, some two and a half years later: it produces no
condensation of its own.[41]

## Sailing with no water aboard, and a tiller stop

"Zoë" has sailed with **no water aboard**, and "did just fine" — though
[Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md) judged her to look "a little low in
the water," suggesting either **removing some ballast** or simply day-sailing
without filling the tanks, rather than always loading her to full cruising
trim. This is a small but concrete real-world data point on how a Paradox
handles lightly loaded, complementing Larry Whited's stability diagnosis for
"[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)"'s own unballasted launch (see
[weight, ballast, and displacement — an unloaded launch](/design/weight-and-ballast.md)).

A **tiller stop**, once fitted, "does help a lot" with the recurring
difficulty of finding the right spot to tie off the tiller while working the
yuloh — a problem that "does take some getting used to."

## Press coverage: two 2003 magazine articles

"Zoë" and her 2001 shakedown cruise reached print twice in the same year.
*Messing About In Boats*' August 15, 2003 issue carried an article with
photographs from the six-day, west-Florida shakedown cruise (see
[sea trials route](#sea-trials-route-cedar-key-to-clearwater) above),
praising "Zoë" throughout, listing several in-progress improvements made
along the way, and including tips on catching fish for dinner underway. A
change of postal address had left the boat's builder roughly four issues
behind on his own subscription, without a copy in hand until the editor
arranged to send the missing copies. The article was later scanned and
uploaded to the group's files section — after a few failed attempts, and
with the photos stripped out to cut file size — as both a full-color version
(nearly 2 MB, a long download over dial-up) and a smaller photo-free version,
several months after the original print date.

The article's UK readership led to its reproduction on the
**paradox-uk.co.uk** website (copyright remaining with the original author),
posted as "Zoë's Sea Trials" at
`www.btinternet.com/~w.serjeant/paradox/trials.htm`. See
[Small Sailboats / Paradox-UK websites](/references/paradox-uk-website.md)
for that site. Reading the article prompted a UK builder's own
**topping-lift question** — full detail at
[furling system — a UK builder's topping-lift
question](/design/furling-system.md#a-uk-builders-topping-lift-question) —
along with an unanswered question about whether "Zoë" keeps a chain and warp
permanently attached to her stowed main anchor.

The *Small Craft Advisor* Sept/Oct 2003 issue then carried "Cedar Key Small
Boat Meet," which devoted substantial space to "Zoë" and
"[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)" — the two Paradoxes present — praising both,
and noting at one point that "Zoë appeared to be sailing to windward higher
and faster than my Sea Pearl with its leeboard down." The article also
mentioned seeing designer [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md), described
as "the reclusive designer," at the same gathering, and ran four photographs
of "Zoë" and "Whisper" alongside others from the meet. See
[the Cedar Key small-boat meet](/operations/group-cruise-reports.md#the-2003-cedar-key-messabout-dates-and-logistics)
for the gathering itself.
