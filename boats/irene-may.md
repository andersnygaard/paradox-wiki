# Irene May

**"Irene May"** is [Ian Lang](/people/ian-lang.md)'s [Paradox](/paradox.md), under construction in **North London, England**. Ian gathered UK plywood and Douglas Fir quotes through May and June 2007, cleared his garage into a skip, and ordered his wood from Robbins; the timber arrived unexpectedly on a Friday in late June 2007, marking the effective start of construction. He named the boat **"Irene May"** after his late mother, who he judged would have been thrilled to have a ship named after her. For Ian's plywood/timber-sourcing record and person-level detail, see [people/ian-lang.md](/people/ian-lang.md).

## Materials arrive, and building to the stock plan

The plywood and Douglas Fir arrived unexpectedly, and despite good packaging two sheets suffered corner damage Ian judged he could work around. He intends to build to the plans as drawn, with one exception: adding a window in the cabin roof. Progress was expected to be slow at first, since a china cabinet — to keep his wife happy — competed for his workshop time.[1]

## A shrinking garage, and encouragement from other UK and US builders

Piling up a few sheets of plywood was enough to make an "adequate" garage feel suddenly small — a common complaint echoed by builders further along in their own builds.[1] [Bill Serjeant](/people/bill-serjeant.md), having launched his own Paradox "[Faith](/boats/faith.md)" earlier the same month, wished Ian the same fun and satisfaction his own build had given him.[2] [Pete Martin](/people/pete-martin.md), mid-build on "[JoHanna](/boats/johanna.md)" and tripping over his own chine and sheer strakes laid out flat in his own limited space, sympathized directly and suggested Ian stall the china cabinet until winter, since the weather would likely be unsuitable for that kind of joinery anyway.[3] A US correspondent congratulated Ian with a reflection on the appeal of a project's very first days — "the perfection of a dream made physical" — and recalled, as a boy, telling his mother he would one day name his own boat after her, Martha Louise, the same way Ian had just done for his.[4]

## Build-order advice: fit out the small parts before finishing the hull

Once Ian confirmed he was ready to cut wood, the advice offered was to build all of the boat's smaller parts — mast, rudder, yuloh, and the like — before finishing the hull itself. Working on these while the hull is still flat and leaning against the wall is easier than doing so once the hull fills the workspace outright, and finishing the hull last means the boat is ready to launch as soon as it is done, rather than sitting unusable while the smaller parts are still being fitted out.[5] Ian judged the advice good and would broadly follow it, though he counted himself one of those builders who needs a half-finished hull to spur him on to complete the smaller parts. With a large, insulated shed as well as the garage, he could work in a controlled environment year-round, and had already started marking up and cutting the plywood parts so the sides could be scarfed and glued while the weather stayed warm. Fitting bulkhead 2 and the transom to the starboard forward side panel brought a brief panic before he realized the plans called for bulkhead 2's butt join to move 50 mm toward the centre — a correction present in the amended drawing all along, once studied closely enough.[6]

## A skin reaction laminating the deck beams

Laminating the curved deck beams with West System epoxy, Ian developed a painful, itchy skin rash on his arms, hands, and face despite gloves, protective clothing, and a well-ventilated shed; the rash eventually narrowed down to a likely latex-glove allergy rather than the epoxy itself, prompting a switch to vinyl gloves. See [people/ian-lang.md — a skin reaction to West System epoxy](/people/ian-lang.md#a-skin-reaction-to-west-system-epoxy) and [epoxy brands, chemistry, and cure behavior — responses to a skin sensitivity case](/construction/epoxy-brands-and-chemistry.md#responses-to-a-skin-sensitivity-case-alternative-brands-resin-types-and-glovemask-technique) for the full account.[7][8]
