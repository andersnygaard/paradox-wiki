# THU (Eerik Mee)

**THU** is [Eerik Mee](/people/eerik-mee.md)'s Paradox, built in **Estonia** from **July 2015** and **launched on an inland Estonian lake on 5 July 2017**.[1][2] It is unlike any other boat in the archive in that it departs from the plans in **two** large ways at once and was still **built and sailed**: it is scaled up **20%** on every dimension, and it carries a **purpose-designed cambered Chinese junk rig**.[3][4]

Those two departures put THU under the two longest-running "argued but never built" threads in the record. It is the archive's **only built-and-sailed example of the [larger Paradox](/design/larger-paradox.md)** — the scale-up the mailing list discussed roughly forty times without a completed build — and, alongside [Planktos](/boats/planktos.md), one of only two junk-rig Paradoxes on record actually built and sailed, and the **only one with a junk rig designed from scratch** rather than converted from the stock lug.[3][4] Its whole story is on [Eerik Mee's blog](/references/eerik-mee-blog.md), captured as this bundle's source.

## The 20% scale-up

Mee sized the boat up **uniformly, ×1.2 on every plans measurement**, to fit himself: "I multiplied just all measurements with 1.2, so yes, it is also wider and higher. LOA turned out about **5.05 m** and width about **1.5 m**."[3] Those figures land within centimetres of the worked 20% scale-up the list had carried since 2008 — LOA 5.04 m, beam 1.48 m — so THU is, in effect, that paper boat built (see [larger Paradox](/design/larger-paradox.md#a-worked-20-scale-up-dimensions-sail-area-and-displacement)). The **stock scantlings were kept**; only the **spars scaled** with the hull, the mast growing to **6 m**.[4][5] Mee recalculated plywood consumption before buying material and noted a little creativity was needed to optimise it — using 3×12 stock instead of 2×18 for the rudder, for instance — but found the drawing "easy to follow" once every dimension was multiplied through.[1]

The larger hull is heavier and stiffer to handle ashore: launching solo, Mee tied the boat to his car and reversed to push her the last 50–150 m into the water, and he judged the heavier mast a possible "hassle in heavy wind and slippery cabin top," part of why he intended her to be **moored rather than trailered**.[5][6]

## The junk rig

THU's rig is the archive's only **purpose-designed cambered junk sail** on a Paradox. Rather than convert the stock lug (as was done on [Planktos](/boats/planktos.md)), Mee designed a **six-panel cambered junk sail** from scratch, following **Arne Kverneland**'s method and drawing on the **Junk Rig Association**: **8% camber** built into the panels, the three lower panels identical, sewn on Odyssey sailcloth with the mast-side batten pockets in wear-resistant PVC truck-tent cloth.[4] He rejected a flat-cut sample plan on both looks and performance — "The HML is actually flat cut sail with not so good winward performance. It's the AK's camber that improves it" — and modified the shape toward a Reddish-type roach.[7] The battens and boom are 30×2 mm aluminium tube, the yard 50×2 mm.[4] The mast itself is round, built in birdmouth style and tapered towards both top and bottom, with the halyard sheave mounted inside so the halyard runs on and off it from the best angle.[14]

Carrying that taller rig in balance drove the boat's most consequential structural change: the **mast moved from aft of bulkhead 2 to as far forward of it as possible**.[7] Mee wanted more mast below deck for the 6 m spar and clearance between the transom and the upper sheet (about a 10° angle), and ran the halyard and throat/yard parrels down into the ventilation trunk aft of the mast.[7] The junk rig also needs **more control lines than the stock boat**: "We have 5 camcleats in total, because of the junk sail. Most Paradoxes have 2 or 3."[8] A three-sheave deflector on the mast keeps the lines clear of the lower sail on the starboard downwind tack.[8]

Where the long list-era junk-rig argument (see [junk rig](/design/ideas/junk-rig.md)) had gone untested, THU supplies data: the sail was given the **camber** the theory said flat panels lacked, the **mast was moved forward** to hold balance, and the boat sails. But the same record shows the balance and sheeting problems were **not fully solved on the water**: the boom twisted too much under sheet, Mee experimented with block positions without settling it, and one tack showed noticeable **lee helm**, which he corrected by adding water ballast forward and tightening the tack parrel to move the centre of effort back.[6][8]

## Ballast, and the systems boat

THU carries about **240 kg of ballast**. Mee first sailed her on ~240 kg of mixed ballast — "about 100 kg of lead and the rest … water" — then cast the lead proper as **twelve pigs of ~20 kg (45 lb) each**, poured into drywall-panel moulds buried in earth, under EU rules that by then required buying the lead on a company's account.[9][10] He later shared a video on the [Facebook group](/references/paradox-facebook-group.md) showing the casting process, offered as a reference for other builders facing the same job.[13] The main ballast compartment sits **aft of bulkhead 3** (rated by Mee at ~220 kg), with water ballast available forward for trim — the same forward water ballast he used to cure the lee helm.[9][8]

Unusually for a Paradox — where many builds are motorless or sculled — THU is a **fully powered and instrumented boat**. She carries an **electric outboard** on a scratch-built GRP transom bracket, **two flexible solar panels** (one on the cabin top under an opening hatch), a **LiFePO₄ battery**, a charge controller, an inverter (Mee "pumped my tires up from the boat battery"), a **depth sounder** with an internally bonded transducer, and **LED cabin lighting** made from strip inside transparent hose.[10] A masthead light runs up a hose inside the mast, with a copper-tube lightning conductor grooved into the mast's front face.[4] A cast-and-webbing **PVC sea anchor** (800 mm) and a bow anchor complete the outfit.[11][10]

## Build details worth noting

Several of Mee's methods are recorded in enough detail to be useful:

- **Hull and rudder.** Hull sides scarfed and milled in pairs to match, bottom glassed with peel-ply, chine runners shaped and glassed; the rudder blade was **laminated with lead cast into it**, and the stock built up from **three layers of 12 mm plywood** to ~40 mm finished, hung on a **cast-epoxy lower gudgeon plus a bought upper pintle/gudgeon set — both used, because the scaled-up rudder is heavy**.[1][8]
- **Windows.** Side windows are **triplex laminated glass** (two 3 mm layers with a film between); the curved front windows were **cut from a car windshield** at a windscreen-replacement shop.[11]
- **Antifouling and insulation.** The faired bottom was coated with a **copper-powder/epoxy** mix; the interior is insulated with **XPS foam**, faced with light glassed fabric and glued in with polyurethane.[1][11]
- **A sealable cabin hatch.** The sliding hatch was built with its front edge lower than the back, so it compresses a gasket harder as it closes and barely touches when cracked open — a home-worked take on a watertight companionway.[11]

## How she sails

The launch trials were promising on the one count the theory most feared. Paradox is normally awkward to tack; THU **tacked without tricks**. Mee first tried the tricks other Paradox sailors use — turning slowly into the wind, shifting weight forward and to windward — to "No success," then tacked "without any tricks … and it was a success. As were all next tackings."[2] She sailed under 3 to 5 of her 6 panels on the first day and was out about **four times in 2017**, with more sailing in 2019.[2][10]

What the record does **not** yet settle is her performance to windward. Boom twist and sheeting were unresolved, and Mee was explicit: "It is not yet possible to make any conclusions on her pointing ability, more trials will have to be conducted for that."[2] She was **day-sailed on a lake**, never cruised, so — unlike [Planktos](/boats/planktos.md) or the [Dave Skaife](/people/dave-skaife.md) blog boat — THU documents that a scaled-up, cambered-junk Paradox floats on her lines and sails, but not how she holds up over distance.[2][6]

Posting to the [Facebook group](/references/paradox-facebook-group.md) at the end of his 2020 season, Mee reported that sandy-beach beaching had mostly not happened in practice — conditions were either too windy and wavy or too rocky, so he stayed on anchor or in a marina instead, and hoped to have more courage with beaches the following season.[12] Sailing in Estonia was mostly a weekend activity for him, and he found less socialising along the way than he had imagined.[12] Manoeuvring in and out of marinas in wind was a recurring problem because the wind blows the bow off course, and he considered adding a small fin under the bow to counter it; he also found the electric outboard a bit weak in wavy conditions and wanted to move to a bigger motor and more batteries for a longer motoring range.[12]

Two gear failures needed fixing that season: the rudder-blade lifting line had jumped off the side of the rudder more than once, jamming the rudder to one side — a dangerous situation in strong wind — and the halyard had once come off the masthead sheave and stuck to one side, which he planned to cure with a new, wider sheave.[12]
