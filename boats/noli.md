# Noli

**"Noli"** — originally announced under the working name **"Paranigma"** — is a lighter, shallower-draft [Paradox](/paradox.md)/[Enigma](/matt-layden/enigma.md) hybrid built on the **Isle of Wight, England**, by a `paradoxbuilders` list member posting as **"Jeff"** (Yahoo handles `apcf14`/`roverticket`, signing "Jeff UK"). See [Jeff (UK, "doryman_uk")](/people/jeff-doryman.md) for the person-level record, including an open question over whether this builder is the same "Jeff" as an earlier (2001-2002) correspondent of that name. Hull parts and bulkheads for a related project had already been cut by 2005; the "Paranigma" name — a Paradox/Enigma portmanteau — was disclosed in September 2006, and the boat was renamed **"Noli"** in January 2007, after a childhood pet-name of the builder's late mother, Nora. Built to the same length as stock Paradox but roughly **500 lb lighter** and drawing only **7 in**, her builder restated her as, in effect, "a Paradox-sized Enigma." Progress reports ran from 2006 into 2008; the archive's last dated update (April 2008) still had her unlaunched, painting under way, with a late-June/early-July target.

## Origins: an abandoned motor cruiser, and the "Paranigma" name

By September 2006 the builder disclosed, for the first time on the list, a **17 ft Paradox-type multi-chine motor cruiser** — sized for a 2 hp Honda outboard and sharing Paradox's own accommodation — whose hull parts and bulkheads had already been cut a year earlier, the project derailed only by scaffolding erected next door. In the same message he named his still-pending next design **"Paranigma,"** with the stalled motor cruiser to follow once Paranigma was finished. Full thread context at [boats/enuf.md](/boats/enuf.md#a-near-miss-paradox-type-motor-cruiser-and-enuf-as-a-talking-point-for-motorboating).[1]

## Dimensions and a construction schedule

Corrected against an onlooker's first impression that she ran nearer 16 ft, Noli was confirmed as **just under 14 ft** — the same length as stock Paradox — but with only **7 in of draught** against the original's deeper draft, and about **500 lb lighter**.[2][3] Her construction is far lighter than Paradox's throughout, her sail area smaller at **86 sq ft**, and she carries about the same amount of ballast despite the weight saved elsewhere.[4][5]

The full construction schedule: a hull of **1/4 in and 3/8 in Gaboon marine plywood**, with **three layers of glass on the bottom** back to just past the halfway mark, tapering to two layers toward the transom and a single layer on the sides and deck; bulkheads all **3/8 in Gaboon marine ply**; deck **1/4 in Gaboon marine ply**. The builder budgeted **350 lb for crew and stores** (against his own 196 lb weight), targeting a **558 lb hull weight** with ballast to bring her down to her marks, and judged Noli's rocker fairer than stock Paradox's — though he still rated Paradox the better sea boat for his own coastal Solent sailing, where half an hour should reach shelter. He deliberately did not plan to publish plans for Noli at the outset, so as not to "step on Matt's toe's" (see [below](#a-reversal-on-publishing-nolis-plans-and-a-design-debt-to-matt-layden-and-sven) for the later reversal of that decision).[6][7][8]

## NOLI's own dimensions, and a frame-2/frame-3 modification for extra sleeping room

Noli's interior figures: a **1900 mm** sleeping-area length — matching the stock Paradox figure — and **920 mm** between the bins at frame 3. Lowering frame 2's opening flush with the cabin sole, reducing its lower-corner radius to 50 mm, and trimming the opening back to the bin and mast box lets a sleeper's feet extend forward into the "stateroom"; narrowing frame 3 itself by 1 in and glassing it directly to the hull, with the side panel there thinned from 1/2 in to 3/8 in, was offered as a way to reduce the pressure the frame otherwise bears — even with the bins left out, frame 3 still stands in the way. Noli's own sides are built from **1/4 in** marine-grade plywood, with frame 3 at **3/8 in**. Cruising two aboard a stock-sized cabin was judged more realistically solved by camping ashore than by both sleeping aboard, in contrast to Matt Layden, whose own slight build let him and a partner sleep aboard together.[9]

## NOLI's ply weights, a before/after lift test, and a roof-rack capacity figure

Answering a UK builder's own car-topping weight sums: **1/4 in Gaboon marine plywood** for the sides and **3/8 in** for the bottom, the 1/4 in ply weighing only **16 lb** a sheet — one reason to use marine grade over exterior ply. Before glassing Noli (three layers on the bottom), the builder could lift her by the bow or stern alone with one hand; afterward it took two. A VW Golf's roof rack is rated for **220 lb**, and since such figures are usually understated, Noli empty should in principle be roof-rackable — though he intended to stick with a trailer regardless.[10]

## Chine-runner construction

Dry-fitting Noli's first **chine runner**, the **20 mm Scottish Douglas Fir** proved tough enough to break a drill bit, requiring saw cuts along two edges of each laminated layer to bend it around the hull; Noli's chine runners measure **54 in long by 2 5/8 in wide**, cut at 35 degrees with a planned hollow added to the top face.[11]

## A beamless, laminated cabin top

Noli's cabin top dispensed with the plans' roof beams altogether: a roof laminated from **two layers of 3.5 mm plywood**, resting on the 1/2 in ply cabin sides and stitch-and-glued into place, with an **external beam** at the forward hatch coaming and a **truss made from a bent aluminium tube**, running from the cockpit coaming, fastened in the middle under the hatch coaming, to the opposite side — carrying the roof's downward load and side thrust without internal beams. The forward window frame was similarly built from two laminated layers of plywood, stitched and glued into position, judged as strong as the beamed original but lighter.[11]

## NOLI's foredeck built in two halves, avoiding the tight-curve problem

Rather than fighting the stock foredeck panel's tight curve near the stem, the builder built Noli's foredeck from lighter, thinner 1/4 in marine ply, laid up in two halves to save material. The bulkheads were cut oversized before installation, then a **kingplank** was fitted to give a straight centerline through to the stem; a fair 1/4 in square batten, clamped to each bulkhead and resting on the kingplank and both sheer clamps, gave a line to cut and plane to, with the sheer clamps themselves pre-beveled to the deck edge's approximate angle. A layer of glass cloth plus the cockpit carlin (running the full length from transom to stem) made the resulting deck strong despite its lighter build; a few places where the deck did not quite meet a bulkhead were left as-is, since a fillet was going there regardless.[12]

## NOLI's chine runners finished, and a trailer purchase

By late April 2007 the chine runners were finished and their underside glassed, using PeelPly over the glass for a smoother finish and less later fairing work. Trimming the surplus glass cloth from around the chine runners' edge is best done while the glass is still "green" (not fully cured), running a knife around the edge using the underside as a guide — combined with the PeelPly finish, this leaves a smooth, rounded edge at comparatively little extra effort.[13][14]

A new combi trailer, sized for Noli's much lighter build, was bought secondhand at a discount off list price — chosen specifically because her lighter construction let a lighter, cheaper trailer suffice, unlike the far costlier trailer a stock-built Paradox required. The builder proposed the UK builders then racing toward a launch — himself with Noli, [Bill Serjeant](/boats/faith.md) with Faith, and [Alastair](/people/alastair-law.md) with [Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md) — might eventually meet up and sail in company, suggesting Poole Harbour or the Solent.[14]

Around the same time, small fit-out purchases continued cheaply from UK high-street retailers, including a portable toilet comparable in finish to a name-brand unit.[15][16]

## Sail and sailmaker: Graham of CAWS Sails

For Noli's sail, the builder used his own local sailmaker, **Graham of CAWS Sails** in Cowes on the Isle of Wight — a fellow one-man loft who had already cut him several sets of sprit, lug, and Bermudan sails, always setting well, including a set some 25 years earlier for a Dart 18 catamaran that set without a wrinkle — good enough that the builder began winning club races with them until told the sails were not one-design; forced back onto slower class-mandated sails, he placed second, sold the boat, and gave up racing rather than being told what to sail. CAWS Sails tries a finished sail on the boat itself and makes further alterations if not satisfied. He confirmed he would take Matt Layden's own dimensioned sailmaking recipe to CAWS Sails when ordering Noli's sail.[17][18]

Materials sourcing for the build used the cheaper "Far Eastern" marine ply rather than a premium UK grade, judged worth the price saving; the only issue in the whole build was one small deck-edge delamination near the mast, fixed with a glue squirt, tape, a cap strip, and glass. **MAS Low Viscosity Epoxy Resin** was the recommended epoxy, with UK epoxy prices running well above US prices for the same product.[19]

## NOLI's vee-shaped washboard, and a Hunter 19 recalled

The builder agreed a vertical-lift washboard, of the kind favored by [Bill Serjeant](/boats/faith.md), was the sounder design, but chose Noli's own washboard as a traditional vee shape instead — less for looks than because the only piece of plywood on hand was an offcut from a project some 15 years earlier, salvaged rather than buying a new sheet, with two small pieces added at the top to make it fit.[20]

He also recalled owning an earlier boat of his own, a **Hunter 19** bought semi-fitted-out and named **"Willing Griffin,"** and restated Noli's washboard as a single piece rather than split top-and-bottom, fitted with a small window, given the opening's overall height of only about 10 in.[21]

## NOLI's washboard fastening, and a solar vent on the foredeck

Noli's washboard is held with two small bolts through the outside of the cabin, the washboard edges, and the retaining lip, each a round-headed bolt with the head outside and a wing nut and washer on the inside. A solar-powered vent sits on her foredeck in place of the vent trunk fitted in stock Paradox — though solar vents, the builder joked, do little for a warm night once it's moonlight rather than sunlight doing the work.[22]

## NOLI's glassing schedule, and a lighter-hull counter-argument

Noli carries three layers of fiberglass cloth on the bottom just past bulkhead 3, tapering to two layers back to the transom — a schedule settled after a conversation establishing that damage on a comparable hull always happened forward of Little Cruiser's lowest point.[23] Set against Paradox's much heavier fixed bottom, Noli's own 3/8 in bottom leaves her displacing just over 500 lb less for a comparable size — on one view, that lighter displacement could make her less, not more, susceptible to hull damage, though the point remains untested and was offered half in jest. See [hull scantlings — the case for over-construction](/design/hull-scantlings.md#the-case-for-over-construction-field-evidence-hazards-and-a-counter-argument) for the case for Paradox's heavier alternative.[24]

## NOLI's bottom thickness, and a Potter comparison

Watching a fellow UK builder's newly dry-mocked-up hull, the builder suggested it could be built stitch-and-glue with a bottom panel of either two layers or a single thickness of 1/2 in ply, reasoning from Noli's own bottom — built to only **3/8 in** — itself justified against the original wooden West Wight Potter's even wider bottom panel, built to only 5/16 in for broadly similar duty to Paradox.[25] Answering a West Wight Potter owner's account of a hull-holing grounding, he noted that boat's GRP construction and stepped cabin top still call for care over where to beach, and that a thick bottom under a thick layer of glass does not by itself license beaching anywhere.[26]

## NOLI's own thicknesses cited as precedent for a Paradox in cheaper marine ply

Noli's own stitch-and-glue construction — 6 mm marine ply topsides, 9 mm bottom — was later cited elsewhere on the list as precedent for building a stock Paradox lighter in marine ply rather than to the plans' heavier exterior-ply specification. Full text and citation at [plywood grade and foam flotation debate — a cheap 9mm plywood substitution, confirmed and extended](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#a-cheap-9mm-plywood-substitution-confirmed-and-extended).[27]

## The origin of NOLI's name

Noli takes its name from a pet-name of the builder's late mother, Nora, dating to her school days.[28][29]

## A reversal on publishing NOLI's plans, and a design debt to Matt Layden and "Sven"

Having earlier decided not to publish Noli's plans "so as not to step on Matt's toe's," the builder reversed course once several members wrote asking directly: once Noli was launched and performing to his liking, he would release his drawings free as a download to this and related groups. Using them would still mean separately buying Matt Layden's own Paradox drawings from Dave and Mindy Bolduc, "two boats for the price of one," plus a basic CAD software package, some of it available free online. He framed Noli's design as a collaboration with Matt Layden despite never having spoken to him directly, "it always felt like he was looking over my shoulder," and credited Matt's own years of research with models and prototypes with bringing his latest design to the drawing board together with **Sven** — a research partnership distinct from, and earlier than, Sven Yrvind's own later, separately documented visit to consult Matt about a project of Sven's own (see [Matt Layden biography](/matt-layden/biography.md)). No boat-design textbook, in his view, could have produced a boat like Paradox. Working to "the minimum of information" himself, as a hobby builder, he did not want the chore of producing sale-ready drawings or explaining them to a first-time builder; he would instead release his own dimensioned panel-layout and rig/general-arrangement drawings — essentially the same approach as Paradox's own — as a modification to Paradox, with community help and further files-section photos expected to see a builder through.[30][31]

## A removable hardtop concept, and NOLI restated as a "Paradox-sized Enigma"

Answering a newcomer's proposal for a lighter, car-topping Paradox/Enigma hybrid with a bolt-off, invertible cabin top (see [car-topping conversion ideas](/design/variants/car-topping-conversion-idea.md)), the builder set out a removable hardtop of his own devising for a car-topped Enigma-type boat: built to the same shape as Enigma's original folding soft top, bent from 1.5 mm plywood with the front windscreen stitch-and-glued, glassed inside and out, and window apertures cut and glazed with 1.5-2 mm clear acrylic — a weight about the same as the soft top and its internal tubing, workable enough to leave fitted to the boat on the roof rack.[32]

Comparing his own build directly against Matt Layden's Enigma — built from 1/4 in topsides and 3/8 in bottom — the builder restated Noli as effectively **"a Paradox sized Enigma,"** built to the same-sized materials; reducing the draught to 7 in and tweaking the design on computer had shaved just over a third of stock Paradox's weight off her, to the point that, empty, she might for a laugh even be cartoppable herself.[33]

A 2008 outside listing of Layden-lineage micro-cruisers under construction or already sailing named Noli a **"4.24 m length Enigma,"** alongside Little Cruiser and the Enigma 460 prototype, pointing to a "MY NEW BOAT.pdf" build update posted in the EnigmaBuilders-as-Designed-by-Matt_Layden Yahoo group's files section.[57]

## Paint advice for a fellow UK builder, and cabin-sole matting and small gear for NOLI

Answering a fellow UK builder's interior-paint question, the builder recommended a clear primer sealer for both interior and exterior surfaces — two coats, rubbed down lightly between them, then the usual undercoat and topcoat — sourced cheaper in bulk tins from International or a general paint/DIY shop, and reported using ordinary floor paint for his own interior finish, rating it hardwearing.[34]

For Noli's own fit-out, small gear and finishing touches — cabin-sole non-slip matting sold for garage and workshop floors, camping mats for the cabin-side lining, LED lighting, and solar battery charging — were all sourced cheaply from UK high-street and online retailers.[35][36][37][38]

## Build timeline: stalls, weather, and progress reports, 2006-2008

The builder reported cutting all his own panels indoors, with outdoor assembly beginning in June 2006, and building-work noise and weather next door cutting his working efficiency to roughly 20-25%; he used "Far Eastern Ply" from an Isle of Wight boatyard for the hull and 1/4 in decking, and judged in hindsight it was worth paying more for a better grade after a couple of delaminations appeared in the decking.[39]

By early December 2006 the build was stalled by an unrelated home-renovation project: an unfinished garden wall had left the workspace roof open to the weather, and cold, damp conditions were holding up glassing the hull's deck. The rudder, stock, and mast/spar/chine-runner material were all in hand, with the chine runners to be fitted once a few days of fine weather allowed.[40]

By January 2007, replying to Alastair, the builder reported "Paranigma" still not test-fitted with a folding bicycle, and separately noted storm damage to his own work area — scaffolding removed but a neighboring development's hole in his roof still unrepaired after gales recording 100 mph at the Needles — while hoping to get Paranigma afloat that year.[41]

By late January 2007 "Paranigma" had been renamed **"Noli,"** with the builder reporting he had begun installing foam camping-mat insulation, held with spray adhesive. Asked directly for photographs of the project, he pointed to an article he had posted in the Microcruisingpics forum's files section (titled "MY NEW BOAT"), with further pictures in that forum's photo section, and reported the boat had progressed somewhat further than shown there before weather stopped outside work, leaving only the mast and spars still under way.[42][43][44]

A follow-up asked whether the PDF plan/photo link for Paranigma/Noli was dead.[45] The pictures and construction sketch were found in the photo album, drawing a comment that a couple of extra feet of length seemed to offer noticeably more room.[46]

By mid-March 2007 the builder was again hoping to have Noli in the water by June — cabin top and hatch under way alongside cut rudder blanks and a mast and spars in progress — though a rush work commitment had limited him to only about two hours on the boat during a rare stretch of fine weather; he came down with the flu shortly afterward. Full thread, including his exchange with Bill Serjeant and Al on sail comparisons and a proposed three-boat UK meet-up, at [Faith — fibreglassing the hull, and fairing woven roving before painting](/boats/faith.md#fibreglassing-the-hull-and-fairing-woven-roving-before-painting).[47][48]

## A coincidental paint-colour match with Faith

Watching [Bill Serjeant](/boats/faith.md#yulohs-first-use-recorded-on-video)'s video of Faith's yuloh in use, the builder noticed his own rowing skiff, already finished in a yellow shade called "Mayonnaise" with a light grey rub-rail stripe, matched by coincidence the colour scheme going onto Faith — and was considering the same shade for the whole of Noli. Five weeks of rain had by then cost him against a plan to finish glassing Noli's deck, transom, and sides, though the cabin interior was cleaned and filled and a hatch opening enlarged in the meantime.[49]

## Build timeline (continued): weather, the yuloh, and storage, into 2008

By late January 2008, with the build halted by cold, rain, and wind, work was expected to resume once the weather improved, with a launch date still hoped for that June. For propulsion, the builder remained committed to the yuloh over an outboard, but not built exactly to Matt Layden's own drawing: he had begun studying the more efficient Japanese **VE-SCULL** design, which achieves laminar flow, and had already ordered a length of spruce toward a simple improvement of his own — a fence fitted to the underside of the blade to reduce tip vortex. For water storage, the gap under Noli's own cabin sole fore and aft of bulkhead 3 was sized to take 2-litre bottles, giving about 32 litres — roughly a week's supply.[50][51]

## Painting begins, and a spring 2008 launch target restated

By April 2008 the hull was being painted in epoxy high-build ahead of the final paint job, with Perspex offcuts bought for the side and rear windows and a matching piece still being sought for the front. A late-June or early-July launch remained the target — the last dated update on record.[52][53]

## Reception, recognition, and later status

Seeing photographs of Noli's progress, Bill Serjeant judged her lines "sweet" — likely to prove a slippery Paradox type with a good turn of speed, her extra length and lighter construction expected to leave stock Paradox behind in most conditions.[54] Thanking Bill for the compliment, the builder reported the build "giving me the greatest buzz" despite having designed and built about a dozen boats before it.[55]

Approving of Noli as an example of *informed* modification during a wider design-philosophy debate on the list (see [paradox.md](/paradox.md#citations)), a fellow builder asked whether Noli yet had a proposed launch date — the question went unanswered on the list.[56]

No confirmed launch report appears in the archive after the April 2008 update.
