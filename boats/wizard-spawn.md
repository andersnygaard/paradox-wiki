# Wizard Spawn

**"Wizard Spawn"** is [Rodney Cannon](/people/rodney-cannon.md)'s Paradox, under construction in **Venice, California**. Rodney announced starting the build on 7 August 2008, three months ahead of his own stated schedule — see [construction/build-effort.md](/construction/build-effort.md#practicing-epoxy-and-glassing-on-a-simpler-boat-first) for the practice-boat debate (Puddle Duck Racer versus a single-sheet 8-footer) that followed that announcement. The next day he moved from planning to the boat's first real parts: the deck and cabin beams.

## Starting on the beams: jigs, sizing confusion, and a lesson in plywood

Rigging the beam-laminating jigs took a full day's work, after which the plans' instruction to laminate all deck and cabin beams to one long length and cut each to size later — adding 3 in of working length to each end, per Don Elliott's construction manual — proved hard to picture in the abstract; a short break away from the plans was enough to make the answer click without further help.[5][6][8] The jig design itself was credited, three weeks later, to another builder's own build-photo website — [Cachivache](/boats/cachivache.md), under construction in Patagonia — judged considerably easier to follow than the construction manual's own method.[24] Along the way came a small realization common to first-time builders working from a stock materials list: beams are conventionally laminated from thin plywood rather than solid timber, leaving a stock of nice mahogany, bought before that was understood, without an obvious use in the beams themselves.[8]

A concrete worked example from another builder's own boat answered the sizing question directly: laminating all beams, deck and cabin, from **9 mm plywood**; ripping the deck-beam strips the full length of a sheet (about **1,210 mm**) and cutting each strip in half, giving plenty of margin over the **~1,190 mm** the longest deck beam actually needs; a full set of **seven deck beams at five laminates each** (35 pieces); and roughly **ten cabin beams**, of varying height and width, all laminated to a uniform **900 mm** length before individual trimming. Two extra sheets of 9 mm ply covered these beams plus other small parts such as lazarette bins. The glue-up itself followed the standard two-step process — wet out both surfaces with unthickened epoxy, let it soak in, then butter on epoxy thickened with a cabosil-and-cellulose-fibre blend and clamp to squeeze-out, left a full 24 hours to cure.[7] See [deck and cabin beam lamination](/construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md) for the general technique this worked example confirms.

Fitting a laminated-plywood beam carries one further caution, repeated independently to Rodney from firsthand experience: a beam can delaminate within the plywood's own glue lines, quite apart from the epoxy bond holding the lamination together, and is more of a risk on a free-standing beam than on one bonded directly to a bulkhead. See [fastening and gluing — a delamination caution from experience](/construction/fastening-and-gluing.md#fastening-laminated-ply-panels-a-delamination-caution-from-experience) for the full account, first reported from [Faith](/boats/faith.md#a-laminated-hatch-beam-that-delaminated-in-the-ply-itself)'s own hatch beam and repeated here to a new builder just starting his own.[9]

## Mahogany for the interior, inspired by another builder's finish

The mahogany Rodney had already bought — the stock left without an obvious job once he learned the beams themselves are laminated plywood — was in fact bought for the interior joinery, not the beams: having admired photographs of [Mark Waters](/people/mark-waters.md)'s finished interior enough to e-mail him directly and ask what wood he had used, Rodney settled on the same mahogany for his own boat. Whether to rip the wood into strips or bend it in as a single piece remained an open question even after weighing a document on the choice already circulating in the group's files section.[11]

## Encouragement from established builders

Starting a build drew encouragement from three builders further along. [Stephen Yahn](/people/stephen-yahn.md) framed epoxy as simple and nearly foolproof, with nails and screws providing a permanent mechanical backup to the glue joint regardless, and suggested the rudder, mast, boom, yard, and yuloh as good parts to build first; he also passed along his own solution for keeping hands clean without resorting to solvents on bare skin — see [epoxy safety and personal protection — surgical gloves as an alternative to solvent hand-cleaning](/construction/epoxy-safety-and-ppe.md#surgical-gloves-as-an-alternative-to-solvent-hand-cleaning).[1] Pete Martin, sixteen months into his own build with no particular hurry (built as a retirement boat), pointed to Jimmy Lu's rapid build of "[Auspice](/boats/auspice.md)" — finished in under eight months — as proof a Paradox can go together quickly, while noting his own more leisurely pace was a matter of choice rather than difficulty; he recommended studying other builders' progress photos as one of the most useful resources available, naming Little Jim, Faith, Auspice, and Cachivache by name.[3] Answering the same thread, Al (Alastair Law) restated the roughly 18-month rule of thumb for a typical build and added a pointed piece of realism for anyone hoping to launch on a compressed schedule: chasing a fast finish leaves no time for side projects like a practice boat, so a builder aiming for a near-term launch should set the practice-boat idea aside entirely.[4]

## Locating the mainsheet, and adopting Glen Maxwell's rerouted lead

Working from the plans and Don Elliott's construction manual side by side, Rodney could not find where the mainsheet was meant to run: the vent-box drawings show only two lines passing through, while interior photographs of "[Zoë](/boats/zoe.md)" show three, and a link that once illustrated Glen Maxwell's own routing had gone dead.[12] The mainsheet itself is shown on Drawing 1, in the lower right-hand zone, not among the vent-box lines at all; a separate rope on Drawing 11, run along the tiller, lifts the rudder blade. Zoë's third vent-box line is not part of the stock design — it exists only because she carries an added topping lift, a widely adopted owner's fix documented in full at [furling system — what the topping lift does, rope choice, and use](/components/rig/furling.md#what-the-topping-lift-does-rope-choice-and-use).[13]

Rodney acknowledged the answer good-naturedly, having overlooked the one drawing sheet that actually carried it.[14] Pete Martin filled in the practical choice behind Zoë's own arrangement: rather than the stock lead, follow the reroute Glen Maxwell made to keep the mainsheet clear of his self-steering wind vane — a turning block fitted under the deck, water draining to the baffle below and out through the tiller opening — a lead that also cuts down fouling for a boat carrying an outboard, as Rodney intended to fit. See [mainsheet, outboard, and rudder fouling — the rear-deck-entry question resolved](/components/mainsheet-outboard-fouling.md#the-rear-deck-entry-question-resolved-attachment-point-not-entry-point-is-the-real-hazard) for the reroute itself; photographs of both the new mainsheet lead and the topping-lift arrangement are in the group's "Zoe's Vane Gear" and "Topping Lift" photo folders.[15]

Rodney adopted the same lead for his own boat, having already downloaded the group's whole file and photo archive early on, and asked in turn whether the self-steering vane-gear plans in the files section were finished yet.[16] They were not: light-air performance was still unsatisfactory, so the vane blade — now built of plywood — had gained more forward area to make it easier for the wind to turn it, rather than enlarge the vane further. See [Glen C. Maxwell — continued vane refinement, and a family health setback](/people/glen-maxwell/build-and-zoe.md#continued-vane-refinement-and-a-family-health-setback) for the fuller, ongoing story of that gear and the family circumstances slowing its testing.[17] Concluding that he had badly underestimated his own build time and that a wind vane was likely many months off, Rodney chose to keep running his mainsheet as Glen and Pete had described, preferring a complete picture of the whole project before committing to any one system.[18]

## Choosing an epoxy: MAS for general work, System Three reserved for the tanks

Having already used **MAS** low-viscosity 2:1 epoxy resin, bought locally at marine stores, for his deck beams, Rodney weighed using it for the rest of the boat too, reserving **System Three** specifically for the water tanks, and asked whether anyone had run into trouble with the MAS brand.[19] [Mark Waters](/people/mark-waters.md) recommended System Three from firsthand experience, having built the whole of "Raffles" with it for its price, easy mixing, and customer service, and pointed to **Aircraft Spruce** in nearby Corona as a Southern California supplier stocking both a wide range of structural epoxies and System Three itself.[20] Rodney judged he would have used System Three from the start had he known of a local source, admitting an impatience with waiting on mail orders even while content to take his time on the build itself.[21] A further, independent voice found nothing wrong with MAS either, reasoning that a brand already proven on the beams should work fine elsewhere in the build too, and passed along a further eBay-sourced generic-epoxy tip from a San Francisco builder.[22] Weighing Pete Martin's own MAS-to-Low-Viscosity switch (see [people/pete-martin.md](/people/pete-martin.md#steering-line-block-placement-and-a-further-epoxy-exchange)) against the mail-order hassle of System Three, Rodney settled on finishing the bulk of his build with **West System** instead — more readily available and cheaper near him — while keeping the System Three he had already ordered specifically for the water tanks, to be done strictly per the plans and manual; the MAS used on his deck beams had in the meantime proved very strong, leaving him satisfied that brand matters less than following the mix and use instructions correctly.[23] Full brand comparison: [construction/epoxy-brands-and-chemistry.md](/construction/epoxy-brands-and-chemistry.md#mas-epoxy-validated-for-general-use-and-a-california-supplier).

## Sourcing plywood locally

Weighing the two 2008 Sailrite sail listings alongside his own build, Rodney also reported on the more mundane search for materials close to home: after visiting five lumber yards over the course of a month, he finally found one carrying the "good stuff" needed for the build. See [Sail — choosing between the two 2008 Sailrite listings](/components/rig/sail.md#choosing-between-the-two-2008-sailrite-listings) for the sail-plan comparison this update accompanied.[25]

## A build-sequencing strategy: the sail kit before hull assembly

Working in a limited build area, Rodney planned to complete as much of the boat's separate sub-assemblies as possible before joining the hull together, sewing the newly ordered Sailrite sail kit among them — reasoning that finishing every piece he could in advance would let him go sailing the same day the hull itself was complete, weather permitting.[26]

## A name from a later census

Nothing in the archive's own record of this build gives the boat a name — she is spoken of only as Rodney's boat, or simply "Paradox," throughout. The name **"Wizard Spawn"** surfaces only in a later, external source: [Bill Serjeant's 2014 worldwide builders' census](/references/builders-and-owners-census-2014.md#the-table) lists "Rod Cannon" of "USA, Venice CA" as still "In build" six years after construction began, his boat named "Wizard Spawn."[10]
