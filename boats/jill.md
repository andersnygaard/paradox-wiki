# Jill

**"Jill"** is [Jack Gardiner](/people/jack-gardiner.md)'s Rhode Island-built [Paradox](/paradox.md), launched at **Gaspee Point, Narragansett Bay, Rhode Island**, on **11 September 2004** — a deliberately chosen 9/11 anniversary date and the historic site where the Providence sloop *Hannah* lured and grounded HMS *Gaspee* in 1772, one of the "first blows for freedom" in American colonial history. Jack built her stitch-and-glue rather than to the plans' nailed construction, named her in his wife's honor, and the boat became known by the nickname **"Jill Baby."**

## Construction

Jack built "Jill" **stitch-and-glue**, joining panels without traditional wire stitches — clamping or pinning the joints with sheetrock screws instead — using West System epoxy and silica-thickened fillets, 4 oz/2 in-wide tape on the inside joints, and 12 oz biaxial cloth on the bow glue-ups. He installed chine logs and sheer clamps laminated from plywood and epoxy rather than filleted-and-taped, judging the latter too expensive for the material involved, with 50 in-wide 12 oz biaxial cloth on the bottom. Finished materials totaled **3 gallons of West System epoxy**, 12 oz biaxial cloth on the hull in place of the specified woven roving, and two extra layers of 2 oz glass on the upper hull to stop checking — no nails or screws anywhere but the fittings. The completed hull was skinned in two layers of 2 oz fabric over exterior-grade plywood, insulated with "blue" (extruded) foam that does not collect water, a sewn rubber-roofing tiller boot, and rubber-clad commercial windows (rather than the stock lexan) sized to a minimum bend radius by raising the cabin height and mast slightly.

Interior joinery was largely finished in **white oak and mahogany**, with the hatch — "incredibly strong" — the slowest single piece to complete; the cabin windows were reset in uncured black rubber bedding, cutting glare and improving the seal over the original material. Jack built a **square (rather than the plans' curved) rubber tiller-hole boot** with a cam-cleat mounted on it, though reaching inside for the cleat proved awkward in practice. His mast, boom, and yard were built from 1x4 in clear Douglas fir porch flooring, and he chose 12 oz biaxial cloth with no mat backing over woven roving, reasoning biax bonds better into an epoxy matrix, is reputed stronger for its weight, uses less epoxy, and had already proven itself on a 19 ft powerboat he had built and dived off for years without wear-through.

## Launch: 11 September 2004

Jack put "Jill" in at Gaspee Point starting in about six inches of water, poling off with an oar into a calm sea with a light (roughly 5 mph) northeast wind. The first three tacks were spent learning the boat and lost ground beating to windward, but once clear of the cove and properly trimmed she "took to doing what she is hailed to do, beat to wind! and points very well." After about an hour of tacking upwind — through sunset, and past a barge and a tanker leaving the port of Providence, whose wake the boat rode with "nothing but joy" and "very easy motion" — Jack returned to the ramp to a small crowd of onlookers curious about the boat's very short beam, met with "nothing but kudos and comments."

A Paradox should be ballasted up to its full cruising displacement — a 9 in design draft — since the boat "loves the weight," and keeping the chine runners submerged as deep as possible maximizes windward performance. Jill was already sitting on her waterline but short of the full 9 in draft and felt a little tender as a result; lacking enough ballast that day (a few bags of sand were the only stopgap, and daylight ran out), Jack beat to windward for about eight tacks before running back downwind, and judged she would perform even better once properly weighted down. He was struck by how little wake the boat threw for her short beam, and reported that once trimmed up she held her heading "without fuss for long periods." Still lacking her rear window and worried in advance about a following sea, he found the concern unfounded once underway.

Jack's first photographs of the finished boat showed a hand-cranked ratchet-drive propeller unit — mistaken at first for a [yuloh](/components/yuloh.md) — a small gearbox and ratchet clamped to an outboard mount, worked by lifting and lowering a handle to turn a large, high-pitched aluminum prop on both the up and down stroke; tested afterward in a barrel of water and confirmed working. A friend who found the mechanism funny had given it to Jack along with a period Montgomery Ward 1.5 hp gas outboard (judged late 1940s/early '50s), joking that Jack "needed his polish outboard." The same friend also left behind a military-surplus Evinrude twin 40 hp outboard for Jack's 16 ft Navy combat rubber raiding craft (CRRC/"seal boat") — a boat little used since 9/11 given the Coast Guard's heightened attention to that class of craft. Jack had not yet started building his own yuloh at that point, though he still meant to.

## A small-craft advisory, and a rudder blade that saved the trailer

A week later, on 19 September 2004, Jack took Jill out into Narragansett Bay in a forecast 10-15 kt breeze that built to 30-40 mph once he cleared a sheltered launch cove. A foul reefing drum forced a beach stop to clear the line and re-set the rig before he pressed on around the point, half-reefed, making about 6 mph on a broad reach. A fellow builder arranged to meet him on a beach 1.5 miles on to try the boat and take pictures, but declined once he saw the now-stronger conditions firsthand ("all I saw was fear in [his] eyes"). No other boats were out in the wind, and Jill was hard to spot against moored boats until close in with her sail reefed.

Getting the boat back onto the trailer proved the real ordeal of the day: the tow vehicle's rear tires sank into the wet sand, and Jack wedged the boat's own rudder blade under the deepest-sunk tire for traction — the blade held and the truck climbed out undamaged, "it saved me!!" A passing local, a commercial tugboat hand, recognized the chine runners on sight as the same "rolling blades" fitted to some tugs. Jack judged he was never in real danger aboard the boat itself and could likely have beaten home to windward if needed, but chose to end the outing early rather than push a still-unfinished boat; the day's official marine forecast, checked afterward, had upgraded to a small craft advisory (20-25 kt, gusts to 30). Once ashore, a gust was seen to jump the hull sideways enough that it could catch a crew member's foot against a chine runner while wading alongside in shallow water — the narrow runners being a real ankle hazard in that situation.

## A windward-performance trial, and "check the tides"

In late October 2004, a deliberate **windward-performance trial** ran out with the friend who had helped build the boat: starting in light air and a calm sea on the outgoing tide, the pair ran with wind and current about three miles out into the bay, then turned and tacked back, pointing well enough to hold their own racing a few other small sailboats home. Partway back Jack noticed they were losing ground while still tacking well, and recognized the tide had turned — reliably, in his home waters, the trigger for a fall weather shift. The wind freshened and began backing and veering in circles as forecast; other sailboats around them struck sail and headed in, and a grounded boat nearby was just floating free as the current picked up. A family aboard a lobster boat held station about fifteen feet off for some fifteen minutes, plainly fascinated by the sight of the boat.

After a long struggle to reach the cove, Jack gave up beating in, ran off for the point's protected side, beached Jill, and walked her along the shore for a few miles before paddling her back to the ramp — drawing the plain lesson **"check the tides."** He planned to find a sheltered inland lake to sail as the season turned cold, noting the cabin kept him warm regardless. He had by then fitted a 30 lb-thrust electric trolling motor as auxiliary power, conceded he would eventually have to build the yuloh he had never gotten to, and — having studied an existing topping-lift fix — planned to rig something similar of his own.

## The "paradox mpeg movie" thread: two days in 20-35 mph wind

The following summer, Jack filmed Jill under sail and shared the footage (a roughly 30-megabyte video, passed around via BitTorrent since it exceeded his e-mail provider's size limit).

Jill under sail: **two days out in a steady 20-35 mph wind out of the south**, working close along a lee shore and briefly into the stronger gusts while carrying, by his own admission, too much sail. The boat carried a 6 hp outboard on a folding mount, idling in the footage, and was loaded with **two adults plus tools used as ballast** and food, her water tanks left unfilled — Jack expected even better manners once they were topped up. She took freighter wakes from every angle on the river with no fuss, and a bungee cord clipped behind the top of the outboard and led to the cleat eliminated the sheet snagging he had fought before, "a blessing actually."

A second round of beaching footage, shot from another boat to get a proper look at Jill from outside rather than from aboard her, gave a full set of technical particulars: a **6 hp outboard** mounted on a folding aluminum bracket, idling just above idle, drove the boat well under power alone; sailing with the motor down but switched off, she heeled the other way and still sailed properly despite the drag. The water ballast tanks were empty throughout, and Jack judged she would perform even better filled; about **80 lb of lead** ballast had by then been added, bringing her onto her lines well with two adults aboard even with the roughly 60 lb outboard's weight offset by a small ballast shift. His GPS logged a cruising speed around **5.5 mph** and a recorded peak of **8.1 mph**, reached crossing a tanker's wake in a channel with a strong current running.

A further video captured Jill motoring out of Narragansett Bay toward open water, passing close by a powerboat near the Jamestown Verrazzano Bridge (West Passage, Narragansett Bay) and taking the wake from the side without incident — filmed specifically to document how well the hull handles the close powerboat passes she draws often in that water, given her short beam.

## A sandbar grounding, reefing-early doctrine, and a luff rope for the sail crease

A comparable early scare came on his second or third outing, when river current and swirling fall winds blew Jill onto the lee side of a sandbar, forcing a half-mile barefoot walk in the water to free her. Jack now keeps two pairs of diving rock boots, a dry suit, and a wet suit aboard as a result, calling the barefoot walk something to "NEVER" repeat. The clearest lesson from both groundings: **reef early** — too much sail out is the real trouble, since once the yard spins inside the furling loop and starts winding up the sail itself, nothing can be done but lower away and hand-reef from there.

Around the same time Jack mounted a **6 hp outboard**, larger than he otherwise wanted, specifically so he could respond quickly if his 102-year-old grandfather (who lived alone until a fall broke his hip at 101) needed help at a moment's notice; he planned to downsize to a 2.5 or 3 hp motor once that need passed.

On the sail-crease/boom-taper question itself, reshaping the boom's taper is the wrong fix — changing the boom's geometry risks the sail no longer reefing and furling correctly. Instead Jack added a **luff rope** to the sail's leading edge, which let him adjust the sail and noticeably lessened the crease at the head — a different fix from the [boom-end foam tip](/components/rig/index.md#rolling-boom-and-sail-foot), aimed at the sail rather than the spar.

## A 6 hp outboard, an onboard generator, and local sailing conditions

By January 2006 Jack was running a **6 hp Johnson** outboard on Jill, weighing about **60 lb** — heavier than the 2-3 hp motors most other Paradox owners favored, offset by shifting ballast to compensate. Run at low rpm the motor "motor sails very well," and Jack planned to downsize to a 3 hp unit once budget allowed. He also fitted a **1,000-watt two-stroke generator** aboard to keep the boat's batteries charged. The generator itself was a gift: a friend had picked up an abandoned, barely used generator left behind after a trailer-home move and sold it to Jack for $100, and it ran well and quietly. It would stow in the forward compartment, alongside a large deep-cell marine battery, with a 12-volt fan mounted in the vent trunk to exhaust fumes. He had not yet built mounting brackets for either the generator or battery at that point.

Jack's home sailing grounds were Rhode Island waters near Newport — strong currents and tides, changing winds, rocky coasts, and heavy boat traffic. A standing personal constraint governed how far he could range from home: his grandfather, then **103 years old**, lived with Jack's mother, and Jack needed to be able to get home quickly if a problem arose.

Days after describing the generator, Jack faced a medical emergency of his own: a right tonsil infection had closed his windpipe almost to the point of requiring an emergency surgical airway, and he had just been discharged from the hospital, with doctors still investigating the cause. He recovered without further complication, later resuming both his building progress and the sails documented above.

## Why the sail uses sleeves rather than lashings, and a first sail of the 2006 season

The Paradox sail is fitted with **sleeves** on the yard and boom rather than simply lashed on as a matter of Matt Layden's own preference for simplicity over complication. On his own bottom layup Jack used a **single layer of 12 oz biax cloth**, taking about a gallon of epoxy — noting that at the time Matt built the original Paradox, biax cloth was either not yet available or harder to source in a cut piece, so Jack had to buy a full 150-yard roll to get his single layer. See [rolling boom and sail foot](/components/rig/index.md#rolling-boom-and-sail-foot) for the sail's sleeve construction in full, and [why the mainsheet presented at the helmsman's back is not awkward in practice](/components/rig/index.md#why-the-mainsheet-presented-at-the-helmsmans-back-is-not-awkward-in-practice) for related rig detail.

Jack finally got Jill out for her **first sail of the 2006 season**, in 6-10 knot winds, sailing for about four hours without once touching the tiller before motoring back as the wind died at dusk.

## A 2008 fuel-and-motor-mount exchange: gas can, bungee, and ratchet strap

Nearly two years later, in a newcomer thread about outboard motors generally, Jack gave the fullest account yet of his own motor-mount and fuel arrangement: the outboard sits on a folding mount, offset to one side, with the plastic gas can stowed between the two rear cleats and held down with a bungee cord looped over the cover to the cleat — the sheet stays clear of it unless too much slack is let out, in which case the rudder is usually the real culprit. He judged the arrangement doesn't strip the boat of her sailing ability or stability, since ballast is adjusted to compensate, and would not let the outboard decision alone rule out a Paradox.[35] A sturdier alternative followed: a 1½ in ratchet strap, the same type used to strap lumber to a truck bed, has never once let go; he also floated building a small, low-profile tank directly into the rear deck itself — vented and drainable rather than sealed — as an alternative to a portable can, noting the folding mount's ability to lift the motor clear of the water has kept swamping from ever being a problem. Full debate at [auxiliary outboard power](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-folding-outboards-mount-and-fuel-tied-down-between-the-rear-cleats).[36]
