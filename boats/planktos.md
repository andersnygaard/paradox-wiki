# Planktos (Roselt Croeser)

**Planktos** is [Roselt Croeser](/people/roselt-croeser.md)'s Paradox, built in **Western Australia** between **early 2013 and the end of 2016** and cruised out of Perth, most notably on a shakedown passage north to **Shark Bay**.[1][2] She is the archive's only documented Paradox **converted from the stock lug to a junk rig and sailed** — one of only two junk-rigged Paradoxes built and sailed on record, the other being [Eerik Mee](/people/eerik-mee.md)'s [THU](/boats/thu.md), which carries a purpose-designed junk rig rather than a conversion (see [junk rig](/design/ideas/junk-rig.md)) — and one of the few builds on record from outside the mailing-list era, worked up from the plans alone.

She is also the fullest single statement in the archive of what a **deliberately frugal** Paradox costs and what it is like to live with: the build ran to roughly **$2500** in materials, and the boat carries **no electrical system at all**.[3]

## The build: four years, four locations

The build took **from early 2013 to the end of 2016**, run alongside owner-building a house — the two projects competing for the same time — and moved through **four different locations** over its span.[2] The result was deliberately a **working boat finish**: "very strong, very simple," built using what was already to hand wherever possible.[2] The **mast was repurposed from a sunken boat** and the **sail came from an SS18**, so the two most expensive items in a small boat's rig cost nothing.[2][3]

The finished assessment of the design as a building proposition was unreserved: Paradox "must be the easiest type of boat to build," on the strength of the **quality of the plans** and of the **builders' group** behind them.[3]

## Costs

The **overall build cost was approximately $2500**, "mostly wood, epoxy, glass and paint," with the mast and sail repurposed rather than bought.[3] The **trailer cost more per dollar of the project than most of the boat**: about **$2300**, plus roughly **$200** of modifications.[3] Set against the build figures recorded elsewhere in the archive, this is a low-end result, achieved by salvaging the rig and accepting a working finish.

## The junk rig

Planktos was built to the stock **lug rig with roller furling** and then **changed to a junk rig**.[1] The verdict after cruising her is favourable and specific: the junk rig "works very well" — **easy and very quick to reef and unreef**, with **very good sail twist control**, and **cheap, light-weight fittings**.[4]

This is one of only two places the archive's long theoretical argument about junk-rigging a Paradox meets a real built boat, and the only one where the stock lug was **converted**. The objections raised in that discussion — spar weight aloft, the extra line to manage, sheeting a sail that already overhangs the transom, and the risk of moving the centre of effort forward of the hull's centre of lateral resistance — are not addressed point by point in the report, and no detail of how the sheeting or the mast position was resolved is recorded.[1][4] What survives is the outcome rather than the method — for the method, worked through on a purpose-designed junk rig, see [THU](/boats/thu.md).

## Systems: none

Planktos carries **no electrical system**.[3] Navigation and power are handled entirely by portable equipment: a **hand-held GPS**, **Navionics on a mobile phone**, **power-bank batteries**, and a **10 watt solar panel** to keep them charged.[3] For a boat sailed to Shark Bay and back, this is the archive's most minimal outfitting on record.

## How she sails

The flat bottom and shallow draft are judged "excellent for carefree sailing and anchoring": lying **overnight in knee-deep water**, stepping off into **ankle-deep water**, **drying out level**, no anxiety about running aground, and an easy shove off the beach.[3] She is **easy to launch and retrieve**, and **very forgiving** under way, with an easy motion.[3] The flat bottom also means **very little hobby-horsing** and an easy motion at anchor.[4]

Sailing from inside the cabin is recorded with a dry qualification — there is "no frying in the cockpit on hot days, instead you bake" — a Western Australian gloss on the enclosed-cockpit trade-off.[4] Going to windward in rough water is "challenging," as with all small boats.[4] And she is "definitely a 1 person boat except for very short outings."[4]

The stated ceiling is **around 20 knots of wind**; "beyond that it is survival."[3]

## The group record: the junk rig proven, speeds, and the sale

Croeser (posting as "Waldo Cruiser") added a fuller first-hand account of Planktos on the [Paradox Sailboat Facebook group](/references/paradox-facebook-group.md). On the junk rig he is specific and favourable: the sail was a **hand-me-down adapted from another project** — so its bottom panel never set perfectly, though "the back 80% of it worked fine" — and the **mast was left in its original Paradox position** rather than moved.[5] In Western Australia's wind he was "often reefed down to three of the four panels," and rated the rig, on balance, **less twist-prone than the lug, more reliable and quicker to reef, and more efficient** thanks to better control of sail shape; sheeting to the end of each batten gives "excellent control."[5][6] Planktos was featured as the **Junk Rig Association's "boat of the month"** in December 2023.[6]

His performance figures fill out the design's real numbers: sustained **5.5 knots** on a broad reach in about 15 knots of wind, a general cruising average of **2.5 knots**, an **80-nautical-mile overnight run averaging 3.3 knots**, and the ability to hold 2–2.5 knots even in light air — "as Yrvind says, it's all relative."[7] For steering he simply **ties the tiller rope in place** rather than fitting any self-steering gear, keeping the boat "good at being simple," and sculls in calms with a **6-foot oar paddled over the side, SUP-style**, rather than a yuloh; he steers from **inside the cabin**, wedged into the leeward corner on a beanbag cushion with a clear view through the windows, the boat sailing better heeled.[8] He fitted a **removable panel at the back of the doghouse** to cut the claustrophobia of the enclosed cockpit.[8] Planktos was offered for sale in Western Australia and **sold in July 2023 to Kevin Williamson**, who kept her junk rig, fitted a **folding mast** to ease unstepping it for bridges, then **extended the mast** for more sail area — Croeser measured the enlarged sail at about **8.3 m²** — and renamed her **"Skye."** She remains a junk-rigged Paradox in Western Australia under her new name (see [junk rig](/design/ideas/junk-rig.md)).[9][10]

## Shark Bay, and what was contemplated after

The **shakedown cruise was to Shark Bay**, roughly 800 km north of Perth up the Western Australian coast, and the photographs record her under junk sail on open water, at anchor off the dune coastline, and beached and dried out on the sand at low tide.[1] A closing list of what might be attempted "if you are really brave" names three longer passages: **Perth to Shark Bay**, the **Abrolhos and back**, and **Cape Leeuwin and back**.[3] These are stated as prospects at the April 2021 presentation, not as voyages made.
