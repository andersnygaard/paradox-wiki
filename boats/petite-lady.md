# Petite Lady

**Petite Lady** is Hugh Miller's [Paradox](/paradox.md), built in New Zealand and completed at the end of 2003 — the archive's first New Zealand build, distinct from a separate prospective New Zealand builder mentioned around the same time. A haul-out report Hugh Miller posted directly to the list in January 2007, after four years afloat on a tidal mooring with no maintenance beyond periodic salt-water washing, stands as one of the archive's fullest single accounts of long-term, low-upkeep Paradox ownership.

## A 2003 build, reported secondhand

Petite Lady's construction was reported to the `paradoxbuilders` list secondhand throughout, via [Dave Bolduc](/people/bolduc/dave.md), since her builder was not yet posting directly. In June 2003 she was nearing completion — deck, bottom, cabin, interior, rudder, spars, sail, and running gear all finished, built to the plans except for the spars, which were made round using the birdmouth method rather than the plans' box-section design.[1] A further round of photographs followed in August 2003, by which point Hugh hoped to have her sailing by the New Zealand summer.[2][3] By Christmas 2003 she was reported finished and named Petite Lady, with completion photographs posted to the group's Files section.[4][5] By June 2004 Hugh Miller was reported to be enjoying the finished boat, with a further round of sailing photographs posted.[6]

## The 2007 haul-out: four years afloat, no deviations beyond curved cabin ends

Years of only secondhand updates ended in January 2007, when Hugh Miller posted to the list directly for the first time. In his 71st year, he had just brought the boat home from her tidal mooring for the first time in four years and reported her still in excellent condition despite no maintenance beyond periodic salt-water washing: her copper-dust-and-resin bottom coating, applied at launch and refreshed yearly with a scotchbrite pad, needed only burnishing; her interior — hinged floors over the side panels for bilge access, water carried in separate containers rather than relying solely on the built-in tanks — remained sound; and his only deviations from the plans were curved fore-and-aft cabin ends and shorter windows than drawn. He operates a curved Chinese-style yuloh on a stainless towball fitting for short, unhurried trips, and a 2 hp air-cooled Honda outboard when covering distance or catching a tide, preferring the yuloh to avoid the mainsheet fouling the motor. Having nearly sold the boat before this haul-out prompted him to strip her interior, he found instead that he still loved and used her, judging the design's own thinking-through so complete that he had no wish to change anything about it and no interest in the radical modifications some builders proposed. His longest passage on record is a 40 nm coastal cruise, and the boat once brought him home safely in 30-knot winds.[7]

## Paint scheme and the annual Mahurangi Regatta passage

Petite Lady is glassed all over, including the cabin sides. Her topside paint scheme is Interlux Brightside Yellow over an oil-based undercoat, with white house paint on the cabin sides and a tinted deck; all the paint remained in good condition years on, apart from a few chips, and could still have seen further service without becoming scruffy.[8] Hugh Miller's annual passage to the Mahurangi Regatta covers 32 nm each way, typically 8-9 hours in steady wind; on one occasion he continued a further 8 nm past the regatta to Kawau Island, then returned around the island to his mooring in a single roughly 12-hour day — just over 40 nm, the longest passage on record above. Most of his trips are uneventful and weather-picked, often built around a six-hour tidal sail with lunch taken adrift, and by his own description he sails at a relaxed pace, content to steer from inside the cabin in rain or cold.[8]
