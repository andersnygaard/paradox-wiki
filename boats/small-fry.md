# Small Fry

**"Small Fry"** is a Paradox built to [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)'s design by **Geoff Davis** and sailed on the coast of **Western Australia**, her transom lettered **"SMALL FRY / FREMANTLE"** for her home port.[1][5] She is the archive's only documented Western Australian Paradox — the previously recorded Australian builds are both Queensland boats, [Walden](/boats/walden.md) and [Aussie Paradox](/boats/aussie-paradox.md), on the opposite side of the continent.

Small Fry was a **completed and cruised boat before her builder moved on to a larger project**, a William Atkin *Perigee*; the Paradox is described as the previous boat-building venture of the two.[1] The photographic record of her is dated by a **10 April 2014** capture in Shark Bay, published on a personal site assembled across **2017-2018**.[1][8]

Small Fry has also rounded **Cape Leeuwin** — the exposed southwestern tip of the Australian continent, where the Indian and Southern Oceans meet — and continued on "for several days without putting in," a sustained open-water passage well outside the sheltered, ultra-shoal cruising grounds documented below.[9]

## Shark Bay, and a design suited to the ground it sits on

Shark Bay drew the strongest verdict in the record: it **"must have been created with Matt's design in mind,"** and is recommended without qualification to **"anyone looking for an exciting wilderness in which to go exploring with an ultra shoal draft sailing boat."**[1] The photograph carrying that judgement shows Small Fry lying to her own reflection on dead-flat water at dusk, **waiting on the morning tide to float her after drying out overnight** when the tide went out — the boat treated not as stranded but as simply parked until the water came back.[1]

That is the practical case for the design's **9 in draft** stated from the cruising end rather than the drawing-board end, and it is echoed at the other extreme of the same coast: the anchorage at **Jurien Bay** is **"open only to very shallow draft boats,"** making it **"so good to cruise in a paradox."**[7] The photograph there shows her afloat in water clear enough to read the sand ripples beneath her, well inshore of where a keelboat could lie.[7]

## Drying out as ordinary practice, not incident

Ultra-shoal draft is recorded here as an **anchoring advantage first and foremost** — it "opens up all kinds of opportunities for anchoring" — with the consequence stated dryly: she "has **dried out in some slightly odd places at times**."[5][6] The archive's other drying-out material treats taking the ground as an event to be managed (see [Faith — drying out on a sunken mooring block](/boats/faith.md#drying-out-on-a-sunken-mooring-block-during-the-west-country-cruise) and [beaching stern-first for a comfortable sleeping angle](/components/rudder.md#beaching-stern-first-a-sleeping-comfort-technique-and-why-it-doesnt-damage-the-rudder)); this record treats it as the normal daily rhythm of the cruise, the tide dictating when the boat is a boat.

The stern photograph taken aground on a sand spit shows how little the practice costs the boat: she sits **upright and level on her flat bottom** in a drained tidal channel, the **transom-hung rudder blade still fully lowered** below the level of the hull bottom, with no legs, props, or beaching gear of any kind in use.[5]

## Stiffness at rest, measured by a bird

While cruising the **Pelsaert Group in the Abrolhos Islands**, Small Fry was visited by a **Brahminy Kite**, and the encounter produced an unusually concrete measure of the boat's behaviour at anchor: **the force of the bird taking off was enough to rock her as much as a sailor moving about on deck**.[2] For a boat of roughly 1,400 lb design displacement (see [weight and ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md)), that is a real datum on how a lightly-loaded Paradox responds to a small impulse at rest, and sits alongside the archive's other anchor-motion observations — see [pounding: rare at anchor, occasional underway](/operations/sailing-characteristics.md#pounding-rare-at-anchor-occasional-underway) and [why the seat's position damps the felt motion](/operations/sailing-characteristics.md#why-the-seats-position-damps-the-felt-motion).

## The galley bench under the hatch

The **removable seat doubles as a galley bench**, carrying a **small butane-cartridge camp stove** judged **"adequate for simple meals of a hot beverage."**[4] The siting is the point: because the bench sits **directly under the hatch**, **"excess heat and steam does not build up in the cabin"** — the open hatch acting as the galley's flue.[4] See [interior — the removable seat as a galley bench](/components/interior.md#the-removable-seat-as-a-galley-bench-and-why-the-stove-sits-under-the-hatch) for the same arrangement stated generally, and [cabin heating — window condensation](/operations/cabin-heating.md#window-condensation-causes-and-mitigation) for why keeping cooking moisture out of the cabin matters in a boat this size.

## Trailering on an oversized utility trailer

Small Fry travels on an **oversized utility trailer** that **"works as a boat trailer, and can be used for many other things when not launching the boat"** — a galvanised, mesh-sided box trailer carrying the hull on its bed, with a spare wheel stowed beneath the bow.[3] That dual-purpose choice is the practical answer to how a Fremantle-based boat reaches cruising grounds several hundred kilometres up the coast, and it inverts the archive's usual trailer problem: rather than adapting a boat trailer to a Paradox (see [trailering](/operations/trailering.md)), a general-purpose trailer is bought large enough to do both jobs.

## What the photographs show

The published photographs are the fullest colour record of a finished Paradox in the archive outside the [microcruising.com](/references/microcruising-website.md) material, and several details are legible in them:[1][3][5][7]

- **Finish**: high-gloss **black topsides** over a **white bottom and boot-top** — an unusual choice for a boat kept in the Western Australian sun, and the reverse of the light topsides most builders on record chose.
- **Rig**: the standing [lug sail](/components/rig/index.md) set in the driveway is a **tan/rust-coloured** cloth on wooden spars, with the unstayed, varnished wooden mast carrying only running lines; the sail is shown **rolled on the boom** at rest, the design's [roller furling](/components/rig/furling.md) in its stowed state.
- **Cabin**: large, dark-tinted cabin windows carried well around the forward corners of the cabin. Whether this represents a departure from the stock window arrangement is not established here — no dimensions are on record for either, and the record carries no builder's comment on the choice.
- **Fit-out**: a **solar panel** mounted flat on the cabin top abaft the hatch, and spars stowed along the side decks.
