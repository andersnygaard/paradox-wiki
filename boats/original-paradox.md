# Original Paradox

The **original Paradox** is the prototype built by designer
[Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md) himself — the first example of the
[Paradox](/paradox.md) design, and "one of Matt Layden's most successful
micro-cruiser designs." Matt built her in **1993**, returning to
[chine runners](/design/chine-runners-and-windward-performance.md)
after concluding that [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md)'s
centerboard "did not seem to improve windward performance sufficiently to merit
its construction." As of July 2001 it was one of only two Paradoxes known to be
sailing (the other being [Don Elliott](/people/don-elliott.md)'s; see
[Don Elliott's Paradox](elliott-paradox.md)).

# Design particulars

The Bolducs' website gives the canonical design figures for the Paradox (which,
being a one-off design built to plan, are also the original boat's own
particulars):

| Particular | Metric | Imperial |
|---|---|---|
| LOA | 4.2 m | 13' 10" |
| LWL | 4.1 m | 13' 5" |
| Beam | 1.23 m | 4' 0½" (≈48") |
| Draft | 0.23 m | 9" |
| Sail area | 9.3 m² | 100 sq ft |
| Displacement | 640 kg | 1,410 lb |

The site's five "interesting details" for Paradox are: **chine runners**;
**70 litres of fresh-water ballast**; **self-righting**; **positive buoyancy**;
and **all lines lead into the cabin**. The 70 L (~154 lb) of water ballast is
the designer's own stated ballast figure for Paradox and should be read
alongside the heavier lead-ballast arrangements builders adopted for their own
boats (Matt's boats in fact use a *layered* scheme — fixed ballast, water-tank
ballast, and stowed stores that also act as ballast); see
[weight and ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md).

# The build and launch

- Built in **about 4 months of full-time work** (see
  [build effort](/construction/build-effort.md)).
- Matt **had no car** and hauled all the materials to a **remote building site
  in the woods on his bicycle trailer** (the little trailer is visible in the
  "para2" construction photo).
- **Launched by skidding / lowering it down a steep ravine to the Salmon River**
  below the site (photo "para4") — "no wonder these little boats have to be
  built tough."

Matt sails it single-handed from inside the [cabin](/paradox.md) and stows
anchors below as ballast using a stern-bucket routine (see
[ground tackle](/operations/ground-tackle.md)); it is the boat sailing Exuma
Sound in the well-known 25 kts / 6 kts photo (see [rig and sail](/design/rig.md)).

# Cruising record

Per [microcruising.com](/references/microcruising-website.md) (which calls
Paradox Matt's **fifth home-built sailboat**):

- **1993** — after launching, Matt sailed her from Connecticut down the
  Intracoastal Waterway and out as far as the **Ragged Islands** in the Bahamas.
- **1994** — the original Paradox cruised **in tandem with the Bolducs'
  [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) to the Bahamas** (Bimini,
  Chub Cay, Nassau, the Exumas, Long Island, Conception Island, Rum Cay, Spanish
  Wells, Eleuthera) — a notable early [buddy-boat cruise](/operations/group-cruise-reports.md)
  between the two designs, and the trip on which they met [Chris Morejohn](/people/chris-morejohn.md)'s
  family living aboard the 32' sharpie "Hogfish Lips."
- **2003** — Matt entered the boat, on 8 January, in the **300-mile
  [Everglades Challenge](/operations/group-cruise-reports.md)** — a ~260-mile
  WaterTribe race from Fort Desoto to Key Largo (class 4/open/experimental) —
  and took **first in her class and overall honors** (Matt dated the win to
  2003 in his 2019 Cedar Key talk). He planned to sail to the start from his
  home in Stuart, Florida, and sail back home again afterward — see
  [weight, ballast, and displacement](/design/weight-and-ballast.md)
  for that mention, including a launching-rules aside about the race's
  above-high-tide-line launch requirement. Word of the entry spread quickly:
  within two days, one list member had already written, in an unrelated reply
  in the "buy Paradox plans" thread (see
  [Paradox building plans — a sixteenth recurrence](/references/building-plans.md)),
  "I'm eager to follow Matt's progress in the Watertribe adventure race. This
  will add new dimensions to the capabilities of Paradox as an all-around
  microcruising adventure boat."
- The original Paradox has logged roughly **3,000 miles**, "probably a little
  more seaworthy" than the much higher-mileage Little Cruiser — while both are
  "tough coastal cruisers," not offshore boats (see
  [blue-water suitability](/design/blue-water-suitability.md)).

## Glen Maxwell sails alongside Matt Layden before the 2003 Everglades Challenge

In the days before the 2003 Everglades Challenge began,
[Glen C. Maxwell](/people/glen-maxwell.md) sailed alongside Matt Layden in
person at the race's starting point, **Ft. Desoto** (FL) — the only firsthand,
in-person account on record of Matt Layden himself under sail. The two sailed
together on **Thursday and part of Friday**, before Matt hauled the boat out
of the water to prepare for the race.

Matt's **haul-out rig** was two rollers purchased for the purpose, combined
with a **four-part block and tackle tied to a six-part block and tackle** for
mechanical advantage — since it was unclear whether outside assistance would
be allowed during the actual race haul-out, Matt made a point of hauling the
boat out entirely **by himself**. Matt's "secret weapon" for the race was his
ability to **sleep while underway and keep going** — sailing (or drifting)
through the night rather than stopping.

Sailing two Paradoxes together was **a first even for Matt himself** — the
designer had apparently never before sailed his own boat in company with
another Paradox — and Glen called it "a real treat." The comparison was
lopsided: Matt's **windward sailing ability was far superior**, and Matt
suggested alterations to Glen's own sail after watching it perform. Matt also
passed on a recommendation that **Dabbler Sails** (already known as a supplier
to [Whisper](/boats/whisper.md), see [rig and sail](/design/rig.md)) was "very
good at building these lug sails" — sailing in company with the original
Paradox gave Glen "real promise" that a well-made sail could perform much
better than his own, never fully satisfied since it was first set. No
photographs survive of the two Paradoxes sailing together.

The WaterTribe race-tracking site showed Matt had already **checked in at
Checkpoint 2 (Placida, FL)** at 09:30 that morning, continuing on toward
**Sanibel** via the inside route, with the WaterTribe forum mentioning that
Matt had been **helping a fellow competitor** along the way.

This account stands as strong independent evidence that **Matt Layden's
reputation as a superior sailor of his own designs was not exaggerated**:
Glen, an experienced professional boat builder who had sailed his own Paradox
"Zoë" for well over a year by this point, still found Matt's windward
performance "far superior."

A fuller, more specific retelling given six weeks later added that the **sail
problem Matt identified was insufficient draft** (Glen's own sail was "cut too
flat," while Matt's needed "a little more draft" even so) and a separate
**yard-bowing fix**. See
[rig and sail](/design/rig.md)
and [matt-layden/biography.md](/matt-layden/biography.md)
for the fuller account.

## Matt's haul-out rollers

The **rollers** Matt used to haul Paradox out solo at Ft. Desoto, described in
full via a hand-drawn sketch:

- The rollers were **6 in long, 2 in diameter boat rollers**, mounted on
  trestles built from **2× stock on 2×4s**.
- Matt **carried both rollers stowed in the starboard bilge compartment**,
  sized specifically to stow there.
- He used **two sets**, moving the aft roller forward to the bow as the
  boat's weight came onto it — "they worked great."
- Overall roller-trestle dimensions were about **30 in long by 12-14 in
  wide**.
- Each roller trailed a **line long enough to cleat off**, so that a roller
  would not drift away in the surf before Matt could retrieve it.

This is the fullest technical description on record of how Matt hauls the
original Paradox out of the water single-handed — a practical, entirely
home-devised solution consistent with his boat's design philosophy of being
sailed and handled without outside assistance. See also
[rig and sail](/design/rig.md) and
[furling system](/design/furling-system.md) for how Matt reefs his sail
underway.

## The 2003 Everglades Challenge: checkpoint progress

The **Everglades Challenge**'s official standings, via the WaterTribe results
site
(`watertribe.com/Challenge/Results/ResultsAll.asp?table=Everglades2003`),
tracked Matt's progress down the Florida coast:

- After Checkpoint 2 (67 of about 300 total miles), Matt was running roughly
  **10 hours behind the paddling kayakers** in the field, but had reported "a
  good night's sleep while sailing offshore" — the first real-time evidence of
  Matt actually using the "sleep while underway" tactic he had described to
  Glen (above).
- Matt then became the **first competitor to reach Checkpoint 3**, logging in
  at 14:40, and continued "headed offshore."
- As the field converged there, the showing prompted talk of **"a new paradigm
  for the Watertribe and adventure racing."**

## The finish: Checkpoint 4 and the win

Matt's own checked-in comment on reaching **Checkpoint 4 (Flamingo, 273
miles)** at 07:45 read: **"Spectacular sailing: Surfing at 6 knots with
dolphins on the bow off Pavillion Key. Diminishing wind, varying to NNE
overnight. Caught the tide at East Cape. Onward!"**

The WaterTribe discussion forum recorded a stream of updates on the field:

- **Team Weed Warrior** (an outrigger canoe) reached Checkpoint 3 second,
  **9 hours 20 minutes** behind Matt's solo Paradox; **Team Nootka** (a
  racing canoe) took third, a further 3 hours 50 minutes back. The first
  three finishers at CP3 were, in order, **Class 4 single male (Matt), Class 4
  double male, Class 4 double male** — with the open question of whether
  Matt's alternate propulsion, his **Chinese yuloh**, could keep pace with the
  paddling competition if the wind died or went on the nose.
- At **Checkpoint 4**, Matt's lead had if anything grown: he arrived roughly
  **7.5 hours ahead of the second-place boat's own Checkpoint 3 time** — well
  past race officials' prediction that sailboats would remain competitive in
  the Everglades Challenge for only "at least one more year."
- The reason for Matt's decisive lead: the "roller-launching design was
  another stroke of genius"; the competing designs were mostly narrow racing
  kayaks and canoes with small auxiliary sails, "not very dry or seaworthy,"
  which had to land and make camp to eat and rest, while Matt "just continues
  to sail snug as a bug" — sleeping underway per his stated race tactic —
  giving him a large advantage crossing open bays where waves could swamp the
  paddlers. During the race, Matt **stopped and rescued an overturned kayaker
  and towed him into shore**.
- The wider fleet was surprised the **Seapearl** (a light trimaran design also
  entered) "has not been more competitive."

Within hours of the finish, the race's own official results listing —
posted under the subject line **"MATT WINS!"** — gave contemporaneous
confirmation of the win, matching the win already known from
[microcruising.com](/references/microcruising-website.md):

| Checkpoint | Miles | Date/Time | Matt's comment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Start (Fort Desoto) | 0 | 3/8/03 07:00 | Sailed to the start from Stuart, FL via the Okeechobee Waterway, scouting the Grande Tours and Venice checkpoints along the way; planned to sail the race outside as much as possible, weather permitting. |
| CP1 (Venice Inlet) | 41 | 3/8/03 22:40 | Nice ENE wind in the morning, light/variable to calm in the afternoon; "lots of sculling-assisted kayaker off Anna Maria am"; continuing outside. |
| CP2 (Grande Tours) | 67 | 3/9/03 09:35 | Wind light ENE veering S by dawn; "got some sleep on the offshore back"; proceeding inside to Sanibel. |
| CP3 (Chokoloskee) | 174 | 3/10/03 14:40 | Nice wind overnight, calmed by morning; napped off Naples; "easy day so far"; proceeding outside. |
| CP4 (Flamingo) | 273 | 3/11/03 07:45 | "Spectacular sailing: Surfing at 6 knots with dolphins on the bow off Pavillion Key"; diminishing wind, varying NNE overnight; caught the tide at East Cape. |
| **Finish (Key Largo)** | **304** | **3/11/03 17:51** | **"Just got in - comments to follow on discussion forum a bit later tonight."** |

The listing's own boat description, entered by Matt himself pre-race, is the
fullest self-description on record of the original Paradox as a race entry:
**"Paradox 14' coastal cruising sailboat, with chinese yuloh secondary
propulsion. This is a heavy, long-range expedition cruiser and stretches the
limits of beach-launchability (I'll be using rollers and block & tackle,
should be fun to watch...). She is not fast but can keep sailing in any
weather and run hairy inlets safe & dry."** At the finish, **"nobody has
reached CP4 yet and he is finished"** — underscoring the scale of Matt's
margin over the rest of the field.

The next day, a forum post from Matt's own **wife/friends/family** reported a
collect phone call from Matt himself in Key Largo: **"He sounded tired but in
good spirits. Good race Matt (you crazy sailor). Please get some sleep now."**

This is contemporaneous, primary-source confirmation of the result already
known from
[microcruising.com](/references/microcruising-website.md):
Matt Layden and the original Paradox finished the ~300-mile 2003 Everglades
Challenge **first overall**, well ahead of every paddled entry, racing solo
and using only sail and yuloh power.

## Why a heavy monohull beat the light multihulls

The win prompted discussion of *why* a heavy, displacement monohull had so
thoroughly out-sailed lighter multihull and paddled competition. The
explanation, from a sailor with direct comparative experience owning a 21 ft
monohull, a Sea Pearl trimaran, and an SP28: **wave action slows a light
Seapearl significantly, especially to windward**, where it is "constantly
thrown off the waves," while "the heavy Paradox powers through the slop with
inertia," its motion also "much better on the body."

The result was also credited to Matt's own skill as distinct from the boat
itself — "obviously Matt knows how to use a yuloh as well as being a very
skilled sailer, strategist and navigator" — summed up memorably: **"Perhaps he
may have created a class of his own! The microcruiser class. Or gotten himself
banned altogether!"**

## Other boats that could have raced, and a wish for a larger Paradox

Discussion of the race's result drifted, in the following days, from *why*
Paradox won toward *what else could have* — and, in passing, back to the
recurring wish for a larger Paradox.

A two-person Sea Pearl crew, rowing in shifts from two stations, was floated
as potentially "more competative" than a solo entry, since two rowers could
keep the boat moving around the clock even in a calm — "sleeper would make
good ballast." The race's rules were seen as having drifted from their
original form, prompting speculation that a **minimum length rule**, or a
separate class for "overweight or overlength boats," might be needed for
boats like Paradox in future years — the same enlarge-Paradox territory the
record keeps returning to (see
[stretched Paradox](/design/variants/stretched-paradox.md)), alongside
speculation about whether Matt had simply caught favorable wind shifts and
whether the Everglades Challenge committee ("the Chief") might set a 15 ft
minimum entry length the following year. A competitor on Cape Sable was
separately reported to have watched a 21 ft Sea Pearl sail by at sunset.

No Hobie-cat-style catamarans had entered, though one "would be very
competitive" against a design "never thought of... as a racing boat, more of
a slow but steady cruiser." A **tacking proa built from a Hobie 18 hull**,
sailed two-up under the name **"Weed Warrior,"** had raced (and finished
second, as reported above). Separately, a competitor known as "Crazy Russian"
had entered a catamaran of his own; sleeping aboard a cat underway, or rowing
one short-handed against no wind, was thought likely to be much harder than on
a monohull, and Crazy Russian was believed to have planned to use a sliding
rowing seat borrowed from a racing shell. A heavy-cat entry was judged to
demand "a very skilled operator," doubtfully able to meet the race's
self-rescue requirement given how changeable Florida weather is that time of
year over a 270-mile course — though "not unreasonable for a seasoned racer."
Wished-for future entries included a Pacific proa like an Ilikai/Mbuli or a
fast trimaran, and — returning to the enlarged-Paradox wish on record
elsewhere (see
[stretched Paradox](/design/variants/stretched-paradox.md)) — a marginally
larger Paradox (+10%), similar in length to Little Cruiser: **"I am proud that
Matt was so successful, and it speaks volumes for the design in terms of
safety and reliability. Now if Matt would just draw the larger version, I'd be
first in line."** A **two-person, more-heavily-canvased 21 ft Paradox** was
floated as "pretty competetive" in its own right, with the Everglades
Challenge imagined spawning a "microcruiser racing" tradition of its own —
**"The beer and pizza America's Cup! What could be more fun than that, every
March in the Keyes?"**

Three days after the finish, an outsider's admiring reaction arrived from a UK
correspondent: **"If you were to write a film script with a story line based
on the results of this race, no one would believe it. But our man has brought
home the bacon. What a man. What a boat. Well done Matt. Well done PARADOX. I
bet Dave and Mindy are proud."** — along with good-natured ribbing about the
delay in posting finish photos: **"Its been three days since Matt got in. I
thought you 'Yanks' were organised!"** — a small sign of how far word of
Matt's win had already traveled by mid-March 2003, reaching correspondents
outside North America.

## After the win: a visit, a new sculling oar, and hardware changes

**Matt reckoned he averaged about 2 knots on the calm section of the
Everglades Challenge** under yuloh power alone — the only sculling-speed
figure on record tied specifically to that race (see
[sculling and yuloh technique](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md)).

**[Dave Bolduc](/people/bolduc/dave.md)** and Mindy visited Matt Layden that
spring, and during the visit Dave himself got to **scull the original
Paradox** for a while. Matt gave Dave direct, hands-on coaching to improve his
own sculling technique, most notably emphasizing **putting the whole body into
the stroke** — advice consistent with the full-body technique Dave would
later describe at length on microcruising.com (see
[sculling and yuloh technique](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md)).

Dave also reported that Paradox was now fitted with a **new sculling oar**,
**straighter** than the one shown in the plans and than her previous oar, with
a **wider and longer blade**. He found the new oar **more powerful** than the
earlier design — the wider blade let the sculler control its pitch more
easily — but it demanded **much more wrist action**, and after a while
sculling it his own right wrist started hurting. Matt told Dave that **his own
wrist had hurt during the first few days of the Everglades Challenge** using
the new oar — a rare, direct account of a cost Matt personally paid using his
own new gear during the race he had just won. Weighing the trade-off, Dave
said he still preferred [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md)'s own
more curved sculling oar. Photographs of the new oar showed that the **tips of
both the oar and the rudder** had been **reinforced with bronze rod**.

Further photos showed the **bronze rod stock** at the tips of both the rudder
and the yuloh, and a **string run over the cleats** to keep the mainsheet from
catching on them. The sheeting arrangement itself had **not** changed — an
earlier picture had simply shown the **prototype** setup, while the version on
the plans is a **two-to-one purchase**. The mainsheet does indeed catch on
things in practice, chiefly the **rudder head**, the **yuloh mount**, and the
**cleats**, but Matt's own fix for the problem was "very simple and works
well" (the specific fix itself was not detailed further). See also
[furling system](/design/furling-system.md) for more on the rolling-boom
mainsheet arrangement.

## A new, more weatherly sail (February 2003)

The original Paradox's sail was itself refreshed shortly before the
Everglades Challenge win: in February 2003, about a month before the race,
[Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md) made a **new sail** for the boat,
keeping the old sail as a spare. He later shared the new sail's complete
dimensions and cutting technique with the list (see
[matt-layden/biography.md](/matt-layden/biography.md)
for that first-ever list post, and
[construction/sailmaking.md](/construction/sailmaking.md)
for the full technical recipe).

The new sail is a deliberate refinement over the original design: a
**shortened foot** and a **raised peak** for higher aspect ratio and a more
weatherly set (full or reefed), at the cost of some furling neatness — a
small triangle at the peak no longer rolls up with the rest of the sail and
must be scrunched and stuffed under the boom by hand when stowing. Matt still
endorsed the original plans sail as fine if well cut, but said he would use
the new cut again if making another. This answers, concretely, what Matt
would change about his own sail after Glen Maxwell's earlier account of Matt
judging his own (evidently pre-February) sail in need of more draft — see
[rig and sail](/design/rig.md).

[Dave Bolduc](/people/bolduc/dave.md), who had recently sailed the original
Paradox himself (see
[above](#after-the-win-a-visit-a-new-sculling-oar-and-hardware-changes)),
gave the only independent eyewitness verdict on how the new sail actually
performs: "this new sail sets very nicely."

## Surviving Hurricanes Frances and Ivan (2004)

The original Paradox and [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md) both came
through the **2004 Atlantic hurricane season** safely. The eyes of both
**Hurricane Frances** and **Hurricane Ivan** passed directly over Matt's home
in **Jensen Beach, Florida** that season. Matt had spent the preceding years
reinforcing the house, which came through both storms with only minor
damage; the original Paradox herself was hauled out of the water ahead of
time and brought home, sustaining nothing worse than a few small fallen
branches.[44]
