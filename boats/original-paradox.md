# Original Paradox

The **original Paradox** is the prototype built by designer [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md) himself — the first example of the [Paradox](/paradox.md) design, and "one of Matt Layden's most successful micro-cruiser designs." Matt built her in **1993**, returning to [chine runners](/chine-runners/windward-performance.md) after concluding that [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md)'s centerboard "did not seem to improve windward performance sufficiently to merit its construction." As of July 2001 it was one of only two Paradoxes known to be sailing (the other being the [second known Paradox](elliott-paradox.md)).

# Design particulars

The canonical design figures for the Paradox (which, being a one-off design built to plan, are also the original boat's own particulars) are:

| Particular | Metric | Imperial |
|---|---|---|
| LOA | 4.2 m | 13' 10" |
| LWL | 4.1 m | 13' 5" |
| Beam | 1.23 m | 4' 0½" (≈48") |
| Draft | 0.23 m | 9" |
| Sail area | 9.3 m² | 100 sq ft |
| Displacement | 640 kg | 1,410 lb |

The five "interesting details" for Paradox are: **chine runners**; **70 litres of fresh-water ballast**; **self-righting**; **positive buoyancy**; and **all lines lead into the cabin**. The 70 L (~154 lb) of water ballast is the designer's own stated ballast figure for Paradox and should be read alongside the heavier lead-ballast arrangements builders adopted for their own boats (Matt's boats in fact use a *layered* scheme — fixed ballast, water-tank ballast, and stowed stores that also act as ballast); see [weight and ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md).

# The build and launch

- Built in **about 4 months of full-time work** (see [build effort](/construction/build-effort.md)).
- Matt **had no car** and hauled all the materials to a **remote building site in the woods on his bicycle trailer** (the little trailer is visible in the "para2" construction photo).
- **Launched by skidding / lowering it down a steep ravine to the Salmon River** below the site (photo "para4") — "no wonder these little boats have to be built tough."

Matt sails it single-handed from inside the [cabin](/paradox.md) and stows anchors below as ballast using a stern-bucket routine (see [ground tackle](/operations/ground-tackle.md)); it is the boat sailing Exuma Sound in the well-known 25 kts / 6 kts photo (see [rig and sail](/components/rig/index.md)).

# Cruising record

Paradox is Matt's **fifth home-built sailboat** (see [microcruising.com](/references/microcruising-website.md)):

- **1993** — after launching, Matt sailed her from Connecticut down the Intracoastal Waterway and out as far as the **Ragged Islands** in the Bahamas.
- **1994** — the original Paradox cruised **in tandem with [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) to the Bahamas** (Bimini, Chub Cay, Nassau, the Exumas, Long Island, Conception Island, Rum Cay, Spanish Wells, Eleuthera) — a notable early [buddy-boat cruise](/merits/bahamas-and-exumas.md) between the two designs, and the trip on which they met a family living aboard the 32' sharpie ["Hogfish Lips"](/people/chris-morejohn.md).
- **2003** — Matt entered the boat, on 8 January, in the **300-mile [Everglades Challenge](/merits/everglades-challenge.md)** — a ~260-mile WaterTribe race from Fort Desoto to Key Largo (class 4/open/experimental) — and took **first in her class and overall honors** (Matt dated the win to 2003 in his 2019 Cedar Key talk). He planned to sail to the start from his home in Stuart, Florida, and sail back home again afterward — see [weight, ballast, and displacement](/design/weight-and-ballast.md) for that mention, including a launching-rules aside about the race's above-high-tide-line launch requirement. Word of the entry spread quickly: within two days, an unrelated reply in the "buy Paradox plans" thread (see [Paradox building plans — a sixteenth recurrence](/references/building-plans.md)) already read, "I'm eager to follow Matt's progress in the Watertribe adventure race. This will add new dimensions to the capabilities of Paradox as an all-around microcruising adventure boat."
- The original Paradox has logged roughly **3,000 miles**, "probably a little more seaworthy" than the much higher-mileage Little Cruiser — while both are "tough coastal cruisers," not offshore boats (see [blue-water suitability](/design/blue-water-suitability.md)).

## Glen Maxwell sails alongside Matt Layden before the 2003 Everglades Challenge

In the days before the 2003 Everglades Challenge began, another Paradox owner sailed alongside Matt Layden in person at the race's starting point, **Ft. Desoto** (FL) — the only firsthand, in-person account on record of Matt Layden himself under sail. The two sailed together on **Thursday and part of Friday**, before Matt hauled the boat out of the water to prepare for the race.

Matt's **haul-out rig** was two rollers purchased for the purpose, combined with a **four-part block and tackle tied to a six-part block and tackle** for mechanical advantage — since it was unclear whether outside assistance would be allowed during the actual race haul-out, Matt made a point of hauling the boat out entirely **by himself**. Matt's "secret weapon" for the race was his ability to **sleep while underway and keep going** — sailing (or drifting) through the night rather than stopping.

Sailing two Paradoxes together was **a first even for Matt himself** — the designer had apparently never before sailed his own boat in company with another Paradox — an occasion called "a real treat." The comparison was lopsided: Matt's **windward sailing ability was far superior**, and Matt suggested alterations to the other Paradox's sail after watching it perform. Matt also passed on a recommendation that **Dabbler Sails** (already known as a supplier to [Whisper](/boats/whisper.md), see [rig and sail](/components/rig/index.md)) was "very good at building these lug sails" — sailing in company with the original Paradox gave "real promise" that a well-made sail could perform much better than the other owner's, never fully satisfied since it was first set. No photographs survive of the two Paradoxes sailing together.

The WaterTribe race-tracking site showed Matt had already **checked in at Checkpoint 2 (Placida, FL)** at 09:30 that morning, continuing on toward **Sanibel** via the inside route, with the WaterTribe forum mentioning that Matt had been **helping a fellow competitor** along the way.

This account stands as strong independent evidence that **Matt Layden's reputation as a superior sailor of his own designs was not exaggerated**: an experienced professional boat builder who had sailed his own Paradox "Zoë" for well over a year by this point still found Matt's windward performance "far superior."

A fuller, more specific retelling given six weeks later added that the **sail problem Matt identified was insufficient draft** (the other owner's sail was "cut too flat," while Matt's needed "a little more draft" even so) and a separate **yard-bowing fix**. See [rig and sail](/components/rig/index.md) and [matt-layden/biography.md](/matt-layden/biography.md) for the fuller account.

## Matt's haul-out rollers

The **rollers** Matt used to haul Paradox out solo at Ft. Desoto, described in full via a hand-drawn sketch:

- The rollers were **6 in long, 2 in diameter boat rollers**, mounted on trestles built from **2× stock on 2×4s**.
- Matt **carried both rollers stowed in the starboard bilge compartment**, sized specifically to stow there.
- He used **two sets**, moving the aft roller forward to the bow as the boat's weight came onto it — "they worked great."
- Overall roller-trestle dimensions were about **30 in long by 12-14 in wide**.
- Each roller trailed a **line long enough to cleat off**, so that a roller would not drift away in the surf before Matt could retrieve it.

This is the fullest technical description on record of how Matt hauls the original Paradox out of the water single-handed — a practical, entirely home-devised solution consistent with his boat's design philosophy of being sailed and handled without outside assistance. See also [rig and sail](/components/rig/index.md) and [furling system](/components/rig/furling.md) for how Matt reefs his sail underway.

## The 2003 Everglades Challenge: checkpoint progress

The **Everglades Challenge**'s official standings, via the WaterTribe results site, tracked Matt's progress down the Florida coast. After Checkpoint 2 (67 of about 300 total miles) he was running roughly **10 hours behind the paddling kayakers** in the field, but had already gotten a full night's sleep while sailing offshore — the first real-time evidence of the "sleep while underway" tactic he had described before the start (above) actually paying off. He then became the **first competitor to reach Checkpoint 3**, logging in at 14:40 on 10 March and continuing offshore, a showing that led other list members to remark on a heavy cruising monohull competing on genuinely new terms in adventure racing.

## The finish: Checkpoint 4 and the win

Matt reached **Checkpoint 4 (Flamingo, 273 miles)** at 07:45 on 11 March, his lead over the field having grown further: he arrived roughly **7.5 hours ahead of the second-place boat's own Checkpoint 3 time**, beating race officials' own prediction that sailboats would remain competitive in the Everglades Challenge for only one more year. His own checked-in comment on reaching Checkpoint 4 read: **"Spectacular sailing: Surfing at 6 knots with dolphins on the bow off Pavillion Key. Diminishing wind, varying to NNE overnight. Caught the tide at East Cape. Onward!"**

Onlookers credited the win to a combination of boat and tactics: Matt's solo roller-launching system let him land and relaunch without outside help, while the competing field — mostly narrow racing kayaks and canoes carrying small auxiliary sails — had to come ashore and make camp to eat and rest; Matt instead kept sailing and slept underway as planned. **Team Weed Warrior** (an outrigger proa) reached Checkpoint 3 second, **9 hours 20 minutes** behind Matt's solo Paradox; **Team Nootka** (a racing canoe) took third, a further 3 hours 50 minutes back. During the race, Matt **stopped and rescued an overturned kayaker and towed him into shore**.

Matt finished at **Key Largo, 304 miles, at 17:51 on 11 March** — first overall and well ahead of the entire paddled field; at the moment he crossed the line, no other competitor had yet even reached Checkpoint 4. His own pre-race entry description, filed with WaterTribe, is the fullest self-description on record of the original Paradox as a race entry: **"Paradox 14' coastal cruising sailboat, with chinese yuloh secondary propulsion. This is a heavy, long-range expedition cruiser and stretches the limits of beach-launchability (I'll be using rollers and block & tackle, should be fun to watch...). She is not fast but can keep sailing in any weather and run hairy inlets safe & dry."** The next day, a collect phone call reached the list from Matt himself in Key Largo, sounding tired but in good spirits.

Matt Layden and the original Paradox finished the ~300-mile 2003 Everglades Challenge **first overall**, well ahead of every paddled entry, racing solo and using only sail and yuloh power.

## Why a heavy monohull beat the light multihulls

The win prompted discussion of *why* a heavy, displacement monohull had so thoroughly out-sailed lighter multihull and paddled competition. The explanation, from a sailor with direct comparative experience owning a 21 ft monohull, a Sea Pearl trimaran, and an SP28: **wave action slows a light Seapearl significantly, especially to windward**, where it is "constantly thrown off the waves," while "the heavy Paradox powers through the slop with inertia," its motion also "much better on the body."

The result was also credited to Matt's own skill as distinct from the boat itself — "obviously Matt knows how to use a yuloh as well as being a very skilled sailer, strategist and navigator" — summed up memorably: **"Perhaps he may have created a class of his own! The microcruiser class. Or gotten himself banned altogether!"**

## Other boats that could have raced, and a wish for a larger Paradox

Discussion of the race's result drifted, in the following days, from *why* Paradox won toward *what else could have* raced or might race in future years. (The recurring wish, aired here too, for a larger Paradox to build and race is covered in full at [stretched Paradox](/design/ideas/stretched-paradox.md) and [larger Paradox](/design/larger-paradox.md) and not repeated here.)

A two-person Sea Pearl crew, rowing in shifts from two stations, was floated as potentially "more competative" than a solo entry, since two rowers could keep the boat moving around the clock even in a calm — "sleeper would make good ballast." The race's rules were seen as having drifted from their original form, prompting speculation that a **minimum length rule**, or a separate class for "overweight or overlength boats," might be needed in future years, alongside speculation about whether Matt had simply caught favorable wind shifts and whether the Everglades Challenge committee ("the Chief") might set a 15 ft minimum entry length the following year. A competitor on Cape Sable was separately reported to have watched a 21 ft Sea Pearl sail by at sunset.

No Hobie-cat-style catamarans had entered, though one "would be very competitive" against a design "never thought of... as a racing boat, more of a slow but steady cruiser." A **tacking proa built from a Hobie 18 hull**, sailed two-up under the name **"Weed Warrior,"** had raced (and finished second, as reported above). Separately, an entrant known as "Crazy Russian" had raced a catamaran; sleeping aboard a cat underway, or rowing one short-handed against no wind, was thought likely to be much harder than on a monohull, and that entry was believed to have used a sliding rowing seat borrowed from a racing shell. A heavy-cat entry was judged to demand "a very skilled operator," doubtfully able to meet the race's self-rescue requirement given how changeable Florida weather is that time of year over a 270-mile course — though "not unreasonable for a seasoned racer." Wished-for future entries included a Pacific proa like an Ilikai/Mbuli or a fast trimaran.

Three days after the finish, an admiring reaction arrived from outside North America: **"If you were to write a film script with a story line based on the results of this race, no one would believe it. But our man has brought home the bacon. What a man. What a boat. Well done Matt. Well done PARADOX. I bet Dave and Mindy are proud."** — along with good-natured ribbing about the delay in posting finish photos: **"Its been three days since Matt got in. I thought you 'Yanks' were organised!"** — a small sign of how far word of Matt's win had already traveled by mid-March 2003.

## After the win: a visit, a new sculling oar, and hardware changes

**Matt reckoned he averaged about 2 knots on the calm section of the Everglades Challenge** under yuloh power alone — the only sculling-speed figure on record tied specifically to that race (see [sculling and yuloh technique](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md)).

That spring, a visitor got to **scull the original Paradox** for a while. Matt gave direct, hands-on coaching to improve the visitor's sculling technique, most notably emphasizing **putting the whole body into the stroke** — advice consistent with the full-body technique later described at length on microcruising.com (see [sculling and yuloh technique](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md)).

By this point Paradox was fitted with a **new sculling oar**, **straighter** than the one shown in the plans and than her previous oar, with a **wider and longer blade**. The new oar proved **more powerful** than the earlier design — the wider blade let the sculler control its pitch more easily — but it demanded **much more wrist action**, enough that after a while sculling it the sculler's right wrist started hurting. Matt's **own wrist had hurt during the first few days of the Everglades Challenge** using the new oar — a rare, direct account of a cost Matt personally paid using his own new gear during the race he had just won. Weighing the trade-off, [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md)'s own more curved sculling oar was still preferred. Photographs of the new oar showed that the **tips of both the oar and the rudder** had been **reinforced with bronze rod**.

Further photos showed the **bronze rod stock** at the tips of both the rudder and the yuloh, and a **string run over the cleats** to keep the mainsheet from catching on them. The sheeting arrangement itself had **not** changed — an earlier picture had simply shown the **prototype** setup, while the version on the plans is a **two-to-one purchase**. The mainsheet does indeed catch on things in practice, chiefly the **rudder head**, the **yuloh mount**, and the **cleats**, but Matt's own fix for the problem was "very simple and works well" (the specific fix itself was not detailed further). See also [furling system](/components/rig/furling.md) for more on the rolling-boom mainsheet arrangement.

## A new, more weatherly sail (February 2003)

The original Paradox's sail was itself refreshed shortly before the Everglades Challenge win: in February 2003, about a month before the race, [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md) made a **new sail** for the boat, keeping the old sail as a spare. He later shared the new sail's complete dimensions and cutting technique with the list (see [matt-layden/biography.md](/matt-layden/biography.md) for that first-ever list post, and [construction/sailmaking.md](/construction/sailmaking.md) for the full technical recipe).

The new sail is a deliberate refinement over the original design: a **shortened foot** and a **raised peak** for higher aspect ratio and a more weatherly set (full or reefed), at the cost of some furling neatness — a small triangle at the peak no longer rolls up with the rest of the sail and must be scrunched and stuffed under the boom by hand when stowing. Matt still endorsed the original plans sail as fine if well cut, but said he would use the new cut again if making another. This answers, concretely, what Matt would change about his own sail after the earlier account of Matt judging his own (evidently pre-February) sail in need of more draft — see [rig and sail](/components/rig/index.md).

The only independent eyewitness verdict on how the new sail actually performs, from a Paradox owner who had recently sailed the original Paradox (see [above](#after-the-win-a-visit-a-new-sculling-oar-and-hardware-changes)): "this new sail sets very nicely."

## Surviving Hurricanes Frances and Ivan (2004)

The original Paradox and [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md) both came through the **2004 Atlantic hurricane season** safely. The eyes of both **Hurricane Frances** and **Hurricane Ivan** passed directly over Matt's home in **Jensen Beach, Florida** that season. Matt had spent the preceding years reinforcing the house, which came through both storms with only minor damage; the original Paradox herself was hauled out of the water ahead of time and brought home, sustaining nothing worse than a few small fallen branches.[43]
