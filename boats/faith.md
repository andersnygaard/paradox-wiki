# Faith

**"Faith"** is [Bill Serjeant](/people/bill-serjeant.md)'s UK-built [Paradox](/paradox.md), built strictly to Matt Layden's original plans. Bill started construction in August 2005 after a kit-Paradox deal fell through, and launched Faith in mid-June 2007. Her fuller day-by-day cruising record lives at [references/bills-log.md](/references/bills-log.md), the blog Bill kept throughout the build and her cruises; this page covers the durable build story, her specifications, and her notable voyages. For Bill's community/registry role and person-level record, see [people/bill-serjeant.md](/people/bill-serjeant.md).

## A kit Paradox that fell through, then a scratch build begins

In January 2005, Jordan Boats agreed to supply Bill a "kit" Paradox, working with Matt Layden on the project — but by July 2005 the kit would not be ready after all, the panel design/cutting process needing more research than could be resolved soon. The ten sheets of plywood bought for the kit were passed on to Bill regardless, arriving 1 August 2005 cut to the layout given on sheet 6 of the plans (two 18 mm sheets for the bottom, rudder case and bulkhead 3; four 12 mm sheets for the sides, bulkhead 2, the rudder blade, the cabin sole, shelves and the transom; three 9 mm sheets for the decking, bulkheads 1 and 4, and storage bins; one 6 mm sheet for the cabin roof, hatch cover and a ventilation baffle), and he built from Matt Layden's original plans instead — crediting the decision partly to trying out ["Little Jim"](/boats/little-jim.md) firsthand at Plymouth. He wanted a kit purely for speed rather than to avoid building; he still believed one could be made to work if the bottom were built from three thinner plywood layers rather than the plans' single-thickness panel. Full account at [references/paradox-uk-website.md — a UK kit-built option: Jordan Boats](/references/paradox-uk-website.md#a-uk-kit-built-option-jordan-boats).[1][2][3][4]

Deck beams were laminated from five strips of 5 mm plywood over a jig, each originally clamped 48 hours before Bill halved that once he learned other builders found 24 hours enough, pushing his production rate toward three beams a week. All seven beams are identical but for length. With the garage barely 8 ft wide against Paradox's 4 ft beam, every small part was built first and the hull itself reserved for when there would be room to work around it. Full technique record at [construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md](/construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md#why-4-ft-strips-are-long-enough-for-every-beam).[5][6][7][8][9][10]

## Ballast, rudder, and mast: autumn 2005

In September 2005 Bill cast the rudder's own lead ballast into its trailing edge, using a mould built from an oak plank and softwood wedges — Matt Layden had calculated the job would need "3 kilos of lead, which proved to be accurate." The lead was later faired and GRP-sheathed using a homemade sanding board built to a Don Elliott design. The hull's own main ballast called for ten pigs of scrap lead at around 40 lb each, some 400 lb in total.[11][12][13]

Fitting the mast together in October 2005, Bill routed the masthead light's wiring into the hollow mast to a plug-in socket at the base, applied the plan's fibreglass reinforcement at the head, and rejected two Windex wind-indicators in turn for lacking a suitable mount or requiring a masthead VHF aerial he had no intention of fitting. Paradox's construction — many small parts joined by epoxy — suited a builder with only an hour or two to spare at a time, since work could run in parallel while earlier glue-ups cured.[14][15][16][17]

## Winter hibernation, a Birdwatcher temptation, and naming her "Faith"

By November 2005 the build had stalled for the winter: epoxy needs above 15°C to cure, and with overnight frosts setting in Bill paused formally until spring. Work resumed in March 2006 after "a winter's hibernation" — the hull itself barely started, though the mast, boom, yard, rudder, stock, tiller, laminated beams, reefing mechanism and ballast were all already made. That same month, weighing a Phil Bolger "Birdwatcher" as an alternative project, Bill judged Paradox the easier of the two to launch and recover while trailer-sailing and, having already bought the wood and fittings, chose to continue. On 17 March 2006, after "a long deliberation considering many names," he settled on "Faith" — "I require faith more than anything to make my dream of ownership a reality"; the roughly eighty candidate names weighed first are recorded at [people/bill-serjeant.md](/people/bill-serjeant.md#the-small-sailboats-paradox-uk-web-presence). Putting in some five hours a day, Bill cut all four frames, the transom and the side panels by the end of March, working now on a home-built mobile trolley that let the boat be wheeled in and out of the garage.[18][19][20][21][22][23][24]

By mid-May 2006 the sheer clamps, chine logs, floor supports and water-tank frames were fixed, the transom baffle fitted, and the lower rudder gudgeon cast in a mould. Over a bank holiday weekend the bottom's two large panels were joined indoors on the lounge floor through a 48-hour cure, then levered onto the upturned hull — in what turned out to be "the wettest May since 1983." By late October 2006 the build had reached the interior fit-out; Bill judged the design "well designed in the ergonomic sense," with the steering lines and transverse seat both set at a comfortable working height, and built simple stowage racks and a table doubling as chart table and galley support. There is no standing headroom unless the hatch is open.[25][26][27]

Shaping the boom and yard, Bill raised a long-running question about the yard sagging under the sail's own weight and cured a persisting sag by working on the yard's curvature rather than recutting the sail — a boom is curved specifically so it keeps its shape when furled for reefing. Full technical discussion at [components/rig/index.md](/components/rig/index.md#which-side-of-the-boom-and-yard-faces-up-and-curing-persistent-yard-sag).[28][29][30][31]

## Build-cost comparison, and a hull-assembly milestone

Against an "it's only 14 feet" quip about the scale of the commitment, Bill offered his own comparative data point: having also built a 25 ft boat, he judged there was markedly less work in that bigger boat than in the Paradox he had underway, at only marginally lower cost, and judged even a full-time professional with power tools could not complete a Paradox in under six months. See [operations/secondhand-market.md](/operations/secondhand-market.md#build-vs-buy-secondhand-a-uk-newcomers-cost-benefit-hesitation) for the fuller build-versus-buy discussion this fed into. By April 2006, with fine weather arrived, Bill was expecting to begin assembling the hull within days, with practically every other part already built.[32][33]

## A dry assembly of the hull, named "Faith"

Days later, Bill posted photographs of Faith's first **dry assembly** — the hull's panels and bulkheads fitted together unglued, to check the fit before committing epoxy. The stem joint drew praise for how well it had come together. He glued the floors and vent trunk permanently in place within days, and cut in the frames; being retired, he could put in about four days a week on the build.[34][35][36][37]

## The bottom fixed, chine runners complete, and a two-month pause for Australia

By mid-June 2006 Bill reported the hull's bottom "well and truly fixed" and the chine runners just completed, with glassing the bottom next — but had to stop building for two months while away in Australia.[38]

## Chine-runner and gudgeon photos, and Mark Waters' Paradox

Bill uploaded photographs of Faith's chine runner and lower gudgeon, and reported putting a third layer of glass on her bottom ahead of the Australia trip. He also praised photographs of a fellow UK builder's completed Paradox, Mark Waters', calling the work "superb."[39][40]

## Faith's rudder: a sheet-fairlead fix, and boom-tang strength

Back from Australia, Bill posted photographs of Faith's completed rudder, which drew two independent technical replies. The rudder's height lets the mainsheet drop into the gap between the rudder stock and transom on a broad reach or run, forcing the sheet to slide up over the pintle/gudgeon on every adjustment; Bill fitted a small wooden fairlead insert to fix it, the same problem and fix independently reported by another builder — tracked in full at [components/rudder.md](/components/mainsheet-outboard-fouling.md#rudder-stock-height-and-mainsheet-fouling-at-the-transom-notch). A second, separate issue was flagged in the same photos: the stock mainsheet/topping-lift tang is not strong enough and bends under load. Bill held off strengthening his own until he could load-test the stock part, then reinforced it before launch.[41][42][43][44][45]

## Lower pintle: an alternative to drilling the rudder stock

Bill devised and photographed a method of installing the rudder's lower pintle onto the rudder stock without drilling a hole for it — a technique he judged simpler and more accurate than drilling, since a mistake there means a major rebuild.[46][47][48]

## Ballast-access and tank-top questions

Once Faith's rudder was sorted, Bill worked through how to fix down the water-ballast tank tops (a thickened-glue-and-hand-fillet technique through the access hatches) and how to reach the inner ballast bolts once the floors were in place, settling on the tank top's sides built as separate, removable "planks" for full ongoing access to both the lead ballast and its plumbing. See [design/weight-and-ballast.md](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#reaching-the-ballast-fastenings-after-the-floors-are-in) and [construction/water-tank-lining.md](/construction/water-tank-lining.md#fixing-the-tank-tops-in-place) for the full technique.[49][50][51][52][53][54]

## A removable-floorboard technique for full storage and ballast access

Bill built his own aft floorboards to the plans but without hinges, supported instead on removable under-flaps taken from the storage-bin sides, so either side of the floor could simply be lifted and slid forward to open the whole storage compartment beneath — the same "planks" approach extended to the water-tank sides for forward ballast access. See [components/interior.md](/components/interior.md#a-removable-floorboard-technique-for-full-storage-and-ballast-access) for the cross-linked technique.[55]

## Cabin sole panels revisited, a case against built-in water tanks, and a washboard endorsed

Bill set out his own bin-and-floor system in fuller technical detail: bin sides bolted to aluminium angle (rather than screwed, so they could be removed for maintenance and better withstand gale loads), with lift-out floors sliding forward or sideways for access instead of the plans' hinges. Given the choice again, he would not build in water tanks at all — the frame is strong enough without fixed soles over the tank area — preferring loose floors held by a clasp through a full capsize. On a rear-cabin-trunk washboard, originally opposed on structural grounds, Bill came round to it after a season actually sailing Faith, on condition the washboard sides are cut vertical so it cannot work loose without a full, deliberate lift — citing the Fastnet Race, in which several yachts lost washboards to exactly that failure mode.[56]

## A trim question, and Little Jim's tacking quirk

Bill asked how closely a Paradox's fore-and-aft trim needs watching, having assumed the boat was heavy enough that shifting the ballast a little one way or the other would make no real difference. The answer drew on ["Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)"'s own occasional tacking trouble — see [components/rudder.md](/components/rudder.md#sudden-total-loss-of-rudder-effect-mid-tack-and-tacking-technique) for the full account.[57][58][59]

## Build progress photographs, and comparing notes with another builder

Bill posted further "Faith" progress photographs, and, comparing notes with another builder working concurrently, judged his own work not quite as finished in places as he would have liked but was satisfied overall with how the hull was shaping up. His next tasks were the electrics and stove, then painting the interior and fitting the decks.[60][61][62][63][64]

## A drinking-water pump, and photos on smallsailboats.co.uk

Rather than build a bladder or built-in tank system, Bill solved his own drinking-water supply by fitting a hand pump to a large plastic camping-style container stowed beside the stove in the lazarette, keeping spare water in bottles forward to balance the aft water's weight. The galley stove itself lifts clear of its supporting clips easily for access, and was the same unit already proven crossing the North Sea and the English Channel aboard an earlier boat.[65][66][67]

## Tools used building Faith, revisited

Bill used neither a table saw nor a bandsaw to build Faith, relying instead on an ordinary rip saw, hand planing and sanding, and an electric jigsaw for shaping plywood, with the rip saw also cutting the mast, boom, and yuloh. The one indispensable tool was the clamp — more than 30 were needed for the sheer strakes and chine logs alone. See [construction/build-effort.md](/construction/build-effort.md#tools).[70]

## A start-versus-finish perspective, and further deck photos (December 2006)

Congratulating [Pete Martin](/people/pete-martin.md) on starting his own build, Bill offered a veteran's perspective on the hardest part of any Paradox project: making the actual start. By comparison, Bill's own build — under way since late August 2005 — had by this point reached the decks.[71]

## Deck-beam length, a tiller-curve mistake, and the helm seat

The deck-beam length can be measured straight off the lines drawing (sheet 2), adding 3-4 inches; for the general chord-length table across all seven beams see [construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md](/construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md#deck-beam-dimensions-and-the-deck-curves-true-shape). Bill's own tiller lacked enough curve, leaving only a close shave past the last deck beam; the fix was to thin that beam rather than remake the tiller. His helm seat is laminated from strips of wood, made a bit wider than the plans' own dimension for extra support, with a large hand grip borrowed from Alastair's "Little Jim."[72][73]

## Sheer clamp, bulkhead #1, and mast support: Bill's own choices

Bulkhead #1 is a safety feature limiting how far floodwater can spread in a wild seaway; a genuinely watertight hatch or fully sealed forward section would be a stronger but more involved option. Faith carries no added buoyancy and instead relies on being built strong to the plans throughout — Bill kept bulkhead #1 solid as drawn, unlike Alastair, who cut into his and backed the opening with foam. Bill separately fits an additional mast support at the top of the vent box, on the starboard side.[74]

## A snowfall pause, and a comparison against a firsthand New Zealand report

By January 2007 Faith's decks were in place, with sailing hoped for once warmer weather arrived; a fresh fall of snow had brought building to a halt for the moment. Faith was built strictly to plan, though without insulation — Bill chose to sail a season without any first, planning to add foam only if condensation actually proved a problem in practice. He judged Matt Layden had already thought the design through carefully for open-sea integrity and watertightness, and stuck to the original cabin design rather than modifying it.[75][76][77]

## Fibreglassing the hull, and fairing woven roving before painting

Bill fully encapsulated Faith's hull with woven roving and epoxy over "a brilliant week for boatbuilding," and compared fairing methods and build timelines with [Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)'s own build to gauge how much time remained before his own targeted early-June 2007 launch. He judged his Mk1-design sail — bought secondhand from Glen Maxwell, the earlier and lower-peaked pattern rather than Glen's later "improved" higher-peaked Mark II — worked well once a persistent yard crease was cured by tightening the end ties; a technical exchange on yard curvature and Matt Layden's own eye for it is at [people/glen-maxwell/matt-layden-eyewitness.md](/people/glen-maxwell/matt-layden-eyewitness.md#matt-laydens-eye-for-yard-curvature-demonstrated-on-another-builders-sail). Bill laid out a firm cruising plan for Faith once launched: the West Country by late June 2007, on to Plymouth and Falmouth by mid-July, and, weather permitting, across to the Isles of Scilly.[78][79][80][81][82][83][84][85][86]

## A yuloh pivot socket, mainsheet snagging, and a cruise plan toward the Scilly Isles

Fresh yuloh-plans photos drew out the choice between the plans' ball-joint pivot and a plain oarlock (the way Matt Layden sculls in a hosted video clip); the ball joint was confirmed as the right choice for Paradox given her hull weight, transom height, and stowage, since yulohing and sculling are different actions. The socket-material question worked through a shortlist — tufnol, copper pipe, stainless tube, graphite-loaded epoxy — before Bill made his own with a longitudinal shaft curve worked in by wedge. The same exchange settled a mainsheet-fouling problem at the yuloh's pivot ball, fixed with a wooden pad atop the pintle bracket. For the fuller technical record see [components/yuloh.md](/components/yuloh.md#ball-joint-versus-oarlock-and-pivot-socket-wear-over-time). With the technical questions settled, Bill firmed up his cruising plan for "Faith" once launched, inviting Alastair to join any part of a West Country cruise toward the Scilly Isles.[87][88][89][90][91][92][93][94][95][96][97][98][99][100][101][102][103][104]

## The cabin trunk roof, and building the hatch

Bill fitted Faith's cabin trunk roof and the hatch runners next, judging a window worth fitting into the hatch since seeing the sail from inside while sailing is otherwise difficult — one technique assembles the hatch first and cuts the window aperture afterward, avoiding the risk of the plywood bending crooked. His own hatch sides were a full metre out of true before bending and glassing corrected them, drawing a dry aside that he had built the hatch triangular rather than rectangular; the runners came from teak scraps sourced at the Beale Boat Show.[105][106][107][108]

## Cutting the polycarbonate windows

With the hatch trial-fitted "more by luck than judgement," Bill turned to shaping Faith's windows. Coarse teeth, run at a slow jigsaw speed, cuts polycarbonate best — a fine blade run fast melts the plastic swarf into a clogging lump. With windows and hatch done, only stowage racks, deck painting and hardware, Coppercoat antifouling (rated for roughly ten years), and topside painting stood between Faith and rigging her for the water.[109][110][111]

## Faith's transom camber, and a pine cleat test for Pete Martin

Faith's own transom shows 5 mm of curvature worked into both edges, matching bulkhead 4's specified camber — an improvement to appearance, though a perfectly straight-sided transom remains workable for a builder who prefers it. For interior cleat wood, Bill's field test settles pine against Douglas fir: drive a bronze ring nail into a pre-drilled sample and see whether it splits.[112][113][114]

## Antifouling before a month-long mooring, and the "Enuf" trailer bought secondhand

Faith was coppercoat-antifouled ahead of launch specifically because she might spend long periods on a mooring, with some cruises expected to run over a month. Rather than commission a new trailer, Bill sourced [Derek Clark](/people/derek-clark.md)'s old trailer from his Paradox "[Enuf](/boats/enuf.md)" secondhand.[115]

## Trailer braking, and a firmed-up West Country cruise schedule

A purpose-built trailer avoids brake problems from submersion at launch and recovery, at the cost of extra strength — and so weight — in the trailer itself; the secondhand trailer from Derek Clark had none. With that settled, Faith's West Country cruise plan firmed up further: Exmouth to Dartmouth and back, then Plymouth, the Tamar, and Falmouth to sail the Fal and, weather permitting, on to the Isles of Scilly, with a loose plan to meet [Jeff UK](/people/jeff-doryman.md) and [Alastair](/people/alastair-law.md) around Falmouth/Newlyn/Penzance in mid-July.[116]

## The front window fitted, and the remaining finishing list before launch

By early May 2007 Faith's front window was attached, leaving the back and side windows already in place, with deck painting under way. The remaining finishing list ran: fitting deck fittings and rigging the boat, plus a more substantial bolt through the rudder and its stock. The same week, Faith's hatch was finished with a large polycarbonate window giving a clear view of the sail from inside — chosen with a second purpose in mind, letting sunlight reach a solar panel mounted on the cabin roof.[117][118][119]

## The hull painted, and a launch planned at Burnham-on-Crouch

Within days only the hull and bottom paint stood between Faith and the water. A first launch was planned for Burnham-on-Crouch, hoped for in early June, ahead of sea trials on a cruise to the Walton Backwaters.[120]

## Waiting on warm weather for the antifouling, and a new UK builder welcomed

By the end of May 2007, only warm, settled weather stood between Faith and her launch — the Coppercoat antifouling needed a day above 18°C to apply and a week to harden. A new UK builder, Ian Lang of North London, announced his own intent to start building; Bill named Friday 8 June as Faith's likely launch date, with an open invitation to visit and see her in person.[122][123][124][125]

## A second North London builder's plywood and timber sourcing

Ian Lang cleared his garage and ordered plywood and Douglas Fir from Robbins — the most expensive quote he gathered but the most dependable quality. When it arrived he named his own Paradox **"Irene May,"** after his late mother — full account at [boats/irene-may.md](/boats/irene-may.md). Bill congratulated him, wishing him the same fun and satisfaction his own build of Faith had given him.[126][127][128][129]

## A 22 mm vent trunk drain proven in a brief force 6

Bill used a 22 mm drain tube for the vent trunk, against Matt Layden's 1 in nominal pipe specification, judging 32 mm harder to drill accurately for. The strongest wind he had sailed Faith in by that point was brief periods of force 6, waves never breaking on deck, and even an occasional full vent box let only a minimal amount of water reach the cabin.[130] The vent trunk's whole side panel — rather than only its top half, as built on another Paradox — is removable on Faith and has never leaked; the drain hole under the mast step sits above the waterline, and even in rough conditions, with waves constantly washing over the foredeck and water flowing into the vent box, this has caused no problems.[223]

## "Faith" is born: off the trailer and onto dry land

Faith's hull left the garage for the front lawn in early June 2007 — "born" at last, in Bill's own description, after nearly two years of building — to await her first entry into the water a few days later at Burnham-on-Crouch. Bill floated the idea of linking up during the coming cruise with the Dinghy Cruising Association's North West Holiday Meeting at St Just in Roseland, running 7–14 July.[131][132]

## "Faith" was launched

**"Faith" was launched** in mid-June 2007, with Bill starting a dedicated cruise/build blog at `bills-log.blogspot.com` to record it. Faith's own log fixes the moment precisely: launched at Burnham Marina, Essex, at about 1900 on 10 June 2007 — "not the most graceful of launches for a maiden event," needing to be jigged off her trailer. A first attempt at the yuloh that evening failed to hold a course against a beam wind, leaving Faith to drift alongside the harbourmaster's launch; filling the water ballast tanks afterward "made the boat much more stable."[133][134]

## Faith afloat at Burnham Marina, and a first sea trial

Bill put Faith through two days of trials to check her handling. On calm water the first day the boat sailed flawlessly, and gave the chance to work out yuloh technique: moving the shaft horizontally and twisting it at the end of each stroke generated power, where a figure-eight, punt-style stroke did not — full write-up at [operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md#a-figure-eight-stroke-does-not-work-a-horizontal-twist-does). The second day's Force 3-4, gusting 5, brought the real test and a first tacking failure: changing from starboard to port tack, Faith stalled head-to-wind and drifted back, resolved by freeing the sheet to feather the sail — a technique shared with sailing a junk rig. Over-canvassed on purpose to test her stability, Faith heeled to 30 degrees in the gusts without any anxiety about capsizing; sailing with the hatch closed made oilskins unnecessary even in drizzle. Two days of sailing were judged a clear success.[135][136][137][138][139][140][141]

## Rowing thoughts, and the yuloh's limits as a paddle

Standing and facing forward to row with conventional oars was judged impractical, since the cabin top obstructs the stroke on both sides; the yuloh itself can be used as a paddle in a pinch, but only for a short time, since it takes a lot of energy to keep up. See [operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md#whether-paradox-can-be-rowed-with-conventional-oars).[142]

A fellow builder noticed his own boat's paint colour coincidentally matched Faith's. A run of rain meanwhile cost five weeks against Bill's plan to finish glassing Faith's deck, transom, and sides, the wet, windy spell being exactly the wrong weather for sheathing outdoors.[144][145][146][147]

## Hints on gluing up the hull, a single two-hour session

Answering a newcomer's worry over how to glue up a Paradox hull without losing symmetry, Faith's own experience gave a full worked method: a good dry fit first, then straps, ropes, clamps and shaped wooden blocks to spring the bow and stern into place, fitting the side panels to bulkhead 3 first, then 2, then 4, then 1 and the transom, and the stem last. Faith's whole hull went together this way in one sitting of about two hours, with a centreline check needing no adjustment. See [construction/hull-assembly-sequence.md](/construction/hull-assembly-sequence.md#gluing-up-the-hull-one-sitting-or-several-and-locating-the-bulkheads-with-temporary-pins) for the full technique.[148]

Faith's own delayed West Country cruise plan drew a weather update in early July 2007: the Azores high pressure system was gradually advancing north, easing the wind and offering a glimmer of sunshine after what had been the wettest June on record.[149]

Faith's yuloh video drew a comparison from a much more practised sculler: using a yuloh eventually becomes automatic enough not to think about, settling into a "falling leaf" pattern on its own.[150]

## A 140-mile cruise eastward from Falmouth, with Little Jim

The long-planned joint West Country cruise toward the Scilly Isles went ahead in mid-July 2007, though the Scillies themselves were not reached: unsuitable weather ruled out the crossing, and Faith and [Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md) instead sailed **140 miles together eastward from Falmouth**. Sailing alongside Little Jim's owner taught Bill boat-handling technique firsthand — trimming the sail by yuloh and deploying the anchor. For the full day-by-day account of this cruise, see [references/bills-log.md](/references/bills-log.md) and Alastair's own trip report, published that October in the Dinghy Cruising Association's bulletin.[151][152][153][154][155][156]

## Plymouth to Exmouth, then 140 miles on to Essex

The passage from Plymouth to Exmouth was the opening stage of that wider cruise. Leaving a quiet anchorage near Millbrook Creek on 17 July against a forecast SW 5/6, the two boats felt the southerly's full force crossing Plymouth Sound before bearing off toward Salcombe as the wind built through force 5 and 6. The full circumstantial log — the Splat Cove beaching, the Brixham ebb, the Pole Sands shortcut off Teignmouth — is at [references/bills-log.md](/references/bills-log.md).[155][157]

## Two days of force 5 off Bolt Head, and the tacking fix confirmed again

The liveliest sailing of the cruise came off **Bolt Head and Bolt Tail**, working into **Salcombe** in roughly a force 5 with a big swell and confused, cliff-bounced seas; both boats handled the conditions impeccably, with no anxiety aboard either one. Based on that experience, Paradox should ride out a force 8 lying a-hull — uncomfortable for the crew, but safe — with anything stronger, especially running downwind, better met tracking slightly off dead downwind with a long warp trailed astern. This firsthand judgment adds a second British-built, British-sailed data point to the lying-a-hull question tracked at [operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#a-british-isles-sailors-case-for-lying-a-hull-and-paradoxs-flat-bottom). The one recurring idiosyncrasy found across the cruise — slow tacking in piled-up seas, fixed by reversing the rudder to pay her off — is likely common to all Paradoxes; apart from that quirk, Faith performed brilliantly, arguably the best boat her owner had ever sailed.[158]

## Drying out on a sunken mooring block during the West Country cruise

During the same cruise, Faith dried out directly on top of a submerged concrete mooring sinker, her bow left about 3 ft in the air. Bill climbed in and waited out the tide; once she was back on her trailer, no damage to the bottom could be found — a concrete demonstration of the confidence a heavily over-built bottom is meant to provide.[159]

## Video evidence: an easily driven hull with almost no wake

New video from the cruise, shot at an estimated Force 2 to 3, showed no bow wave or wake — consistent with an easily driven hull: heavy displacement carries her way well, low wetted area keeps drag down, and with the sail properly balanced the rudder runs almost neutral.[160][161][162][163]

## Reassurance on a wavy chine before the chine logs go on

Answering a newcomer's worry over an unfair, wavy chine noticed during a dry-fit disassembly, the chine can be expected to pull fair once the chine logs go on — fitting them on a very hot day helps considerably, and laminating each chine log from two thinner parts rather than bending it at full dimension makes the job considerably easier.[164]

## Comparing Faith to an earlier Bermudan-sloop dinghy, "Harriott"

Faith invited comparison against an earlier 14 ft Bermudan sloop, "Harriott," a centreboard cruising dinghy with a beam of about 5.5 ft, a displacement of 650 lb, and 96 sq ft of sail — dependent on her centreboard for windward performance, unable to sail efficiently to windward in under 4 ft of water. Faith, by contrast, is a little shorter and narrower (just over 4 ft beam) with only 9 in of draught, sails to windward in 2 ft of water heeling around 14 degrees, and carries a much heavier 1,410 lb displacement on only 100 sq ft of sail. Both boats will self-right, but Faith's enormous stability and lesser sail area make her much the more forgiving of the two. Paradox's advantages over an open cruising dinghy: the crew is never exposed to the elements, there is no centreplate to jam, and there is much more usable stowage space aboard; a Roamer-type dinghy points closer to windward, is faster in light air, can be sailed two-up, and can carry an outboard without fouling the mainsheet. Paradox is better described as a **single-handed miniature yacht** than as a dinghy.[165]

## The Cobnor week: a Dinghy Cruising Association meeting, and falling overboard

In mid-August 2007 Faith joined the Dinghy Cruising Association for a week's meeting in Chichester Harbour, launched at Emsworth Marina on 17 August. On the fourth day, 20 August, trying to anchor off Cobnor beach as the tide turned, wind and current carried her off her intended spot and Bill went over the side. He had forgotten his buoyancy aid, and waterlogged clothing left him "floundering"; he worked his way to the stern and hauled himself aboard using the boarding step built into the rudder stock, losing his cap, a shoe, and his spectacles in the process. The incident later settled his preference for a snug-fitting buoyancy aid over a lifejacket when working the boat. The rest of the week ran less eventfully — a near-gale force 8 was ridden out safely at anchor, and Faith's mast jammed while being unstepped when rainwater draining down the deck ventilation pipe, exactly as designed, swelled the wood at its base. Bill's own verdict from the week: "Paradox can work to windward, but not handily as a Wayfarer or a Mirror dinghy... The boat is very strong and she can take the ground; she can be sealed from the elements to provide relative comfort." ## A spring 2008 cruise begins, weatherbound at Ramsgate

## Gale-bound at Brighton: a petrol-storage routine, and a case for reliability over electric power

During a weather-bound stop at Brighton on the same cruise, Bill set out his own long-practiced petrol-storage routine on the list, replying to a fuel-storage question revived elsewhere on the group: two five-litre cans kept in the forepeak, inspected often for leaks, the cabin always kept clear of fuel fumes, and the stove never lit while fuel is being handled — a regime carried over from earlier boats. He judged a petrol engine more reliable than an electric motor for covering real distances, framing the underlying choice as a matter of personal risk each sailor must weigh and accept for themselves; his own circumstances made the case directly — "There's no way I would be at Brighton without the engine." Full technical debate this fed into, including a battery-as-ballast/electric-auxiliary proposal from elsewhere on the list, at [operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md — a 2008 recurrence](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-2008-recurrence-a-published-account-quarter-tank-proposals-and-a-pivot-to-electric-power).[195]

The two boats crossed to Tresco on 18 May 2008. ## Scilly Isles storage, water, and a battery-maintenance routine

For the kind of cruising the Scilly Isles crossing involved, Faith's storage proved ample: the forepeak was never filled completely, leaving room for the bower anchor in its own purpose-built rack and a spare two-gallon can of drinking water — a spare that in the event went unused, since a similar can fitted with a hand pump in the lazarette let water be topped up at least every second day. Bulkhead #2's ridge into the forepeak — the same ridge Little Jim's owner has already cut away for his own folding bicycle's stowage (see [Little Jim — the forward-stateroom access, cut down for bike stowage](/boats/little-jim.md#the-forward-stateroom-access-cut-down-for-bike-stowage)) — is the one change Faith herself could still use, since checking and topping up the battery, done with a pipette rather than removing the battery itself, is a regular chore through that same forepeak access. After the cruise, plenty of canned food remained in the under-floor stowage, and perishables such as rice, biscuits, cereal, and chocolate, kept in plastic containers under the floor, stayed dry except for a meagre amount of condensation collecting there in extremely cold weather, which was attributed to Faith carrying no foam lining on her hull.[225]

## A laminated hatch beam that delaminated in the ply itself

While cruising in company through the Scilly Isles, a problem surfaced with Faith's hatch: its forward beam, laminated from plywood rather than solid wood, began to delaminate. On inspection, it was the plywood itself coming apart within its own lamination, not the epoxy joints bonding the beam to the hatch structure — a caution for fastening any laminated ply panel with nails or screws through it, rather than relying on the panel's own internal glue lines alone to hold it together over time. See [construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md](/construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md) for the beam-lamination technique this qualifies, and [construction/fastening-and-gluing.md](/construction/fastening-and-gluing.md#fastening-laminated-ply-panels-a-delamination-caution-from-experience) for the general fastening lesson it prompted.[208]

## A waterproof camera case suggested, and why a long lens won't fit

A **waterproof case for an underwater-capable digital camera** need not cost much: one such case, made for a Fujifilm compact and bought on eBay, cost under £20 including postage, worked to a depth of 10 m, and is available in sizes to fit most compact camera models.[212][213] A camera with a **10x optical zoom**, however, does not fit inside a compact-camera case at all — it protrudes too far in front, the trade-off for a lens that can fill the frame with a distant subject shot from the boat itself. **Cameras, like boats, are always a compromise.**[214]

## Refuelling practice underway, and a name's double meaning

The refuelling photographs (see [auxiliary outboard power — a side-mounted motor-and-fuel sponson considered and rejected](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#a-side-mounted-motor-and-fuel-sponson-considered-and-rejected-a-three-position-transom-bracket)) drew a wordplay reply worth recording in its own right: "Faith serves well."[215] Bill's answer took the phrase two ways at once. Going without a safety harness while refuelling was one of several risks he had knowingly accepted over the years — a harness gets in the way and can cause its own trouble, so he preferred free movement except in conditions severe enough that he would not be refuelling at all; running the engine in gear while it idled, he had found, made the boat noticeably steadier for the job. He closed by admitting he might one day take a risk too far, crediting "Faith" — the boat and, in the same breath, his own religious faith — with having kept him safe so far: "as Steve points out, 'Faith' has been on my side, or rather shall I say, my God. May He bless and guard you too."[216]

## A tide-and-wind case for the outboard, on the Blackwater

Replying to a suggestion elsewhere on the list that he give up his engine for sail and yuloh alone, Bill set out a concrete case for keeping it, drawn from an outing on the **Blackwater** estuary days before: with the wind out of the east at force 4 and the flood tide running the same way, he wanted to make two miles to windward as far as **Bradwell Power Station**, then run back to circumnavigate **Osea Island** before returning to his mooring, east of the island, by high water. The engine made the whole plan possible and pleasurable; without it, he judged, sailing would have been out of the question, since within the time his drying mooring allowed him he could not have left at all — wind and tide together would have carried him west toward Maldon, where he would have had to wait out the flood before the ebb could bring him back against the wind, quite possibly too late to reach his mooring before it dried. More generally, he judged handling Paradox among moored boats or in open water once the wind reaches force 3 or more calls for an engine, a yuloh being "virtually useless" in that kind of close-quarters work. He restated the safety value of carrying an engine at all, pointing to the West Country cruise's own precedent that a Paradox fitted with one can even tow another, as Faith and Little Jim had already done for each other between Fowey and Plymouth (see [above](#to-the-scilly-isles-and-back-with-little-jim)). A follow-up asking how much fuel the outing took drew a direct answer: no precise check was made back on the mooring, but the engine ran about an hour in total, burning at most half a litre — a rate Bill worked out at roughly six nautical miles to the litre, so that a 5-litre can is good for about 30 miles and his usual two 5-litre cans, some 60 miles, in calm conditions. Given the choice, he took petrol over an electric motor without qualification — power, speed, and duration all favour it — and argued a petrol outboard turns Paradox into a genuine motor-sailer able to convert instantly into a motorboat, capable of exploring the canals of Europe wherever petrol is on hand, an ambition he would not attempt relying on a yuloh alone. On the yuloh itself he was blunter still: useful on a Paradox only in near-calm conditions, since working it in a force 2 or better takes real effort just to hold a course, and even keeping the rudder raised — a tip that helps but does not fully solve the problem — still leaves the bow fighting to be blown off to one side or the other; he suspected Sven Yrvind's own boats fare better sculled in a breeze thanks to a forward drop board giving the sculler something to brace against, a feature Faith and Little Jim both lack. Glen Maxwell endorsed the whole case without qualification.[217][218][219][220] The exchange closed with a simple thank-you for fuel figures that turned out more reassuring than expected.[221] A fellow builder's own account of the same stretch adds a specific reason the learning curve ran as long as it did: despite decades of general sailing experience before Paradox, the engine got reached for every time conditions turned hard to windward in tight quarters, leaving fewer chances to build sail- and yuloh-handling skill in precisely the conditions that would have taught it fastest. See [Little Jim — a deliberate no-motor choice, and the case for leaving it at home](/boats/little-jim.md#a-deliberate-no-motor-choice-and-the-case-for-leaving-it-at-home).[222]

## A topped-up boom's other uses: readiness, yuloh clearance, and a rain awning

Beyond furling mechanics, Bill found several further reasons to keep the boom topped up. Running the engine, he sometimes topped up the furled sail to keep it clear of his head, leaving the boat ready for instant hoisting whenever he wanted to proceed under sail; he also found the yuloh usable while the furled sail was topped up in this way. A tent or awning could be deployed from a topped-up boom, particularly useful when at anchor during a rain storm. See [Furling system — a second use for the topping lift](/components/rig/furling.md#a-second-use-for-the-topping-lift-readiness-yuloh-clearance-and-a-rain-awning) for the wider exchange this answered, including an alternative sun-and-rain fix — a hatch-mounted umbrella — that needs no topping lift at all.[224]

## Appraising Faith: dimensions, ballast, and handling, in retrospect

Fully ballasted, Faith displaced 1,410 lb — 397 lb of lead and 154 lb of water — on a hull just over 4 ft in beam; her shallow 9 in draught let her sail to windward in 2 ft of water, and, despite a flat and heavily rockered bottom, she was self-righting and would sit upright unsupported on a beach. The build itself ran from the plywood's delivery in August 2005 to her launch in June 2007 — some twenty-two months, not the "year and a half" Bill's own retrospective accounts sometimes gave. Running downwind from Salcombe toward Brixham she "hardly rolled... because of her flat bottom and chine runners," and her stowage struck Bill as offering "enough stowage space for an ocean crossing."[188]

## A cruise account published on CD, and a 2009 UK gathering proposed

Bill's rewritten and illustrated account of Faith's cruising, "Faith's Cruise," was made available on CD to anyone donating toward the upkeep of the Small Sailboats web site (`www.smallsailboats.co.uk/cd/cd.htm`).[226] He also floated sailing Faith to Holland the following year to explore its inland waterways,[226] and, taking an interest in Noli and the stretched Enigma 460 prototype then both nearing completion (see [boats/noli.md](/boats/noli.md) and [Enigma 460 — a longer, plywood-cabin variant](/matt-layden/enigma.md#enigma-460-a-longer-plywood-cabin-variant-2007)), proposed the UK's Paradox-family builders arrange a get-together in 2009 to see the boats on the water together.[226]

## Sold in 2009: the forepeak reworked, a higher-peaked sail, and the yuloh reshaped

Faith was sold in 2009 — Bill's own account of it characteristically brief: "I sold my Paradox 'Faith' to the very first person who offered to take her unto himself," with a "pang of guilt" afterward. By May 2013 her owner was Jim Hall, "an accomplished sailor who [had] built a 21 ft yacht and sailed her by himself to far away places." Jim Hall had by then removed the forepeak's partition and widened its entrance, added a drinking-water tank forward, and improved access to the battery; moved the mainsheet lead forward onto the stern deck to stop it snagging the outboard; and fitted a new, higher-peaked sail — "the biggest improvement by far," which "transformed the boat so that she sails better to windward and tacks more positively," to the point that the engine was rarely needed. He had also reshaped the yuloh, giving it more twist, so the boat now had three independent means of propulsion. By 2014 Jim Hall "makes good use of her" and had recently had her repainted, closing out Faith's record in this bundle as a boat still actively sailed, close on a decade after her launch.[189][190][191][192]
