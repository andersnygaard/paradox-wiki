# Faith

**"Faith"** is [Bill Serjeant](/people/bill-serjeant.md)'s UK-built [Paradox](/paradox.md), built strictly to Matt Layden's original plans. Bill started construction in August 2005 after a kit-Paradox deal fell through, and as of the archive's latest record (October 2006) the build was still underway, unlaunched, with a hoped-for launch the following summer. For Bill's community/registry role and person-level record, see [people/bill-serjeant.md](/people/bill-serjeant.md).

## A kit Paradox that fell through, then a scratch build begins

In January 2005, Jordan Boats agreed to supply Bill his first "kit" Paradox, working with Matt Layden on the project — but by July 2005 the kit would not be ready after all, the panel design/cutting process needing more research than could be resolved soon. The plywood that would have gone into the kit was supplied regardless, and Bill chose to start building from Matt Layden's original plans instead, crediting his decision partly to trying out ["Little Jim"](/boats/little-jim.md) firsthand at Plymouth, cheered on with "Have at it Bill!!!! Good luck on your adventure!!!" Full account at [references/paradox-uk-website.md — a UK kit-built option: Jordan Boats](/references/paradox-uk-website.md#a-uk-kit-built-option-jordan-boats).

Bill began cutting and laminating deck-beam strips within days, practicing the lamination technique before committing to the actual beams and taking care to leave each beam clamped a full 24 hours rather than rush the cure. All beam strips are cut a uniform 4 ft across a sheet of plywood and trimmed to length once fitted, since the boat's widest point (bulkhead #3) carries no deck beam at all. Full technique record at [construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md](/construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md#why-4-ft-strips-are-long-enough-for-every-beam).

## Boom and yard curvature, and a persisting sag question

Shaping and sanding Faith's boom, Bill noticed the plans draw one side of both the boom and yard straight and the other curved, without stating which side should face up, and separately raised a longer-running concern about the yard sagging under the sail's own weight (worsened to windward by sheet load on the boom's end) — including whether a suggested fix of recutting a dart-shaped strip from the sail itself was the best solution. The boom has no fixed top or bottom in practice, and the recommendation was to work on the yard's own curvature rather than have the sail recut, since a yard is easy to swap back if an experiment fails. The boom is curved at all because furling it for reefing removes the sail's draft so it sets correctly reefed as well as full — reassurance Bill took as a cue to press on, reporting the stempost made and the frames and cleats next on his own build. Full technical discussion at [design/rig.md — which side of the boom and yard faces up, and curing persistent yard sag](/design/rig.md#which-side-of-the-boom-and-yard-faces-up-and-curing-persistent-yard-sag).[6][7][8][9]

On the recurring plywood-grade and durability debate, Bill gave his own specification for a properly finished Paradox: heavy fiberglass sheathing regardless of ply grade beneath it (per the plans' sheet #5, 4 mm of fiberglass with the sides and deck each getting one layer), sealed with three coats of epoxy and a compatible top-coat paint — a combination his own marine-plywood boats, including his epoxy-and-marine-paint-finished "50/50" canoe, have shown gives a 20-year-plus life expectancy. See [plywood grade and foam flotation debate — long-term plywood durability, resale value, and why to expect neither](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#long-term-plywood-durability-resale-value-and-why-to-expect-neither).[10]

Bill mixes UK Epoxies' resin at a 2:1 ratio using dispensing pumps he rates excellent for guessing minute quantities accurately — his own Paradox being the third boat built using the same pair — and, against a warning that hardener crystals can foul such pumps, found that periodically clearing the nozzles with a bit of wire is all the maintenance his have ever needed. See [plywood grade and foam flotation debate — long-term plywood durability, resale value, and why to expect neither](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#long-term-plywood-durability-resale-value-and-why-to-expect-neither).[11][12]

Turning to window glazing, Bill asked where to source Lexan in the UK and whether Perspex is the same material — prompting sourcing tips and a polycarbonate-versus-acrylic technical comparison. Bill reported that the UK price of a 6 mm Lexan sheet had actually fallen between 2003 and 2006, "not often prices get cheaper." Full technical and sourcing detail at [window glazing — Lexan versus Perspex/acrylic](/construction/window-glazing-materials.md).[13][14][15][16]

Working on his stern baffle, Bill raised a spar-and-sail road-stowage question — how practical it is to pass the mast, boom, yard, and sail through the transom hatch for trailering, and whether the hatch is worth fitting at all. See [transom deck plate — stowing the mast, boom, yard, and sail through the transom hatch for road travel](/construction/transom-deck-plate.md#stowing-the-mast-boom-yard-and-sail-through-the-transom-hatch-for-road-travel).[17] Confirmed his own `smallsailboats` site and photo album as his, on the "Enuf" photo update. See [boats/enuf.md — a carpal-tunnel operation halts progress, and a five-week pause](/boats/enuf.md#a-carpal-tunnel-operation-halts-progress-and-a-five-week-pause).[18]

## Build-cost comparison, and a hull-assembly milestone

Against an "it's only 14 feet" quip about the scale of the commitment, Bill offered his own comparative data point: having also built a 25 ft boat, he judged there was markedly less work in that bigger boat than in the Paradox he had underway, at only marginally lower cost — having started in August 2005, he did not expect to finish before summer 2007, and judged even a full-time professional with power tools could not complete a Paradox in under six months. See [operations/secondhand-market.md — build vs. buy secondhand](/operations/secondhand-market.md#build-vs-buy-secondhand-a-uk-newcomers-cost-benefit-hesitation) for the fuller build-versus-buy discussion this fed into.[19]

A week later, with fine weather arrived, Bill reported good progress and pointed to fresh photographs of the latest parts at his gallery (`www.smallsailboats.co.uk/paradox/con_gallery.htm`), expecting to begin assembling the hull within days, with practically every other part already built.[20] The progress drew congratulations.[21][22] Bill promised further updates and planned to attempt a dry fit on the first good day.[23] He was finding the build a challenge but was excited to see the boat gradually taking shape.[24]

When an Amsterdam builder announced he could not finish his own Paradox, "RHXII," and offered her for sale, Bill offered to add his e-mail address to the UK registry to help find a buyer — see [operations/secondhand-market.md — an abandoned build for sale: "RHXII"](/operations/secondhand-market.md#an-abandoned-build-for-sale-rhxii-april-2006).[25] He also followed up off-list, praised the "RHX11" photos already posted to the group, and called it a sadness the boat could not be finished.[26]

## A dry assembly of the hull, named "Faith"

Days later, Bill posted photographs of Faith's first **dry assembly** — the hull's panels and bulkheads fitted together unglued, to check the fit before committing epoxy.[27] The stem joint drew praise for how well it had come together, one builder admitting that if his own stem had fitted as cleanly he would have left the foredeck off so everyone could see it.[28][29] Bill explained the fit had cost him most of the wood he started with: he had begun with three large, expensive blocks, and by the time the stem was fully shaped there was very little of them left.[30][31] The pace of the work drew remark — from only the small parts shown in Bill's first photo album, before the cold weather set in, to very nearly a complete hull within a few short weeks of the cold snap breaking.[32][33]

## Gluing the frames and floors, and the vent trunk

Within days of the dry assembly, Bill glued the floors and the vent trunk permanently in place, and cut in the frames — reporting he had begun the frames as early as late February despite the outward appearance (from his first photo album) of having only made small parts before the cold weather set in; being retired, he could put in about four days a week on the build.[34] He posted a photo of the completed vent trunk and, the next day, planned to apply epoxy to permanently join the frames and side panels.[35][36]

## Ballast-tank plumbing, and an off-centre tank divider

Turning to the built-in ballast tanks, Bill asked how the plans' 12 mm copper fill/suction pipe performs in practice, and how the tanks are best filled, drained, and closed off for rainwater collection. Matt Layden empties his own tanks with a fuel bulb specifically to encourage fresh-water conservation. See [transom deck plate — the fill/suction copper pipe: sizing, timing, and the fittings that connect to it](/construction/transom-deck-plate.md#the-fillsuction-copper-pipe-sizing-timing-and-the-fittings-that-connect-to-it) for the full exchange.[37][38][39] Bill thanked each contributor in turn.[40][41]

Fitting the floor supports into their dados, Bill separately noticed the plans' central tank divider sits off-centre to port rather than on the boat's centerline — checked against Matt Layden's own drawings and other builders' completed boats, and confirmed correct despite an apparently contradictory plan-view drawing. See [water tank construction and lining — an off-centre tank divider, confirmed correct against the plans](/construction/water-tank-lining.md#an-off-centre-tank-divider-confirmed-correct-against-the-plans).[42]

The fill/suction thread also drew a side discussion on alternatives to the tanks altogether (sea water in place of fresh, or skipping the tanks for plain water bottles) and, separately, on a third ballast method — sheet lead bedded permanently in place with a sand/epoxy infill, proven on Bill's own prior 25 ft Folksong. See [transom deck plate — the fill/suction copper pipe](/construction/transom-deck-plate.md#the-fillsuction-copper-pipe-sizing-timing-and-the-fittings-that-connect-to-it) and [weight and ballast — installing the lead ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#installing-the-lead-ballast).[43][44][45][46][47]

Around the same time, against a concurrent hull-fairing report, Bill noted the same wet weather was holding up his bottom-piece bevelling, with the epoxy needing several days' cure once applied and a suitable outdoor temperature not always available. See [Enuf — rubbing down, and the first coat of pigmented resin](/boats/enuf.md#rubbing-down-and-the-first-coat-of-pigmented-resin).[48]

## The bottom fixed, chine runners complete, and a two-month pause for Australia

By mid-June 2006 Bill reported the hull's bottom "well and truly fixed" and the chine runners just completed, with glassing the bottom next — but had to stop building for two months while away in Australia. He hoped to meet an Australian builder there and see his Paradox "Aussie Paradox," though a couple of emails had so far gone unanswered. He gave this update while welcoming a new Ithaca, New York builder to the list — see [people/matt-noonan-ithaca.md](/people/matt-noonan-ithaca.md).[49]

## Chine-runner and gudgeon photos, and Mark Waters' Paradox

Days after [the site outage](/people/bill-serjeant.md#a-june-2006-site-outage), Bill uploaded photographs of Faith's chine runner and lower gudgeon, and reported putting a third layer of glass on her bottom, hoping to finish glassing the hull's underside before leaving for a month in Australia — see [the bottom fixed, chine runners complete, and a two-month pause for Australia](#the-bottom-fixed-chine-runners-complete-and-a-two-month-pause-for-australia) above for the trip itself. He also reported seeing photographs of another builder's Paradox, calling the work "superb" and planning to add them to the Paradox site.[50] The chine-runner and gudgeon workmanship drew a compliment in turn, with the remark that skills like that made a kit hard to see the point of, except perhaps to save time.[51]

He then posted the gallery link itself to the group (`www.smallsailboats.co.uk/paradox/mw_gallery.htm`), drawing further unprompted praise for the finish — see [people/mark-waters.md](/people/mark-waters.md) for the full exchange.[52]

Days later, Bill separately posted a further selection of "Enuf" photographs to his `smallsailboats.co.uk` site, crediting the pictures' source — the same set posted to the group under the subject "Enuf with her sail up," showing the hatch cover's current low profile and a furling snag that morning. See [boats/enuf.md — Enuf with her sail up](/boats/enuf.md#a-lower-hatch-cover-and-the-furled-sail-riding-forward-over-the-drum).[53]

When a new personal website appeared with photographs of "Zoë," Bill welcomed it — noting it turned up some photos new to him too, and singling out the bunk-cushion setup as looking "very comfortable" — and offered to link it from `paradox-uk.co.uk`, if he could manage it before leaving for a two-month tour of eastern Australia. See [a personal website and Zoë's bunk cushions](/people/glen-maxwell/build-and-zoe.md#a-2006-personal-website-and-zoe-bunk-cushions).[54]

## Faith's rudder: a sheet-fairlead fix, and boom-tang strength

Back from Australia, Bill posted fresh photographs of Faith's completed rudder to his `smallsailboats.co.uk` gallery.[55] The photos drew two independent technical replies from builders who had already sailed the stock design. The rudder, built to the plans' specified height, lets the mainsheet drop into the gap between the rudder stock and the transom on a broad reach or run — starboard tack in particular — forcing the sheet to slide up over the pintle/gudgeon on every sail adjustment. The fix was a small wooden insert filling that gap to act as a fairlead: not essential to sailing the boat, but a real improvement to how easily the sheet runs. This is a second, independently reported instance of the same rudder-height/mainsheet-fouling problem tracked in full at [design/rudder.md — rudder-stock height and mainsheet fouling at the transom notch](/design/rudder.md#rudder-stock-height-and-mainsheet-fouling-at-the-transom-notch).[56]

A hardware-strength problem was separately flagged in the same photos: the stock mainsheet/topping-lift tang, as fitted, is not strong enough and will bend under load — on "Whisper," three tangs bolted together had to be fitted in place of the single stock part to stop the bending, and the same problem had been met on "Zoë," though without record of how it was resolved.[57]

Bill fitted the wooden fairlead insert the same day, reporting his own steering line worked well once it was in.[58] On the tang question, he had deliberately held off strengthening it, intending to load-test the stock part first before modifying anything — but conceded the point, and resolved to reinforce it before Faith's planned launch the following summer.[59] The same tang had bent on another boat, solved with a heavier West Marine part; a second, unrelated rudder problem was separately described — the raised blade banging its trailing edge against the transom — fixed with a small stainless bracket and a length of rubber hose acting as a stop, recorded in full at [design/rudder.md](/design/rudder.md#rudder-stock-height-and-mainsheet-fouling-at-the-transom-notch).[60]

## Why he wanted a kit, and a possible plywood-bottom kit method

Asked about the kit Paradox that fell through (see [a kit Paradox that fell through, then a scratch build begins](#a-kit-paradox-that-fell-through-then-a-scratch-build-begins) above), Bill explained his reason for wanting one in the first place was purely **speed of construction** — not a full kit, just the main components. He still believed a kit could be produced, but judged the construction method would need rethinking first: the bottom, for instance, could be built up from three layers of thin plywood, with the first layer stitched to the sides and the remaining layers applied over it, rather than the plans' own single-thickness bottom.[61]

## A second topping-lift question

Weeks after the mast-wiring exchange, Bill raised the topping-lift question afresh — undecided whether to fit one at all, drawn to Matt Layden's own without-a-topping-lift practice as being in keeping with the design's overall simplicity. Two more builders answered from experience, both recommending a topping lift regardless; see [furling system — a second, later topping-lift question, and two more confirmations](/design/furling-system.md#a-second-later-topping-lift-question-and-two-more-confirmations).[62]

A third, more detailed topping-lift question followed once Bill had settled on fitting one: it worked through his own reading of the plans' deck-tube layout at the mast and compared a port-side topping-lift rig against a starboard-side alternative believed to be in use on "Whisper." The answers came in full — the masthead-eye arrangement, the corrected deck-tube order, and the reasoning behind a multi-block purchase — and Bill thanked each contributor in turn for the explanation, closing out the thread. See [furling system — a third topping-lift question: reading the deck-tube layout at the mast](/design/furling-system.md#a-third-topping-lift-question-reading-the-deck-tube-layout-at-the-mast).[63][64][65]

## Lower pintle: an alternative to drilling the rudder stock

Bill devised and photographed a method of installing the rudder's lower pintle onto the rudder stock without drilling a hole for it — a technique he judged simpler and more accurate than drilling, which he shared as a "UK2" photo-folder posting. Drilling was agreed to be the harder way to do it, the setup being nerve-wracking since a mistake there means a major rebuild; the approach drew further praise.[66][67][68]

## Ballast-access and tank-top questions

Once Faith's rudder was sorted, Bill raised two further late-build questions: whether other builders had found the inner ballast bolts hard to reach once the overlapping floors were in place, and, separately, how best to fix down the tops of the water ballast tanks. The tank-top question was answered first, with a thickened-glue-and-hand-fillet technique applied through the access hatches that has kept the tank tops themselves leak-free; see [water tank construction and lining — fixing the tank tops in place](/construction/water-tank-lining.md#fixing-the-tank-tops-in-place) for the full technique.[69][70][71]

On ballast access, the answer offered a tapped-lead-pig-and-plate method; Bill then filled in the fuller picture of his own arrangement — top wooden retaining pieces bolted into a floor block and the chine log, which let the pigs shift fore and aft but hid the real difficulty at two bolts under the ballast-tank top's flap — and proposed a removable plank in the flap's place. A counter-suggestion of small access holes above the bolt heads was in turn rejected, since fixed side pieces at that scale would still be awkward to reach through and would also block off the jubilee-clip joint between the ballast tank's copper and PVC plumbing. Bill settled on building the water-ballast tank top's sides as separate, removable "planks" instead — giving full, ongoing access to both the lead ballast and that plumbing joint. See [weight, ballast, and displacement — reaching the ballast fastenings after the floors are in](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#reaching-the-ballast-fastenings-after-the-floors-are-in) for the full exchange.[72][73][74]

## A removable-floorboard technique for full storage and ballast access

Bill also described his own broader solution to floor and locker access, prompted by a "Haliyard and Reef lines" thread that had turned to a plan to split floor hatches ahead of the seat. Bill built his own aft floorboards to the plans, but without hinges: instead, he supported them on removable under-flaps taken from the sides of the storage bins, so that either side of the floor can simply be lifted and slid forward, opening the whole of the storage compartments beneath — a plan he judged with an eye to posting photographs of it. He also fitted side flaps/planks either side of the water tanks, giving good access to the forward ballast — the same "planks" solution he had just settled on above. See [design/interior-bins-and-floor.md — a removable-floorboard technique for full storage and ballast access](/design/interior-bins-and-floor.md#a-removable-floorboard-technique-for-full-storage-and-ballast-access) for the cross-linked technique.[75]

## A trim question, and Little Jim's tacking quirk

Bill asked how closely a Paradox's fore-and-aft trim needs watching, having assumed the boat was heavy enough that shifting the ballast a little one way or the other would make no real difference. The answer drew on "[Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md)"'s own occasional tacking trouble — see [design/rudder.md — sudden, total loss of rudder effect mid-tack, and tacking technique](/design/rudder.md#sudden-total-loss-of-rudder-effect-mid-tack-and-tacking-technique) for the full account. Bill looked forward to finding out whether "Faith" would show the same trim sensitivity once launched.[76][77][78]

## Build progress photographs, and comparing notes with another builder

Bill posted a further round of "Faith" progress photographs to his own website, showing the number 2 deck beam fitted along with backing blocks for the forward and aft mooring cleats.[79] Comparing notes with another builder working on "Ironsides" concurrently, he judged his own work not quite as finished in places as he would have liked, but was satisfied overall with how the hull was shaping up.[80][81]
