# Faith

**"Faith"** is [Bill Serjeant](/people/bill-serjeant.md)'s UK-built [Paradox](/paradox.md), built strictly to Matt Layden's original plans. Bill started construction in August 2005 after a kit-Paradox deal fell through, and launched Faith in mid-June 2007. For Bill's community/registry role and person-level record, see [people/bill-serjeant.md](/people/bill-serjeant.md).

## A kit Paradox that fell through, then a scratch build begins

In January 2005, Jordan Boats agreed to supply Bill his first "kit" Paradox, working with Matt Layden on the project — but by July 2005 the kit would not be ready after all, the panel design/cutting process needing more research than could be resolved soon. The plywood that would have gone into the kit was supplied regardless, and Bill chose to start building from Matt Layden's original plans instead, crediting his decision partly to trying out ["Little Jim"](/boats/little-jim.md) firsthand at Plymouth, cheered on with "Have at it Bill!!!! Good luck on your adventure!!!" Full account at [references/paradox-uk-website.md — a UK kit-built option: Jordan Boats](/references/paradox-uk-website.md#a-uk-kit-built-option-jordan-boats).

Bill began cutting and laminating deck-beam strips within days, practicing the lamination technique before committing to the actual beams and taking care to leave each beam clamped a full 24 hours rather than rush the cure. All beam strips are cut a uniform 4 ft across a sheet of plywood and trimmed to length once fitted, since the boat's widest point (bulkhead #3) carries no deck beam at all. Full technique record at [construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md](/construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md#why-4-ft-strips-are-long-enough-for-every-beam).

## The plywood cutting schedule, and the pace of laminating deck beams

The ten sheets of Robbins marine plywood arrived on 1 August 2005, cut to the layout given on sheet 6 of the plans: two 18 mm sheets for the bottom, rudder case and bulkhead 3; four 12 mm sheets for the sides, bulkhead 2, the rudder blade, the cabin sole, shelves and the transom; three 9 mm sheets for the decking, bulkheads 1 and 4, and storage bins; and one 6 mm sheet for the cabin roof, hatch cover and a ventilation baffle — with the whole build expected to take "as long as two years or more."[245]

Each deck beam was laminated from five 1220 mm strips of 5 mm WBP plywood — eight gluing surfaces per beam — over a jig built from 16 mm Conti Board, two bonded curved pieces forming the underside curve and a third backing the beam's forward face, the strips brushed with thickened epoxy over a bin-bag-lined jig, bent round it and held first by temporary screws, then by five clamps kept loose enough not to starve the joint. Each beam was originally left clamped 48 hours, until Bill learned "most Paradox builders believe 24 hours is enough," roughly doubling his production rate toward a target of three beams a week.[246][247] All seven deck beams are identical but for length, cut to the fore-and-aft stations the plans give off bulkhead 2. With the garage barely 8 ft wide against Paradox's beam of "slightly over 4 ft" — leaving only about 2 ft of clearance either side once the hull went together — every small part was built first and the hull itself reserved for when there would be room to work around it.[248][249]

## Casting the rudder's lead, and pigs for the hull's main ballast

In September 2005 Bill cast the rudder's own lead ballast into its trailing edge: the blade cut to the plan's profile, six retaining screws set in, a mould built from an oak plank and a strip of softwood clamped under the cavity, wooden wedges filling the gaps at the rounded trailing edge, and the blade levelled before pouring molten lead melted over a butane torch under a saucepan on two bricks. Matt Layden had calculated the job would take "3 kilos of lead, which proved to be accurate."[250] The lead was later filed and faired with thickened epoxy, then GRP-sheathed in 6 oz woven roving using a homemade sanding board — a Don Elliott design, 16½ in of half-inch plywood faced with belt-sander strips and fitted with two handles.[251]

The hull's own main ballast called for ten pigs of scrap lead at around 40 lb each, some 400 lb in total, reasoned from the boat mostly day-sailing without stores aboard to bring her down to her designed waterline.[252]

## Building the mast: wiring, glassing, and a rejected Windex

Fitting the mast together in October 2005, Bill worked out how to route the masthead light's wiring around the halyard sheave through an external channel into the hollow mast, down to a socket near the base where a flex from the battery could be plugged in, the light controlled by a photocell rather than a manual switch.[253] By 19 October the mast had "taken on its final shape" except at the base, left flexible to match the support socket; Bill was surprised "at the slenderness of the upper part of the spar," put down to weight economy and flexibility for dissipating gust loads. He enlarged the sheave slot and began applying the plan's four coats of 6 oz fibreglass over the top 300 mm, recycling a sheave from his own spares box when the right size wasn't for sale, and rejected two Windex wind-indicators in turn — one with no suitable mounting base, the other needing a masthead VHF aerial he had no intention of fitting, relying on a handheld set instead.[254] Reflecting that Paradox's construction — many small parts joined by epoxy — suited a builder with only an hour or two to spare, since work could run in parallel while earlier glue-ups cured, Bill moved the curing mast out of the front hall and into a heated tarpaulin tent in the garage the following day, "in deference to my wife."[255][256] A single shopping trip that October covered the rudder bolt, tiller bolts, lower gudgeon bolts, the mast sheave, hatch-slider retaining bolts, a yuloh lag bolt needing modification, Perspex window screws, brass piano hinges for the floor-locker tops, and the small-gauge copper breather pipes for both water tanks.[256]

## Winter hibernation, a Birdwatcher temptation, and naming her "Faith"

Building happens outdoors, and by November 2005 the build had stalled: the rudder and stock sat untouched for weeks between bad weather and competing priorities,[257] and by mid-month, with epoxy needing above 15°C to cure and overnight frosts setting in, Bill paused formally for the winter and turned to decorating the house instead.[258] Work resumed in March 2006 after "a winter's hibernation": the hull itself was barely started, beyond cutting one frame, though the mast, boom, yard, rudder, stock, tiller, laminated beams, reefing mechanism and ballast were all already made, with only the yuloh left to build before the hull could begin.[259] That same month, weighing a Phil Bolger "Birdwatcher" as an alternative project — having years earlier inspected and passed on a rotten, rusted example in Edinburgh — Bill judged Paradox the easier of the two to launch and recover while trailer-sailing and, having already bought the wood and fittings, chose to continue.[260] On 17 March 2006, after "a long deliberation considering many names," he settled on "Faith" — "I require faith more than anything to make my dream of ownership a reality"; the roughly eighty candidate names weighed first are recorded at [people/bill-serjeant.md](/people/bill-serjeant.md#the-small-sailboats-paradox-uk-web-presence).[259] Putting in some five hours a day, Bill cut all four frames, the transom and the side panels by the end of March; the "rib cage" was nearly ready for assembly by mid-April, and by the end of the month the frames, transom, stem and side panels were being fitted and checked prior to gluing, the work now done on a home-built mobile trolley that let the boat be wheeled in and out of the garage.[261][262][263]

## Chine logs, a gudgeon mould, and the wettest May on record

By mid-May 2006 the sheer clamps and chine logs were fixed to the hull — the chine logs hand-shaped and planed, then forced into place with sash clamps and considerable effort — and the floor supports and water-tank frames were levelled and checked square against the chine logs before bonding. The transom baffle went in around the same time, keeping water from entering through the tiller opening while doubling as a ventilator, and the lower rudder gudgeon was cast in a mould built around a length of tape-wrapped copper tube (forming the pintle hole), packed with chopped strand mat and epoxy that cured faster than expected from its own exothermic heat: "there's a saying that love can cover a multitude of sins, but the boat builder uses epoxy to hide a good many faults!"[264] Over a bank holiday weekend, with the garage given over to other work, the bottom's two large panels were joined indoors on the lounge floor — the carpet protected with plastic sheeting — through a 48-hour cure, then rolled out through the front door solo and levered onto the upturned hull on its trolley; May 2006 was "the wettest since 1983," complicating outdoor work throughout.[265]

## Fitting out the interior: ergonomics ahead of the decks

By late October 2006 the build had reached the interior fit-out. Bill judged the design "well designed in the ergonomic sense": the steering lines sit at a height requiring little effort to hold, and the transverse seat is set at the exact height to see the horizon through 360 degrees while seated. He built simple racks for crockery and cutlery within reach of the galley, planned a waterproof wheelchair-style cushion usable sitting or kneeling at the stove, and built a transverse table doubling as chart table, dining table, or a support for a laptop — positioning the compass, echo sounder and GPS to be read at a glance from the helm. There is no standing headroom unless the hatch is open.[266]

## Boom and yard curvature, and a persisting sag question

Shaping and sanding Faith's boom, Bill noticed the plans draw one side of both the boom and yard straight and the other curved, without stating which side should face up, and separately raised a longer-running concern about the yard sagging under the sail's own weight (worsened to windward by sheet load on the boom's end) — including whether a suggested fix of recutting a dart-shaped strip from the sail itself was the best solution. The boom has no fixed top or bottom in practice, and the recommendation was to work on the yard's own curvature rather than have the sail recut, since a yard is easy to swap back if an experiment fails. The boom is curved at all because furling it for reefing removes the sail's draft so it sets correctly reefed as well as full — reassurance Bill took as a cue to press on, reporting the stempost made and the frames and cleats next on his own build. Full technical discussion at [design/rig.md — which side of the boom and yard faces up, and curing persistent yard sag](/components/rig/index.md#which-side-of-the-boom-and-yard-faces-up-and-curing-persistent-yard-sag).[6][7][8][9]

On the recurring plywood-grade and durability debate, Bill gave his own specification for a properly finished Paradox: heavy fiberglass sheathing regardless of ply grade beneath it (per the plans' sheet #5, 4 mm of fiberglass with the sides and deck each getting one layer), sealed with three coats of epoxy and a compatible top-coat paint — a combination his own marine-plywood boats, including his epoxy-and-marine-paint-finished "50/50" canoe, have shown gives a 20-year-plus life expectancy. See [plywood grade and foam flotation debate — long-term plywood durability, resale value, and why to expect neither](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#long-term-plywood-durability-resale-value-and-why-to-expect-neither).[10]

Bill mixes UK Epoxies' resin at a 2:1 ratio using dispensing pumps he rates excellent for guessing minute quantities accurately — his own Paradox being the third boat built using the same pair — and, against a warning that hardener crystals can foul such pumps, found that periodically clearing the nozzles with a bit of wire is all the maintenance his have ever needed. See [plywood grade and foam flotation debate — long-term plywood durability, resale value, and why to expect neither](/construction/plywood-grades-and-foam-flotation.md#long-term-plywood-durability-resale-value-and-why-to-expect-neither).[11][12]

Turning to window glazing, Bill asked where to source Lexan in the UK and whether Perspex is the same material — prompting sourcing tips and a polycarbonate-versus-acrylic technical comparison. Bill reported that the UK price of a 6 mm Lexan sheet had actually fallen between 2003 and 2006, "not often prices get cheaper." Full technical and sourcing detail at [window glazing — Lexan versus Perspex/acrylic](/construction/window-glazing-materials.md).[13][14][15][16]

Working on his stern baffle, Bill raised a spar-and-sail road-stowage question — how practical it is to pass the mast, boom, yard, and sail through the transom hatch for trailering, and whether the hatch is worth fitting at all. See [transom deck plate — stowing the mast, boom, yard, and sail through the transom hatch for road travel](/construction/transom-deck-plate.md#stowing-the-mast-boom-yard-and-sail-through-the-transom-hatch-for-road-travel).[17] Confirmed his own `smallsailboats` site and photo album as his, on the "Enuf" photo update. See [boats/enuf.md — a carpal-tunnel operation halts progress, and a five-week pause](/boats/enuf.md#a-carpal-tunnel-operation-halts-progress-and-a-five-week-pause).[18]

## Build-cost comparison, and a hull-assembly milestone

Against an "it's only 14 feet" quip about the scale of the commitment, Bill offered his own comparative data point: having also built a 25 ft boat, he judged there was markedly less work in that bigger boat than in the Paradox he had underway, at only marginally lower cost — having started in August 2005, he did not expect to finish before summer 2007, and judged even a full-time professional with power tools could not complete a Paradox in under six months. See [operations/secondhand-market.md — build vs. buy secondhand](/operations/secondhand-market.md#build-vs-buy-secondhand-a-uk-newcomers-cost-benefit-hesitation) for the fuller build-versus-buy discussion this fed into.[19]

A week later, with fine weather arrived, Bill reported good progress and pointed to fresh photographs of the latest parts at his gallery (`www.smallsailboats.co.uk/paradox/con_gallery.htm`), expecting to begin assembling the hull within days, with practically every other part already built.[20] The progress drew congratulations.[21][22] Bill promised further updates and planned to attempt a dry fit on the first good day.[23] He was finding the build a challenge but was excited to see the boat gradually taking shape.[24]

When an Amsterdam builder announced he could not finish his own Paradox, "RHXII," and offered her for sale, Bill offered to add his e-mail address to the UK registry to help find a buyer — see [operations/secondhand-market.md — an abandoned build for sale: "RHXII"](/operations/secondhand-market.md#an-abandoned-build-for-sale-rhxii-april-2006).[25] He also followed up off-list, praised the "RHX11" photos already posted to the group, and called it a sadness the boat could not be finished.[26]

## A dry assembly of the hull, named "Faith"

Days later, Bill posted photographs of Faith's first **dry assembly** — the hull's panels and bulkheads fitted together unglued, to check the fit before committing epoxy.[27] The stem joint drew praise for how well it had come together, one builder admitting that if his own stem had fitted as cleanly he would have left the foredeck off so everyone could see it.[28][29] Bill explained the fit had cost him most of the wood he started with: he had begun with three large, expensive blocks, and by the time the stem was fully shaped there was very little of them left.[30][31] The pace of the work drew remark — from only the small parts shown in Bill's first photo album, before the cold weather set in, to very nearly a complete hull within a few short weeks of the cold snap breaking.[32][33]

## Gluing the frames and floors, and the vent trunk

Within days of the dry assembly, Bill glued the floors and the vent trunk permanently in place, and cut in the frames — reporting he had begun the frames as early as late February despite the outward appearance (from his first photo album) of having only made small parts before the cold weather set in; being retired, he could put in about four days a week on the build.[34] He posted a photo of the completed vent trunk and, the next day, planned to apply epoxy to permanently join the frames and side panels.[35][36]

## Faith's rudder: a sheet-fairlead fix, and boom-tang strength

Back from Australia, Bill posted fresh photographs of Faith's completed rudder to his `smallsailboats.co.uk` gallery.[55] The photos drew two independent technical replies from builders who had already sailed the stock design. The rudder, built to the plans' specified height, lets the mainsheet drop into the gap between the rudder stock and the transom on a broad reach or run — starboard tack in particular — forcing the sheet to slide up over the pintle/gudgeon on every sail adjustment. The fix was a small wooden insert filling that gap to act as a fairlead: not essential to sailing the boat, but a real improvement to how easily the sheet runs. This is a second, independently reported instance of the same rudder-height/mainsheet-fouling problem tracked in full at [design/rudder.md — rudder-stock height and mainsheet fouling at the transom notch](/components/rudder.md#rudder-stock-height-and-mainsheet-fouling-at-the-transom-notch).[56]

A hardware-strength problem was separately flagged in the same photos: the stock mainsheet/topping-lift tang, as fitted, is not strong enough and will bend under load — on "Whisper," three tangs bolted together had to be fitted in place of the single stock part to stop the bending, and the same problem had been met on "Zoë," though without record of how it was resolved.[57]

Bill fitted the wooden fairlead insert the same day, reporting his own steering line worked well once it was in.[58] On the tang question, he had deliberately held off strengthening it, intending to load-test the stock part first before modifying anything — but conceded the point, and resolved to reinforce it before Faith's planned launch the following summer.[59] The same tang had bent on another boat, solved with a heavier West Marine part; a second, unrelated rudder problem was separately described — the raised blade banging its trailing edge against the transom — fixed with a small stainless bracket and a length of rubber hose acting as a stop, recorded in full at [design/rudder.md](/components/rudder.md#rudder-stock-height-and-mainsheet-fouling-at-the-transom-notch).[60]

## Why he wanted a kit, and a possible plywood-bottom kit method

Asked about the kit Paradox that fell through (see [a kit Paradox that fell through, then a scratch build begins](#a-kit-paradox-that-fell-through-then-a-scratch-build-begins) above), Bill explained his reason for wanting one in the first place was purely **speed of construction** — not a full kit, just the main components. He still believed a kit could be produced, but judged the construction method would need rethinking first: the bottom, for instance, could be built up from three layers of thin plywood, with the first layer stitched to the sides and the remaining layers applied over it, rather than the plans' own single-thickness bottom.[61]

## Lower pintle: an alternative to drilling the rudder stock

Bill devised and photographed a method of installing the rudder's lower pintle onto the rudder stock without drilling a hole for it — a technique he judged simpler and more accurate than drilling, which he shared as a "UK2" photo-folder posting. Drilling was agreed to be the harder way to do it, the setup being nerve-wracking since a mistake there means a major rebuild; the approach drew further praise.[66][67][68]

## Ballast-access and tank-top questions

Once Faith's rudder was sorted, Bill raised two further late-build questions: whether other builders had found the inner ballast bolts hard to reach once the overlapping floors were in place, and, separately, how best to fix down the tops of the water ballast tanks. The tank-top question was answered first, with a thickened-glue-and-hand-fillet technique applied through the access hatches that has kept the tank tops themselves leak-free; see [water tank construction and lining — fixing the tank tops in place](/construction/water-tank-lining.md#fixing-the-tank-tops-in-place) for the full technique.[69][70][71]

On ballast access, the answer offered a tapped-lead-pig-and-plate method; Bill then filled in the fuller picture of his own arrangement — top wooden retaining pieces bolted into a floor block and the chine log, which let the pigs shift fore and aft but hid the real difficulty at two bolts under the ballast-tank top's flap — and proposed a removable plank in the flap's place. A counter-suggestion of small access holes above the bolt heads was in turn rejected, since fixed side pieces at that scale would still be awkward to reach through and would also block off the jubilee-clip joint between the ballast tank's copper and PVC plumbing. Bill settled on building the water-ballast tank top's sides as separate, removable "planks" instead — giving full, ongoing access to both the lead ballast and that plumbing joint. See [weight, ballast, and displacement — reaching the ballast fastenings after the floors are in](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#reaching-the-ballast-fastenings-after-the-floors-are-in) for the full exchange.[72][73][74]

## A removable-floorboard technique for full storage and ballast access

Bill also described his own broader solution to floor and locker access, prompted by a "Haliyard and Reef lines" thread that had turned to a plan to split floor hatches ahead of the seat. Bill built his own aft floorboards to the plans, but without hinges: instead, he supported them on removable under-flaps taken from the sides of the storage bins, so that either side of the floor can simply be lifted and slid forward, opening the whole of the storage compartments beneath — a plan he judged with an eye to posting photographs of it. He also fitted side flaps/planks either side of the water tanks, giving good access to the forward ballast — the same "planks" solution he had just settled on above. See [design/interior-bins-and-floor.md — a removable-floorboard technique for full storage and ballast access](/components/interior.md#a-removable-floorboard-technique-for-full-storage-and-ballast-access) for the cross-linked technique.[75]

## Cabin sole panels revisited, a case against built-in water tanks, and a washboard endorsed

Answering a fellow builder's water-tank and rear-sole-plate question in the "Cabin Sole" thread (see [JoHanna — water tanks reconsidered, and a rear-sole-plate question](/boats/johanna.md#water-tanks-reconsidered-and-a-rear-sole-plate-question)), Bill set out his own bin-and-floor system in fuller technical detail than before: the side bin sides bolt to lengths of aluminium "L" section screwed to fixed side shelves, letting wooden ledges along the bottom of the bin sides support the outer edges of two floors laid over the storage area beneath; rather than the plans' hinges, each floor simply lifts out and slides forward or sideways for access, a method that could extend forward to give access to water-bottle stowage too. The small removable sole plates either side of the water tanks, visible in his own build photos, give access to parts of the boat otherwise closed to inspection. Bolting the bin sides to the aluminium angle, rather than screwing them in place as the plans show, lets them be removed for maintenance and better withstands the loads they can see in a gale, when a crew needs something solid to hang onto without being thrown about.

Given the choice again, Bill would not build in water tanks at all: the frame structure is strong enough without fixed soles over the tank area, and he would instead fit loose floors held down by a clasp so they stay in place through a full capsize — the same system already used for his food-storage compartments.

On the rear-of-cabin-trunk washboard question also raised in the same thread, Bill had originally opposed the idea on structural and waterproofing grounds, but after a season actually sailing "Faith" came round to it: sitting on the rear deck with legs dangling into the cabin would be comfortable when conditions allow, and getting in and out would be easier too. His one condition: washboard sides should be cut vertical, so the board cannot work its way out of the side slots without a full, deliberate vertical lift — citing the infamous Fastnet Race, in which several yachts lost their washboards to exactly that failure mode.[226]

The thread closed on a lighter note: countering a closing remark that joining the Paradox Club takes "at least a little" madness, one correspondent suggested membership might instead make a sailor "a little more sane."[227] A further reply agreed the combination is itself the paradox — being mad and sane at the same time — and judged a touch of self-centred eccentricity close to a membership requirement.[228]

## Tools used building Faith, revisited

Answering a recurring "Tools" question, Bill reported having used to date neither a table saw nor a bandsaw on Faith, though both would have been useful; his own build relied on an ordinary rip saw followed by hand planing and sanding, and an electric hand-held jigsaw for shaping every piece of plywood, with the mast, boom, and yuloh all cut and formed with the rip saw. ## Sheer clamp, bulkhead #1, and mast support: Bill's own choices

Asked why one boat's sheer clamp, visible in build photos, was notched roughly 500 mm from the stern rather than running the sheer's full length, the answer given elsewhere confirmed the cutout clears the steering-line lead blocks, which run at sheer height (see [interior bin panels and hinged floor](/components/interior.md#design-intent-watertight-bulkhead-opening-is-optional) and [sheer strakes](/components/sheer-strakes.md) for the fuller technical answer). On whether to leave bulkhead #1 open or solid, Bill explained it as a safety feature that limits how far floodwater can spread in a wild seaway — going further, a genuinely watertight hatch or a fully sealed forward section, would be a stronger though more involved option. Faith carries **no added buoyancy** and instead relies on being built strong to the plans throughout; Bill kept bulkhead #1 solid as drawn, unlike Alastair, who cut into his and backed the opening with foam buoyancy. Bill separately fits an additional mast support at the top of the vent box — a piece of wood glued fore-and-aft on the **starboard** side — a detail he believes Alastair shares.[93]

## Fibreglassing the hull, and fairing woven roving before painting

Bill fully encapsulated Faith's hull with woven roving and epoxy over what he called "a brilliant week for boatbuilding," leaving the surface needing to be smoothed before painting could begin. On finishing fibreglass sheathing generally, his own past boats had been finished with plastic padding filler, which he rated easy to sand to a good result.[97] Epoxy with micro-balloons, also easy to sand, is a second, comparably good option — the choice Bill judged the likely cheaper of the two, having by then already made the beams for Faith's cabin top and hatch and wanting a build-time comparison before starting them himself.[98][99] Al's own cabin-top-and-hatch woodwork, on "Little Jim," was finished and ready for painting in early March after starting the front and rear deck-block lamination the preceding January, with the hatch itself needing a fair amount of fiddling to get it running smoothly; that boat launched the following April.[100][101] Bill targeted an afloat date of beginning June for Faith, reckoning being retired would let him put in good hours whenever the weather and workspace cooperated.[101] Building through an unheated garage in January and February, as Al had, meant few working hours a day and epoxy that was slow to go off — a cure-time caution Bill weighed as he pushed toward his own early-summer target.[102]

Faith's boom and sail drew a further exchange once the fibreglassing news reached the list: Bill judged his own Mk1-design sail worked well despite having little else to compare it against, and had cured a persistent crease running from about a third of the way along the yard to just above the boom's end by tightening the end ties, having originally suspected the sail needed recutting; he had hoped to compare notes against "Enuf" once her sail was properly worked out.[104] Bill bought his own sail secondhand from Glen Maxwell — Glen's "improved," higher-peaked design, which Matt Layden judges more efficient to windward, though Bill preferred the classic sail's neater stowed profile on the side deck.[106] Asking Glen directly whether the newer Mark II sail Glen sailed with instead was really worth an extra half-knot to windward in a force 3, Bill drew instead a technical answer on why the old sail (now his) had never been a fair comparison: a yard-curvature-measuring method and the story of Matt Layden reading the required 2 in of curve by eye. ## Progress photos draw a yuloh-plans question, and a curve correction

Fresh photos of Faith's progress drew a question from a newcomer who had bought the plans but could not find the yuloh drawing among them.[108] Bill's answer pointed to the yuloh diagram on **plans sheet #12**, on its right-hand side, and passed along a correction from his own build: he had failed to put the plans' specified curve into his yuloh's shaft, and the drawing's stated **30 mm longitudinal concave curve** measures closer to **50 mm** against sheet #3, a discrepancy also raised and answered at length in his own March 2007 "Yuloh" thread — see [yuloh and stowed-rig retaining loops — blade camber, the "for'd" nomenclature, and pivot socket dimensions](/components/yuloh.md#blade-camber-the-ford-nomenclature-and-pivot-socket-dimensions). Bill leaned toward the larger curve for his own oar, judging it would fit the deck better once stowed and would give a higher gear in use.[109]

## Comparing the rolling-boom furl to a junk rig's single-line drop

A junk rig lowers and stows in seconds by releasing a single line, letting the sail fold neatly into lazy jacks and stopping the boat almost immediately — a standard against which Paradox's window-shade furling looks, at first glance, more involved, since taking up on one line while gradually releasing another cannot be as fast as dropping a junk sail in one motion. In practice the stock gear is not this fumbling: Bill Serjeant, watching a fellow UK builder work his own boom furling gear, judged it looked "as easy as Matt Layden the designer himself" makes it look, crediting genuine unfamiliarity — a first attempt at Beale Park Boat Show went badly, the sail simply refusing to furl correctly — rather than any real flaw in the mechanism, and expected to get the hang of it with practice.[114][115]

The exchange also drew out Bill's broader verdict on Paradox as he built her: primarily a **one-person cruiser**, though a child and an adult could sail together with some comfort; the boat "looks after herself" with very little attention needed once under way on open water, with no acrobatics required to handle her, and different cushions are worth carrying aboard for varied seating options. Launching and recovery call for a trailer built to the specific boat rather than an off-the-shelf unit — see [trailering](/operations/trailering.md) for the fuller trailer-specification record — with the wheel hubs on a properly sized trailer never getting wet except in rain.[115]

## A yuloh pivot socket, mainsheet snagging, and a cruise plan toward the Scilly Isles

The fresh yuloh-plans photos also drew a follow-up on the pivot itself: whether to use the plans' ball-joint pivot or a plain oarlock, the way Matt Layden sculls in a hosted video clip. The ball joint was confirmed as the choice, since sculling and yulohing are different actions — a builder who already knew how to scull instinctively still made "a hopeless attempt" trying a fellow owner's yuloh at the Beale Boat Show, and had to accept the yulohing technique needed learning on its own terms.[116][117] A counter-view held that oarlocks actually suit sculling *better*, reasoning from an ideal sculling oar's blade curving up at the tip to enhance the sweep's natural twist, against the yuloh's opposite, downward curve — with hull weight, transom height, and stowage still favoring the yuloh specifically for Paradox.[118] For the fuller technical record of ball-joint-versus-oarlock mechanics, pivot-socket construction, and wear over time, see [yuloh — ball-joint versus oarlock, and pivot-socket wear over time](/components/yuloh.md#ball-joint-versus-oarlock-and-pivot-socket-wear-over-time).[116][117][118][119][120][121][122][123][124][125]

Progress on Faith's own yuloh brought a longitudinal curve worked into the shaft by wedge, and an open question on how best to build the pivot socket — resolved over the following exchange into a shortlist of socket materials (tufnol, copper pipe, stainless tube, graphite-loaded epoxy), each with its own wear and repair tradeoffs, cross-linked above.[119][120][121][123][124] The same exchange also settled a mainsheet-fouling question: the sheet can drop into the gap between the rudder stock and transom and catch on the yuloh's pivot ball during ordinary sail adjustments, the same fouling problem already documented for the rudder stock generally (see [design/rudder.md — rudder-stock height and mainsheet fouling at the transom notch](/components/rudder.md#rudder-stock-height-and-mainsheet-fouling-at-the-transom-notch)), and a wooden pad fitted atop the pintle bracket gives the sheet a smooth lead clear of the sharp metal fitting.[119][120] The follow-up on gybing technique that grew out of this exchange — sheeting hard in on a heavy boat versus a junk sail's full-swing release — is carried in full at [yuloh — ball-joint versus oarlock](/components/yuloh.md#ball-joint-versus-oarlock-and-pivot-socket-wear-over-time).[122]

Bill separately raised where to stow a kedge anchor: keeping it on the stern deck risked the mainsheet snagging it, while the foredeck kept mud and weed from being tracked below but meant going forward to anchor, impractical under some conditions. The answer identified a roughly triangular area clear of the mainsheet, running from the cabin's rear to the rudder, as a candidate spot — tried in practice for mounting a Bruce anchor there without ever finding a neat fitting method, though the same area doubles usefully as a place to stand boarding or leaving the boat; going forward to anchor under sail was judged the worse of the two options, since reaching the foredeck safely with the sail up is difficult, and it is sometimes better to have at least part of the sail up before the anchor comes up at all.[125] Bill agreed the stern-deck triangle would be his preference for the kedge if a neat mounting could be found, even at some cost to getting in and out of the boat, and confirmed the plan to reach the West Country by late June, on to Plymouth and then Falmouth by mid-July, and, weather permitting, across to the Isles of Scilly by mid-to-late July — a trip he "really hoped" to make together. Falmouth as the departure point for Scilly again drew mild surprise, Penzance being the more expected jumping-off point, but Bill's own plan was to hop first from Falmouth to Newlyn to rest, then make the full day's crossing from there to St Mary's in the Scillies.[126]

For the socket-material suggestions this same exchange drew — lignum vitae, a stainless ball in a copper cup, Acetal/UHMW, and a teflon-coated ball — see [yuloh — further candidate socket materials](/components/yuloh.md#ball-joint-versus-oarlock-and-pivot-socket-wear-over-time).[127][128][129][130][131][132][133]

With the technical questions settled, Bill firmed up his cruising plan for "Faith" once launched: sail from Exmouth to Dartmouth and back during the last week of June, then trail to Plymouth for a cruise up the Tamar and back, on to the Yealm, and back to Plymouth; from there trail to Falmouth for a few days on the Fal estuary and, weather permitting, cross to the Isles of Scilly by mid-July, returning to Falmouth by the end of July. He invited Alastair to join any part of the trip, noting the Dinghy Cruising Association might be holding West Country meetings around the same time.[119] Two DCA meetings — on the Tamar 2 June and the Exe 16 June — would just be missed by that schedule, but the crossing to Scilly itself was one he expected to be able to join for. Falmouth as the jumping-off point for Scilly drew mild surprise, Penzance being the more expected departure point — but the plan held regardless, with the closing word of confidence: "I have faith in you."[122]

## The cabin trunk roof, and building the hatch

Days after cutting the cabin top panels, Bill fitted Faith's cabin trunk roof and the runners and transverse pieces for the hatch, with building the hatch itself the next task.[135] A window is worth fitting into the hatch, since seeing the sail from inside while sailing is otherwise difficult; one recorded technique assembles the hatch first and cuts the window aperture afterward, avoiding the risk of the plywood bending crooked if the aperture is cut before the hatch is glued up.[136]

Bill started the hatch by attaching its sides, finding them out of true by a full metre before bending and glassing both sides the next day — a discrepancy not expected to cause a problem — and made his own runners from teak scraps sourced at the Beale Boat Show, close to but not exactly matching Matt Layden's own drawn dimensions.[137] The out-of-true sides drew a dry aside that he had built the hatch triangular rather than rectangular; assembling a curved hatch is straightforward in principle, needing mainly a clear, unobstructed run at the bend and, ideally, a smaller audience.[138] Since no sea state on the inland lake where "Little Jim" had been sailed and demonstrated could plausibly have damaged a Paradox, there had been no real anxiety in lending the boat out for a novice's sail, however clumsily handled — that toughness gives a Paradox owner confidence and relaxation not available in a more fragile boat.[138]

## Cutting the polycarbonate windows

With the hatch trial-fitted successfully — "more by luck than judgement" — Bill turned to shaping Faith's windows and asked whether a fine, medium, or coarse-tooth blade cuts polycarbonate best.[139] Coarse teeth, run at a slow jigsaw speed, is the answer: a fine blade run fast melts the plastic swarf into a lump that clogs the blade instead of clearing it.[140] With the windows and hatch done, the tasks still remaining were stowage racks for the yuloh and sail, painting the deck, fitting the deck hardware, turning the hull on the lawn to apply Coppercoat antifouling (rated for roughly ten years), and painting the topsides and remaining bare wood — order not yet fixed — after which only rigging stood between Faith and the water; Bill still expected three weeks or more before launch, weather permitting.[141]

## Faith's transom camber, and a pine cleat test for Pete Martin

Fresh photographs of Faith's back and side windows in place, with the front window expected the following day, drew a comparison from a builder just starting real cutting on his own boat.[142] Answering that builder's transom-camber question, a photograph of Faith's own transom shows 5 mm of curvature worked into both edges, matching bulkhead 4's specified camber — judged an improvement to the boat's appearance, though a perfectly straight-sided transom, as drawn, remains a workable alternative for a builder who prefers it. All of Faith's own deck beams are ply. For interior cleat wood, a simple field test settles pine against Douglas fir: drive a bronze ring nail of the size intended into a sample piece and see whether it splits — pre-drilling the pine first is essential for a fair trial, since a nail driven without a pilot hole is likely to split even wood that would otherwise be fine.[143][144]

## Trailer braking, and a firmed-up West Country cruise schedule

Because Paradox is heavy for her size, her trailer ideally needs auto brakes, but the secondhand trailer bought from Derek Clark (see [antifouling before a month-long mooring, and the "Enuf" trailer bought secondhand](#antifouling-before-a-month-long-mooring-and-the-enuf-trailer-bought-secondhand) above) has none — a possible later conversion, complicated by the risk of brakes fouling when immersed for launch and recovery; a purpose-built trailer avoids the problem by never submerging its axles at all, but costs more to build the heavier a trailer needs to be. Cutting the surplus glass cloth from around a chine runner's edge while the glass is still "green" (not fully cured) — running a knife around the edge using the underside as a guide — saves a lot of later work and can be done neatly; combined with a PeelPly finish over the glass, it leaves a smooth, rounded edge.

A rough schedule to meet up with "[Jeff UK](/people/jeff-doryman.md)" and NOLI, and [Alastair](/people/alastair-law.md), was set for the Falmouth/Newlyn/Penzance area around mid-July, kept loose and coordinated as each boat's own progress allowed, with a further, later hope to meet up again in Poole Harbour or the Solent later in the season. ## A 22 mm vent trunk drain proven in a brief force 6

## Faith afloat at Burnham Marina, and a first sea trial

Days after launch, photographs of Faith afloat at Burnham Marina were posted, with the boat due out of the water again ahead of a West Country cruise starting toward the end of June.[181] Asked how the sail had performed, Bill reported putting Faith through two days of trials to check her handling.[182] On calm, smooth water the first day the boat sailed flawlessly, and gave the chance to work on yuloh technique: sculling in a figure-of-eight, the way a punt is sculled, did not work, but moving the shaft horizontally and twisting it at the end of each stroke did generate power — a technique that has its own dedicated write-up at [sculling and yuloh technique — a figure-eight stroke does not work; a horizontal twist does](/operations/sculling-and-yuloh-technique.md#a-figure-eight-stroke-does-not-work-a-horizontal-twist-does).[182]

The second day's wind, Force 3 to 4 gusting 5, brought the real sailing test and a first tacking failure: changing from starboard to port tack, Faith stalled head-to-wind between breaking waves roughly her own length apart and drifted back; reversing the rudder only put her back onto starboard tack, and only freeing the sheet, deliberately practised over several more attempts, let her complete the tack — feathering the sail this way to avoid weathercocking the boat, a technique shared with sailing a junk rig. Paradox does not spin on a sixpence and needs to be sailed through a gentle curve to keep her way on. Over-canvassed on purpose to test her stability, Faith heeled to 30 degrees in the Force 5 gusts without any anxiety about capsizing. Sailing with the hatch closed made oilskins unnecessary even in drizzle, with the hatch window giving a clear view of the sail; the cabin layout worked well in practice, food stored in unhinged under-floor boxes reachable by sliding them forward while seated, and a cup of tea could be made from the seat in about three minutes without moving. The chart table, stowed forward of bulkhead 3, could be set up either there or in front of the crew, though laying the chart on the floor was preferred in practice; the compass, mounted on its own gimbal, does not intrude on the cabin and can be read from anywhere inside. Two days of sailing were judged a clear success, drawing many questions from curious spectators ashore — a reception any Paradox owner should expect at nearly every launch and recovery.[182][183][184][185][186]

The blog's own contemporaneous log adds specifics from those first days out. Sailing the day after launch in a light north-easterly, Bill worked through all points of sail and the yuloh, tried the boat with the hatch closed in drizzle — the forward window "slightly fogged up" but the cabin stayed "snug and dry" — and found she would hold a course for minutes at a stretch without a hand on the steering line.[268] Choosing the Crouch over the Walton Backwaters for the next day's test came down to tide timing rather than distance — reaching the Backwaters needed 4 knots to clear Stone Banks off the Naze before the flood swept the boat toward Clacton, with no slack left against the following day's forecast of SW 6 — and that sail taught Bill to raise the rudder before anchoring by the stern, to keep the anchor rode clear of it, and to reef promptly rather than let the boat hobby-horse over-canvassed as wind and tide built off Creeksea.[269]

## Hints on gluing up the hull, a single two-hour session

Answering a newcomer's worry over how to glue up a Paradox hull without losing symmetry, Faith's own experience gave a full worked method: get a good dry fit first, with the major nail holes drilled and the boat checked square, temporary cleats/blocks screwed to the side panels at each bulkhead station and sheathed in plastic bin liner so they do not stick once epoxy goes on. A combination of straps, ropes, clamps, and shaped wooden blocks springs the bow and stern sections into place. Fit the side panels to bulkhead 3 first, then 2, then 4 — gluing and nailing each before moving on — then 1 and the transom, and the stem last; the whole of Faith's hull went together this way in one session of about two hours. Because symmetry had already been established at the dry-fit stage, the wet assembly went into place automatically, confirmed with a centreline check needing no adjustment. See [hull assembly sequence — gluing up the hull](/construction/hull-assembly-sequence.md#gluing-up-the-hull-one-sitting-or-several-and-locating-the-bulkheads-with-temporary-pins) for the full technique, and a complementary staged-assembly method using temporary wire nails as locating pins.[197]

## Sheeting like a junk sail to windward, and a second topping-lift fix

Faith's topping lift, once slack, can let its aft end catch in the furling sail and snarl the furling drum; keeping the lift taut while the sail rolls, and making sure the yard does not foul the wrong side of the lift while hoisting, avoids the problem — on balance, the benefits of carrying a topping lift outweigh this drawback.[205] Sheeting in too hard going to windward stalls the boat; the fix is to sheet the standing lugsail the way a junk sail is trimmed, with the clew held over the leeward quarter (the point where the transom meets the hull side) so the wind runs along the sail at a shallow angle of incidence, unlike a Bermudan rig.[206]

## Plymouth to Exmouth, then 140 miles on to Essex

Faith and Little Jim made the passage from **Plymouth to Exmouth** together, logged at `bills-log.blogspot.com`.[207] This leg was the opening stage of the pair's wider West Country cruise (see [a 140-mile cruise eastward from Falmouth, with Little Jim](#a-140-mile-cruise-eastward-from-falmouth-with-little-jim) above), which the two boats went on to complete together, sailing 140 miles from Falmouth back toward Essex without reaching the Scilly Isles.

Faith's own blog carries this leg in full circumstantial detail. Leaving a quiet anchorage near Millbrook Creek shortly after 0830 on 17 July, against a forecast SW 5/6 decreasing 3–4, the two boats felt the southerly's full force crossing Plymouth Sound, held a course past the eastern breakwater, tacked outside the Mewstone off the Yealm, and bore off to windward of Udder Rock into a swell heavy enough that "for seconds at a time we could not see our companion boat," the wind building to force 5 and 6 with no sign of easing.[270] Off Bolt Head and Bolt Tail the spring ebb made the seas "more awesome than we had expected," squalls shooting over the hilltops and down the valleys to lay both boats on their sides as they scooted up the estuary to Salcombe; sails came down and the yulohs took the boats alongside the public pontoon.[270] That evening's plan to settle for a quiet night on the beach at Splat Cove went wrong: surge from the still-boisterous wind bounced the boats on the sand, and at 0400 Bill had to move the anchors further up the beach so both craft could be pulled clear as the tide returned; by 0700 they were afloat again, sculling and paddling out past Salcombe's sandbar into the near-calm that followed.[270] Off Brixham the ebb ran stronger than expected before the boats worked north to gain the harbour and a Sailing Club mooring. Leaving Brixham the next, becalmed morning, Bill found his yuloh's pivot hole had not been cut wide enough to let the blade rotate properly, and paddled slowly in Little Jim's wake rather than sculling — "I had not made the pivot hole in the shaft sufficiently wide to allow me to rotate the blade properly." Off Teignmouth and Dawlish the boats crossed the shifting Pole Sands to save nearly three miles, both grounding as the tide ebbed out from under them and waiting out the flood at anchor before the last short run into Exmouth.[270]

## Two days of force 5 off Bolt Head, and the tacking fix confirmed again

Once home from the Plymouth–Exmouth leg, Bill modified his yuloh's pivot socket so it swivels fully side-to-side as well as in the vertical plane, and raised the height of the ball, matching a modification already proven on Little Jim's own yuloh; Little Jim's owner had used the yuloh throughout the cruise and found it perfectly adequate, never once needing the electric outboard stowed in the forecastle, and outpaced Bill when the latter tried to keep up by paddling instead.[208]

The liveliest sailing of the whole cruise came off **Bolt Head and Bolt Tail**, working into **Salcombe** in roughly a force 5 with a big swell; confused seas bounced back off the cliffs, at times hiding each boat from the other when both dropped into troughs, but both boats handled the conditions impeccably, with no anxiety aboard either one — a demonstration of the design's buoyancy and ease of handling under real pressure.

Based on that experience, Paradox should ride out a **force 8 lying a-hull** — uncomfortable for the crew, but safe; anything stronger, especially running downwind, is better met tracking slightly off dead downwind with a long warp trailed astern. This firsthand judgment, from a boat actually sailed hard rather than merely surveyed at the dock, adds a second British-built, British-sailed data point to the lying-a-hull question tracked at [heavy-weather tactics — a British Isles sailor's case for lying a-hull, and Paradox's flat bottom](/operations/heavy-weather-tactics.md#a-british-isles-sailors-case-for-lying-a-hull-and-paradoxs-flat-bottom).[209]

The one recurring idiosyncrasy found across the cruise, likely common to all Paradoxes, is slow tacking in piled-up seas: from starboard to port tack she can stick head-to-wind. The fix is to reverse the rudder so she pays off onto the new tack, helped by releasing the sheet at head-to-wind and by a crew member moving forward into the cabin and sitting to starboard; in any smooth or smoothish sea she tacks normally regardless. Apart from that one quirk, Faith performed brilliantly across the cruise — arguably the best boat her owner has ever sailed.[209]

## Video evidence: an easily driven hull with almost no wake

New photographs and video of Faith from the West Country cruise were posted to `www.smallsailboats.co.uk`, credited to Al Law, viewable via links on the site's Editorial page.[210] Asked to estimate the windspeed shown in the first video, given the pace the boat appeared to be making, the answer was a **Force 2 to 3 Beaufort**, judged from the state of the water alone.[211] Paradox is an easily driven vessel: her heavy displacement carries her way well once moving, her wetted area is small enough to keep drag low, and her narrow beam reduces resistance to forward motion. ## Comparing Faith to an earlier Bermudan-sloop dinghy, "Harriott"

Faith invited comparison against an earlier 14 ft Bermudan sloop, **"Harriott,"** a centreboard cruising dinghy 2 in longer than Faith with a beam of about 5.5 ft, a displacement of 650 lb (56 lb of removable lead ballast plus an 80 lb iron centreplate), and 96 sq ft of sail (105 sq ft with the genoa set) — dependent on her centreboard for windward performance, unable to sail efficiently to windward in under 4 ft of water at her 3.4 ft board-down draught. Faith, by contrast, is a little shorter and narrower (just over 4 ft beam) with only 9 in of draught, sails to windward in 2 ft of water heeling around 14 degrees, carries a much heavier 1,410 lb displacement (397 lb lead ballast plus 154 lb water ballast), and — with only 100 sq ft of sail — is slower than a Roamer dinghy. Both boats will self-right, but Faith's enormous stability and lesser sail area make her much the more forgiving of the two.

Paradox's advantages over an open cruising dinghy: the crew is never exposed to the elements, with no tent to erect at night — rain or spray on deck simply means closing the hatch and sailing from within; there is no centreplate to jam; the boat should not capsize except in the severest conditions, and even then should self-right, as should a Roamer, though Faith is clearly the boat to be in under such conditions; and there is much more usable stowage space aboard. A Roamer-type dinghy has four countervailing advantages: she points closer to windward, is faster in light air, can be sailed by more than one person at a time, and can carry an outboard without the engine fouling the mainsheet. Both boats sit similarly when dried out except that a Roamer does not sit level, and both trail behind an ordinary family car; building a Paradox takes longer, at about the same material, sail, and rigging cost.

Paradox is better described as a **single-handed miniature yacht** than as a dinghy, and is the preferred boat for the kind of sailing enjoyed aboard Faith, despite her lack of an engine and her slowness tacking, especially in short, choppy seas — better suited to open water, coastal cruising in particular, than to narrow rivers or small lakes. Learning to handle the yuloh took a while; in strong winds it is effectively unusable, and steering by yuloh calls for raising the rudder and balancing the strokes against whatever part of the blade remains in the water. Choosing between the two boat types comes down to whether a second crew member is wanted, where the sailing will be done, and whether an engine is really needed.[217]

## The Cobnor week: a Dinghy Cruising Association meeting, and falling overboard

In mid-August 2007 Faith joined the Dinghy Cruising Association for a week's meeting in Chichester Harbour. Launched at Emsworth Marina on 17 August, she needed a passing yacht's tow clear of a backwater where wind and current stopped the yuloh from making headway, then sailed a clear close reach down the Emsworth Channel and on toward Cobnor Point — too heavy, at over a Wayfarer dinghy's weight, to use the Cobnor Activities Centre's own restricted slipway, and so anchored instead a cable or so off it, with no other boats yet arrived.[271] A wet, gusty Saturday was spent sheltering under the closed hatch, watching the Centre's young sailors work the harbour in conditions that kept Faith herself ashore;[272] the following afternoon she got out among the DCA fleet on a light nor'westerly, "matching" the smaller dinghies present on a reach, if not quite to windward.[273]

On the fourth day, 20 August, a good force 4 north wind sent Faith "flying" back down the harbour toward East Head; trying to anchor off the Cobnor beach as the tide turned to ebb, wind and current carried her off her intended spot, and neither the yuloh nor paddling from the foredeck could correct it — Bill went over the side instead. He had forgotten his buoyancy aid, and two sweaters and an anorak, waterlogged, left him "floundering." He held onto the boat, worked his way to the stern, and — "with an enormous effort" — hauled himself aboard using the boarding step built into the rudder stock, surprised both by the weight the soaked clothing had taken on and by how hard it was to lever himself out of the water. He beached that evening to rinse the clothes ashore, found his lost cap and a shoe on the beach, but never recovered his spectacles, and read charts and text with a magnifying glass for the rest of the trip.[274] Retold five years later, the same account adds that the incident settled Bill's later preference for a snug-fitting buoyancy aid over a lifejacket when working the boat — easier to swim in, even though a lifejacket would right an unconscious wearer that a buoyancy aid will not.[275]

The rest of the week ran less eventfully. A near-gale force 8 was ridden out safely at anchor on good holding ground.[276] Beached near the slipway on another day, Faith ground audibly over sharp flint — feared at the time to have damaged the hull, but a later inspection found "no discernable wear" — and Bill drew his own verdict on the design from the week: "Paradox can work to windward, but not handily as a Wayfarer or a Mirror dinghy... The boat is very strong and she can take the ground; she can be sealed from the elements to provide relative comfort. With food and drink available it does not matter if plans are changed by the weather."[277] Nearing neaps, with the tidal range down to 1.5 m, she grounded briefly on the sill at the entrance to Emsworth Yacht Harbour, confirming at least 1 ft 6 in of water is needed to cross it safely, and was retrieved on her trailer that evening.[278] Her mast jammed while being unstepped and was only freed the next morning: rainwater draining down through the deck ventilation pipe, exactly as designed, had swollen the wood at its base — "a simple matter to rectify" once home, after more than seven days and nights afloat on the trip.[279]

## A spring 2008 cruise begins, weatherbound at Ramsgate

A new cruise got under way in early April 2008, logged as before at `bills-log.blogspot.com` (mirrored through `www.smallsailboats.co.uk`), uploaded only where a WIFI hotspot could be found.[230] Five days out, the cruise was weatherbound at Ramsgate with snow in the forecast — met with a Woolworths hot water bottle as the trump card against the cold.[230] The report drew outside interest beyond the usual builder circle: one reader followed the blog daily, tracking the reported positions on an atlas, impressed by the distance already covered and asking whether a 48-hour non-stop passage might be attempted if wind and weather turned favourable, and whether Faith could hold a steady course long enough for her skipper to get a nap underway;[232] another, a West Wight Potter owner following along from the San Francisco Bay area, admitted the blog had him seriously weighing a small-boat cruise of his own.[233] Cruise-blogging technique drew a question of its own — see [Dave Bolduc — cruise blogging](/people/bolduc/dave.md#operations-and-cruising-practice) for the full exchange.[231]

The voyage also prompted a lighter digression on what luxury means for a boat this small, opened by a question about the laptop being used to post updates and a wish that a microcruiser had room for "a wine cellar and a hot tub."[234] A wine-bag mount built into a side bin, giving wine "on tap," was offered as a realistic middle ground — worth the trouble for a glass or two at anchor watching the sun set.[235] The exchange widened into an explicit rejection of the old, deliberately spartan school of small-boat cruising in favour of comfort pursued in miniature — harder to achieve aboard a microcruiser, and so judged sweeter once achieved — closing with the open question of what actually constitutes luxury on a boat this size.[236] The heating side of an answer to that question — a coal-stove precedent and a proposed miniature heater design — is cited in full at [cabin heating](/operations/cabin-heating.md#a-charcoal-or-kerosene-can-heater-and-the-transom-cook-stove-alternative).[237]

Following the cruise from a distance drew its own tips: satellite imagery was recommended as a way to pinpoint Ramsgate and its marina precisely and trace the rest of the route in detail sharp enough to make out people, cars, and boats,[238] while a reader still on a dial-up connection found an ordinary paper atlas no real hardship, content to fill in the missing detail by imagination.[239]

Whether a fellow builder's own nearly finished boat would be launched in time to cross paths with Faith along her route was raised as a related question.[240] The same digression drew firsthand comment on what luxury actually means aboard a small cruiser — contrasting a leaky, aptly named open boat from decades earlier with the wine, good books, and phone connectivity of present-day cruising; full account at [Dave Bolduc — early cruising and today's comforts](/people/bolduc/dave.md#biography).[241] The launch-timing question was answered with a construction-progress update — hull painting under way, a sail on order from a Cowes sailmaker, Perspex cut for the side and rear windows, and a late-June/early-July target still standing — full account at [Jeff (UK) — painting begins, and a spring 2008 launch target restated](/people/jeff-doryman.md#painting-begins-and-a-spring-2008-launch-target-restated).[242] Further online video was flagged for the benefit of a reader still on dial-up — Faith under sail in company with a fellow UK builder, plus separate footage of Matt Layden's Enigma and of Little Cruiser — and the same exchange drew anticipation for photographs of the nearly finished boat's own build and eventual launch.[243][244]

## The primary log: Hullbridge to Ramsgate, then Dover and a first lock at Eastbourne

Faith's own blog fills in the passage the mailing-list account only summarises. Launched at the Hullbridge Ferry Road slipway on 2 April 2008, she crossed the Thames from Maplin Sands by "ferry gliding" against the ebb, passing a wind farm of "at least 30" generators "in regimented rows" near the Red Sand Tower, and anchored that night in the River Swale; by 3 April the Honda 3.2 outboard was "purring away," a full one-litre tank lasting two hours at an average 3 knots, and by 4 April she had rounded North Foreland to reach Ramsgate.[280] Leaving Ramsgate on 7 April against the tide, "'Faith' dug her lee chine in deep; spray spattered my spectacles," and the engine went in near Deal once the current began pushing her off course.[281] Two days later, motoring the whole way from Dover to Eastbourne in a flat calm — "none of this would have been possible with the Honda 2.3, which I've named Sherpa" — she rounded Dungeness under a gun-range launch's orders to hold course 240°, then locked through into Sovereign Harbour for the first time in her career; the petrol cap, lost overboard off Newhaven, was replaced on the spot with a scrap of plastic and a length of cord.[282] A further day was lost at Brighton Marina waiting for a proper replacement cap, during which a swollen cabin floorboard was eased where it had begun to stick.[283]

## Gale-bound at Brighton, then round to Lymington on an ailing engine

A gale then pinned Faith into Brighton Marina for several days; moved to a quieter berth behind a motor yacht, she needed no ship's-battery charging thanks to "the effective solar panel," though the VHF and a phone were topped up from shore power.[284] Clear at last, she rounded Selsey Bill in a rising force 5 against the tide — "water frequently crashed over the boat, but I remained dry inside the cabin" — motored into Chichester Harbour after dark to dry out at anchor, and left again on the rising tide ("getting out of Chichester Harbour against the incoming flood tide was only possible because of the engine"); the outboard failed on the final approach to Lymington, but a controlled sail under reduced canvas still brought her in at "1300, which must be a record for the boat, taking only 5 hours and 40 minutes."[285]

Storm-bound then at Lymington for two more days, the outboard's reluctance to start was traced by a local mechanic to fuel bought at Brighton and an incorrectly sold spark plug; both were put right.[286] Leaving on 19 April, the engine failed again almost at once, so Faith held close inshore past the Shingles Bank — "the grave of many unwary sailors" — under sail; a strong ebb, then a dead calm that had her sailing backwards, forced a diversion into Poole Harbour's South Deep Channel to anchor off Goathorn Point, and the engine failed once more two days later, "possibly due to fuel starvation," leaving a shallow, engineless passage east of Furzey Island in only 3 ft of water — "my Honda had become a temperamental machine willing only to work when it decided."[287] The following day the engine ran cleanly throughout, skirting the Lulworth firing range between its danger buoys under a Lowrance GPS's guidance, to reach Weymouth; a further day there was lost to a strong westerly.[287] A local mechanic's tip — find a compression load, then pull the cord smartly — cured the starting trouble for good, and on 25 April Faith rounded Portland Bill timed against the tide to dodge the Race, a wave briefly knocking the outboard clear of the water mid-passage, before a 33-mile crossing of Lyme Bay brought her to the Exe by 2130 that night.[288]

## West Country: Dartmouth, Salcombe, and Plymouth for the Transat start

Leaving Brixham on 28 April in a freshening force 5, "sometimes wave tops swept the deck and crashed into the cabin front, but I was dry, warm and secure below"; the engine ran out of fuel entering the Dart and Faith was towed the last stretch to a mooring.[289] After a few days at Dartmouth — where unused spare clothing, carried just in case, was noted as helping trim the boat to her waterline — she rounded Start Point for Salcombe on 2 May.[289] A week on, at Calstock on the River Tamar, "I spent a very uncomfortable night because the boat settled at an angle on the sloping mud," and a second failed spark plug was swapped before she reached Plymouth.[290] From there she made for the River Yealm, her 9 in draught letting her cross the bar without difficulty while two deeper-drafted yachts waited outside for water — "without the engine this cruise would not have been possible."[291] At Plymouth Yacht Haven, waiting for Little Jim to arrive, Faith recorded her best sailing of the whole cruise off Mount Batten Pier, "speed to windward on average 3.5 knots" and "the boat consistently sail[ing] herself"; she watched the start of the Artemis Transat from anchor off Cawsand on 11 May, and the following day Little Jim was launched at Mount Batten to join her.[292]

## To the Scilly Isles and back, with Little Jim

The two boats crossed to Tresco on 18 May, "both boats scraping their rudders on the golden sand" over the shallows between Samson and Crow Point on a falling tide, and anchored where "apart from our Paradoxes there was only one yacht at a mooring."[293] A single 190 g Gaz canister lasted eight days aboard — "very good," since "mostly they last a week" — and the same questions greeted the boats at every anchorage: "How old is the boat?" and "How far have you come?"[294] A severe gale warning for 26 May sent Faith's anchors down fore and aft at New Grimsby; as the rudder touched sand, Bill jumped from the stern to reset the anchor higher up the beach ahead of the blow, and she was aground within minutes, the wind not easing until midnight.[295] Three days later the pair crossed to St Agnes, landing at a rocky lee shore by dropping one anchor off and sculling to a second set in a crevice above the waterline, then crossed to Newlyn on 30 May entirely under power in a dead calm.[295] Rounding the Lizard the next day, "the Honda 2.3 continued to purr all the way to the Helford River"; days later, hugging the cliffs near Polperro, "I suppose the shallow draft of Paradox gave me the confidence to do so," and a plastic bag fouling the propeller off Looe Island cleared easily, "which wouldn't have been the case with a fixed propeller." From Fowey to Plymouth the little outboard towed both boats — Little Jim included — some 23 miles in just over 8 hours at nearly 3 knots.[295] Hauled out at Queen Anne's Battery on 4 June, unstepping the mast and emptying the water ballast for the road took 45 minutes, and the drive home took 8 hours with three stops — closing a cruise of nearly two months and some 800 miles from the River Crouch that reached its main objective, the Scilly Isles, even though the Jester Azores Challenge start at Plymouth was missed to weather at the very end of May.[296]

## Appraising Faith: dimensions, ballast, and handling, in retrospect

Fully ballasted, Faith displaced 1,410 lb — 397 lb of lead and 154 lb of water — on a hull just over 4 ft in beam; her shallow 9 in draught let her sail to windward in 2 ft of water, and, despite a flat and heavily rockered bottom, she was self-righting and would sit upright unsupported on a beach. The build itself ran from the plywood's delivery in August 2005 to her launch in June 2007 — some twenty-two months, not the "year and a half" Bill's own retrospective accounts sometimes gave.[297] Loaded for a fortnight's cruise, and before the yuloh's pivot socket was modified for full side-to-side articulation, it could only be used as a paddle; afterward it could generate "about half a knot in smooth water."[297] Running downwind from Salcombe toward Brixham she "hardly rolled... because of her flat bottom and chine runners," and her stowage — a lazarette behind the removable transverse seat for warps, fenders, pots, boots and vegetables, shelves and under-shelf compartments either side of the cabin, food under the floorboards, and bins fore and aft for the battery and seldom-used gear — struck Bill as offering "enough stowage space for an ocean crossing."[297]

## Sold in 2009: the forepeak reworked, a higher-peaked sail, and the yuloh reshaped

Faith was sold in 2009 — Bill's own account of it characteristically brief: "I sold my Paradox 'Faith' to the very first person who offered to take her unto himself," with a "pang of guilt" afterward.[298] By May 2013 her owner was Jim Hall, "an accomplished sailor who [had] built a 21 ft yacht and sailed her by himself to far away places."[299] Visited that month at a boatyard on the Isle of Wight, Jim Hall had by then removed the forepeak's partition and widened its entrance, added a drinking-water tank forward, and improved access to the battery; moved the mainsheet lead forward onto the stern deck to stop it snagging the outboard; and fitted a new, higher-peaked sail — "the biggest improvement by far," which "transformed the boat so that she sails better to windward and tacks more positively," to the point that the engine was rarely needed. He had also reshaped the yuloh, giving it more twist, so the boat now had three independent means of propulsion.[300] By 2014 Jim Hall "makes good use of her" and had recently had her repainted, closing out Faith's record in this bundle as a boat still actively sailed, close on a decade after her launch.[301]
