# Cachivache

**"Cachivache"** — Spanish slang for a piece of junk or a knick-knack — is [Alberto Callejon](/people/alberto-callejon.md)'s [Paradox](/paradox.md), under construction from May 2008 at **Rawson, Chubut**, on Argentina's Patagonian coast. Alberto styled her on the list as **"el Paradox mas austral,"** the most southerly Paradox on record.

## Starting construction on the Patagonian coast

Before starting, Alberto weighed whether the design could handle his home coast's strong wind and sea, a stretch of open South Atlantic at 43°21′S, 65°03′W with almost no local sailing tradition beyond rowing and occasional windsurfing; see [blue-water suitability — a high-latitude query from Argentine Patagonia](/design/blue-water-suitability.md#a-high-latitude-query-from-argentine-patagonia) for the reassurance this drew. Construction began in May 2008, with early progress photos posted to a "Cachivache" folder in the group's photo section.[1] Alongside starting the hull, he raised the recurring question of building the bottom and sides in sheet steel rather than plywood — see [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative) — a 2008 recurrence, from a builder in Patagonia](/design/ideas/aluminum-construction-idea.md#a-2008-recurrence-from-a-builder-in-patagonia) for the sizing advice this drew, and Alberto's own non-technical contributions (an offered instant-messenger contact for Spanish-speaking builders, and a search for Don Elliott's out-of-print construction manual) on [people/alberto-callejon.md](/people/alberto-callejon.md).

## Enlarging the cabin to the hull's edge, considered and answered

While laying out Cachivache's bulkheads, before cutting, Alberto considered enlarging the cabin out to the hull's edge and asked whether doing so would affect any of the boat's other design parameters, echoing an idea raised once before and left untested at the time. A reply the next day answered directly against the idea: the stepped-in side decks stow the lowered sail and the yuloh, a wider cabin top makes it harder to reach the anchor or fend off from forward, it adds windage especially to windward, and the cabin's usable interior space would not even increase unless the internal side bins were removed too — so, on balance, little was seen to recommend it. See [flush cabin sides for Paradox (speculative)](/design/ideas/flush-cabin-sides-idea.md#a-2008-recurrence-answered) for the fuller record of both messages.[2][3]

## A drain-pipe material question, where PVC is hard to find

Asking about the PVC pipe shown on drawing 9 of the plans, Alberto drew an answer that read the question as one of materials sourcing: the pipe carries waste water, and where PVC itself proves hard to obtain locally, copper pipe — or the lead pipe once standard before PVC became common — would work as a substitute.[4][5]

## Reinforcing the hull for Patagonian seas

Construction advanced further that July: panels were reinforced with angle iron and stainless steel bolts and laminated with fiberglass on both sides — extra strengthening measures taken specifically against the rough conditions of the Patagonian sea (see [blue-water suitability — a high-latitude query from Argentine Patagonia](/design/blue-water-suitability.md#a-high-latitude-query-from-argentine-patagonia) for the conditions themselves) — with new build photographs due in the group's "CACHIVACHE" folder within a few days.[6] New photographs duly went up in the album later that month, drawing further encouragement from the list, with more images following within days.[7][8][9]

## A sail-geometry question for the newer rig

Framing Cachivache's rig, Alberto asked for the distance between the mast and the forward end of the yard on Paradox's newer sail — almost certainly the [Mk2 sail](/components/rig/mk2-sail.md) current at that date, since the design's plans were not revised again until the 2023 Mk3. The question drew no dimension in immediate reply, only a pointer to the group's own Files section, where a "New Sail Plan" folder already held the drawing itself.[14][15] A day later, a fellow builder measured the drawing directly and supplied the missing figure: about 800 mm from the mast centerline to the sail's forward top corner along the top edge, or 900 mm to the halyard attachment point along the same edge — a dimension not otherwise given on the plan (see [Mk2 sail — locating the sail's forward top corner](/components/rig/mk2-sail.md#locating-the-sails-forward-top-corner-and-tuning-balance-through-the-sleeve-gap) for the full figure and the sail-balance-tuning technique it enabled).[16]

## A personal website for the build photos, and an access problem

By mid-August 2008, a fuller set of building photographs was posted to a personal website rather than the group's own photo folder.[10] The link proved unreachable for at least one overseas list member, whose browser simply timed out trying to reach it; contacted directly, Alberto reported the site working fine from his own end, leaving the access problem unresolved on record.[11] Two further list members confirmed the site loaded without any trouble from their own connections — one had checked it several times with no issue, the other praised the photographs directly — narrowing the access problem to that one report rather than any fault with the site itself.[12][13] Revisiting the question weeks later, the browser-timeout report was updated rather than resolved: the site was eventually reached, though it still usually times out, tentatively put down to the long internet path between the UK and Argentina rather than to any fault with the site itself.[15]

## A polyester-and-epoxy question

Weighing how best to impregnate Cachivache's wood, Alberto asked whether a diluted coat of polyester resin applied first would still let epoxy resin be laminated over it afterward. The answer: not reliably, since polyester resin typically leaves a wax on the surface that must be removed before epoxy will adhere at all, and diluting the polyester does not impregnate the wood in any case — only the solvent penetrates, not the resin. A professional's caution, from prior experience at a marine-resin manufacturer, put it more bluntly still: never put epoxy over polyester; seal the wood with an epoxy sealer first, then laminate with epoxy resin. Full exchange and the fallback advice for a tight budget: [epoxy brands, chemistry, and cure behavior — epoxy over polyester](/construction/epoxy-brands-and-chemistry.md#epoxy-over-polyester-wax-impregnation-and-a-professionals-caution).[21][22][23] Alberto settled on laminating with epoxy resin just above the waterline, painting everywhere else.[24] A further reply arrived a day later, after the thread had already effectively closed, offering yet another combination — epoxy resin itself diluted with acetone in place of a polyester pre-coat, sidestepping the adhesion problem altogether.[25]

## Continued progress into the Patagonian winter

By late August 2008, the hull's sides were finished, with the remaining work hoped to be done by the end of the Patagonian winter so assembly could begin.[17] New photographs of the finished sides went up in the "Cachivache" folder the following day.[18] The build photos drew a further, unprompted compliment: another builder credited the site directly with the design of his own beam-laminating jigs, judging the method shown there considerably easier than the one given in the construction manual — see [Wizard Spawn — starting on the beams](/boats/wizard-spawn.md#starting-on-the-beams-jigs-sizing-confusion-and-a-lesson-in-plywood) for the fuller account of that build.[19] A further photo went up the following week, along with a wry aside that the Patagonian cold had returned just as it seemed to be easing, with a hope that shifting weather patterns would not draw the low temperatures out any further.[20]
