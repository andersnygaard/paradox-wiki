# Scout

**"Scout"** is Sean Mulligan's [Paradox](/paradox.md), sailed on **Lake Havasu, Arizona**; she was built to a high standard in Texas by **[Lezlie Henson](/people/lezlie-henson.md)** under the name **"Tardis,"** a build Henson documented step by step on her own blog.[10] Henson sold her **almost finished and never sailed** her; Mulligan then bought her, finished her, launched her on **12 September 2013**, and renamed her "Scout," documenting the completion and her first season on his own blog, [Full n By](/references/full-n-by-blog.md).[14][19] He founded the **Havasu Pocket Cruiser's Convention**, and, with his wife Jo, also owns and sails a trailer sailer named "Dauntless."[1][2]

## The build: a stock Paradox named "Tardis"

"Tardis" was **[Lezlie Henson](/people/lezlie-henson.md)'s** first from-scratch boat, built to the plans in the Houston, Texas area (Pasadena, then Kemah) across roughly two years — the bulkheads first dry-fitted in January 2011, the bottom glued on by March, and the boat all but complete by early 2013 — and documented step by step on her blog.[10] It is a **stock Paradox**, built carefully and a little beyond the minimum. Henson **insulated** the hull (not all Paradoxes are), covering the insulation with 5 mm ply screwed to epoxied dowel pegs, and built the plans' **400 lb of ballast** as lead "pigs" in bolt-down "pens" alongside water-tank ballast that her husband **Dale**, an electrical engineer, plumbed with 3-way valves for ballast use only.[11] She made her own **gimbaled stove** from aluminium angle to swivel both ways, modelled on **[Glen Maxwell](/people/glen-maxwell.md)'s** stove for "[Zoë](/boats/zoe.md)" rather than pay for a bought one — the "single burner Coleman stove fitted to gimbals" a UK observer would later note at second hand.[12][3] A copper-flashing ground plate was bedded over woven roving on the bottom, the hull finished in Glidden Porch & Floor off-white with a navy stripe and bright-varnished spars and trim, and non-skid applied to the decks per Charlie Jones's method on "Traveler."[13] She built to the plans plus **[Don Elliott](/people/don-elliott.md)'s** construction manual, with questions worked out on the **[Paradox mailing list](/references/mailing-list.md)**; the full first-hand record is preserved at [Lezlie's World](/references/lezlie-henson-blog.md).[10]

## An unfinished "Tardis," and a UK import cost estimate (January 2013)

While still owned by Lezlie Henson and offered for sale at Kemah, near Houston, Texas, for "in the region of 3,000 US dollars" (about £1,861), "Tardis" was unfinished: her window hardware and materials were not yet bought or fitted, her boom was unfinished, and she lacked a yuloh, anchors, fenders, and "the usual cruising gear," though she already carried a single-burner Coleman stove fitted to gimbals.[3]

Henson was selling because the project had lost its purpose. Begun as a dream shared with her husband, "Tardis" became "a reminder of a dead dream" after their separation; she lost her motivation to finish and launch, and sold her almost complete, in February 2013, **without ever having sailed her** — "I do wish I had been able to sail her... at least once."[14] Her own blog recorded the January 2013 interest from the United Kingdom — a phone call and forum discussion of the sale — the seller's side of the same episode as the import estimate below.[15]

A UK enthusiast priced shipping her from Houston to Felixstowe, itemizing freight and the various port and handling charges along the way. Because an unfinished, amateur-built boat needed its own HM Revenue and Customs commodity code, and the boat and her secondhand trailer needed separate VAT invoices, importing her to Britain would have added substantial cost and complexity on top of the purchase price.[4]

## Finishing "Tardis," and her first launch as "Scout" (2013)

Mulligan bought "Tardis" about **95 % complete** and finished her over the summer of 2013, documenting the work on his blog, [Full n By](/references/full-n-by-blog.md). He rated Henson's build "first class," and the completion was mostly small work, though it took him longer than he expected.[16] He found the mast built to a variation from the plans — a stainless eyebolt let into the side of the mast in place of the plans' internal halyard sheave, with a built-up platform around the masthead for an anchor light.[16] Inside, he left out the plans' removable bow partitions (which Henson had already built) and moved the battery **forward and on-centre**, to trim the boat against his own weight aft and open the whole forward compartment for a bedroll.[17] He judged Henson's sail — sewn from a **Sailrite** kit — to be the later **Mark-II Paradox mainsail**, extended its yard about eight inches, and re-drilled the outhaul to cure a wrinkle in the foot.[17] He made **aluminium brackets** for the mainsheet cam-cleat and a yuloh storage bracket, and later added transom **mainsheet fairleads**, a PVC cabin **air scoop**, masthead wiring run out above the deck rather than through it, and bow and stern **dock lines pre-rigged on both sides** — a running effort to cut rig time and make her easier to single-hand.[16][18]

She was rigged and **launched for the first time on 12 September 2013** — the hull's first time ever afloat, since Henson had sold her unsailed — and Mulligan rated her the fastest, easiest boat he had ever rigged.[19]

## Renamed "Scout," and a fast trailer-to-sailing routine on Lake Havasu

Under Mulligan's ownership on Lake Havasu, "Scout" runs from trailer to sailing in under **20 minutes**, helped by a non-tidal slipway with an adjacent jetty where she is moored to add water ballast and finish preparations before setting off — the same long-tow-bar launch method used elsewhere on the Paradox fleet, demonstrated on video reaching "trailer to sailor in under 10 min."[5][6] Like [Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md), "Scout" is sailed largely by yuloh in calms rather than by her outboard.[7] The yuloh built for her by a friend had its non-symmetrical blade cut the wrong way round; Mulligan re-worked it — flipping the blade and moving a sharper curve into the loom above the pivot so it self-orients leading-edge-down on both strokes — to lift his sustained sculling speed from about **1.75 knots** toward 2.[20] His own blog records a first autumn-2013 season of day-sails and overnight rendezvous at **Steamboat Cove**, about 6.5 nm south of Lake Havasu City, in winds up to **15–25 knots**.[21]

## Heavy-wind sailing, and the 2014 Texas 200

Video posted of "Scout" documents her sailing in **25+ knot** wind on Lake Havasu and in brisk conditions on Thompson Bay, and shows her tacking upwind under sail.[8] "Scout" also ran the **[Texas 200](/merits/texas-200.md#the-texas-200-and-a-port-townsend-wooden-boat-show-showing)** cruise in 2014, and during that event towed a fellow competitor's boat, "Crippled Duck," through 20–25 kt wind.[9]

## Later ownership: Chris Curtis, then Lee (Maine)

Mulligan later sold "Scout" on to **[Chris Curtis](/boats/curtis-paradox.md)** of **Sandpoint, Idaho** — the boat's third owner, giving the hull an ownership chain of **Henson → Mulligan → Curtis**. Under Curtis she has been **shown several times at the Port Townsend Wooden Boat Show** (pictured there around 2017), and in 2019 he called her "a great boat in almost every way."[22] Curtis sailed her for many years and camped aboard many nights, "primarily sailing her in North Idaho and the Puget Sound," day-sailing her hard on **Priest Lake** — where a gust once knocked her down, though not past about 90°.[23][24] He rated her built "to a very high standard," her SailRite sail "beautifully made by Lezlie," fitted with a **30-watt solar panel** and charge controller and insulated to design; her one departure from the plans was a **matchboard (hatchboard) closing the aft cabin** in place of a fixed panel, which he counted an improvement.[23] Her 2014 [Texas 200](/merits/texas-200.md#the-texas-200-and-a-port-townsend-wooden-boat-show-showing) run was during Mulligan's ownership.

After years of use Curtis began a refit — she needed her deck hardware re-bedded, her windows reinstalled and repainted, and a small leak repaired at the vent-hole end-grain — but it stalled when he moved on to a much larger boat, and he offered her for sale from 2022.[23] In **August 2024** he sold her to **Lee, of Maine**, her fourth owner, who trailered her some 3,500 miles home — extending the chain to **Henson → Mulligan → Curtis → Lee**.[25]
