# JoHanna

**"JoHanna"** is the intended name for [Pete Martin](/people/pete-martin.md)'s [Paradox](/paradox.md), under construction in **Cheltenham, England**. Pete priced UK plywood suppliers and ordered plans in October 2006, then began construction on **1 December 2006**, marked by building a jig for laminating the deck beams. For Pete's pre-build ballast-material research and person-level record, see [people/pete-martin.md](/people/pete-martin.md).

## Ballast: lead against concrete, and a bladder-tank alternative

Ahead of starting, Pete weighed the plans' built-in, epoxy-lined water tanks against loose water containers, having found collapsible bladder tanks that seemed a workable substitute needing no venting. Lacking lead of his own, he also asked about **concrete** as a ballast alternative. Lead proved the ideal, easy-to-cast choice — old wheel-balancing weights a practical source, bread tins serving as ready-made pig moulds, with loose water containers more versatile than built-in tanks since they let other heavy gear share the same stowage space — while concrete, at under three times the density of lead, would need roughly four times the volume for the same ballast weight, at a real cost in stowage space; rebar-reinforced concrete or iron pigs narrow that gap somewhat but still need two-and-a-half to three times the volume of lead. See [weight, ballast, and displacement — ballast material choice: lead against concrete, iron, and other alternatives](/design/weight-and-ballast.md#ballast-material-choice-lead-against-concrete-iron-and-other-alternatives) for the full figures.[111][112][113]

A follow-up correction to the concrete-versus-lead figures noted that water carried as ballast is itself a consumable worth roughly another 150 lb when full, and revised the iron/steel-versus-lead volume ratio down from roughly 2.5 times to somewhat less than two times; Pete confirmed the boat under discussion was his own and reported watching for cheap surplus lead rather than paying scrapyard price.[114][115]

## Workspace: garage fabrication, car-port assembly

The build is based entirely at home: panels and small parts are fabricated in the garage, with full assembly planned for a car port enclosed on three sides and due to be fully enclosed within a few months for a reasonably dry, if not especially warm, working space — a plan set back at the outset when a gale tore a sheet of corrugated plastic off the car-port roof.[3]

## Cabin beam length and tiller offsets

Working out ahead of where construction had actually reached, Pete marked out cabin-beam stock and a tiller template from the plans, but found neither dimension clearly stated — the cabin beam length nowhere given outright, and the tiller shape needing to be extrapolated from sheets #4 and #11 in the absence of any offsets.[4] Deck-beam lengths can be taken straight from the **lines drawing (sheet 2)**, adding **3 to 4 inches**; the full chord-length table for all seven beams is at [deck and cabin beam lamination — deck beam dimensions](/construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md#deck-beam-dimensions-and-the-deck-curves-true-shape). Working out a tiller's curve from the plans alone is a known open question with no stated offsets; for the extrapolation and wire-template techniques used to solve it, see [rudder — tiller construction](/components/rudder.md#tiller-construction-laminated-wood-versus-bent-metal-tube).[5]

## Sheer clamp notch and bulkhead #1

Comparing build photos, Pete noticed the sheer clamp on one boat under construction was notched roughly 500 mm from the stern, unlike other builders' photos where it ran the sheer's full length, and separately asked whether bulkhead #1 should be left open (with limber holes to drain water to the bilge) or solid, and whether the mast would still fit internally if the bulkhead were left solid.[6] Both questions have settled answers: the sheer-clamp notch and bulkhead #1's watertight-versus-open trade-off are covered in full at [interior bin panels and hinged floor — design intent: watertight bulkhead, opening is optional](/components/interior.md#design-intent-watertight-bulkhead-opening-is-optional) and [sheer strakes](/components/sheer-strakes.md).[7][8] A second, competing reading of the same sheet-3 cutout, and bulkhead #1's role as sealed buoyancy that can trap water rather than shed it, are covered at [sheer strakes](/components/sheer-strakes.md#sheer-clamp-notch-near-the-stern) and [interior bin panels and hinged floor — a sealed forward compartment, and the case for an access hatch](/components/interior.md#a-sealed-forward-compartment-and-the-case-for-an-access-hatch).[9]

## Cabin beam jig questions from sheet 10

Working ahead of where construction had actually reached, Pete marked out chipboard for a cabin-beam jig and worked through sheet 10's full set of six cabin-top beams and three hatch-cover beams — mostly differing dimensions, bevels on the foremost cabin beam, a hatch-locking notch on one of the two aft cabin beams, and a weather-seal notch at the forward hatch opening — asking whether each notch is better routed from a full-sized beam or laminated in with the notch already in place. See [deck and cabin beam lamination — cabin-top and hatch-cover beam bevels](/construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md#cabin-top-and-hatch-cover-beam-bevels-open-questions-from-sheet-10) for the full technical detail. The foremost cabin beam's own pencilled note gives it at **52 mm**, its forward bevel and the deck beam's own bevel both shaped freehand with an angle grinder and sanding disc, sighted by eye standing in the roofless cabin against the beam's side-to-side curve; the lower of the two aft cabin beams is noted at **75 mm**, its rear bevels ground the same way. Whether to route a notch from a full-sized beam or laminate it in place is left to whichever is easiest for the builder — a router gives an easier route for both the hatch-lock notch and the forward weather-seal notch, but a beam without one can simply be laminated with a narrower bead of material on top instead.[12] Pete thanked Al for the reference figures, noting he had annotated his own plans accordingly and expected to use a router for the hatch and cabin beam notches once he was further along, likely once the Christmas period was past.[13]

## Pricing UK marine plywood, and an MAS epoxy and filler choice

Pricing UK marine plywood in March 2007, Pete found three BS1088 sources at widely differing prices — Robbins' basic grade, a cheaper "Far Eastern" grade, and Okume — and asked the list about the cheaper grade's tradeoffs and how much epoxy resin the full build would need.[116] Weighing epoxy brands ahead of real cutting, Pete settled on the **MAS** product line and asked a detailed follow-up on which MAS resin to use for which task: whether MAS Low Viscosity resin — recommended for wetting out cloth — should be reserved for sheathing, fairing, and filling, with the FLAG resin (recommended for bonding and laminating, less prone to slumping) used instead for his first tasks of bonding and laminating; and which MAS hardener speed to choose, leaning toward the medium hardener for its balance of working time against amine blush, while expecting to need a slower hardener for time-consuming steps like fitting the sheer clamps and chine logs. He also asked which filler to use for gluing and bonding — Cabosil seemed the standard choice — but was unclear on when to use cellulose fibres versus wood flour instead, expecting cellulose fibres to suit gap-filling.[117]

## A boat shed sorted, and open cutting and bulkhead-curve questions

After a long gap, Pete found and fitted out a **15 x 7 ft boat shed**, just large enough for the build with outdoor space alongside for good weather, and cut cardboard mock-ups of bulkheads 2, 3, and 4 to get a feel for their true size before ordering Far Eastern marine plywood for the real cutting. Marking out the mock-ups, he noticed bulkhead #3 shows no side cleats and bulkhead #4 shows only a dotted-line hint of one — the same question already settled for bulkhead #3, whose side panel glues and nails directly onto its edge rather than needing a cleat (see [hull assembly sequence — bulkhead #3 has no side cleats, by design](/construction/hull-assembly-sequence.md#bulkhead-3-has-no-side-cleats-by-design)); Pete leaned toward fitting cleats to bulkhead #4 anyway. He also raised three cutting and interior-wood questions, plus a Don Elliott lamination suggestion, answered below.[17]

Standing bulkheads in a row after cutting them out gives a misleading first impression of a Paradox's final size and feel: the real hull, once assembled, proves far sturdier — bits can be leaned on without them moving — and noticeably roomier inside than loose cardboard or plywood mock-ups suggest. There are no side cleats on bulkhead #3; bulkhead #4 does carry them, on the aft face, with the plans' dotted line simply marking that hidden detail. A jigsaw cuts the plywood's curves well enough on its own, with no need to plane down afterward; bevels should wait until after the first trial fit, since cutting them beforehand risks getting them wrong before the actual fit is known. The curved bulkhead sides' offset from true is measured at the midpoint of the vertical section. On Don Elliott's suggested 6 mm plywood deck beams, laminating ply would likely work, but is worth a test beam first, since deck beams must bear a person's full weight walking on them.[18]

For interior cleats, Douglas fir throughout is workable but not trouble-free: the sheer bends acceptably, but the chine log is "a pig," bending far less willingly — building a chine log again, laminating it in place from at least two layers of DF is the preferred approach over trying to bend a single solid piece. Cutting the sheer and chine stock to the plans' finished size, rather than leaving excess for later fairing, is not the risk it might seem: epoxy fills gaps readily enough that a slightly imperfect fit still ends up structurally stronger than the surrounding wood, and the construction sequence itself makes a serious cutting mistake genuinely difficult to make. Working on the excavation of the *Mary Rose* (Henry VIII's flagship, sunk in the Solent) turned up a telling precedent for tolerating small building errors: a rebate cut for a stringer in one of the main ribs, found to be in the wrong place, had simply been filled with a block of wood nailed in — proof that "even the King's master craftsmen got it wrong occasionally."[19]

Once the cardboard bulkhead mock-ups were actually set out and joined together in their correct positions, rather than just cut and stacked, the boat's true size and layout finally came to life for Pete — a shift from the earlier impression that the mock-ups looked too small. With the boat shed itself proving too hot to work in during a spell of warm weather (solved by opening the doors wide for fresh air), plywood ordered, and enough working space secured for poorer weather ahead, the build reached the point of turning from mock-ups to real cutting.[20]

## Bulkhead #3's deck-area curve, and the rudder-stock cutting order

Laying out bulkhead #3 once his ten sheets of plywood arrived, Pete found the "deck" area — from the sheer line toward midships — drawn as a straight line on the plans, unlike every other bulkhead, which follows the crown shown on bulkhead #2; he asked whether that area should be cut straight as drawn or given the same curve as the other bulkheads, judging either way would make little practical difference, before fitting the jigsaw with a blade recommended to resist tear-out on the outer veneers. He also asked whether the rudder stock could safely be cut from bulkhead #3's own sheet at the same time, worried about leaving enough material for the bottom's forward section, which he expected would need to be fitted to the actual assembly rather than cut to a fixed size in advance.[21][22] A practical plywood-handling tip answered in passing: a 4 in C-clamp fastened to a sheet's edge makes a serviceable handle for dragging and carrying a full sheet alone, and the jigsaw blade choice is best read off the manufacturer's own packaging, with fine teeth giving smaller, cleaner splinters.[22]

The deck-area curve question has no practical consequence either way: the top and bottom of every bulkhead get faired in to match the deck and bottom curvature after the sides and bulkheads are assembled, so cutting it straight or curved makes no difference to the finished boat.[23] The rudder-stock question turned out to matter more on UK-sized plywood sheets than on the wider US stock the plans assume: there is not enough width on a UK sheet to cut both complete rudder halves from bulkhead #3's waste without the tip of the "beak" running off the sheet's edge. One workaround is to cut one rudder half from the bulkhead #3 waste (accepting the outside grain running the wrong way on that half) and the other half moved outboard a few inches from its plan position, losing more of the beak but leaving enough material for the bottom; a separate beak infill piece is then cut from scrap and the three parts assembled together. Fitting every part onto a single sheet alongside the transom can also require reordering the parts' layout from the plans' own arrangement, as shown in the "drawing changes" file the group's Files section carries.[24] Weighing that answer, Pete leaned toward leaving the rudder stock for a later sheet rather than risk the bottom's material margin.[25]

Handling the first of his ten sheets of 18 mm plywood alone, Pete found it far stiffer than expected: standing in the middle of a sheet laid flat on blocks, it barely flexed under his own weight, leaving him wondering how such a stiff sheet could be made to follow the bottom's rocker curve at all. The technique that worked on another builder's own hull needs no special jig: starting at the bow, nail and glue steadily toward the stern, since ring nails hold the curve down section by section as they go in, with a helper's weight on the trailing end of the sheet useful for the final few nails. See [Little Jim — bending the bottom plywood to the rocker curve, recalled](/boats/little-jim.md#bending-the-bottom-plywood-to-the-rocker-curve-recalled-april-2007) for the boat this technique was demonstrated on.[26]

## Cutting bulkhead 2, the transom, and side panels from one sheet

Fitting bulkhead 2, the transom, and the forward side panel onto a single sheet proved a tight puzzle: cutting the bulkhead and transom first, then making the second side panel and using it as a template for the "hard to fit" first side panel, left almost no spare wood but got both panels cut. With bulkhead 2, both forward side panels, and the transom cut, and bulkhead 3 and the two aft side panels marked and ready for the saw, a fresh question arose over transom camber: the plans draw the transom with camber shown on its side-panel edges but no dimension stated, whereas bulkhead 4's side-panel edges do carry 5 mm of specified camber, leaving open whether the transom edges should get the same 5 mm by inference or stay straight as drawn.[27]

The answer, illustrated against Faith's own build: applying the same 5 mm curvature to the transom edges, matching bulkhead 4, gives a result some builders think adds to the boat's appearance, while others may prefer perfectly straight sides — either is workable.[28] A follow-up trial with scrap plywood found 6 mm deck-beam stock far too stiff to bend, 12 mm bent easily (with some expected spring-back), and 9 mm was chosen as the working thickness, reserving the 6 mm sheet for non-structural interior bin sides that could be replaced or supplemented later.[31]

On cleat wood, kiln-dried pine on hand was tested against Douglas fir by driving a bronze ring nail into a piece and watching whether it split — pre-drilling the pine first is the correct way to run that test, since driving a nail without a pilot hole is not a fair trial and does not by itself rule pine out.[28][29]

## A window-removal and trolley-wheel question, and Faith admired

Faith's near-finished photographs prompted a question about whether her windows get removed before painting and other finishing work, to protect them from paint spots and dust — and, separately, where the wheels for a builder's makeshift plywood-moving trolley (see [build effort and workspace — a rope sling, castor trolley, and rolling technique for moving a full sheet alone](/construction/build-effort.md#workspace)) were sourced, useful for moving a hull outdoors on hot days after a boat shed reached 30°C mid-build well before summer.[31]

Faith's windows are fixed in place before painting rather than removed, with no real difficulty keeping them free of paint; a genuine problem instead turned up at the front of the cabin top, needing a built-up fillet to accommodate the forward curve of the 6 mm polycarbonate glazing — a curve worth allowing for from the outset, since fitting it after the fact used up ply that could otherwise have been saved.[32] Bulkhead #4 carries a bowed-out side shape matching the transom, part of a set of subtle asymmetric curves — the transom and bulkheads #2 and #4 all bowed slightly outward, only the midship bulkhead #3 straight-sided — visible looking down the hull from astern and judged, though subtle, a deliberate part of the design for windward sea-boat performance: as the boat heels to windward the leeward edge of the stem cuts the water, and the resulting flow is disturbed at the chine runner in a way that helps break the boat free of what holds her back, forming a run of small waves along the leeward side rather than one large hollow, with the water finally slipping unimpeded off the corner of the transom "like a slippery canoe stern." The plywood-moving trolley's wheels came from a UK hardware chain (Homebase) — plastic, and three of them had since buckled under Faith's increasing weight as the build progressed, a caution toward genuinely tough wheels for the job.[32]

## Sourcing Douglas Fir, and sawn versus planed timber

A local timber yard's promise of Douglas Fir in stock proved unreliable — a second visit found a different assistant unable to supply it without a two-week special order — leading to a switch to Robbins of Bristol for the full cutting list (cleats, floor joists, chine, sheer, and stem). A quote for **planed** timber came back at **£450**, roughly double an initial estimate; since the great majority of that timber would never be on view, planed stock was judged money wasted, and a recalculation using **sawn** timber instead — cut to size from three 2.5 m boards — brought the cost down to about **£150**, a substantial saving.[33] Sawn timber was confirmed as entirely adequate: cleat sizing is not critical, with plus-or-minus 5 mm accurate enough for the job, a lesson learned firsthand after over-buying planed stock on an earlier build.[34] Buying sawn also leaves room to buy an electric hand planer with part of the savings, useful for taking off the rough sawn surface where needed.[35]

By this point in the build, two of JoHanna's seven deck beams were finished, with a third curing in the kitchen for warmth and the rest expected complete within the week; the rudder blades were marked out, with the rear side panels, bulkhead 3, and the two rudder blades next to cut. Progress without the interior joinery pieces was expected to slow considerably, and a first attempt at scarfing scrap plywood was planned as practice before committing to the actual side panels, a step approached with some trepidation.[33]

## Ring-nail sizing, and a bronze-nail offer

Ordering ring nails from Robbins alongside the timber raised a sizing question: the plans specify 2 lb of 2-1/2 in **#10** nails, but Robbins does not stock 2-1/2 in in that gauge — only the heavier **#8** once the length passes a certain point — leaving open whether to order the heavier nail as specified or substitute a shorter 50 mm **#10** instead, on the reasoning that these nails see use mainly on the bottom attachment (some builders, including Glen Maxwell, used no nails at all).[36] An electric hand planer is worth the modest extra cost against the wood savings already made, useful too for shaping scarf joints with practice; sawn boards can carry surprisingly many splinters, worth working carefully around even brushing dust off an edge.[37] The 2-1/2 in **#8** is the correct substitute, bought from Robbins and used successfully; the plans' quantities are close to accurate in practice, with more of the 1-1/4 in size left over than any other length ordered.[38] A UK builder some way further along a Paradox build offered a large surplus of bronze nails left over from his own hull, more than enough to build a second boat, at a price below Classic Marine's — the shop he had originally bought from himself.[39]

## All bulkheads and panels cut, and a dry mockup

Over a Bank Holiday weekend in early May 2007, all of JoHanna's bulkheads and panels were cut out, followed by a mini dry mockup that suggested the parts would indeed fit together when the time came to assemble them for real, with progress photos posted to the build blog.[40] The dry-mockup milestone drew comment on Paradox's bottom-panel thickness: JoHanna's builder was told he could likely build a stitch-and-glue bottom from two layers of 1/2 in plywood, or even a single 1/2 in thickness, reasoning by comparison to a boat with an even wider bottom panel built to only 5/16 in.[41] A thicker, single-sheet bottom close to the plans' full specification was judged worth keeping regardless, for the reassurance of a solid margin when drying out on an unfamiliar bottom.[42] For the fuller bottom-thickness debate this exchange opened up, see [hull planking — how thick does the bottom really need to be?](/construction/hull-planking.md#how-thick-does-the-bottom-really-need-to-be). Weighing the suggestion, JoHanna's builder chose to plod along and build as close to the plans as possible instead, having already bought the plywood as specified — allowing a lighter build overall would be possible with stitch-and-glue, but reasoning that a genuinely lighter boat would be better served by the [Enigma 460](/matt-layden/enigma.md#the-enigma-family-the-360-and-the-plywood-cabin-460) once its plans were ready.[43]

## Vent trunk drain pipe sizing: metric tube against the plans' 1 in nominal pipe

Weeks later, sizing the vent trunk's own drain, Pete weighed the plans' 1 in nominal drain pipe against the metric PVC plumbing stock available in the UK — 22 mm, 32 mm, or 40 mm — worried that leftover 22 mm tube from a bathroom job might prove too small if a load of green water ever filled the vent trunk quickly, but unsure whether 32 mm could even be drilled for accurately.[68] US "pipe" sizing is not the same measurement as "tube" sizing: a nominal 1 in US pipe actually measures 1.315 in (33.4 mm) on the outside diameter, with the true figure depending on the pipe's Schedule (a rating tied to pressure rather than exact size) — Schedule 40, the common household-plumbing standard, runs about 33.78 mm outside diameter, while heavier Schedule 80 (dark grey) is reserved for areas needing more strength and is not to be confused with the similar-looking light grey used for electrical conduit; pipe under 12 in is sized nominally by inside diameter, while larger pipe is sized by outside diameter instead — a holdover from when pipe was made only of iron, with some plastic-pipe makers later adopting the old iron sizes (IPS) and others not. Tubing, unlike pipe, is always sized by outside diameter.[69] Because the vent trunk's own deck ventilation hole is only about 120 mm in diameter, only a limited amount of water can enter it at once even in a breaking sea, and a 22 mm drain has proven adequate in practice sailing a Paradox in brief force 6 conditions with waves never breaking on deck — even if the vent box did fill occasionally, the amount of water reaching the cabin would be minimal; drilling accurately for a 32 mm hole was judged the harder, riskier option.[70] Weighing the advice, Pete settled on his own leftover 22 mm tubing rather than attempt the larger, harder-to-drill 32 mm size.[71]

Separately, for a vent trunk built up from glued wooden blocks with a groove cut to accept the pipe liner (rather than drilled), copper tubing can double usefully as a lightning-ground conductor for a crystal radio, bonded into the groove with thickened epoxy.[72] Having already glued his own vent-trunk base together before hearing of the grooved-block method, and lacking a router at the time, Pete concluded he would have used it had a router been available — a tool a co-worker handed him, unprompted, the very next day.[73] For a builder who has already glued the blocks together, cutting them in half, routing a passage for the drain, and gluing them back together is a workable alternative to drilling — avoiding an expensive, hard-to-source drill bit, the difficulty of drilling straight, and the risk of leaving voids where the pipe passes through the glue line.[74]

## A sheer and chine strake beveling question

Shaping his first sheer strake to the plans, Pete wondered whether it would be easier to plane the sheer's top surface and the chine's bottom surface after fitting them into the boat rather than beforehand, since both would likely need fairing into the deck and bottom regardless, and a long, unwieldy piece of lumber is harder to clamp securely while still loose. ## Hints on gluing up the hull

With his cleats bevelled and locating nails already in use, Pete asked the list for hints and pitfalls on gluing up the hull itself — unsure whether it needed to go together in one sitting or could safely be done in stages, and worried that early joints would set before the last was even started. Two independent, complementary techniques answered the question in full: gluing the whole hull in one roughly two-hour session, working outward from bulkhead 3 with the symmetry already fixed at the dry-fit stage; and using temporary wire nails as locating pins, which lets a hull be assembled and disassembled repeatedly and glued up bulkhead by bulkhead without losing alignment, since a nailed joint cannot move regardless of cure state. See [hull assembly sequence — gluing up the hull: one sitting or several, and locating the bulkheads with temporary pins](/construction/hull-assembly-sequence.md#gluing-up-the-hull-one-sitting-or-several-and-locating-the-bulkheads-with-temporary-pins) for the full technique and a cautionary account of what goes wrong locating panels with screws instead of nails.[54][55][56][57][58]

Thanking both correspondents, Pete confirmed he was already using nails as locators throughout and had temporary blocks screwed to the side panels at each bulkhead station, and expected reassembly with glue to be straightforward once everything was checked true at the dry-fit stage. He still had cleat bevels to finish, had not yet touched the transom, and needed to coat the bulkheads and inside of the side panels with epoxy before the wet build — expecting the vent trunk, whose top he had built but not yet fitted to the side panels or mast step, to need finishing first, pushing the wet assembly out another week or two. He judged a glued hull achievable by late July.[59]

## Coating the bulkheads and panels: before or after the wet assembly

Coating the bulkheads and side panels with epoxy ahead of the wet assembly, as Pete had planned, is not the only order that works: it can just as easily be done afterward, provided the deck is not yet fitted, and doing so gives a cleaner bonding surface for the epoxy joints between the side panels and the bulkheads/frames, and for the chine logs and sheer strakes as well. See [hull assembly sequence — epoxy-coating the bulkheads and side panels: before or after the wet assembly](/construction/hull-assembly-sequence.md#epoxy-coating-the-bulkheads-and-side-panels-before-or-after-the-wet-assembly) for the full technique.[60]

## A chine-fairness worry during dry assembly

Preparing for the wet assembly of the hull after a dry fit, Pete noticed while bending down to check the fit of the stem that the chine was not fair — a "3"-shaped wave running between the stem, bulkhead 1, and bulkhead 2, though the chine looked fair over the rest of the boat and the sheer line looked fair throughout. Clamping a 25 x 25 mm external piece of fir to the outside of the chine noticeably improved its line, encouraging since the actual chine log, more than double that piece's size, was expected to be even more effective at pulling the panels straight. Every bulkhead measurement checked against the plans, and reducing the stem's width slightly at its lower part made little difference to the wave. Not having built a boat before, Pete could not tell whether such waviness before the chine log goes on is normal and self-corrects once the log pulls the side panels fair, or a sign of a genuine problem, since the cleat bevels and the stem all fit snugly and removing the stem and pulling the panels together at the bow left the wave unchanged.[66]

The worry was answered with reassurance drawn from experience: such waviness is expected to pull fair once the chine logs go on, helped by fitting them on a hot day and by laminating each log from two thinner parts rather than bending it at full, unlaminated dimension. See [boats/faith.md — reassurance on a wavy chine before the chine logs go on](/boats/faith.md#reassurance-on-a-wavy-chine-before-the-chine-logs-go-on) for the full answer.[67]

## Water tanks reconsidered, and a rear-sole-plate question

Weighing the plans' built-in, epoxy-lined water tanks against the standing consensus that there is nothing good to be said about drinking from an epoxy-lined tank (see [water tank construction and lining](/construction/water-tank-lining.md)), Pete leaned toward using the tank compartments for water ballast only — which felt wasteful of otherwise-usable space — or building a couple of flexible tanks instead; by then he had also been collecting 2-litre pop bottles, gathering enough to keep him watered for well over a week with space still to spare in the tank area, leaving him weighing whether to build the tanks as designed or turn the whole area into extra storage for water bottles, provisions, and ballast (see [water tank construction and lining — not using the built-in tanks at all](/construction/water-tank-lining.md#not-using-the-built-in-tanks-at-all-individual-containers-instead)). His leaning plan for the forward sole was a single rigid piece, forming a box section with the tank tops, opened by a couple of lift-off or hinging hatches rather than the plans' continuous brass hinge specified for the rear sole plate — a plate he suspected, from photos posted of it, differs from the small removable sole plates fitted either side of the water tanks on Faith's own build (see [interior bin panels and hinged floor — a removable-floorboard technique for full storage and ballast access](/components/interior.md#a-removable-floorboard-technique-for-full-storage-and-ballast-access)). Also still tempted to omit the rear of the cabin trunk in favor of a drop-in washboard, as on Little Cruiser and NOLI, Pete liked the idea of being able to sit on occasion on the rear deck with his legs dangling into the cabin, provided the fitting could be made strong, lockable, and watertight enough to keep most of the stock design's benefits and safety.[87] The reply drew on firsthand experience with the same choices: building the tanks to plan rather than converting them to storage, since hatch covers can always be cut in later; replacing leaked water ballast with lead while keeping the tank compartment's air space nearly sealed to preserve flotation reserve, with a return to water ballast planned if genuinely watertight, preferably metal, inspection hatches can be sourced (see [water tank construction and lining — lead replacing leaked water ballast, and a hinge-material alternative to brass](/construction/water-tank-lining.md#lead-replacing-leaked-water-ballast-and-a-hinge-material-alternative-to-brass)); the brass sole-plate hinge collecting grit over time, with a webbing hinge as the planned fix if it ever fails; and the drop-in washboard judged sound, provided it does not compromise the cabin trunk's own structure.[88] A fuller reply followed a day later, giving Bill Serjeant's own bin-and-floor system in more technical detail, a case against building the water tanks at all, and his own change of heart on the washboard question — see [Faith — cabin sole panels revisited, a case against built-in water tanks, and a washboard endorsed](/boats/faith.md#cabin-sole-panels-revisited-a-case-against-built-in-water-tanks-and-a-washboard-endorsed).[89]

## Washboard design: a vee shape, a divided board, and a tapered opening

Following up on his own water-tank and rear-sole-plate questions, Pete confirmed he had been won over on leaving out the built-in tanks in favor of simple lifting or hinged sole plates, still undecided between a traditional V-shaped washboard and one with parallel sides, planning to mock one up in scrap plywood to test its rigidity and strength before committing, with the final call deferred until ready for the decking; side shelves and side bins were nearly finished, and he was weighing stove options for the aft galley. A further, more contentious idea under consideration: two sealed, vented lockers built into the aft quarters, each holding a 5-litre fuel can against a possible future outboard, accessible only through a deck hatch and clear of the tiller's full range of movement and the steering lines; the extra weight aft would need offsetting with more ballast forward, but even used only as sealed, empty cans the lockers would add 5 litres of reserve buoyancy if the boat were ever flooded.[90]

Jeff UK's own washboard on NOLI took the traditional vee shape rather than a vertical-lift design, chosen less for the look than for the offcut of plywood on hand — left over from a project some 15 years earlier — which only just yielded the part once two small pieces were added to the top, sparing a whole new sheet.[91]

Bill Serjeant's own boats have used a washboard divided into upper and lower halves: an overlapping flange along the lower edge of the upper board seals against wind and water when both are in place, the option to close only the lower half suits milder conditions, and two half-size boards stow more easily below decks than one large one while needing less clearance beneath the boom. On a Hunter 19 built this way, a small window let him see astern with the boards sealed in place.[92]

Following the same question, Nels asked how a tapered washboard would behave if the hatch overlapped the top of the opening, noting his own tapered companionway opening uses delrin sliders — some resistance, but always sliding easily — that have never leaked, with a porthole built into the board itself.[93]

A washboard's straight sides can also serve a purpose: a screw-in deck plate mounted in the upper half restores visibility astern when sealed below, and an angled washboard's tendency to come out more easily than a straight-sided one is a feature rather than a fault; a heavy U-bolt fitted under the companionway for a safety-harness attachment can double as a mount for a wood cleat, and, with the boards themselves reinforced on the inside with strips of timber, a line led to the upper board and cleated tight in heavy weather stops them lifting out at the cost of some convenience.[94]

## Drop board options: a photographed technique, and a Lexan alternative considered

Nels opened a "Drop board options" photo folder with three captioned images of one washboard-and-hatch arrangement, a design he called a "compromise" since the board has to be lifted almost halfway up before it comes free, alongside a second, lighter plywood board fitted with a bug screen for summer use that lacks the same delrin runners and is less convenient to use; he invited further ideas for the folder, floating a screened insert with a sliding shutter and a shielded vent of the kind used for attic ventilation.[95]

Weighing the idea, Pete had considered a Lexan board along the lines of Little Cruiser's own (see `microcruising.com/lcwindow.htm` for photographs and a write-up), but judged a double washboard not really worth the trouble on Paradox given the board is only 260 mm tall to begin with; the three-position drop-board idea seemed a neat one and little effort to fabricate, worth keeping in mind as a possible later addition even if not essential.[96]

A "Wileyport" was suggested as a further option for the folder: a rectangular opening covered with insect screening, fitted with brackets that let a Lexan window sit either vertical against the opening or propped at an angle with the top open for airflow while still shedding spray, held in place by friction wedges; one example sits at the forward end of a Bolger Micro's cabin, above the free-flooding bow well, with a second at the stern ahead of a similar well, both left open for air circulation even under a winter cover. Phil Bolger uses the fitting often in his own designs.[97]

The three-position idea drew a mixed verdict of its own: appealing in principle, but in practice a hatch cracked open a little has always supplied enough ventilation, and Paradox's own drop-board opening is already larger than the hole cut only for the tiller in Little Cruiser's transom; more ventilation is rarely the top want when it is raining in the UK.[98]

NOLI's own washboard is held with two small bolts through the outside of the cabin, the washboard edges, and the retaining lip, each a round-headed bolt with the head outside and a wing nut and washer on the inside; her solar vent sits on the foredeck in place of the stock vent trunk, joked to do little for a warm night once it's moonlight rather than sunlight doing the work — met with the dry reassurance that no sleep need be lost over it. See [components/vent-box.md](/components/vent-box.md) and [boats/noli.md](/boats/noli.md) for the fuller record.[99]

## Deck-beam fairing, corner posts, and interior paint answered

Pete's three open questions above — the low deck beam at bulkhead 1, the sheet-10 corner-post detail, and interior paint (see [a deck-beam shim, a corner-post plans question, and an interior-paint question](#a-deck-beam-shim-a-corner-post-plans-question-and-an-interior-paint-question)) — were all answered within a few days. The low beam turned out to be a common problem needing fairing on every deck beam, not a measuring mistake on Pete's part, with a build-photo comparison offered alongside the option of leaving deck-beam cleats proud to shape once the deck itself goes on for final assembly; full technique at [deck and cabin beam lamination — shimming deck beam #1](/construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md#shimming-deck-beam-1-and-its-neighbor-to-fair-the-centerline).[103][104] The sheet-10 corner-post detail was explained in full — see [deck and cabin beam lamination — corner post angles on sheet 10](/construction/deck-and-cabin-beams.md#corner-post-angles-on-sheet-10-the-30-degree-and-14-degree-window-seating-detail) — and Pete expected the fit would become self-evident once he actually came to cut the posts out.[105] For interior paint, a clear primer sealer and even ordinary floor paint were recommended for a hardwearing finish, household-quality paint over sanded epoxy was confirmed adequate, and a UK primer formulated to stick to epoxy was recommended with a cold-weather caution; full technique at [epoxy pre-coating technique — interior finish](/construction/epoxy-coating-technique.md#interior-finish-bare-wood-epoxy-varnish-cetol-or-paint).[102][103][104] Pete settled instead on a Dulux "Super Grip" primer — a cheaper UK-sourced alternative, also formulated to stick to glass and ceramics, bought through his wife's staff discount at a UK hardware chain.[105] For cabin-sole non-slip matting, see [interior bin panels and hinged floor — cabin-sole non-slip and kneeling comfort](/components/interior.md#cabin-sole-non-slip-and-kneeling-comfort) for the fuller range of options discussed; Pete found rubber floor mats sold locally in adequate supply but stayed undecided on buying a pack, going instead in search of cheap camping mats to line the cabin's sides, having previously owned similar mats bought new for about a pound each some years before, when the same product now runs closer to £10. New photographs of the day's progress went up on his building blog that same afternoon.[105]

## A trailer pattern passed on, and a snagging outboard cage (2013)

JoHanna's own trailer became the pattern for Minnow's replacement trailer in October 2013: Pete recommended **De Graaff Trailers**, and supplied the information needed to have the replacement built on the same **SB 730/2 ADJ** pattern as his own (by then no longer in production) but fitted with **flat rubber rollers** in place of JoHanna's round ones, plus guide arms to keep the boat straight when retrieved from the water in a side wind or current.[118] Retelling the choice the following year, Pete confirmed he remained satisfied with his own trailer overall, but that switching to flat rollers was the one change he would make fitting it out again.[119]

JoHanna also carries a homemade **outboard cage**, fitted to cut down the mainsheet fouling the propeller — a snag common to any outboard-equipped Paradox — though even with the cage the sheet still occasionally tangles in the propeller; leading the mainsheet from the aft deck instead of from the transom, a change made afterward, was thought likely to solve the problem outright.[120]
