# Overview

The **Paradox** is a 13' 10" hard-chine plywood micro-cruiser designed by **Matt Layden**, sailed single-handed entirely from within a small, fully enclosed cabin. Her signature [chine runners](/chine-runners/index.md) supply lateral resistance in place of a centerboard, and she carries a mix of fixed lead and water ballast low in the bilge for stability. She is a flat-bottomed **sharpie** — a label the record treats as settled, even as it divides on which historical sharpie lineage she actually descends from (see [sharpie lineage](/design/sharpie-lineage.md)) — and is the subject of the `paradoxbuilders` / `paradoxsailboat` mailing-list community whose archive this bundle summarizes.

This concept is the **trunk** of the bundle: broad, well-supported facts about the boat live here or are promoted here as evidence accumulates; specific details live in the linked branches below.

How far a Paradox may be modified before the result is properly a different boat — a **Paradox derivative** — is a distinct, recurring question with its own body of debate and precedent; see [modifying Paradox and derivative identity](/design/modifying-paradox.md).

# Defining features

The boat's now-well-documented, recurring characteristics — each backed by a mature branch of the tree:

* **Chine runners — the boat's signature feature** — the tapered fins running along the hull's chine line are the first thing most people notice about Paradox and the innovation that most defines it: with **no centerboard or daggerboard**, they (with the heeled hull and the big rudder) supply *all* the lateral resistance that lets a flat-bottomed, shallow-draft boat sail to windward, quantified by Matt Layden's own sea trial (VMGW ≈ 2.2–2.3 kt at ~51° off the wind) and called "the greatest innovation for small cruising boats in the last century." They have their own branch — see [chine runners](/chine-runners/index.md).
* **Heavy internal lead and water ballast, low in the hull** — a small, heavy boat with a lot of stability: most weight sits low **in the bilge** (≈930 lb of a ≈1,365 lb loaded boat, including **400 lb of fixed lead** as ten 40 lb pigs plus up to **150 lb of water ballast** in two tanks), so the boat is expected to "plough through" a 2–4 ft chop rather than be knocked about; the waterplane sinks the hull only ~1 inch per 200 lb added; a correctly ballasted boat is claimed to **self-right from 165° of heel**, a figure now corroborated by two independent sources — see [weight and ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md).
* **Simple lug-type rig on a rolling boom, worked entirely from the enclosed helm** — a lugsail-type sail whose yard is held to the mast by the halyard alone (apparently no parrel), set on a rolling boom; the mast steps through reinforced deck [mast partners](/components/rig/mast.md); running rigging and control lines are led back into the cabin, and **all sail handling, including reefing, is worked from the helm station with no need to go forward** — a defining, recurring theme — see [rig and sail](/components/rig/index.md). The stock **rolling-boom furling system** is the design's single most-discussed mechanical feature: it works from inside the cabin, but the stock gear needs a **topping lift** to be practical (the field-proven fix); Matt Layden himself instead furls using a simple **foot-pressure technique** on the furling line and halyard, freeing both hands — see [furling system](/components/rig/furling.md). In light air, tiller-rope friction lets the boat hold a course unattended well enough for the solo sailor to leave the helm briefly — see [self-steering](/operations/self-steering.md).
* **Yuloh (sculling oar) auxiliary propulsion** — a stowable [yuloh](/components/yuloh.md) and the lowered mast/sail are both retained on deck by brass tube loops, one to a side — see [Deck — stowage loops for the yuloh and lowered rig](/components/deck.md#stowage-loops-for-the-yuloh-and-lowered-rig).
* **Single-thickness plywood construction** — a stitch-and-glue-adjacent plank-on-bulkhead plywood hull with an **18 mm ply bottom and 12 mm ply sides**, epoxy-glued and fastened from a 10-sheet, four-thickness bill of materials — see [hull scantlings](/design/hull-scantlings.md) and [construction](/construction/index.md) for the build sequence (bulkheads and stem set up, side panels pulled to the stem and dry-fit/beveled, then glued, faired, epoxy-sealed, and painted).
* **Fully enclosed cabin, sailed from within** — the boat is sailed single-handed from an enclosed cabin, apparently fitted with a small cabin stove; the interior includes open bin panels between bulkheads #1 and #2, a battery behind the port panel, and a toggle-hinged floor — see [interior bins and floor](/components/interior.md).

Further design detail (rudder/chine experiments, furling, glass-bottom idea, and the enlarged/derivative [variants](/design/variants/index.md) family) lives under [design](/design/index.md).

A recurring owner-reported limitation, from a comparative appraisal by an owner who sailed one of Matt's other designs for years and set it against the whole family, is a **hesitancy going about from starboard tack to port tack** — attributed to the mast being offset to port with the sail set on the port side of the mast, adding drag on that side through the turn — and a related complaint that she is **"not the most spontaneous sailboat for launching and recovering from the water, particularly when being managed by a lone crew,"** making casual day-sailing from a road trailer less practical solo than with some other owners' more purpose-built trailer fittings.[1] See [chine runners and windward performance](/chine-runners/windward-performance.md) for the fuller tacking-behavior record this criticism sits alongside.

Blue-water capability is an **open, debated question** rather than a settled fact, argued both ways by comparing Paradox to the structurally different "Bris"/Sofia microcruisers — see [blue-water suitability](/design/blue-water-suitability.md). The boat's routine real-world benchmark is coastal/open-water passages such as the Gulf Stream crossing to the Bahamas (see "Capability at a glance" below), not a confirmed ocean-crossing pedigree.

# Capability at a glance

* Home-buildable in a **small workspace** (as little as ~2 ft longer than the boat). One build, "Zoë", was first estimated (July 2001) at roughly ~500 hours; a fuller, superseding account (January 2002, given twice — once summarizing the whole project, and again in answer to a later question) puts the true total at **around 1,000 hours over about two years** of on-and-off work — see [build effort](/construction/build-effort.md).
* A capable pocket cruiser: at least one Paradox has completed the [Texas 200](/merits/texas-200.md#the-texas-200-and-a-port-townsend-wooden-boat-show-showing), and a Paradox has been sailed on **Exuma Sound in 25 kts of wind at ~6 kts** under heavily reefed sail with the crew below (see [rig and sail](/components/rig/index.md)). The **Gulf Stream crossing to the Bahamas** is recorded as the Paradox's standard real-world open-water benchmark — routine enough to be cited as tougher than a Southern California-to-Catalina crossing (see [Southern California and the Catalina crossing question](/operations/southern-california-and-catalina-crossing.md)) — and Matt Layden himself is reported (secondhand, unconfirmed) to have lived aboard his own Paradox for two to three years cruising from Nova Scotia to the lower Bahamas (see [Matt Layden (biography)](/matt-layden/biography.md)).
* Built **tough and simply**: the designer built the original in **~4 months**, hauling materials to a remote woods site by bicycle trailer and launching it down a ravine (see [original Paradox](/boats/original-paradox.md)).
