# Overview

The **Paradox** is a small, home-buildable plywood microcruiser sailboat
designed by **Matt Layden**. It is the subject of the `paradoxbuilders` /
`paradoxsailboat` mailing-list community whose archive this bundle summarizes.

This concept is the **trunk** of the bundle: broad, well-supported facts about
the boat live here or are promoted here as evidence accumulates; specific
details live in the linked branches below.

# Defining features

The boat's now-well-documented, recurring characteristics — each backed by a
mature branch of the tree:

* **Chine runners — the boat's signature feature** — the tapered fins running
  along the hull's chine line are the first thing most people notice about
  Paradox and the innovation that most defines it: with **no centerboard or
  daggerboard**, they (with the heeled hull and the big rudder) supply *all* the
  lateral resistance that lets a flat-bottomed, shallow-draft boat sail to
  windward, quantified by Matt Layden's own sea trial (VMGW ≈ 2.2–2.3 kt at ~51°
  off the wind) and called by Sven Yrvind "the greatest innovation for small
  cruising boats in the last century." They have their own branch — see
  [chine runners](/chine-runners/index.md).
* **Heavy internal ballast, low in the hull** — a small, heavy boat with a lot
  of stability: most weight sits low **in the bilge** (≈930 lb of a ≈1,365 lb
  loaded boat, including **400 lb of lead** as ten 40 lb pigs), so builders
  expect it to "plough through" a 2–4 ft chop rather than be knocked about;
  the waterplane sinks the hull only ~1 inch per 200 lb added; a correctly
  ballasted boat is claimed to **self-right from 165° of heel**, a figure now
  corroborated by two independent sources (a Bolduc-published interview and
  a builder's own plans/articles paperwork) — see
  [weight and ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md).
* **Simple lug-type rig on a rolling boom, worked entirely from the enclosed
  helm** — a lugsail-type sail whose yard is held to the mast by the halyard
  alone (apparently no parrel), set on a rolling boom; the mast steps through
  reinforced deck [mast partners](/design/mast-partners.md); running rigging
  and control lines are led back into the cabin, and **all sail handling,
  including reefing, is worked from the helm station with no need to go
  forward** — a defining, recurring theme confirmed by builders — see
  [rig and sail](/design/rig.md). The stock **rolling-boom furling system**
  is the design's single most-discussed mechanical feature: it works from
  inside the cabin, but several builders found the stock gear needed a
  **topping lift** to be practical (the field-proven fix); Matt Layden
  himself instead furls using a simple **foot-pressure technique** on the
  furling line and halyard, freeing both hands — see
  [furling system](/design/furling-system.md). In light air, tiller-rope
  friction lets the boat hold a course unattended well enough for the solo
  sailor to leave the helm briefly — see
  [self-steering](/operations/self-steering.md).
* **Yuloh (sculling oar) auxiliary propulsion** — a stowable sculling oar and
  the lowered mast/sail are both retained on deck by brass tube loops, one to
  a side — see
  [yuloh and stowed-rig retention](/design/yuloh-and-stowed-rig-retention.md).
* **Single-thickness plywood construction** — a stitch-and-glue-adjacent
  plank-on-bulkhead plywood hull with an **18 mm ply bottom and 12 mm ply
  sides**, epoxy-glued and fastened from a 10-sheet, four-thickness bill of
  materials — see [hull scantlings](/design/hull-scantlings.md) and
  [construction](/construction/index.md) for the build sequence (bulkheads and
  stem set up, side panels pulled to the stem and dry-fit/beveled, then
  glued, faired, epoxy-sealed, and painted).
* **Fully enclosed cabin, sailed from within** — the boat is sailed
  single-handed from an enclosed cabin, apparently fitted with a small cabin
  stove; the interior includes open bin panels between bulkheads #1 and #2, a
  battery behind the port panel, and a toggle-hinged floor — see
  [interior bins and floor](/design/interior-bins-and-floor.md).

Further design detail (rudder/chine experiments, furling, glass-bottom idea,
and the enlarged/derivative [variants](/design/variants/index.md) family) lives
under [design](/design/index.md).

Blue-water capability is an **open, debated question** rather than a settled
fact: builders have argued both sides, comparing Paradox to Sven Yrvind's
structurally different "Bris"/Sofia microcruisers — see
[blue-water suitability](/design/blue-water-suitability.md). The boat's
routine real-world benchmark is coastal/open-water passages such as the
Gulf Stream crossing to the Bahamas (see "Capability at a glance" below), not
a confirmed ocean-crossing pedigree.

# Capability at a glance

* Home-buildable in a **small workspace** (as little as ~2 ft longer than the
  boat). Builder [Glen C. Maxwell](/people/glen-maxwell.md) first estimated
  (July 2001) roughly ~500 hours to build his "Zoë"; his own fuller,
  superseding account (January 2002, given twice — once summarizing the whole
  project, and again directly to a later questioner) puts the true total at
  **around 1,000 hours over about two years** of on-and-off work — see
  [build effort](/construction/build-effort.md).
* A capable pocket cruiser: at least one Paradox has completed the
  [Texas 200](/operations/texas-200.md), and a Paradox has been sailed on
  **Exuma Sound in 25 kts of wind at ~6 kts** under heavily reefed sail with the
  crew below (see [rig and sail](/design/rig.md)). The **Gulf Stream crossing
  to the Bahamas** is recorded by two independent builders as the Paradox's
  standard real-world open-water benchmark — routine enough to be cited as
  tougher than a Southern California-to-Catalina crossing (see
  [Southern California and the Catalina crossing question](/operations/southern-california-and-catalina-crossing.md))
  — and Matt Layden himself is reported (secondhand, unconfirmed) to have
  lived aboard his own Paradox for two to three years cruising from Nova
  Scotia to the lower Bahamas (see
  [Matt Layden (biography)](/matt-layden/biography.md)).
* Built **tough and simply**: the designer built the original in **~4 months**,
  hauling materials to a remote woods site by bicycle trailer and launching it
  down a ravine (see [original Paradox](/boats/original-paradox.md)).
