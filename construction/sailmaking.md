# Sailmaking (home-built lug sail)

This concept documents how the lug sail for the [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) — a Matt Layden micro-cruiser closely related to the Paradox — is lofted, cut, and sewn at home. It complements the design-level [rig and sail](/components/rig/index.md) concept (which covers the Paradox's own simpler, flatter-cut sail, sourcing a sail commercially, and a capsule account of this same home-sewing effort) and [furling system](/components/rig/furling.md) (which covers how the finished sail is reefed and stowed on the rolling boom); those pages cover the *design*, this one covers the *craft* of actually building the cloth. It also extends [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md)'s own sail-dimensions table (the sail this article is about) and its note that the sail is broadseamed and has a segmented leech.

## Sail history: four generations, 1988–2008

Little Cruiser has had, in order:

1. **1988** — the **original sail**, made by **Matt Layden** himself. This sail still exists and serves as the **loft pattern and baseline** for every sail made since — as late as 2008 it was spread back out on the floor to recheck all the measurements before starting a new one.
2. **1994** — a **professionally made replacement sail**. It was **cut too flat** and its **leech stretched badly**; it was **retired after just one season**.
3. **1998** — the **first home-made sail**, sewn after the 1994 sail's failure.
4. **2008** — a **new sail project**, begun earlier in the year, paused for a cruise, then resumed that October — documented in the photo log referenced throughout this page.

This is a more granular history than [rig and sail](/components/rig/index.md) records for this same boat: there, the account telescopes the 1994 failure and the 1998 rebuild into a single retelling (an expensive **Gambell and Hunter** sail that lasted one season because its leech was "cut on the bias and went slack," prompting the purchase of a **$250 old commercial zig-zag sewing machine** and an exact home-sewn copy of Matt's original). The two accounts corroborate each other on the essential point — a commercially bought sail failed at the leech and was replaced with a home-built copy of Matt Layden's 1988 original.

## Design principle: building draft into flat cloth

Sailcloth panels start out flat. Shape (draft, or camber) is built into the finished sail by **joining those flat panels along seams that are not cut dead straight** — either by **broadseaming** (overlapping the seam allowance by a varying amount along its length) or by using **tapered panels**. The effect is the same one you get pinching a flat napkin between two fingers: a flat sheet develops a 3D curve wherever the seam takes in more material than a straight line would.

How much draft to build in is itself a design choice tied to the boat: a **heavy, slow-moving boat like Little Cruiser** carries a sail with **a lot of draft (camber)**, while **lighter, faster boats — multihulls in particular — carry flatter sails with much less draft**. This is the same distinction [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) draws when comparing this sail to Paradox's own simpler, flatter-cut lug: a sailmaker unfamiliar with small heavy cruisers might otherwise deliver a flat, low-draft sail suited to something like a Sunfish rather than a boat that needs to be dragged along by its rig.

## Lofting: tracing the old sail on a concrete slab

Rather than drafting a new shape from scratch, each new sail is **lofted directly off the old one**:

- The old sail is spread out on a **concrete slab**.
- The **four corners are pulled taut and taped down** with duct tape.
- The rest of the sail is then **taped down every foot** with blue masking tape, so it lies flat and true.
- Every edge is **traced in pencil**.
- Everything is then **measured**: the **location, shape, and width of every broadseam** (a broadseam's *shape* — not just how much material it takes in — determines the resulting sail shape; broadseams at the foot, for instance, flared out significantly, producing a very full/round shape there), plus the position of every **corner patch, reef patch, and batten**.
- Lines are drawn connecting the corners to establish a **baseline** for the new sail.

### Checking the yard's bend to shape the head

Because this is a **lug rig** (see [rig and sail](/components/rig/index.md) for how the yard is fastened to the mast and sail), the **head** of the sail is set by the **yard**, a spar with its own natural bend. Before cutting the head panel, the yard's actual curve — not just the lofted pattern — is checked to determine the best shape for the head of the sail; the yard's own bend, not just the pattern, informs how much round is cut into that edge.

The segmented leech is worked in more procedural detail than the general lofting method above: a **test center leech panel** is made out of **rosin paper**, its **miter cuts marked**, and the **top leech panel is cut with two miters**, followed by assembling and mitering the adjoining panels (a top miter and a bottom miter, worked and completed in turn). This matches [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md)'s already-recorded sail dimensions, where the **leech is segmented into three sections** (1590 mm, 1615 mm, 1620 mm) and the **two horizontal leech seams are tapered** — the miter-and-taper work described here is how those segments and seams are actually cut and joined.

## Cloth and panel assembly

- Cloth: **36"-wide, 4 oz Dacron**.
- Panels are laid over the lofted marks with a **6" overlap** all the way around each seam.
- Panel direction is **alternated** from one panel to the next as they are laid down.
- Broadseams are drawn onto the cloth in pencil, using a **wooden batten** to fair the curve by eye.
- Panels are stuck together with a special **double-sided seam tape** — chosen specifically because it is **repositionable**, so a seam can be peeled up and adjusted before it is committed to with stitching.
- Before any sewing, the taped-together sail is **"inflated"** — picked up and **shaken so it fills with air** like a parachute — as a quick visual fairness check, catching any lumpy or uneven seams while they can still be repositioned.

Only after the taped sail passes this shake-test does the panel layout get retaped flat to the slab, realigned to the original marks, and its outer edges (foot, luff, head, leech) drawn in per the lofted measurements — again faired in with a batten — before being cut free.

## Sewing rig: a gravity-fed, hand-crank Singer

The sewing setup is deliberately low-tech and improvised around a single **heavy, inexpensive, roughly 75-year-old industrial zigzag Singer sewing machine**, onto which a **hand crank** was mounted:

- The sail is **rolled up on each side of the seam** being sewn, leaving just the seam itself exposed and manageable.
- A **sheet of plastic** is laid over a **small hill** next to the concrete lofting slab.
- The hand-crank Singer sits at the top of that slope, on the plastic.
- As the crank is turned, **gravity pulls the rolled sail down the hill and through the machine** — the hill does the work of feeding the sail's bulk through a domestic-scale machine instead of it having to be wrestled by hand.

This setup is consistent with, and gives the missing mechanical detail behind, the mailing-list account of buying an "old commercial zig-zag sewing machine" for $250 to copy Matt Layden's sail (see [rig and sail](/components/rig/index.md)).

### Corner patches and finishing

- Corner patches are cut from **extra scrap sail cloth**, with the patch's **weave kept aligned** to the weave of the panel it reinforces (not cut at a random angle).
- Each patch is first **tacked in place with a light coat of 3M 77 spray adhesive**, holding it accurately positioned while it is then **sewn** on permanently.
- **Tablings** (the folded/reinforced hem along each edge) are sewn on.
- **Batten pockets** are made and sewn in.
- **Webbing** is added at the **clew, tack, throat, head, and reef points** for these high-load corners and reef patches to grab onto.

## Reference recommended

***The Sailmaker's Apprentice*** by **Emiliano Marino** stands out as the one book that proved invaluable to the effort, praised for its detailed guidance specifically for **four-sided (lug) sails** — the same book recommended in the mailing-list sailmaking thread (see [rig and sail](/components/rig/index.md)). **Sailrite** kits are a lower-effort alternative, coming with panels **precut and seams pre-marked**, making them quicker to assemble than lofting and cutting a sail entirely from scratch as described above.

## Matt Layden's sailmaking recipe for a more weatherly Paradox sail

[Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)'s first-ever post to the `paradoxbuilders` mailing list (see [matt-layden/biography.md](/matt-layden/biography.md) for the fuller record of that landmark post) laid out his full dimensions and technique for a **new sail he made for the original Paradox in February 2003** — the only sailmaking document on record written by the designer himself, distinct from the home-sewing account of copying Matt's much earlier (1988) sail documented throughout the rest of this page.

### The new sail's dimensions and design intent

Matt experimented with the overall shape relative to the sail as drawn in the plans: he **shortened the foot** (reducing the boom's overhang) and **raised the peak** (increasing aspect ratio). The trade-off: the new cut does not reef and furl quite as neatly as the original, which had prioritized that over set — a triangle of sail at the peak no longer rolls up with the rest and must be manually scrunched up and stuffed under the boom when stowing. In exchange, the new sail sets **slightly better and points more weatherly**, both at full hoist and reefed. Matt endorsed the original design too: "I still stand by the [old] sail as in the plans, it works fine and if you have one that is well cut there is no need to change it, but I would go with the new version again if making another."

Overall dimensions, as laid out on the loft floor (outside corners of the quadrilateral, including the flattened head and foot sleeves — the sail's actual line is these measurements minus the sleeve width and a seam allowance):

| Edge | Length |
|---|---|
| Foot | 3180 mm |
| Luff | 2630 mm |
| Leech | 3700 mm |
| Head | 2580 mm |
| Tack to peak | 4150 mm |
| Throat to clew | 4322 mm |

### Cloth, seams, and broadseaming

With **36 in (914 mm) cloth**, the sail is cut in **3 panels/seams**, laid out at 90 degrees from the leech (a vertical cut is acceptable for such a low-aspect sail, but the standard horizontal-cut convention cloth is designed for is stronger and stretches less predictably even though wider cloth is now commonly available). A **25 mm seam overlap** is used — wide enough for **3 runs of zigzag stitching**, with room left to adjust the broadseam later. Broadseam schedule (amount of overlap at the luff, mid-panel, and leech, plus the curve's character):

- **Bottom seam**: 65 mm at the luff, 25 mm at 50%, 45 mm at the leech; a very full-ended curve, flattish in the middle.
- **Middle seam**: 45 mm at the luff, 25 mm at 45%, 37 mm at the leech; a rather parabolic curve, stronger in the middle than at the ends.
- **Top seam**: 38 mm at the luff, 25 mm at 40%, 33 mm at the leech; about a circular arc.

### Rounds — correcting the outline after seaming

Once the seamed panels are laid back on the floor marks, a further set of corrections is made to the outline itself:

- **Foot round**: 28 mm total (14 mm to the sail, 14 mm to the sleeve), maximum at 50% from the tack, a very full-ended curve similar to the bottom seam.
- **Head round**: 40 mm total (20 mm to the sail, 20 mm to the sleeve), maximum at 35% from the throat, a full-ended curve nearer a circular arc than the foot. This figure was tuned to Matt's own **hollow spruce yard**, which he judged a little stiffer than the yard detailed in the plans — a builder with a more flexible yard should start with 10-15 mm more head round and 4-5 mm more top-seam broadseam, since it is easier to flatten a sail afterward than to add fullness; alternatively, a too-flat head can be corrected by stiffening the yard instead (see the related [rig and sail](/components/rig/index.md) discussion on yard-bowing and stiffening).
- **Luff round**: about 8-10 mm at 40% up from the tack, a circular arc or slightly parabolic — this round flows back into the sail's belly, and the luff becomes hollow in use.
- **Leech hollow**: about 50 mm at 60% up from the clew, a parabolic curve, slightly stronger in the top section than the bottom.

### Corner patches, cut tabling, and the luff tape

Foot and head sleeves get **doublers at each end** before being sewn on, extending 25-30 cm past the sleeve edge into the sail body to double as corner patches (staggered slightly so one side is larger than the other) — two layers being all these lightly-stressed corners need. The **tack patch** runs well up the luff and about 25 cm aft, forming a wear patch where the sail bears on the mast when reefed (the same mast-bearing contact point documented from actual sailing experience in [rig and sail — sail bearing on the mast and mast offset](/components/rig/index.md)). Sleeves are sewn on long, then trimmed back flush with luff and leech.

For the **leech**, a **cut tabling** is used: an extra ~20 cm of cloth is left at the leech during layout and seaming, then cut off and re-sewn as the tabling/hem — stitched exactly along the marked leech line (25 mm forward of the cut edge), then folded 360 degrees around so the two 25 mm flaps are enclosed between the sail and an 80 mm tabling flap, before being sewn through with 3-4 runs of stitching. The result: a narrow (25 mm) 4-thickness band followed by a wider (80 mm) 2-layer band — a tapered thickness that gives good support, the right stretch behavior, and resistance to wear from fluttering.

The **luff tape** is either a folded piece of sailcloth or two layers of 2 in (51 mm) tape, built up into a **4-layer strip** (two 25 mm layers, one 35 mm, one 45 mm) to taper the thickness into the sail body. The luff should **not be roped** as in traditional sailmaking, since roping would clump on the boom and spoil the set when reefed. The key technique is to **pre-tension the luff tape** before attaching it: clamp each end, stretch it snugly along the floor to strong points, measure its clamp-to-clamp length, then stretch it a further 20-25 mm beyond that relaxed length using a tackle or comealong. The sailcloth's luff is then basted into the tape's center fold (double-sided carpet tape or contact cement) under very little tension — when the stretched tape is released and finally sewn on, the luff scrunches up slightly but pulls back out under sailing tension when hoisted. The luff tape must be genuinely strong, since it carries real load even where the adjacent sailcloth, an inch away, is only lightly loaded — it also runs around one side of the foot and head sleeves and is sewn back onto itself there, with a short loop of webbing or folded tape at each sleeve end to take the tensioning lashings to the boom and yard.

Matt candidly noted this recipe only "scratches the surface of sailmaking," recommending a proper sailmaking book or a local sailmaker's advice for anyone missing the finer illustrated detail. The post was archived to the group's files section for future builders approaching the point of ordering or sewing their own sail — see [matt-layden/biography.md](/matt-layden/biography.md) for the fuller thread, including firsthand confirmation from someone who had recently sailed the original Paradox that "this new sail sets very nicely."

The luff tape is not a mere accessory but the sail's actual load path against the mast, carrying real tension even where the adjacent cloth an inch away is lightly loaded — which is why the design calls for the tape to be genuinely strong and properly pre-tensioned before being sewn on.[16] Luff tape is treated as a given of ordinary sailmaking practice rather than an optional detail a builder or loft would plausibly omit — no Paradox sail, or any other sail, has been seen built without it.[19]

### A suspected cause of creasing in the newer sail plan, and the case for a professional loft

Reports of the taller, higher-peaked sail plan **creasing** in use point to a likely cause in the luff tape: without it, the luff shrinks in light air and then stretches again as the sail fills, producing exactly the wrinkling reported. Since Matt Layden's own recipe calls for the luff tape by name, the likely explanation is its omission — whether by a home sailmaker skipping the step or by a professional loft not given the full specification.[17] A sail commissioned from the professional loft Jekylls of Wroxham — which had prior familiarity with the Paradox from its website and was given all of Matt's supplied technical information — was expected to include luff tape, though the point was not independently confirmed by inspection at the time.[18] No sail, Paradox or otherwise, is known to have been built without luff tape — it is standard sailmaking practice a builder or loft would not plausibly omit, which narrows the creasing question to sizing rather than the tape's presence or absence.[19] The originally reported creasing problem itself was left open on record, the question of size closing without a confirmed answer either way.[20] Two independent size datapoints subsequently closed the sizing question: a Dabbler Sails-built sail carries a **1 in or 2 in** tape folded in half over the luff,[21] and the Jekylls of Wroxham sail confirmed to carry luff tape after all carries a **25 mm** tape enclosed inside a folded hem, matching in width and thickness on both luff and leech though sewn with different stitch patterns (two rows of parallel zigzag on one edge, straight stitching on the other), the enclosed tape expected to carry a bit of stretch of its own.[22][23][24]

## Matching Matt's 4.5 oz Dacron cloth weight

A separate note addressed **cloth weight**, distinct from Matt's own dimensional/technique recipe above: Little Cruiser's existing sail (documented earlier on this page) was sewn from **Sailrite's 3.9 oz red Dacron**, which proved adequate, but the *next* sail was planned to switch to the same **4.5 oz cloth Matt used** for his new Paradox sail, expected to prove **more durable and stretch-resistant** — available from **Defender Industries** at a reasonable price. Newcomers to sailmaking are pointed toward the same `microcruising.com` "Sailmaking" article this page already draws on. See [matt-layden/biography.md](/matt-layden/biography.md) for the thread this follow-up continues.

## A cruising boat needs more draft than a light daysailer's lug

When Matt Layden's February 2003 sailmaking recipe (above) was reposted to the list in April 2007 for a UK builder about to order a new sail from his own local sailmaker, the repost came with a standing caution for briefing any sailmaker unfamiliar with the design: a Paradox's lug sail needs **noticeably more draft than a lightweight daysailer's** would call for, since Paradox is a heavy cruising boat rather than a light performance dinghy — the same design distinction [sailmaking](#design-principle-building-draft-into-flat-cloth) draws generally between draft suited to heavy, slow boats and the flatter cut suited to light, fast ones. A sailmaker given only generic lug-sail conventions, without this steer, risks delivering a sail cut too flat for the boat it is actually going on.[25]

## Lessons learned

- The whole process is **time-consuming**.
- It **did not save much money** compared to buying a sail.
- It did produce a **better sail**: **triple-stitched** and **heavily reinforced** beyond what could have been bought.
- The sewing and pattern-making skills learned making sails **carried over directly** to other home-sewing projects aboard — **cushions, awnings, and storage bags**.
- A **sturdy machine and a large open workspace** both make the job meaningfully easier — a practical corollary of the gravity-fed hill-and-hand-crank rig described above.
