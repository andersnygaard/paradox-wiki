# Painting preparation and paint-system choice

Painting an epoxy-coated [Paradox](/paradox.md) is not primarily cosmetic: paint's most important job on this kind of hull is protecting the cured epoxy from ultraviolet light, and without that protection the sun will strip the epoxy off the boat.[1] What follows is the sequence of decisions and preparation that lead up to a paint job — colour, product type, surface preparation, and priming — plus what actually needs painting inside the cabin and the mechanics of laying on the final coats; for the broader debate over which specific topside and bottom paints to use, see [topside and hull painting choices](/construction/topside-painting.md) and [bottom painting over epoxy](/construction/bottom-painting.md).

## Six decisions, in order

A paint job over epoxy comes down to six linked decisions, each with its own reasoning.[1]

1. **Colour.** A paint maker's representative held that only white paint will stand up to the sun, particularly in southern waters; a coloured waterline stripe is unlikely to cause any problem.
2. **Lifespan.** Marine paint gives three to five years of service before it needs repainting; semi-gloss latex house paint gives one to two years. Neither chips nor flakes off in that time, and on a hull with as little surface area as Paradox's, repainting every year is no great burden.
3. **Type.** Any marine paint will work, though an oil-base paint takes more preparation to repaint than an enamel does — and any paint that contains alkyd and calls itself a "polyurethane" is still an alkyd paint underneath the marketing. A single-part epoxy topside enamel, Pettit "Easypoxy," is easy to apply and gives a beautiful finish; a semi-gloss latex house paint, Sears Weatherbeater, also works, though it stays tacky for a long time, a common problem with modern latex paints.
4. **Surface preparation.** The order is not optional: wash the surface first, then sand it lightly. Sanding before washing embeds the amine-blush particles left by curing epoxy into the surface instead of removing them, so the surface needs to be washed clean of blush before it is sanded — sometimes it's best to give it a couple of good washes before sanding at all (System Three epoxy in particular produced very little amine blush to begin with). See [epoxy pre-coating technique](/construction/epoxy-coating-technique.md) for the matching wash-then-sand sequence used between epoxy coats earlier in the build.
5. **Whether a primer is needed.** Professional advice holds that a primer isn't strictly necessary if the surface preparation was done properly — but three reasons argue for using one anyway: it covers the dark colour of the epoxy-coated hull, it gives a base for sanding and spot puttying (see [puttying and fairing](/construction/puttying-and-fairing.md)), and most undercoats are engineered to grip a surface better than paint alone does.
6. **Which primer.** An epoxy manufacturer ran adhesion tests on a range of house primers to settle this: paint a sample board with the primer, cut a crosshatch pattern through it with a knife, stick on a strip of high-adhesion tape, then lift the tape — a primer failed if it came away with the tape. Nine house primers passed. For interior work, a fast-drying, water-based primer/sealer — Zinsser Bullseye 1-2-3, dry in 30 to 45 minutes and top-coatable within 60 — was used throughout. For exterior work, a marine high-build brushing undercoat, Pettit No. 6148 or 6149, was preferred.

## What needs painting inside

Once the epoxy coating is done, the practical answer to how much of the interior needs paint is: not much.[1] Nothing forward of bulkhead #2 needs paint at all, since no sun reaches there. The bilges are best left unpainted so the wood and the condition of the epoxy stay visible underneath — recoating is then simply a matter of sanding and adding a coat, and any repair to the ply or lumber isn't hidden behind a paint film. The full main-cabin painting list comes to seven items: the four side bins, the carlins, the food bin doors (tops only), bulkheads #2, #3, and #4, the water tank top, the two sides of the vent trunk, and the baffle (weather side only) — primed, then given two coats of semi-gloss, brushed on with a foam brush.

**Matt Layden**'s own instruction on timing this work is to "do as much 'inside' work as possible while it's accessible," because "after the deck is on it will take four times as long."

Two small practical notes from the same stage: saved water-tank access-port cut-outs can be wrapped in rag or paper towel to block the holes and keep dust out of the tanks while painting; and foam brushes left standing in water will shed the foam off the handle.

## Final painting

The high-build undercoat goes on with a foam roller, is sanded smooth, and is followed by the single-part epoxy topcoat; clean the roller with mineral spirits between uses.[2] Because these paints are expensive, don't roll the roller out on the upper part of the tray as is usually recommended — load the roller from the bottom of the tray and roll it out on the hull itself, since painting the tray is simply wasted paint. On the topcoat it works best to have a second person follow along behind with a wide, good-quality foam brush, smoothing out the small bubbles the roller leaves; two topcoats are plenty. A secondhand-boat refit's own paint schedule bears this out independently: most surfaces there needed at least three coats — one undercoat and two upper coats — to bring a rough hull up to a presentable finish.[4]

The bottom paint goes directly onto the epoxy: don't carry the topcoat below the waterline, and take the bottom paint two to three inches above it. See [bottom painting over epoxy](/construction/bottom-painting.md) for bonding antifouling durably to the epoxy at that boundary.

## Painting the mast, not varnishing it

The [mast](/components/rig/mast.md) should not be varnished. It is removed and stowed regularly, and will be nicked and scraped in that process; varnish will not stand up to the sun's rays, and redoing a varnished mast in paint later is a big job. Epoxy and white paint from the start avoids all of that.[3]
