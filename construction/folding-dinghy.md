# Folding dinghy (the "Micro Folding Dinghy")

The **Micro Folding Dinghy** is a home-built tender: a flat-folding plywood-and-canvas dinghy that stows aboard the micro-cruiser and unfolds for shore access. It is **based on one of [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)'s early folding dinghies**, with the design "incorporating some of Matt's ideas plus the authors' own" refinements [1]. It is carried aboard [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) as the boat's tender.

## Sizing

The design as drawn is best suited to smaller people or children. It can be scaled up for larger people by increasing beam and freeboard by roughly 2 inches [1].

## Materials

- **Hull panels**: 3 pieces of **1/4" exterior-grade fir plywood** (AC grade) — two side panels and one bottom panel [1].
- **Hinges**: 2"-wide heavy-duty **#4 canvas cloth** [1].
- **Rubrails**: ash or other rot-resistant, flexible wood, **1" wide x 1/8"–3/16" thick x 57-3/4" long**, epoxy-glued to the outside of the port and starboard panels (mirror images of each other) [1]. See [fastening and gluing](/construction/fastening-and-gluing.md).
- **End panels**: 10–12 oz canvas/duck cloth is recommended over vinyl, since 3M 5200 did not adhere well to the vinyl originally tried [1].
- **Adhesive/fastenings**: 3M 5200 adhesive/sealant and 1/4" stainless steel staples.

## Construction sequence

### Laying out the panels

Two sheets of 1/4" AC exterior fir plywood are selected with minimal knots (a single sheet may suffice as an alternate layout). Baselines are drawn and points transferred from the plans; a batten is bent around the points to draw the outline of one side panel and one bottom panel [1].

### Finger-joint hinge tabs

A line is extended 3/8" out from the chines on both panels to locate the finger joints/tabs. Tabs are marked every 6" from bow to stern. The last tab on the side panel is **12" long x 2" wide**, and the last tab on the bottom panel is **12" long x 3/8" wide** — both elongated to form the skegs at the stern [1].

The side panel is cut and alternating tabs are removed (the first tab and the skeg tabs are left intact); this is duplicated on the second sheet, with the panels marked port and starboard. "Spreader" holes are drilled with a 3/8" bit. The bottom panel is cut with the *opposite* tab pattern removed (first tab and every other one), so that the side and bottom tabs interlock with each other [1].

### Signature detail: canvas-cloth hinges

The dinghy's defining construction feature is its hinge: **2" wide cloth hinges made from heavy duty #4 canvas which are reinforced by a series of interlocking finger joints/tabs every 6 inches. At the stern the tabs are elongated to form skegs** [1]. The interlocking plywood tabs carry the structural load of the joint while the canvas provides the actual folding hinge, so the hull can fold flat for stowage yet hold its shape when unfolded.

### Temporary assembly

Before the hinges are bonded, the panels are held in position with **duct tape and wood blocks** — sides initially perpendicular to the bottom, to be flared out later via the spreader sticks. Wood braces/blocks are screwed in place clear of the hinge line [1].

### Bonding the hinges

The area 1" from each hinge line is masked off to contain the adhesive. **3M 5200** is applied to each side of the hinge line, the 2" canvas hinge strip is pressed into place, and it is tacked every inch with **1/4" stainless steel staples**, leaving 1–2" of canvas overhang at each end (to be tied into the end panels later). Excess 5200 is wiped away with mineral spirits [1].

The 3M 5200 is left to cure fully for several days before anything is moved. The hinge flex is then checked; if a hinge is stiff, excess cured adhesive is carefully trimmed from the outside of the hinge line, taking care not to nick the canvas [1]. See [fastening and gluing](/construction/fastening-and-gluing.md) for more on 5200 as a bonding agent elsewhere in the bundle.

### End panels

A template is made from thin cardboard and traced onto the end-panel cloth. Canvas or duck cloth (10–12 oz) is recommended over vinyl, since **3M 5200 didn't adhere well to the vinyl** used on an earlier attempt [1]. Margins are extended 1-1/4" before cutting, and corners are removed per the plan's page 1. The top hem is folded over and sewn, and the panel is attached to the hull ends with 3M 5200 and staples. The hinge overhang is tied in with 3M 5200, and the whole hinge is waterproofed by rubbing 3M 5200 into its full length [1].

### Spreader sticks and finishing

Spreader sticks are beveled fore-and-aft and up-and-down to fit the angled sides, then test-fit and trimmed [1]. Screw holes are filled, the hull is sanded, and it is varnished or painted [1].

### Tow line

Two 3/16" holes are drilled on each side panel near the bow top, and 1/8" cord is passed through and knotted on each end (knots outside). The cord is **deliberately undersized**: anything larger would prevent the sides from folding down easily when the dinghy is collapsed for stowage [1].

## Role in the cruising system

The folding dinghy stows flat aboard the micro-cruiser and unfolds on demand for shore access — a practical necessity for a boat as small as [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md), which has no room to carry a rigid tender. Its folding action has also been documented at anchor in trip photos, separate from the build article itself.
