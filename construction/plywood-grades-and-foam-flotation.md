# Plywood grade and foam flotation debate

A [Paradox](/paradox.md) hull, and its bonded interior foam flotation, can be built from several different plywood grades — ordinary exterior-grade fir, true marine-grade fir, a check-resistant hardwood marine ply such as gaboon, mahogany, or Meranti/Okoume, or MDO/HDO overlay ply for interior work — and the tradeoffs among them were re-argued repeatedly across the archive. The settled answer: exterior-grade fir plywood, sealed and glassed with epoxy, is structurally sufficient and is what [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)'s own plans specify, but it is more prone to face-veneer **checking** and costs more in finishing labor than marine grade or a check-resistant hardwood ply, which cost only a little more up front. Foam flotation, a related but separate question, is safe to bond directly over epoxy-coated fir plywood of any grade and should not be left out of a Paradox; the open choices are foam type, installation method, and placement.

## Choosing a plywood grade: the settled guidance

Four grades of plywood recur in Paradox builds:

- **Exterior-grade ("AC" or "A/B") fir plywood.** Bonded with the same waterproof glue as marine grade — the grading difference is veneer quality and how much core void is tolerated. [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)'s own plans specify exterior grade, and it is what several of the design's oldest boats, including his own boats built before Paradox, were built from, sealed with epoxy inside and fiberglassed outside.[47] Exterior grade is structurally adequate for a boat this size; its real cost shows up as **labor** — it takes noticeably more work than marine grade to bring to a fine finish, and it is more prone to face-veneer checking.
- **Marine-grade fir plywood**, manufactured free of core voids specifically to avoid the checking problem. West System's own construction manual does not recommend Douglas fir plywood below Marine grade, warning that lower grades often carry face-veneer cracks that "can cause severe checking to occur in a finished surface."[2] Even Marine grade, though, is not fully immune — fir plywood can still check over time, especially in exposed storage or under only a single layer of glass, so Marine grade narrows the checking risk rather than eliminating it.[9]
- **A check-resistant hardwood-faced marine ply** — **gaboon, mahogany, or Meranti/Okoume, ideally certified to BS1088** (the lower BS6065 standard is usable but a step down; uncertified plywood merely labeled "marine" by a seller should be avoided outright, since its quality can be worse than construction-grade stock — one recorded sheet sold as "marine ply" turned out only stamped AC exterior[4][5]). These woods do not check whether epoxy-coated or bare, though like all tropical hardwood plywood they need religious epoxy encapsulation to last — in which case either will outlast fir "given the same level of care."[9][12] Okoume in particular runs close to half the weight of cheaper marine-grade lauan, which matters for stores capacity and for how much glass/epoxy a hull can carry weight-free.[48]
- **MDO (medium-density overlay) plywood** — an exterior-grade sheet material made for highway signs and concrete forms — suits interior bulkheads and shelving, or a smooth, easy-to-paint hull finish generally. It resists ordinary fir's checking, needs less sanding, and holds paint noticeably better, at a modest cost premium over plain exterior ply; ordinary fir remains fine for interior bulkheads and shelving whether or not an overlay facing is used.[66]

**WBP ("weather and boil-proof") plywood** shares marine grade's resin glue but not its no-core-voids, single-species-veneer, and odd-equal-layer requirements; since a fully epoxy/glass-encapsulated hull is shielded from the elements that cause checking and rot, and a trailered Paradox spends most of her life dry rather than afloat, the WBP-versus-true-marine distinction matters less here than it would on a boat kept in the water.[98]

A sheet's actual quality is best judged independent of any "marine" label, by its **grading stamp** and by eye: a stamp such as A/A, A/B, A/C, B/C, or C/DX gives the face grade first and the backside grade second.[72] More plies is a good sign — poor-quality wood cannot be peeled into thin veneers without breaking up, and more glue lines mean more moisture barriers — and all plies should be a uniform thickness, since a cheap, thick core paired with a thin, higher-quality skin cannot take much sanding; voids are easiest to spot at a sheet's edges, and better plywood is noticeably lighter than poorer stock of the same thickness.[87] Green-tinted plywood sold at hardware stores is **pressure-treated and should not be used**: its moisture content is wrong for laminating or filleting, epoxy does not bond to it as well as to untreated wood, and pressure treatment's rot-resistance is redundant once a hull is epoxy-coated regardless.[51][52]

Plywood cost is a small fraction of a build's total regardless of grade, so the standing recommendation is to buy the best grade affordable; if economizing, sheathe even a good exterior-grade panel in a light 2-4 oz fiberglass cloth for abrasion resistance and checking protection, rather than pairing expensive marine ply with cheap epoxy.[51] Full epoxy/fiberglass encapsulation, kept up over decades, matters at least as much as the grade underneath it — two of the design's oldest hulls survived decades without ever having been glassed, and possibly not fully epoxied, when built[58] — and a finished plywood Paradox holds little resale value regardless of grade, so the choice is best driven by durability and finishing labor rather than by any hope of recouping the investment.[59]

**Foam flotation** is a related but separate requirement. It is safe to bond foam directly over epoxy-coated fir plywood of any grade — confirmed independently by System Three and West System, the two major marine epoxy manufacturers[1] — and should not be omitted from a Paradox. About 55 lb of buoyancy per cubic foot of foam is a workable rule of thumb.[41] Because the hull's bottom curves like a banana, with amidships as the low point, **ballast belongs low, amidships, and flotation belongs high, toward the ends** — the combination that lets a knocked-down boat come back upright.[38] Foam type, glued-versus-removable installation, and placement are covered in the sections below.

See also [hull scantlings](/design/hull-scantlings.md) for the plywood thicknesses used across the Paradox, [non-skid deck](/construction/non-skid-deck.md) and [bottom painting](/construction/bottom-painting.md) for other epoxy-finishing techniques, and [removable foam flotation panels](/construction/removable-foam-panels.md) for a practical fix to the foam-inspection concern raised below.

## Foam flotation over epoxy-coated fir plywood

Two major marine epoxy manufacturers confirmed in 2001 that it is safe to bond foam insulation over epoxy-coated interior fir plywood, provided the plywood is not wet before coating:

- **System Three**: the fir plywood will not check or rot as long as it isn't wet before coating, and the epoxy coating should protect the surface further.
- **West System**: no problem bonding insulation to epoxy-coated fir plywood — the company builds iceboxes on boats this way routinely.

## A real-world data point: glued foam gone wrong on a neglected boat

A secondhand "[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)" gave a real-world test of glued-in foam quality, rather than a new builder's plan. The boat's original builder had glued foam directly to the hull and then neglected the finished boat, which stood with rainwater in her before she was bought and refitted. Tearing out all the foam for inspection revealed the outcome tracked the quality of the original glue bond exactly: **where the glue had made good foam-to-wood contact, the wood underneath was fine**; **where the bond was poor, real problems turned up, worst of all at the corners**. The practical takeaway for future builders gluing in flotation: pay close attention to the gluing process itself, since a poor bond is where trouble concentrates. Going forward, a **screwed-in installation** is preferable to gluing, based on this firsthand experience — the same real-world case that prompted a restatement of why foam was left out of "[Zoë](/boats/zoe.md)" altogether; see [Zoë — the choice to leave "Zoë" without foam flotation](/boats/zoe.md#the-choice-to-leave-zoë-without-foam-flotation).

## Spray-in foam versus sheet foam for insulation

Commercially applied **two-part polyurethane spray-in foam** — as opposed to the softer one-part foam sold in cans, or two-part foam poured from cans (used successfully for icebox construction, though the pour "a little like the old monster movies") — is a fairly standardized technique for insulating metal boats and could in principle be used aboard a Paradox. A good result depends more on the **skill of the applicator** than on the material, packaging, or equipment. A properly sprayed job stops expanding at the level that still allows for the finished ceiling, since trimming back an over-applied foam surface damages its protective hard skin and lets water in more easily. Because a sprayed foam surface is not attractive, a ceiling of slats or sheet material — with its own furring strips — would still need to be installed over it regardless, though spraying does avoid the time-consuming work of cutting and gluing sheet foam by hand. Sheet foam serves on a Paradox despite the appeal of the sprayed alternative. A source for high-volume urethane spray-in foam in refrigerant-type containers with a flexible spray hose and nozzle, an alternative to the unpredictable, tricky-to-use canned "Great Stuff" and to expensive, poorly-rising canned two-part foam, is `TekSupply.com`.

A separate concern raised about **fixed insulation generally**: whether it may contribute to, and hide, rot — a concern weighed against insulation's contribution to a Paradox's positive buoyancy. The two competing schools of thought:

- **Glued installation**: specified foam board glued with **epoxy** — not contact cement, which is neither trusted nor pleasant to work with — to well-sealed, epoxy-coated surfaces, departing from the plans by adding a **3 mm Okoume veneer** over the foam rather than leaving it exposed to damage. With glued foam there is **no gap** between foam and hull/deck, so condensation cannot form in the first place. Both "[Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md)" and Paradox have proven this painted, contact-cemented-foam-on-fir-plywood approach over time.
- **Removable installation**: fastening through a veneer of 3 mm ply and the foam to blocks fixed to the hull, so the whole panel can be lifted for inspection. This has real merit even for a builder who ultimately decides to glue everything down, because it lets **condensation, rot, mold, and mildew be monitored directly** rather than trusted to proper sealing. The specified **foil-faced foam**'s foil layer is a weak point under removable installation, since its performance in wet, salty conditions is untested; **closed-cell foam rubber** might suit the removable option better, and aluminium tape is a plausible repair for damaged foil facing. With few deck fittings on the Paradox design — and a preference for **bonded** hardware over **thru-bolted** — deck leaks reaching the insulation are not a major worry either way.

The choice is a matter of individual preference with genuine trade-offs on both sides, comparable to the painted-versus-varnished-spars debate — not one with a single correct answer.

**Foam type: white EPS versus extruded polystyrene.** Most builders' interior-insulation photos show plain white **expanded polystyrene (EPS)**, the same low-cost bead foam widely used for flotation. EPS absorbs water — degrading its insulation value more than its flotation value — so **extruded polystyrene (XEPS/XPS, the blue or pink board foam)** should insulate better whether or not it is sealed against the wood with epoxy, as the plans call for.[43]

Much of what looked like white EPS in build photos was likely **Celotex** instead — a yellow, foil-backed rigid foam Little Cruiser was insulated with, and which is **polyisocyanurate-based**: the best rigid-foam insulation available short of spray-applied polyisocyanurate foam, running roughly 20% better than XPS/XEPS for the same thickness but at close to double XPS's cost. Swamp Thing, by contrast, was insulated with plain white polystyrene; the pink foam sold at Home Depot would likely outperform both materials used on those two boats.[44][45] The one real problem with that Celotex was biological rather than structural: a colony of ants once moved into it and took some time to evict.[46]

## Foil-faced foam and radio/GPS shielding

The specified **foil-faced foam** carries a hidden electronic cost, first flagged in the "Chine logs" thread (see [chine runner construction](/chine-runners/construction.md)): its aluminum foil layer **blocks radio signals**, so a GPS or similar unit **will not work well inside a cabin lined with it**. Where foil-faced Johns Manville foam is planned on the hull sides and under the deck, the **cabintop** is the one location most likely to shield a receiver, so the sensible precaution is to **mock up a cabintop with the same foam and test a GPS underneath it** before committing. Some Garmin units are designed to work inside a car — a similarly metal-shielded environment — suggesting reception might still be workable, with an **external antenna mounted in the vent trunk** as a fallback. Lacking a practical way to fit radar to a boat this size, a small **Danish radar reflector** like the one carried aboard "[Zoë](/boats/zoe.md)" serves instead, and the foil facing might actually **aid passive radar reflection**. The foil is also expected to **reduce electrolytic action** between the boat's bronze fasteners and the aluminum once the foam is encapsulated between hull and under-deck veneer, and to need **less adhesive** to bond well than an unfaced foam such as polyurethane (which bonds poorly without a facer) — at a higher material cost offset by better insulating efficiency.

For a dinghy rather than a Paradox, a simpler foam solution suffices: **expanded polystyrene sheet**, sourced from an ordinary builders' merchant, held in place with **hardboard panels** — distinct from the sheet or spray-in polyurethane foam builders use for a Paradox's own flotation and insulation.

**Closed-cell foam of the camping-mat type**, used successfully in motor caravan conversions, is only available in **6 mm (1/4 in)** thickness — too thin to know how much buoyancy it would contribute; a **1/2 in** version exists but is not cheap, and insulating an entire Paradox with it "would cost a mint" though it "would really be warm." Whether **silicone sealant** — layable in a thick bead to leave a deliberate air gap, cuttable and replaceable, reportedly originally developed as a space-program adhesive — could work as a foam adhesive in place of contact cement or epoxy (provided it does not react with either surface) is untested.

## Identifying plywood species: "Paraply" and possible Kauri

A related plywood-identification theme: **"Paraply"** is a plywood species from **South America**. Although Paraply is "great for interior furniture," it should **not be used in boats**, because it will not withstand a **boil test**.

A plywood board sold as "marine ply" may in fact only be **stamped AC exterior** grade rather than true marine grade — one builder's stock, sold as marine ply, was 9 mm 5-ply with two hardwood veneer layers stamped **AC exterior**, whose outer layers resembled **Kauri**. Since New Zealand Kauri (*Agathis australis*) is now illegal to harvest, it was speculated the material might be **Fijian Kauri**, possibly related to Paraply — a species link that was never confirmed. **Practical takeaway:** builders should check the actual grade stamp rather than relying on a seller's "marine" label.

## A firsthand verdict: even marine-grade fir can check

A **Bolger "Junebug"** planked in 1/4 in "Superply" and finished with only Interlux Brightsides enamel — no fiberglass or epoxy — was checking badly after about 18 months in a garage, prompting the question of whether *any* plywood resists checking with or without a glass coating.[7][8] The fullest answer on record: checking on a well-ventilated, indoor-stored interior is not much of a concern regardless of grade, but **AC exterior fir plywood is not worth using again**, and — going further than the earlier guidance — **even Marine-grade fir plywood is probably not worth using again either**, purely because of the checking problem. **Gaboon and mahogany**, sourced from Harbor Sales, are the genuinely check-resistant marine plywoods; for interior bulkheads and shelving, ordinary fir is fine provided it is **MDO or HDO grade, coated both sides**. For a deliberately disposable "proof of concept" boat, exterior AC ply, pretreated per the [Dave Carnell](/references/dave-carnell-articles.md) method and finished in ordinary house paint, could still last 10 years or more.[9] A cautionary data point reinforces the sharpened guidance: a **Searunner 31** trimaran, fiberglassed outside and epoxy-coated only inside by an experienced repeat builder, already showed fiberglass splitting at the cockpit-coaming edges and the beginnings of interior checking after only about four years.[10]

The foam-vs-no-foam question, raised elsewhere in the archive, is on reflection a builder's-discretion **detail** that doesn't touch the boat's geometry or specifications either way — not the "HUGE controversy" it can look like in isolation (see [operations/cabin-heating.md](/operations/cabin-heating.md) for the exchange it arose in). Firsthand professional experience confirms both halves of the guidance above: rot from lack of ventilation, and Douglas Fir marine plywood checking even under glass, have both been witnessed directly.

## From-scratch building experience: Meranti to BS1088, and a tanks-vs-ballast aside

Buying the secondhand "[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)" led to **stopping** a from-scratch Paradox build already underway, in order to get to know the boat first. Two changes were settled on: omitting the **water tanks**, and using a plywood other than fir for the sides and bottom, since checking on existing fir-ply work was marring the paint job.[11]

The most specific plywood-species recommendation on record: **Meranti plywood to the BS1088 standard** — the highest quality, lighter than Okoume, and somewhat less expensive too. Meranti is a bit more rot-resistant than Okoume, though both are tropical hardwoods needing religious epoxy encapsulation, in which case either will outlast fir "given the same level of care." The lower **BS6065** standard is usable but a step down, and uncertified "marine" plywood should be avoided outright, since its quality "can be worse than construction plys." For US east-coast builders wanting the best BS1088 option, **Brunzeel** (Dutch-made) is "the very be$t."[12] A competing, cheaper fix was also offered for the same checking problem: a thin (6 oz or so) fiberglass cloth laid in epoxy should be enough to stop fir plywood from checking without switching species at all — a data point never reconciled with the species-change recommendation above.[13]

On the water-tank question: storing drinking water in small containers instead frees the tank spaces for stowage, since permanent [ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md) already sited there removes any need to use tank water as ballast. On checking, the hull's outside had already been glassed to stop it there, but the interior's many small pieces were judged impractical to glass the same way, making a plywood-species change the cheaper fix — a practical labor/cost judgment specific to one boat's interior geometry, not a rejection of the glass-cloth fix on technical grounds.[14] Two plywood supplier links are on record: `boulterplywood.com` and `hardwoodint.com`.[15] Whether tank water doubles as ballast is really a question of how a boat is used: a local day sailor does better with permanent ballast and small water containers added as needed, while a cruiser making three-month trips would rather conserve every container for food and water than tie the space up as fixed ballast.[16]

## A new builder's exterior-vs-marine choice, and why marine usually wins on labor

A prospective builder's exterior-vs-marine-plywood question drew the fullest, most practically framed answer on record, distinguishing structural adequacy from finishing labor.

**Exterior-grade plywood will serve**, provided the species is chosen carefully: a good-quality Douglas fir exterior ply (Boise Cascade brand judged reliable) works, rather than white pine ply, since the fir grade uses the same glue as marine grade. A distinction runs by hull location — the **bottom** does not need marine grade even for a boat headed for rough or long-distance use, but **marine grade is worth the extra cost for the side hull panels**, which are more visible and easier to work with when finished nicely; the **deck** should avoid any plywood with thin outer veneer layers regardless of grade. [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)'s own plans state exterior ply will serve, guidance proven true in practice, while marine grade is still recommended for a builder planning serious rough-water or long-distance cruising.[18][19]

The point is reinforced by the experience of building "[Zoë](/boats/zoe.md)" in **exterior AC fir**, originally intended as only a quick, thrown-together workboat finish. As the build progressed and the design proved increasingly appealing, a nicer finish than originally planned became worth having — at which point the **extra labor required to bring exterior ply to a genuinely nice finish makes marine plywood "a steal"** by comparison, even though exterior ply's structural adequacy was never in question.[21] Weighing both views, the practical case for marine plywood throughout is straightforward: having gone to the trouble of building the boat, a builder does not want to end up merely "98 percent" satisfied with the result for the sake of a comparatively small saving.[20][22]

## Sourcing fir marine plywood, and the true weight/cost tradeoff against Okoume

The same verdict, given directly: fir marine grade for the hull is enough, sealed with epoxy inside and fiberglassed outside — "Matt did this on all of his boats prior to Paradox," painting rather than finishing bright to save money.[47] Weighing that advice against Okoume brings out the real tradeoff between the two: top-grade Okoume marine plywood runs nearly **half the weight** of cheaper marine-grade lauan, which matters for both stores capacity and how much epoxy/glass can be added without a weight penalty, but good ACX exterior fir still costs only about a third of top marine ply, finishes less well, and does not last as long — so the extra upfront cost of the better material should pay for itself later in cheaper, easier repairs. Five coats of interior epoxy below the waterline (three elsewhere), leaving unexposed surfaces uncovered by paint for easier rot inspection later, round out that plan.[48]

A specific **A/B fir plywood** source — distinguishing marine-grade A/B, free of edge voids, from ordinary exterior-grade A/B — remains unsourced on record.[49] The general sourcing challenge this illustrates — that suitable marine or premium-grade plywood, unlike the plans themselves, often has to be shipped in rather than bought off the shelf locally — is restated in [building plans — source and cost](/references/building-plans.md#source-and-cost).

## A 2006 recurrence: budget exterior ply, and why "green" plywood should be avoided

A prospective builder still waiting on plans raised the exterior-vs-marine question afresh, framed around cost: whether cheap non-marine plywood needs more epoxy to compensate, how it holds up against marine grade, whether hardware-store **green-tinted** exterior plywood is suitable, and where to find inexpensive epoxy.

The fullest answer sharpened the file's existing guidance for a builder working to a tight budget:

- Buy **the best grade affordable**, then sheathe it in a light 2-4 oz fabric — good abrasion protection and easy to repair.
- Pair good plywood with **good epoxy**; cheap epoxy on expensive marine ply wastes the ply's advantage.
- **Green-tinted plywood is pressure-treated** and should not be used — wrong moisture content for laminating or filleting, plus added treatment-chemical concerns.
- **Exterior-grade plywood uses the same glue as marine grade**; the real difference is veneer quality and how much void the grading tolerates. A Paradox is a small boat, not a structure demanding marine grade by necessity.
- Non-marine fir plywood should still be **sheathed to avoid checking**.
- Epoxy quantity is not a major cost factor on a boat this size — buy the best material affordable and get on with the build.

**RAKA's online epoxy guide** (`raka.com/UserManual.html`) is a starting resource for a builder unfamiliar with epoxy. A concrete data point from a completed build: **AC exterior plywood**, the same grade [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md) himself used, gave two full sailing seasons with no problems, and took somewhat more epoxy to bring to a good finish than marine grade would have.

A non-Paradox data point corroborates exterior-ply-plus-epoxy's long-term durability: a 22 ft boat, **"Madrigal,"** built of exterior plywood and polyester resin, sailed from Greece back to England via the French canal system, and lasted about 15 years before finally rotting through after being left outdoors in a field where rainwater got in.

## A July 2006 recurrence, and a recommendation for MDO plywood

The cheap-vs-marine plywood question recurred again in 2006, from a newcomer planning to keep the boat on a trailer over gravel rather than a concrete floor.[64] The study plans themselves already cover plywood choice in detail, and a stock Paradox's assembly sequence is deliberately **self-jigging**, making a poured concrete floor unnecessary.[65] Reassured, the newcomer confirmed a build in **exterior-grade ("ex") plywood** would make the project easier to justify.[67]

One reply named a specific product: **MDO (medium-density overlay) plywood**, recommended over ordinary exterior fir plywood for its very smooth surface (saving sanding time and resisting the checking regular fir plywood is prone to) and its better paint-holding — worth its extra cost for a boat kept in the water for any length of time, and manufactured in Washington State, making it readily available in the Pacific Northwest. A gravel building floor can still be made level with posts driven into the ground and checked 2x4s, without pouring concrete.[66]

Two more replies reinforced the same "spend the savings on plywood, not the floor" advice, since the price difference between good and poor-quality plywood is small next to a build's total cost.[68][69][70] A Paradox could in principle be built with **no fibreglass cloth at all**, using epoxy only to seal the joints, and still last a long time, though adding some glass up to the waterline is worthwhile for abrasion resistance; ordinary exterior-grade house, porch, and floor enamels, brushed or foam-rollered directly over MDO, give a nice semi-gloss finish and hold up to weather and scuffing surprisingly well.[71]

A fuller first-hand data point comes from one finished boat: **exterior-grade fir plywood**, skinned in 2 oz cloth (doubled in many places), used in mixed bay, ocean, and river conditions and beached repeatedly on moderate rubble, showed no bottom damage — the practical rule of thumb is to buy the best plywood a build's budget allows. How to read a **plywood grading stamp** before shopping: a code such as A/A, A/B, A/C, B/C, or C/DX gives the face grade first and the backside grade second.[72] Cheap plywood's real cost shows up later, not at purchase — a soft, water-absorbing core forces repairs at the worst possible time, when a builder wants to be out sailing, not fixing a hull — reinforcing that money is better spent on the plywood itself than saved for extras.[73]

A newcomer noted the Paradox and [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) designs' debt to **Phil Bolger's sharpie philosophy**, and floated researching [stitch-and-glue technique via Sam Devlin's book](/construction/hull-planking.md#stitch-and-glue-as-an-alternative-construction-method) as a path toward a design of his own; **British Standard 1088** is the grading standard Devlin's book favors, though only American "AA"-standard marine plywood was locally available to him.[74][75]

## A double-layer plywood technique for an enlarged Paradox, and testing an unconventional mast wood

A builder planning an enlarged Paradox, having ordered plans and begun clearing barn space for a building platform, asked how the design handles a two-person crew — addressed in full at [crew size and cabin fit — fitting a couple aboard a stock or slightly enlarged Paradox](/operations/crew-size-and-fit.md#fitting-a-couple-aboard-a-stock-or-slightly-enlarged-paradox) — and whether **larch (tamarack)** would serve for masts and booms, addressed at [wood species for a scratch-built wooden mast](/components/rig/mast.md#wood-species-for-a-scratch-built-wooden-mast).[76]

On an **enlarged Paradox's plywood**, one technique is to build the sides as **two bonded layers with opposing seams** — for example, two 1/4 in sheets making up 1/2 in total, or two 3/8 in sheets making 3/4 in — rather than a single thicker sheet. Beyond adding strength, opposing-seam lamination is easier to fair and bend than staggering scarf joints, and gives confidence in the panel's core even where a single thick sheet's core quality might be in doubt; the same doubled-sheet approach scales up to the larger bulkheads an enlarged design needs.[77]

**A laminated, two-layer panel needs no scarf joint at all** — a plain butt joint between sheets, with the edges sealed, suffices, because the lamination itself already supplies the strength: a single layer's scarf-joint gluing area (roughly 384 sq in) or an unscarfed butt's (a mere 36 sq in) is dwarfed by the laminated panel's own full-sheet bonding area of well over 4,600 sq in. Two builders independently reached this same conclusion; one also suggested pre-bending a laminated bottom panel by weighting each end and raising the middle before gluing the layers together, and building a **foam-cored panel** from scratch with a home vacuum-bagging setup as a further step beyond doubled plywood.[78][79][80]

## A 2007 UK shopping-list recurrence: Far Eastern ply and epoxy quantity

A UK builder pricing materials in March 2007 found three BS1088 marine plywood sources at sharply different prices — a basic grade, a cheaper "Far Eastern" grade at roughly half the cost, and Okoume in between — and asked about the cheaper grade's tradeoffs (weight, strength, workability), given that [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)'s own plans specify only AC exterior ply, plus how much epoxy resin a full build would need.[81]

Real-world experience of the cheaper **Far Eastern marine ply** was reassuring: one build used it throughout, with only a single small deck-edge delamination near the mast, easily fixed with a glue squirt, tape, and a covering of glass. UK epoxy prices run noticeably higher than US prices for the same product.[82] A second UK supplier stocked BS1088 marine ply alongside Far East and Tropical plywood in sheet sizes matching the design's own panels, needing no joins at all.[83] On epoxy quantity, the practical answer was to start with a 25 litre drum and buy more once it runs low, by which point the true rate of use becomes clear.[84] A parallel question about resin grade (wetting-out versus bonding/laminating formulations) and filler choice (Cabosil versus cellulose fibres versus wood flour) went unanswered on record.[85]

A second builder pricing the same UK market found a quote that "looked too good to be true," worth inspecting in person before committing, to judge ply count and check for voids.[86] **Assessing plywood quality by eye** answers the recurring "how do you tell if the ply has voids" question: more layers is better, since poor-quality wood cannot peel into thin veneers without breaking up, and more layers means more glue-line moisture barriers; all layers should be a uniform thickness, since cheap cores paired with a thin, higher-quality skin cannot take much sanding; voids show up at a sheet's edges; and better plywood tends to be noticeably lighter than poorer stock of the same thickness.[87] Having settled on the more expensive but dependable-quality option, the next task was building jigs for laminating the deck beams.[88]

## A 2008 recurrence: the cost gap between a "top job" and an economy build

The same evergreen question recurred again in 2008, framed around total build cost: how much extra a highly finished build costs over an economy build, and whether exterior-grade plywood can be used throughout the hull rather than marine grade, given both reportedly share the same glue and the whole boat will be glassed and epoxied regardless.[90] The designer's own plans specify exterior grade, in the hope the better material pays off later in longevity and lower maintenance — "a hap'orth of tar" being a poor place to economize.[91] A firsthand caution reinforced the marine-grade side of the tradeoff: fiberglass sheathing never fully excludes water over the long term, and rot can set in unnoticed long before it surfaces, making the price gap between marine and exterior grade too small to be worth the worry on a boat this size.[92] A finished boat's own experience split the difference: built in exterior grade sheathed in fiberglass, with the real distinguishing factor of a modern glass boat judged to be epoxy rather than old-style polyester — epoxy does not spider-crack the way aged polyester does — with the standing rule simply to use the best a budget allows.[93]

## A cheap 9mm plywood substitution, confirmed and extended

A cost-driven substitution proposal came from a builder who found surplus 9 mm marine plywood: rather than the plans' 12 mm ply for the sides and most bulkheads and 18 mm for the bottom and bulkhead 3, build the sides and deck from 9 mm stock, double every bulkhead nominally specified at 9 mm up to 18 mm, and laminate the bottom from two layers of 9 mm ply.[94]

The proposal drew a confirming answer: Paradox as drawn is very strongly built, and many boats of similar size use 6 mm or even 4 mm ply, so reducing the side panels to 9 mm marine grade should not be a problem so long as the reduced strength is kept in mind during close manoeuvring; the bulkheads will not suffer provided the added thickness sits on the correct side; and a good joint is the one part of laminating the two-layer bottom that needs real care.[95] A construction method for that two-layer bottom followed: scarf the first layer and fit it as designed, then lay the second layer in 150 mm-wide strips set at 45 degrees to the centerline, in the manner of cold-molded construction, to avoid unintentional voids forming between layers, before glassing the whole assembly over as designed.[96] A separate builder reached the same substitution independently, from a different precedent: [NOLI](/boats/noli.md#nolis-bottom-thickness-and-a-potter-comparison), a Paradox-sized but lighter design, is built stitch-and-glue from 6 mm marine ply on the topsides and 9 mm on the bottom — since marine plywood is stronger than exterior grade, 9 mm topsides and a two-layer 9 mm bottom would be just as strong as the plans' 12 mm-sides/two-layer-18 mm-bottom exterior-ply specification, worth doing if the marine ply can be bought at or below exterior-ply prices.[97]

## A worst-case fallback answered, and the BS1088 stamp's reliability doubted further

A worst-case fallback was offered matching the plans as bought: buy three sheets of 12 mm marine ply for the sides and build the rest from 9 mm — the plans do in any case sanction plain exterior-grade ply throughout.[99] A second view trusted gut instinct over the stamp: marine ply's finish generally runs smoother than exterior ply of the same colour, with a boat-links.com plywood FAQ offered as further reading, and a genuine marine-grade stack — if the stack in question proved to be one — judged possibly worth buying outright, price and shipping permitting.[100] Two 1995 issues of *The Boatman* magazine (issues 24 and 25, May/June) were later found to cover exactly this identification question, though unread at the time of reporting them; the plywood stack itself remained unseen, with price still to be negotiated through a hard-to-reach intermediary.[101]

## The BS1088 marine-plywood standard, read in full

The two 1995 *Boatman* magazine issues flagged earlier were tracked down and read, yielding a fuller picture of what BS1088 actually requires. The standard (incorporating BS1203 for adhesive and BS6566 for timber grades) does not require a board to be British-made; its core requirement is a WBP (weather-and-boil-proof) glue line — in practice almost always phenol formaldehyde — proven resistant to weather, micro-organisms, and both cold and boiling water. Ordinary "exterior" grade plywood (the common US/Canadian designation) typically uses the same PF adhesive, so glue type alone does not distinguish the two grades. Veneer species must be at least moderately durable, ruling out birch, alder, poplar, and beech; a special clause allows the lighter but non-durable Gaboon/Okoume provided the species is marked on the board, valued for its low weight in racing dinghies. Face veneers must be free of open defects (limited to 6 pin knots, averaging 2, per square foot), free of end joints, with no more than one edge joint per foot of width and matched for colour; core veneers may carry small splits, unlimited pin knots and edge joints, and unmatched colour, but still no end joints, and manufacturing defects such as bad bonding, pleats, overlaps, and unrepaired gaps are simply not permitted. Finished boards must leave the factory at 6-14% moisture content, sanded equally on both faces, within 0.25% diagonal-squareness tolerance and within +0/-6.3 mm of nominal size; thickness tolerance narrows with nominal thickness (6 mm: +0.04/-0.65 mm; 12 mm: +0.09/-0.82 mm). Face-veneer thickness is set as a fraction of the total lay-up: for 3-4 mm three-ply boards each face veneer must be at least 1/8 of the dry-assembled thickness; for anything thicker (multi-ply), each face veneer must run 1.3-3.8 mm and each core veneer no more than 4.8 mm thick. Dutch-made Bruynzeel plywood, despite matching or exceeding BS1088 in every other respect and carrying a 10-20-year manufacturer's guarantee, fails only the standard's artificial boil-ageing test — one no boat's plywood would actually be subjected to in service. The standard itself is silent on epoxy-encapsulated plywood, leaving that question to a builder's own judgment.[102]

A second opinion, from separate boatbuilding experience, endorses BS1088 in practice: it has held up well across several projects, especially once coated with epoxy. Bad plywood does exist in the market, but genuine marine-rated ply's physical properties vastly exceed amateur-boatbuilding requirements regardless of brand or price — and whether an unverified stack claimed to be BS1088 really is comes down to trusting the seller or supplier, since the stamp itself cannot be checked by eye.[103]

## Long-term plywood durability, resale value, and why to expect neither

Neither "[Swamp Thing](/matt-layden/swamp-thing.md)" nor "[Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md)" was originally glassed (and possibly not epoxied either), yet both survived for decades — Little Cruiser's own eventual rot problem stemmed specifically from having been built without a fiberglass/epoxy sheathing, not from the plywood grade itself (see her 2005 refit, at [construction scantlings and the 2005 epoxy/fiberglass sheathing refit](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md#construction-scantlings-and-the-2005-epoxyfiberglass-sheathing-refit)). Exterior plywood is roughly half the cost of marine grade, and since the hull is going to be covered in epoxy and fiberglass regardless, the underlying wood grade matters less than the quality of that coating.[58]

A scratch-built plywood boat is unlikely to recover its own material cost when sold — close to a settled point among builders, not a live dispute. Building one is best justified by wanting to build and by confidence in the design, not by any expectation of recouping the investment. **Moisture entering through the end grain**, where it becomes trapped between waterproof glue lines and cannot escape, is plywood's real long-term enemy, needing ongoing maintenance attention (an opposing school of thought argues for *not* fully encapsulating wood at all, instead letting it breathe — a live disagreement, not a resolved one). Little Cruiser and Swamp Thing, by 2006 roughly twenty years old, are the design's own proof that plywood construction stands up over decades of real use; Swamp Thing in particular has had multiple owners and has lived outdoors in the tropics for its entire life. Secondhand plywood boats generally sell slowly and for little, though some can be restored cheaply if a buyer doesn't count the labor; a finished Paradox would have a very small secondhand market, but next to no competition within it either.[59]

A Paradox should be **heavily sheathed in fiberglass regardless of the plywood grade beneath it** — the plans call for 4 mm of fiberglass, with the sides and deck each getting one layer of sheathing. Three coats of epoxy will stop water or moisture penetrating altogether, provided the epoxy itself is then protected with several coats of a compatible paint. Applied to boats built in marine plywood, this combination gives a **20-year-plus life expectancy**, so long as the underlying construction engineering is sound.[60]

The marine-versus-exterior-plywood debate is, in the end, unresolvable from general principles alone — "everyone seems to have a point of view and a string of 'facts' to back them up" — with care and maintenance, and understanding the materials used, mattering more than which grade is chosen. Two published authors have been cited on opposite sides of the question — one dismissing "marine ply" as an oxymoron, the other calling plywood the best boatbuilding material available — both experienced commercial builders and writers in their own right; in the end, a builder simply has to make a material choice and proceed.[61][62][63]

## Buoyancy fundamentals: positive, negative, and neutral

A newcomer's basic buoyancy questions — what "buoyancy" means for a Paradox, whether a fully flooded boat should float with her deck at the waterline, and how to work out how much foam a given material needs — drew the clearest statement on record of the underlying physics.

**Positive, negative, and neutral buoyancy.** A **positively buoyant** object floats on top of the water; a **negatively buoyant** one sinks; **neutral** buoyancy is the in-between case — floating with the deck at the surface but not sinking further. USCG-approved two-part pour-in or sprayed foam has a specific buoyancy rating per unit volume, and different foam materials carry different ratings.

**A rule-of-thumb foam figure.** A practical number: about **55 lb of buoyancy per cubic foot of foam** used. A builder can add enough foam, or empty air bladders, bottles, or containers, to make a flooded boat float at whatever level is chosen — foam placed lower in the hull leaves the deck riding higher above the water, at the cost of usable floor space, so the choice is a trade-off rather than a single correct answer.

**Foam changes flooded trim, not the working waterline.** Adding buoyancy foam does not raise a Paradox's normal waterline, since it adds very little total weight to the boat — its effect only shows up in the **flooded** state. Ballast, by contrast — whether lead or a steel hull structure — **does** lower the vessel at the waterline in ordinary sailing trim, whatever material provides it. The two effects (flooded buoyancy and sailing-trim ballast) are governed by different parts of the same buoyancy/ballast balance and should not be confused.

## Ballast and buoyancy placement, and why the Paradox's rocker matters

The fullest statement in the archive of the underlying **placement doctrine** connecting a hull's shape, its ballast, and its flotation:

- A **wooden** hull floats naturally even when swamped or holed; how high she rides depends on the balance between ballast/stores (denser than water) and positive flotation (lighter than water — the lighter the material, the better).
- **Ballast belongs as low as possible; flotation belongs as high as possible** — this combination is what lets a knocked-down boat come back upright.
- Because the [Paradox](/paradox.md) hull is **curved like a banana** along the bottom, the lowest point is **amidships** — the best place for ballast — while the **ends** are the hull's high points, and so the best place for flotation.
- How much flotation a boat needs depends on how much ballast she carries; how much ballast she needs is in turn related to the size and weight of her rig.
- A **steel** hull, unlike a wooden one, sinks on its own when flooded or holed, so it needs **additional flotation beyond a wood boat's** to make up for the weight of the steel itself, on top of the weight of ballast and any stores denser than water — sharpening the open question, for any steel-hulled proposal (see [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative)](/design/ideas/aluminum-construction-idea.md)), of how much extra flotation is needed and how much interior room that leaves on an already-small boat.

As an aside, airplane construction being mostly metal has no real bearing on the question, since amateur-built aircraft are essentially never made of steel — a comparison sometimes raised loosely in these discussions but not actually analogous to a home-built boat. The stated preference remains **wood** over any other building material, for its ease and pleasantness to work, its warmth and quiet, its repairability, and its character.

## Skipping foam lining altogether: stowage space against condensation risk

Not every builder fits interior foam. One reasoned choice against it: leaving the hull sides and interior unlined trades away foam's insulation and reserve-buoyancy benefit in exchange for **more usable side-locker stowage space**, judged worthwhile where condensation is not expected to be a real problem, since sailing is not planned in genuinely cold conditions. On reserve buoyancy specifically, Paradox's strong construction and design are judged unlikely ever to be tested by full flooding in practice — the kind of extreme conditions that could ship that much water, on the order of the Fastnet Race, would in any case still require the main hatch to fail or the cabin top to be damaged before the boat actually took on enough water to matter.[89]

## Wood decks over a steel hull, and material-contact cautions

On whether a Paradox could practically be built with a steel bottom, sides, and stern under a wooden deck, a rough sizing method and a construction caution follow:

- **Estimating steel panel weight:** figure the square footage of the hull's panels and multiply by **4.3 lb per square foot** for a rough weight estimate, remembering to add interior fittings on top of the bare panel figure. **3/4 in square steel tubing** weighs roughly **0.4 lb per linear foot**, depending on wall thickness — usable for framing weight estimates alongside the panel figure.
- **A wood deck over a steel hull is practical**, using square tubing for a gunwale with plywood glued and screwed on top of it.
- **Wood and steel must not be left in direct contact** — a bad combination for both materials over time. The steel needs to be **fully covered** with glue or bedding compound at the joint, or wrapped with heavy tape, to keep the two materials separated.

This is a narrower, practical construction detail distinct from the whole-hull steel gauge/framing debate covered on [aluminum and steel Paradox (speculative)](/design/ideas/aluminum-construction-idea.md#whole-hull-steel-construction-gauge-framing-and-damage-tolerance); see that page for the fuller steel recommendations (12-gauge plate, tube-frame bulkheads, and finishing without epoxy).
