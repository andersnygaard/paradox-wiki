# Bottom painting over epoxy

A finishing technique for getting **antifouling (bottom) paint** to bond durably to an epoxy-coated hull.

## The problem

Antifouling applied over a fully cured epoxy surface tends to **lose its bond over time**, and large sections flake off. This was learned at **Gold Coast Yachts**, where large (~53 ft) wood-and-epoxy charter catamarans repeatedly needed their antifouling replaced under warranty. Sealers and following manufacturers' recommendations did not solve it.

## The technique (bond the paint to green epoxy)

1. Complete all **fairing** first (typically microballoons / microspheres).

2. Apply a **sealer coat of clear epoxy** over the fairing — the paint bonds to *this* coat, not to bare fairing compound.

3. Apply the **first coat of antifouling just as the epoxy sealer is tacking off** — becoming tack-free to a light touch. This forms a chemical bond between epoxy and antifouling that is near-impossible to remove without cutting into the epoxy.

4. Use a **close-nap foam roller** (e.g. the yellow WEST System rollers) with **light pressure** to minimise alligatoring.

5. Apply a **thin** first coat — the epoxy cures more slowly than the paint and may alligator/crack slightly, but a thin coat keeps cracks undetectable and they do not harm the bond.

6. **Do not apply further coats** until the epoxy has cured, usually overnight; afterwards antifouling adheres well to itself.

This process has ~10 years of success behind it. For **topside** paint there has never been flaking when using a **primer** under the paint (except under varnish).

This tip was reprinted in [Duckworks Magazine](/references/duckworks-magazine.md).

## Refitting an existing bottom: strip paint, not glass

When preparing to lay new woven roving onto the bottom of a secondhand Paradox as part of a total refit (see [secondhand Paradox market](/operations/secondhand-market.md) and [checking a used Paradox for rot](/construction/rot-inspection-ice-pick.md)), the question is whether to strip the existing bottom **all the way down** (removing the existing layer of glass along with the paint) or **just remove the paint** before laying the new roving.

- The more thorough approach is to sand the whole bottom down to **bare glass** first, to check whether the existing layer is **well laminated**; grind away and fair (with epoxy/cabosil or microballoons) any loose areas found. Wear a dust mask and proper clothing, since fiberglass dust is both itchy and bad to breathe.

- A simpler answer holds that removing **just the paint** is fine.

The two views are not fully reconciled — the thorough approach implies an inspect-and-repair pass beneath the paint, while the simpler one is a direct, no-caveat "paint only." Read together, the practical takeaway is that paint removal is the minimum needed before re-roving, with a full strip-to-glass inspection as the more cautious option for a boat already undergoing a total refit of uncertain history.

## Refit finding: damp plywood at bulkhead #3

Sanding the paint off a bottom during a refit can turn up trouble spots: in one case, after sanding revealed a dark patch **around bulkhead #3**, grinding the glass off to investigate showed the plywood **damp but not rotten**. The recommended course is to grind the glass off all around the affected area, let it dry, then lay new roving. This is a distinct, later finding from — but at the same hull location as — a half-dollar-sized discolored (and solid, non-rotten) spot found earlier during an ice-pick survey aft of bulkhead #3; see [checking a used Paradox for rot](/construction/rot-inspection-ice-pick.md) for the fuller sequence and the drying/diagnosis advice.

In the case on record, the likely cause was identified as **rainwater collecting inside the hull** from a leaking **hatch**, rather than any preexisting problem with the boat at purchase — "there was little water there when I bought it." Kevlar was not used on this boat's glass layup (see [hull planking](/construction/hull-planking.md)).

## Choosing a bottom paint

Distinct from the bonding technique above — which assumes a paint has already been chosen — is the separate question of which **antifouling product** to actually buy, particularly for a frequently-used, trailer-kept Paradox (roughly a little over a quart covers the hull).

The factors that determine which antifouling paint is needed:

- **Tropical vs. temperate water** — warmer water needs more biocide and the paint remains effective for less time.

- **Hull material** — wood and fiberglass have different paint requirements than aluminum or steel.

- **Fresh vs. tidal vs. marine water** — salinity and nutrient load (tidal areas with land runoff) affect how much marine growth to expect.

- **Frequent haul-out vs. left in the water** — relevant to a trailered boat.

Some antifouling paints (especially **tin-based** formulations) are restricted or banned in fresh water and tightly controlled even in salt water, since tin is a carcinogen that bioaccumulates in shellfish; some paints also stop protecting once dried out after immersion (a West Marine buying-guide article covers the topic further, without endorsing West Marine specifically as the cheapest source). The bottom-line recommendation for a **wood-hulled, frequently-hauled-out "trailer sailor"** like Paradox is a **copolymer ablative paint**, at roughly **$34/quart** from West Marine as a reference price point, though commercial fishing-supply outlets are typically cheaper. DIY "just add cuprous oxide to epoxy" shortcuts are discouraged, since commercial bottom paints include additional biocides/additives beyond cuprous oxide that broaden protection against more types of marine growth — newer "anti-slime" formulations are of particular interest, since "fuzz" reduces performance even on paint that is otherwise doing its job. Finally, bottom paints are not "paint-it-and-forget-it": they need **regular scrubbing** whether in or out of the water, and a trailered boat benefits from scrubbing **soon after haul-out**, before growth has a chance to dry and harden.

This was received as "a very well done explanation." A related question sometimes arises for owners who move between climates seasonally — e.g. sailing a **frigid Lake Ontario** until it freezes, then trailering to **Key Largo** to cruise the Bahamas until the lake thaws again: does this mean repainting the bottom **twice a year**? The answer is no — simply buy **tropical-strength paint** from the start, since the price difference over temperate-strength paint is small and "too much is far better than too little": a boat that never actually leaves the lakes will just see the paint's protection last somewhat longer than needed.

This is the only recorded discussion of bottom-paint **selection criteria** (as opposed to bonding technique) — a practical complement to the application method above, aimed at exactly the kind of frequently-trailered, mixed-fresh/salt-water use case several Paradox owners describe elsewhere in the archive. See also [trailering](/operations/trailering.md) for the trailer-sailor context.

## A hard antifouling paint for a trailered boat

**Pettit Trinidad** keeps marine growth off effectively but tends to rub off when the boat bounces over shallows or dries out at low tide, raising the question of whether a harder finish — such as an **epoxy/copper coating** — would hold up better on a frequently trailered, frequently grounded-out Paradox. Experience with "Zoë" is that West Marine's **CPS** antifouling gives very good results generally, but no paint holds up better than any other on the specific spots that rub against the sea floor. See [choosing a bottom paint](#choosing-a-bottom-paint) above for the general antifouling-selection framework this sits alongside.

## Finding the waterline on the transom

Since the plans do not show a waterline on the **transom**, builders need a practical way to determine where to stop the bottom paint there, having already worked out the waterline everywhere else on the hull from the prints.

One method: stop the bottom paint at the **bottom of the transom**, and about **4 1/4 in up from the chine at the bow** (measuring back a little from the stem to find a flat spot to measure from). To find a level reference line despite the hull's curvature: **level the boat** using the floor boards as a reference, then run a strip of **masking tape** loosely from the bottom of the transom to the bow — standing back to eyeball whether it reads as a fair, straight line, lifting and re-doing it (it can take four or five tries) until it looks right. Alternatively, **with the boat already leveled**, measure up from the floor to the bottom of the transom at each station and mark that same distance along the hull.

A simpler and more precise alternative is a **water level** — a length of hose with a short (12–18 in) length of **clear tubing at each end**, filled with water, held at a known reference point (e.g. the transom waterline) to establish the datum, then carried anywhere else on the hull to read off the true waterline by the water's level in the tubing.

This is the only recorded discussion of how builders actually locate the Paradox's waterline for bottom-painting purposes, distinct from the paint-selection and paint-bonding material above.

## Stripping old bottom paint: sanding, and respirator advice

Adding further layers of glass to a hull's sides (e.g. to "[Whisper](/boats/whisper.md)") first requires removing the **bottom paint applied the previous year** — raising the question of a **safe** stripping method.

The blunt, one-line verdict: **"The only way I know of is to sand it off, a job I hate to no end."** On respirator choice — whether an ordinary dust mask suffices, or something more protective is needed to be well protected while sanding — the recommendation is one of the **better 3M Valved Particulate Respirators**: West Marine #2671147 / 3M #8611, always used with success.

A broader health question sometimes follows: what kind of **filter** is good "when painting and glassing," particularly for someone for whom a chemical filter is a must and for whom concern about health effects (even ordinary house paint can cause illness) is a real barrier to starting a build. The recommended PPE routine: a **3M chemical respirator and gloves** when working with epoxy, and a **good dust mask** when sanding — reported to work with "no problems." Free publications on respiratory protection are available from **West Systems** and **Systems Three**. The encouraging bottom line for a health-anxious prospective builder: **"You can protect yourself !! Don't let it stop you from building."**

The paint-removal question resolves to a straightforward **sanding** job (no shortcut method is on record), alongside the fullest respirator/PPE guidance on record for epoxy and sanding work.

For builders in the UK, whom **epoxy fumes make retch** even through an ordinary pad-type dust mask, one build used a **cartridge filter mask** bought from a UK hardware supplier (Buck & Hickman), fitted with an **ABEK-rated cartridge** (covering inorganic gases), which stopped the epoxy smell getting through entirely; a local tool-hire shop is a likely alternative source if a dedicated supplier is out of reach, and the mask should be sealed in a plastic bag between uses so the filter does not go on absorbing atmospheric chemicals and wear out sooner than necessary. Separately, working with epoxy has its own non-respiratory hazard: **belt sanders**. One builder's thumb was pulled into the machine's roller and badly bruised internally, with surprisingly little skin lost from the outside despite a large blood blister forming beneath — serious enough to require time off work — while a second builder lost a piece of a finger to the same kind of tool while shaping a wooden spoon. The standing advice from both incidents: **be careful with any power tool**, belt sanders in particular.

## Why wax, enamel, and other antifouling shortcuts fail

Builders looking to avoid conventional antifouling paint below the waterline — whether for cost or because a favorite topside enamel seems tempting to extend down there — run into the same wall: nothing else holds up against marine growth for long.

- **Wax** works only until fouling gets a start: barnacles are indifferent to the taste and simply attach to the boat underneath the coating once established, and their "glue" reacts with epoxy to leave blue staining in the laminate that is hard to remove. A boat left in warm salt water for as little as four days can already show heavy barnacle growth. The reported practice of waxing the bottom of the **"Bris"** boat is not, on this evidence, a substitute for antifouling paint in ordinary use.

- **Enamel and linear polyurethanes** (Awlgrip, Imron, Sterling, and similar LPUs) are not formulated for prolonged underwater immersion; cruisers who have used them below the waterline have reported blistering on topsides where a bow wave immersed the paint during ocean crossings. **[Interlux Brightsides](/construction/topside-painting.md)**, a one-part polyurethane topside enamel, falls in this same category and is not recommended by its own manufacturer for use below the waterline.

- **Experimental sprayed silicone bottom paint** allows growth but wipes clean easily with a dinghy brush and can last years — but the coating must never be scuffed by the bottom or a trailer, which rules it out for a boat that is regularly hauled and re-launched, like a trailered Paradox.

- Paint additives — cayenne pepper, antibiotics, tin-based mildewcides — mixed into an otherwise good bottom paint show no measurable performance gain, and more often a drop in performance, when tried as home remedies.

For a boat kept only in fresh water for short stretches at a time, an **epoxy barrier coat alone** can be adequate. For serious cruising — including salt water and extended time in the tropics — the recommended build-up is: (1) an **epoxy barrier coat**, applied to the tacky point, (2) a **vinyl bottom paint**, also applied to the tacky point, then (3) at least two coats (three is better) of a hard-wearing ablative such as **Pettit Trinidad** (the red formulation carries the most copper), chosen with an eye to time spent grounding out on sand, which scuffs bottom paint off. **Interlux Micron**, tinted with green Micron paint (rather than a generic color tint, to keep the mix chemically compatible) or left as Micron Shark White, is an alternative ablative for a boat expected to see lighter service; painting the first coat in a contrasting color makes it easy to see when scrubbing or scraping has worn through to it. A cheaper alternative build-up, from a trailered SeaPearl 28 (kept out of the water between outings): **Copperpoxy**, or the less expensive **VC Offshore** (a copper-loaded finish that also gives a slippery, low-friction surface) over an **Interlux epoxy interprotect** base coat — both simpler to renew than a full ablative system, since the boat spends most of its time on the trailer rather than in the water.

One four-day trial made the case for antifouling paint of any kind starkly: a trailerable Paradox-family boat left in warm salt water without bottom paint fouled so heavily in that short a time that clearing it took a great deal of scraping — the warmer and saltier the water, the faster and worse the growth.

For "Whisper," the settled choice was conventional antifouling rather than any of the alternatives.

## Reusing sound antifouling across a full refit

A full secondhand-boat refit is not automatically a bottom-paint refit: where the existing copper-based epoxy antifouling on a boat coming in for a full topside and interior overhaul was still sound, it was simply left in place and reused rather than stripped, with only a few imperfections resealed with epoxy and a minor leak at the base of a drain hole into the vent box addressed at the same time.[39] This is consistent with the general antifouling-selection guidance above: a bottom paint's job is protection, not decoration, and a coat still doing that job does not need replacing just because the rest of the boat is being repainted.

## Homemade copper antifouling and bottom paint (microcruising.com)

A homemade antifouling recipe learned from [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md) years earlier: mix **a minimum of 1 ounce per volume of copper powder for every 8 ounces of [oil-based] paint**. Matt makes his own bottom paint **thick**, which is harder to apply with a brush but more durable; a **thinner** mix can instead be applied with a spray gun.

For **Enigma**'s bottom, after the boat was holed in a collision (see [epoxy coating technique](/construction/epoxy-coating-technique.md)), cheap **Rustoleum "high performance" enamel** (about $10/qt, Lowes) mixed with the copper-powder recipe above was used. This contrasts with the topside paint used on the same repair — West Marine "Seagloss Pro," the same product **Matt Layden used on Enigma's original build** ($37/qt), versus a cheaper Rustoleum Topside paint ($15/qt) considered instead.
