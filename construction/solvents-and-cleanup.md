# Solvents and epoxy clean-up

The liquids used in building a plywood/epoxy boat take on the character of tools in their own right and belong in the tool kit alongside the putty knives and brushes.[1] Five of them matter for building work: distilled white vinegar, rubbing alcohol (70% isopropyl), denatured alcohol, acetone, and water — paint chemicals are a separate subject.[1] Some are useful everywhere, some are backups, and one is dangerous enough that it should be minimized or avoided outright.[1]

## Decanting the liquids for use

Rather than working straight out of the containers they arrive in, the liquids used most are transferred into purpose-suited dispensers.[1] Vinegar goes into two containers: a one-handed trigger spray bottle for localizing it precisely, and a small-capped ketchup bottle for larger quantities or for wetting a cloth.[1] Water likewise goes into two containers: a bucket with something over the opening that can be knocked off with the back of a hand, and a small thumb-operated pop-up bottle that travels along wherever work is happening.[1] Denatured alcohol, rubbing alcohol, and acetone are used the least of the five, and are left in the containers they came in.[1]

## The strength hierarchy

Vinegar is the shining knight of boatbuilding liquids and is treated as the everyday, first-reach agent — an acidic liquid obtained by fermentation, with a pH around two on a one-to-seven acidity scale, in use by people for at least 10,000 years and known even to antiquity for its ability to dissolve a pearl.[1] It is referred to as nature's cleanser.[1] Every other liquid on the list is a backup to vinegar, ranked by strength: rubbing alcohol behaves as a weak denatured alcohol; denatured alcohol is a genuine solvent, able to deal with epoxy resin before it reaches its gel stage (rubbing alcohol does the same job, but not as well); and acetone is the strongest of the group by a wide margin, and also the biggest villain of the lot — it is absorbed through skin, highly dangerous to breathe, and a fire hazard, and its use should be minimized, avoided where possible, and handled with great care when it cannot be avoided.[1]

## Clean-up divides by what the epoxy has in it

Clean-up procedure depends on which of two forms the epoxy is in.[1] Epoxy with fillers mixed in is by far the easier of the two to clean up, because the fillers break up the epoxy and make it less sticky to work with.[1] Pure, unfilled epoxy is stickier in every form it takes, and removing it from tools or parts requires stronger liquids and more care.[1] (A third, rarer form exists and is treated separately.)[1]

### On tools

Take a tool to the water bucket, dunk it, soak a rag with water, and wipe it clean; if it still is not clean, spray it with a little vinegar and wipe it dry with a towel.[1] Avoid scratching a putty knife's blade surface — gouges give epoxy something to grab onto, working against every later clean-up.[1]

### On parts

Never try to lift wet putty off a part with a rag — it does not work and only wastes effort.[1] Instead, use a putty knife with a bevel edge to remove the bulk of the putty first, putting the excess into a cup or wiping it onto a rag.[1] The knife has to be wiped clean between passes, or gobs already removed simply get redeposited back onto the surface being cleaned.[1] Once finished, spray the knife with vinegar and wipe it clean.[1] For pure, unfilled epoxy the same procedure applies with more care and stronger liquids as needed: spray an open area directly with vinegar and wipe it up, or apply vinegar to a rag or tool where a direct spray isn't practical, moving to progressively stronger liquids if the residue still resists.[1] Any surface that has been worked should always be wiped with water once the epoxy has cured, and the job is done when the surface no longer feels sticky to the touch.[1]

## Keeping the putty knife clean during a filleting or puttying pass

A dirty knife undoes finished work — if traces of putty are left on the blade and it goes back to the surface anyway, it will either scrape what has just been smoothed or leave streaks behind.[3] The rule is to clean the knife by spraying it with vinegar and wiping it with a cloth (or a dedicated wiping board) before returning it to the surface, every single time putty is picked up.[3] A wiping cloth board — a rag rubber-banded over a small board, kept damp with vinegar — is quicker to use in practice than a loose rag and only takes seconds to wipe a knife clean; it should be wiped in one consistent direction rather than back and forth.[3]

## Brushes: cleaning versus discarding

Epoxy can be cleaned off a brush for a while, but the brush eventually becomes useless no matter how carefully it is done.[2] The routine: on a flat surface, press a stick down onto the bristles and work it side to side while moving toward the tip, pushing resin out of the brush, repeating until none remains, then turning the brush over and doing the same on the other side.[2] Next, mash the brush into a tub of waterless hand cleaner (Goop) and work the bristles vigorously with gloved fingertips, wipe on a rag, and repeat the hand-cleaner pass a second time.[2] Finally, pour a teaspoon of acetone into a poly cup, work the brush around in it, wipe the brush back and forth on a rough cloth or surface, rinse it in water, and slap it dry on cardboard.[2] The economics of this argue against bothering, most of the time: at roughly 50 brushes to complete a Paradox at thirty cents each, discarding every brush instead of cleaning them costs about $15, while three tubs of hand cleaner and a pint of acetone run about $7 — a saving of only $8, and likely with leftover acetone besides, which makes cleaning brushes marginal at best.[2] The practical answer is to clean brushes when there is time and the inclination for it, and otherwise simply throw them out.[2]

## Vinegar's effect on curing epoxy

Vinegar can be used deliberately, not just for clean-up, to smooth the surface of a fillet — on an epoxy maker's own advice, it slightly delays the cure of the top surface of the epoxy without affecting the final cure in any way.[5] The tool doing the smoothing can also simply be cleaned with vinegar between passes; an alternative technique smooths the fillet by dipping the tool in unthickened epoxy instead.[5] See also [epoxy fillets](/components/epoxy-fillets.md) for the fillet-forming technique itself.

## The safety boundary: vinegar on tools, never on skin

Vinegar's approval is for tools, not for skin. A published manufacturer's correction holds that although common household vinegars (4 to 10% dilute acetic acid, whether distilled white or apple cider) are sometimes promoted as a "harmless" way to clean epoxy off skin, using it that way instead slightly dissolves the epoxy and then carries it through the skin's protective layers into subdermal tissue, increasing both the likelihood and the intensity of an allergic sensitization reaction.[4] A blend of epoxy and any solvent should never be allowed to reach bare skin.[4][5] The safest clean-up sequence when uncured epoxy has gotten onto the hands is to strip off the thin working gloves, clean and wash the hands, and only then put on heavy, solvent-resistant gloves to do the actual clean-up work — the heavy gloves are too bulky to wear while gluing itself, which is why the thin gloves are worn during the work in the first place.[5] Working clean and wearing protective clothing — gloves and long sleeves — is the best way to reduce the need to expose skin to any cleaning agent at all.[4]

One further rule sits alongside the vinegar boundary: waterless hand cleaner (Goop) should be kept away from the boat, its parts, and even the tools, because epoxy does not bond well against it — it belongs on hands only, cleaned off separately from any surface that still needs to take epoxy or paint.[6]
