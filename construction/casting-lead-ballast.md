# Casting lead ballast (pigs and the rudder-blade pour)

Weight aboard a boat should be treated as a necessary evil, to be watched closely in how it is used and stowed, since it will sink a boat deeper, slow it down, and put greater strain on the sails and spars carrying a heavier load — a framing that applies directly to the lead ballast a [Paradox](/paradox.md) carries as cast pigs, plus a smaller separate pour cast into the rudder blade itself.[1]

## How much lead, and in what form

The design takes between 70 and 180 kg (154–397 lb) of ballast, neither expensive nor hard to make, cast as pigs averaging about 88 kg (40 lb) each; a practical approach is to cast eight pigs first and decide later whether more are needed.[1]

## Sourcing the lead

Lead is often had simply by asking for it, and used wheel weights from a tyre centre's tire-balancing scrap are the standard source.[1] The approach that works is to ask for the manager rather than the staff on the floor, since the floor staff cannot help, to catch him when he is not busy, and to offer to pay for the lead right away — without that offer, he probably will not be bothered with the request.[1] One such visit turned up 200 lb, and two further visits were enough to cover everything needed for the boat.[1] Three hundred pounds of lead has been had for twenty dollars, with another 100 lb given free.[1] This is best done before spring, or the same scrap lead draws competition from fishermen restocking their own tackle.[1]

## Casting the pigs

Pouring the pigs must be done outside, and the job demands complete preparation before the lead is ever melted.[1] The mould is a bread pan of about 9-1/4 x 5-1/4 x 2-3/4 in, buried in the ground and leveled in every direction.[1] The lead is melted in a fairly thick pail fitted with a handle, set up on bricks so the heat source can reach the pail's bottom, using either a plumber's torch on a propane tank or simply a good fire.[1] As the lead melts, the steel clips from the wheel weights float to the surface — being lighter than lead — and are skimmed off with a tool, leaving the melt pure.[1] The molten lead is then poured slowly into the buried pan, wearing heavily padded gloves, eye goggles, and a respirator throughout.[1] It is left to cool undisturbed; cooling it with water is avoided.[1] A builder in a hurry is better off buying more bread pans, at under a dollar each, or making moulds out of plywood, than trying to rush a single pan through repeated pours.[1]

## Why the mould is buried

The pan is buried in the ground for three reasons: it holds the pan in place and level, the cool ground draws off heat so the pour cools faster, and if the pan should leak, the lead does not end up all over the ground.[1] Some cities have laws governing the smelting of lead, and that is worth checking before setting up a pour.[1]

## Timing the pour in the build sequence

The pigs should be cast before reaching the panel-joining stage of the build, because once made they double as heavy weights: they hold side panels down during [scarph gluing](/construction/scarf-joints.md), and they help bend the curve into the bottom panel as it is nailed down.[1]

## Casting lead into the rudder blade

A second, smaller lead pour goes into the [rudder](/components/rudder.md) blade itself, at its rear, to keep the blade down in the water.[2] This is needed because the pivot bolt is deliberately kept slightly loose at all times, so the hauling line can raise the blade easily; the lead's downward force has to exceed the blade's own buoyancy to hold it in its working position.[2] A pocket is cut into the blade, and clearance holes of the correct size are drilled into the blade's meaty areas for the screws that hold the cast lead in place.[2]

## Pouring the blade

Two details decide whether the pour succeeds: the pocket's lower surface must be sealed tight, and its upper surface must be level.[2] A 3/4 x 3/4 in strip of lumber is nailed to the edge of a 1/2 in plywood pad, and that assembly is clamped in place under the pocket so the blade's underside has solid support beneath it; the blade is then weighted down onto it.[2] With the top of the pocket level, the molten lead is poured slowly until it is just above level and no more, since any more will simply run off the board.[2] Waterglass is often recommended in boat books to keep the wood from scorching during the pour, but casting is safe without it; if a bad scorch pit does result, it is simply ground out with a rotary tool and filled with epoxy putty.[2]

## Fairing the lead flush

The poured lead is faired down close to the blade's surface with a coarse file — teeth about 1/16 in high — and then finished with a disc grinder fitted with a 16 grit disc, checking constantly against a batten for fairness.[2] A plane is explicitly not used for this job; it would simply ruin the blade.[2]

## A second, independently documented rudder-blade pour

A separate, fully independent account of pouring lead into the rudder blade gives a different but compatible mould-and-melt technique. The blade's profiled shape is cut first, then six retaining screws are driven into the pocket to key the lead in place; the mould itself is built from a piece of oak plank and a strip of softwood, clamped under the cavity while the blade lies on its side to form a reservoir, with wooden wedges packed into the gaps where the trailing edge is rounded. The blade is levelled with a spirit level, upper side horizontal, before pouring. Melting is done over a painter's butane torch under a saucepan supported on two bricks, ready to pour in about fifteen minutes. Matt Layden's own calculation of the weight needed for this pour — three kilos of lead — proved accurate in practice.[4]

Once cast, the lead is smoothed with a coarse file and sandpaper and any irregularities filled with thickened epoxy, before the blade and its stock are sheathed in GRP using 6 oz woven roving and epoxy filler — the same fairing-and-sheathing sequence as [Fairing the lead flush](#fairing-the-lead-flush) above.[5]

## Trimming with the battery and a spare pig

The cast pigs are not the only ballast aboard: a boxed battery, at roughly 50 lb, is heavy enough to count as trim weight in its own right, and the opposite (starboard) side of the boat is trimmed against it with a lead pig.[3]
