# Fibreglassing technique (cloth and woven roving over epoxy)

Successful epoxy fibreglassing can be done only one way, "no matter what anyone says"[1]. Two distinct finishes are worth telling apart before any cloth goes down, because they are achieved by different follow-up steps: a smooth-appearing surface not exposed to abrasion or constant immersion (a hatch or cabin top, for example), and a surface that must be waterproof and tolerate abrasion (an underwater or below-decks surface)[1]. Fibreglass cloth and woven roving are themselves worked by two entirely different techniques — cloth is spread thin and never pooled, woven roving is pooled and left to soak — but one rule governs cloth wherever it appears on the boat: do not float it[1]. The method applies alike to the [bottom](/components/bottom.md), the [deck](/components/deck.md), the [rudder](/components/rudder.md) and its blade, the cabin structure, and the cloth lining inside the built-in water tanks (see [water tank construction and lining](/construction/water-tank-lining.md)); this page follows Don Elliott's "Building Paradox" series (see [the "Building Paradox" MAIB series](/references/building-paradox-series.md)) part by part.

## The core rule: do not float the cloth

The most important thing in fibreglassing is not to float the cloth[1]. Some instructions call for adding epoxy after the cloth is saturated in order to fill the weave; doing so creates a disaster[1]. Floating happens because adding resin while the underlying coat is still liquid lifts the cloth off the surface it is meant to bond to, leaving a hilly rather than a fair finish[1]. The initial steps below get cloth onto a surface without floating it; woven roving is treated separately, further down this page[1].

## The five-step cloth sequence

1. Putty all surface defects, sand lightly, and brush the surface clean[1].
2. Squeegee on a thin coat of epoxy resin and wait until it cures dry — barely noticeable that it is tacky to the touch, or just cured if the surface is horizontal; never lay cloth on a wet or a tacky surface[1].
3. Lay out the cloth and smooth it out flat by hand[1].
4. Saturate the cloth without floating it off the plywood — a step that goes more readily if the ply beneath has already been pre-coated and cured hard. The aim is to wet the cloth out only enough to adhere it to the surface and fill it with epoxy, by constantly spreading the resin thin with the same pressure used "to squeegee a window," never pouring and pooling epoxy in one area. Work large areas, leaving white patches to come back and fill in, and never add epoxy to an area that has already darkened; if slight white spots appear, give the epoxy time to spread on its own rather than adding more resin, keeping the weave visible throughout. Any small white areas still left at the end are best finished with a brush carrying only a little epoxy[1].
5. Leave the surface to cure until it gels, adding nothing more until then — adding epoxy while it is still liquid floats the cloth and leaves a hilly surface. A few pinpoint white spots, if any remain, are not important: cured epoxy's strength is so great that they do not affect the area at all. Where a waterproof surface is the goal, more epoxy is added over such a spot later, as part of the finish coats described for a waterproof surface[1].

Where the target finish is only a smooth, non-abraded, non-immersed surface — a hatch or cabin top, for example — a second coat of pure epoxy resin is not applied over the cloth; instead a fairing-putty mix is used, and once faired the putty is sealed with a thin coat of epoxy and painted, which is all that surface needs[1]. A cured epoxy coat should always sit under any epoxy putty, so that sanding cuts away the soft putty while leaving the hardened coat beneath untouched — better to add more putty than to sand into that coat; if the epoxy coat has cured for more than four days, its surface should be scuffed before puttying[1].

## Trimming, edges, and dirt

Softening the cut end of a run of cloth is done by scraping the edge with a small Surform tool, then puttying it smooth[1]. Trimming excess cured cloth is easier with the knife tilted rather than held straight up and down[1]. A hollow drinking straw, carried while working, removes specks of dirt or anything else that would streak the finish[1].

## Glassing the rudder and blade

Both the [rudder](/components/rudder.md) and its blade are fibreglassed by the same cloth method described above[1]. After puttying, the surface is sanded smooth and finished with a thin seal coat of epoxy[1].

## Woven roving on the bottom: why it is there

The thick layer of woven roving and epoxy on the [bottom](/components/bottom.md) is not there to strengthen the hull — "it is already strong"[3]. Its purpose is to protect the bottom from the assault of unexpected groundings and deliberate beaching, where a thin 6-ounce cloth and a coat of paint are not enough to put something solid underneath the boat[3]. A further advantage of a thick bottom is that it puts weight low, where it is most wanted[3]. Each layer of woven roving weighs 8 lb; the epoxy across all layers weighs 30 lb; the plywood bottom itself weighs 100 lb — a total of 154 lb[3]. A bottom laid up this way uses three layers of 50-inch wide, 24-ounce woven roving (fibreglass mat is not recommended alongside it) and 2.5 gallons of epoxy, rising to 3 gallons by the time the bottom is faired out[3]. Woven roving takes an entirely different technique from cloth[3].

## The nine-step roving routine

1. Cover the sides and transom with plastic sheeting before starting, or a great deal of epoxy drip will need to be ground off afterward[3].
2. Lay each piece of roving on the hull, mark its outline adding two inches of extra material outside the hull line, cut it while still on the hull, tape both sides all the way around the edge with masking tape, then roll it up and set it aside on a clean surface — repeat until all three layers are cut and rolled[3].
3. Pre-coat the surface after puttying every knot hole, split, and hole. A knot hole should never simply be puttied over — wire-brush it out thoroughly and dust it clean first, and mound the putty slightly over each one to save a return trip[3].
4. Two people are best for this job; even with two, one layer a day is enough[3].
5. After the pre-coat, roll out the woven roving. It helps to remove the resin pump for mixing larger quantities and use calibrated cups, mixing eight ounces of epoxy at a time; mixing errors here can be a disaster, so a second person should check the other's ratio while pouring[3].
6. Unlike cloth, roving is not squeegeed out. Pool it in one area in enough quantity to soak that area, working small specific areas at a time, then level it with the squeegee and give it time to soak in — roving is far harder to float than cloth, so a feel for the right amount takes some working with. If a small area still looks white no matter what is done, it is not a problem; a test showed that even cloth commonly retains some white areas and still has enough strength[3].
7. Roving does work at edges over the radius of the chine runner, but not over a sharp edge — it will not bend over the fore-and-aft edges of the chine runner, the transom, or the stem edge without forcing, so it is only curved slightly there and radiused later; it is too stiff to bend further[3].
8. Trim all edges before proceeding to the next layer[3].
9. After all three layers are on, mix epoxy with Cab-O-Sil and apply it to the bottom with a wide metal putty knife, using hard pressure to thin the coat as it goes on and working it fore and aft. The knife's edge raises thin ridges that should be flattened out as they appear; the goal is the minimum amount of putty possible — without hard pressure the putty goes on "like frosting on a cake" and leaves a hilly surface[3].

## Layer-direction schedule (tentative reading)

A diagram accompanying the roving steps gives a direction schedule for the three layers, though the surviving scan of this figure is only partly legible and the reading below is tentative: the first coat fore and aft, the second athwartship, and the third at 45 degrees to the last coat[3]. After the three layers are on, a light sanding with the orbiting sander and the correct grit disc is followed by filling any low spots with epoxy, microspheres (white shows up best here, applied liberally), and Cab-O-Sil, then sanding smooth[3].

## Applying the same method: the potable water tank

The [water tank](/construction/water-tank-lining.md) lining is a direct application of the cloth "don't float it" method to a five-coat sequence: orbit-fillet all joints and corners first (using a large fillet-forming tool), then pre-coat the tank's interior and let it cure until almost dry to the touch[2]. Cloth is laid in next, cut and folded at the corners with about an inch of overlap, and — as with any cloth application — it is not floated, only wetted out[2]. The coat is left to cure until it is no longer tacky but just gelled, at most three hours, before a second coat is added; that coat is left to gel the same way before two further coats follow the identical procedure[2]. The tops of the tank's internal separators are kept clear of glass and kept clean, and drips are watched for at the rim of the mixing cup rather than the brush, which is where most drips actually come from[2].

## Glassing the deck

Deck cloth can be laid in a single piece or, to save cloth, in four pieces joined by overlapping butt joints cut through both layers together[4]. The surface is wetted out the same way described above for cloth generally, with the cloth run one and a half to two inches over the deck edge[4]. Resin should never be squeegeed over that edge — doing so makes a mess — and a "spot-tack" (or "quick-tack") technique is used instead: dip a one-inch brush in a cup of epoxy, dab a spot onto the cloth hanging over the edge, move the brush along four to six inches, dab again, and repeat over a long distance without trying to make every white patch disappear as it is worked. Coming back afterward to cover the remaining white areas — while continuing to move along rather than dwelling in one spot — reveals the cloth already wetted out with very little further effort, because the epoxy has spread through the cloth on its own as the work progressed[4]. The epoxy is then left to gel[4].

## The cabin structure and sealing plywood edges

On the cabin roof, three pieces of cloth can be used in the same overlapping, cut-through-both-layers manner as the deck[5]. Cloth is worked with particular care to seal every plywood edge from water; on the sides it is run down to the edge of the wood window frame[5]. Once the cloth has gelled, putty mixed with white microspheres is used to fair the entire cabin structure[5].

## The case against filling the weave

A recommendation repeated constantly in fibreglass cloth procedures calls for filling the weave with extra epoxy after the cloth is on, for two stated reasons: abrasion resistance, and to keep sanding from cutting into the cloth's weave[4]. Both are rejected. Real abrasion resistance comes from extra layers of cloth where they are actually needed — the way the [bottom](/components/bottom.md) is protected with woven roving, or an overlap added at a vulnerable edge like the bow — or simply from repainting before the epoxy itself wears through; that reasoning holds for the deck as much as anywhere else on the boat[4]. On the second point, even once every white area has been eliminated, the little bumps visible on a "filled" surface are not the cloth itself but mounds of epoxy sitting on top of it[4]. Adding more resin than necessary therefore only adds unnecessary weight — in the worst possible place if it is done on the deck — along with unnecessary cost and unnecessary work[4].

Fairing is best done within a couple of days of glassing, for a good bond between the fairing compound and the cured epoxy[4]. Filler colour is switched partway through a build: a brown low-density filler is used everywhere up until the cloth goes on, after which white microsphere filler takes over — brown throws a builder off while wetting out the cloth, whereas white, once the cloth is on, shows clearly where the putty has gone and where the low spots are[4]. See also [bottom painting over epoxy](/construction/bottom-painting.md) for the paint system applied once fairing and glassing are finished, and [epoxy pre-coating technique](/construction/epoxy-coating-technique.md) for the underlying panel-coating groundwork this method builds on. [Paradox](/paradox.md)'s bottom, deck, cabin, rudder, and water tanks are all finished by this same family of techniques.

## A general sanding progression, and a rough patch that resisted full smoothing

A general rule of thumb for sanding any surface, on a boat or otherwise: start with a heavy grit and progressively move to finer grits, finishing once the gouge marks the paper itself leaves behind are too small to see or feel.[6] Even followed carefully, this does not always fully rescue a rough area — woven-roving patches along a hull's gunwales, in one case, could be made considerably more presentable this way but not made perfectly smooth.[6]
