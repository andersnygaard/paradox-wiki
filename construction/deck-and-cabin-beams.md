# Deck and cabin beam lamination

The Paradox's **deck and cabin beams** are made from thin timber,
**laminated up to the required thickness** on a purpose-built mold rather
than cut from solid stock — the beams' strength comes from their curved
shape and the epoxy bond, and thin strips are far easier to bend than solid
timber. The standard method:

- The beams are made of **1/4 in timber**, thin enough to bend easily, and
  laminated to thickness on a mold.
- **Two separate molds are needed**, because the **deck** and the **cabin
  top** have different amounts of crown (curvature).
- Beams are laminated **long** and only **cut to their final length
  afterward**, once fitted.
- A practical tip: make **one beam a day** early in the build, so a full
  set is ready by the time they're actually needed — avoiding a bottleneck
  later in construction.

This is a distinct lamination technique from the chine runners (see
[chine runner construction](/chine-runners/construction.md)), which are built
up from small solid-timber blocks or a single kerfed timber rather than thin
laminated strips — reflecting the different loads and shapes of the two
parts.

## Deck beam dimensions and the deck curve's true shape

The plans do not specify deck beam dimensions on every drawing; **25 x 45
mm** is the size used wherever a deck beam is called for (see also the
related [bulkhead #3 side cleats
question](/construction/hull-assembly-sequence.md) on the same drawings).
The deck curve itself is **not a radius**, but more like a **parabola**, so
rather than pulling a radius and drawing an arc, the practical method is to
use the dimensions given on **sheet 7 (bulkhead 2)** to build a mold, then
laminate **all** the deck beams to the same shape on that one mold, cutting
each laminated beam to length afterward for its particular bulkhead. The
**roof beams** (cabin top) are made the same way, but their curve is defined
on **sheet 10** instead — consistent with the deck and cabin top needing
separate molds for their different crowns.

Deck beams can also be laminated from **plywood** rather than solid timber:
a **25 x 45 mm** curved beam built up from **six layers of 1/4 in plywood**
and epoxy, using a **3-ply BC Southern Pine exterior-grade panel** ("basic
cheap exterior ply"), works well. A full set for one boat runs to **seven**
deck beams — six positions plus one deliberate spare, kept both as a hedge
against defects and as a possible extra support for the cabin roof and a
planned **hinged hatch** (see
[Motorsailer Paradox](/design/variants/motorsailer-idea.md#pauls-own-planned-modifications)
for that modification). A beam that comes out looking "a bit dodgy" need not
be discarded — it can still serve as one of the supports between the carlins
and the vent trunk. Approximate chord lengths, laminated to the curve on
drawing #7: bulkhead #1, 720 mm; bulkhead #2, 1,102 mm; bulkhead #4, 1,090
mm; the transom, 820 mm; plus three intermediate deck beams (forward, 930
mm; aft, 955 mm; cabin frame, ~950 mm).

### Plywood versus solid-timber lamination

A laminated-plywood beam holds up as well as one laminated from solid wood,
despite roughly a third of the plywood's grain running in the "wrong"
direction for best support — a concern that in principle risks a "soft,"
flexing deck. In practice the beam's main job is to **take and hold nails**,
which plywood veneers do well; most deck beams glue directly to the
bulkheads, spreading load and stress between deck and bulkhead in a way that
limits flexing where it matters most (a few **intermediate** deck beams
stand unsupported on their own, by contrast). A plywood-laminated beam
flexes only a little, not much, under a builder's full body weight, and a
solid beam would not be expected to perform noticeably better.

These and other techniques were written up in a **"Build Book"** — informal
building notes describing only techniques known to work from an actual
build rather than presenting a definitive method, in the tradition of the
build books used at production boatbuilders like **S2 Yachts** to speed up
the next hull and serve as a personal shop reference. See
[notching the stem](/construction/stem-notching.md) for the Build Book's
earlier mention in the context of stem and chine-log joinery.

## Adhesive choice: epoxy and polyurethane

Deck beams are typically laminated with **epoxy**, but **PL polyurethane
adhesive** also works: a beam glued up with polyurethane held the shape of
the deck beam and was very stiff once cured. This is the only recorded use
of polyurethane, rather than epoxy, adhesive for Paradox deck-beam
lamination.

## Shimming deck beam #1 (and its neighbor) to fair the centerline

A recurring dry-fit finding across at least three independent builds: after
laminating and template-cutting deck beams as above, **beam #1** (at the
forward bulkhead) can come out **below fair at the centerline** once mated
to its bulkhead — along with the intermediate deck beam immediately aft of
it. The shortfall is not a lamination or springback error (recutting a
beam's springback against an accurate template does not remove it); it is
documented on **Hugh's New Zealand Paradox**, which was shimmed at both
beam #1 and the next intermediate beam aft, and recurs independently on
other builds.

[Aussie Paradox](/boats/aussie-paradox.md) hit the identical problem at
**frame 1** and was shimmed about **5 mm at the centerline**; getting a
straight carlin running from frame 2 to frame 4 also required shaving a few
millimetres off **frame 3**. The issue can be avoided on the *intermediate*
deck beams altogether by running them **longitudinally** rather than
athwartships (see [Aussie Paradox](/boats/aussie-paradox.md) for its fuller
set of deviations) — removing the need to fit a curved beam precisely to
each bulkhead's height at that station. On "Little Jim," the same shortfall
likely went unnoticed simply because the build required filling so many
other gaps that one more escaped particular notice.

The underlying cause traces to the plans themselves: bulkhead #3's deck
camber is meant to be plotted directly, without reference to the
deck-camber template used for the other beams, but plotted as designed it
comes out too *high* relative to the fair line — the same discrepancy as
the beam #1 shortfall, met from the opposite direction, and correctable by
using the camber template at bulkhead #3 as well and shimming to the deck
if a gap results. Because the "instant boat"/stitch-and-glue nature of
Paradox's construction gives a builder no full-scale lofting to check
against, minor beam-height mismatches of this kind are corrected
empirically at trial assembly rather than anticipated on paper — and are
common enough, recurring independently on at least three builds, that they
should be expected rather than read as a building error.

**Mounting deck beams to the sheer clamp.** One method for fitting a beam's
height at the clamp: cut an **angled trough** into the sheer clamp and a
matching angle on the end of the beam, which allows the beam to be raised
or lowered to reach its correct height (start a beam slightly high, then
trim down, rather than the reverse). Where a beam comes out a little low,
holding it in position with a screw nail until epoxy thickened to a
peanut-butter consistency cures in the gap fixes it — an application of the
old boatbuilder's jingle, "putty and paint will make her what she ain't."
A beam-height shortfall at the bulkhead is independent of the clamp fit;
intermediate beams mount normally once clamps go in, and an occasional shim
does not compromise the result.

## Chine runners laminated to the sides before the bottom goes on

Beyond the beam-height question, one further sequencing deviation is worth
noting alongside the standard build order (see
[chine runner construction](/chine-runners/construction.md)):
[Aussie Paradox](/boats/aussie-paradox.md)'s chine runners were **laminated
to the hull sides before attaching the bottom**, rather than the more usual
order, with no outcome report on record for this sequencing choice.

## The Build Manual's "Deck Beam Template" and "Laminating Jigs" installment

The group [Build
Manual](/construction/build-effort.md#the-build-manuals-preparations-installment-build-site-and-a-full-tool-inventory)'s
installment on deck-beam templates and laminating jigs — the next
installment after "Preparations" — is the fullest single procedural account
on record for this step. Jigging and laminating the deck, cabin, and hatch
beams is a good place for a builder to get started on construction
generally.

**Building the deck beam template.** Sheet 7, bulkhead #2 gives measurements
to the **top** of the deck beam, with the "deck beam crown typical
throughout ship" — the basis for the template and laminating jig; the cabin
and hatch beam camber is found separately on the upper-left corner of sheet
10. To build the deck beam template: take a **200 x 1220 mm** piece of
suitable sheet material (doorskin, or cheap 6 mm light-colored plywood), draw
a base line 10–20 mm below the top edge and a centerline (CL) at the sheet's
center, then mark 100 mm divisions off the centerline with a straight-edge
and square. Measuring up from the base line, mark each division at the
plotted top-of-deckbeam height; the resulting tick marks, viewed together,
should look symmetrical with no obvious humps or hollows. A flexible batten,
sprung around brads driven at each tick mark (with the brad left proud so the
batten can spring around it, and a brad or weight anchoring each end), draws
the fair line through the marks; fine-tune brad or batten position until the
line is fair and symmetrical, then mark and cut the template to the line,
finishing the edge with a sanding board so the drawn line survives only as a
thin sliver at the edge. Because the two sides of the centerline will not be
a perfect mirror image, mark one side of the finished template "forward" and
the other "aft" to avoid using it backwards later. The same procedure, using
a **120 x 850 mm** blank, produces the cabin and hatch beam template.

**Building the laminating jig.** Because both templates mark the **top** of
the beam, and the laminations wrap **around** the jig, the jig itself must be
built to the **bottom** edge of the beam — found by tracing the top-edge
template's line onto a second piece of material, then running a combination
square set to the beam's height (**45 mm** for the deck beam) along that
traced edge to plot the bottom-edge line. Picture the top-edge template as
12 mm plywood and the bottom-edge template as 25 mm stock, fastened together
with a constant 45 mm gap between them — that lamination, wrapped around the
resulting two-tier jig, comes out square on one side down to the bottom edge,
needing little further milling. Available clamp size sets the jig's
practical thickness: with 6 in C-clamps as the assumed standard, and a 5–6 mm
clamp pad added to each side of a 45–55 mm beam stack, a jig of roughly
100 mm (±) thickness fits the clamps well. A bandsaw can cut jig stock from
ordinary dimensioned lumber; a jigsaw, which will not cut 2-inch stock
square, instead wants the top-edge piece cut from a single sheet of 12 mm
plywood and the bottom-edge piece built up from two or three stacked, equal
12 mm plywood pieces. The same method, at the cabin/hatch beams' own
(different) dimensions, produces their jig.

**Choosing a lamination thickness.** Thicker strips are harder to bend, need
less glue and fewer clamps, waste less material, and show less springback
out of the jig, but are more susceptible to environmental instability over
time; thinner strips are easy to bend, need more glue, more clamps, and waste
more material as sawdust (though they tolerate lower-quality stock), and show
more springback, but are more dimensionally stable long-term. There is "a
happy medium" between the two, and it varies by species and climate — a
strip of Cypress in Florida bends differently than the same-sized strip of
Douglas Fir in Alaska — so a builder should test-cut a few strips between
4 mm and 7 mm, in **25–27 mm** width, and find what works locally before
settling on a thickness and working out the resulting material list (gross
length of each of the seven beams, plus 50 mm each, multiplied by however
many layers reach the required 45 mm minimum thickness, plus a 5–10% waste
allowance). A tablesaw is the ideal tool for ripping the strips, though a
good Skilsaw with a solid fence will also do the job.

**Laminating procedure.** Bundle and tape the cut strips by beam, with a
centerline drawn across each strip's edge to keep the stack aligned to the
jig's own centerline as work proceeds (refreshing the line as sanding and
scraping wear it away). Cut leftover short strips into **clamp pads**,
marked "wax" and coated with paste car wax, to protect both clamps and
laminate from picking up stray epoxy. Wax the jig itself wherever a beam
will contact it, then secure the jig to a sawhorse or in a Workmate. Tape
the first strip of a stack (to keep it from bonding fully to the waxed jig,
and so it can be peeled free if it does stick), then glue up the stack one
strip at a time with epoxy lightly thickened with Cab-o-sil for a visible
white tint thin enough to wick between the layers, applied with an acid
brush. Lay the glued stack onto the jig taped-side down, centerline aligned,
and clamp first at the centerline (perpendicular to the bend), then
alternate additional clamps side to side, horizontally and vertically,
working out to both ends — snug rather than fully tight until every clamp is
in position. Leave the part in the jig a full 24 hours; removing it sooner
raises the risk of springback. Once a batch of beams is out of their jigs, a
planer cleans them up quickly, or a cabinet scraper followed by a sanding
board for a hand-finished result. The cabin and hatch beams follow the same
process once the deck beams are familiar, with some of their laminations
stacked around "spacer" strips per the plans.

**A note on accuracy.** Careful template work keeps parts consistent with
the design; a few millimeters' difference may look inconsequential on a
single part, but boatbuilding errors compound — a mistake made early in
construction tends to become a bigger problem as work progresses. How much
fit-and-finish to chase is each builder's own call, and that judgment,
including where "tricks of the trade" and shortcuts are safe to take,
develops with experience. A couple of millimeters either way will, almost
certainly, make no difference at all to the finished boat.

**Precision versus a workboat finish.** A deliberately "slapdash",
workboat-finish build can turn out exactly the intended sailing boat —
millimeter-level accuracy at the mold stage "just doesn't matter" to the
outcome in practice. At the same time, the design's plywood self-fairs
enough that bulkheads, beams, and floor timbers need not be individually
fair to go together correctly, though early sloppiness still tends to
snowball through the rest of a build. Both positions agree: the Build
Manual's aim is a basis for building this design to its plans, not a
general boatbuilding treatise.

**Lamination thickness and springback in practice.** Thinner laminates hold
their shape better once cured; thicker ones spring back *more* — the
opposite of what intuition suggests when prebending strips against expected
springback. Beams laminated in 5 mm Honduras mahogany needed 30–40 clamps
per beam for a uniform glue-up; in hindsight 7–8 mm strips would have
worked better, and thinner stock's chief penalty is simply needing more
clamps, manageable with a full-length 6–8 mm clamp pad (waxed and
masking-taped) laid along the stack instead of many individual pads.
Prebending is unnecessary — springback becomes negligible once the epoxy
has at least partially cured — though a beam's top edge may still need
sawing to match the template after some springback. *The Gougeon Brothers
on Boat Construction* and the WEST System website's laminating article are
useful general references. Using **7 mm strips, steamed before
laminating**, cuts springback low enough to need only **3 clamps** per
beam — and since deck plywood fitting will require shaving the tops of some
beams regardless, chasing exact beam-mold accuracy at this stage is not
worth over-investing in.

## Drawing bulkhead curvature from the finished beams, rather than the plans

Rather than drawing each bulkhead's top curvature from the plans and then
trimming a beam to match it afterward, a finished, already-laminated beam
can simply be traced directly onto the bulkhead stock, using it as its own
template. The difference between the two approaches is slight, since a
laminated beam shows only about **2 mm of springback** at each end coming
out of the jig. This works because beams are normally laminated before the
bulkheads go into the boat, so if the beams are already made up, using them
as a bulkhead template is a perfectly good "other way to skin a cat." The
exact shape does not matter much either way, since most beams and bulkheads
end up fine-tuned again once the deck itself is fitted.

## Fitting the forward deck skin to a tight curve

The forward deck panel itself — as distinct from the beams underneath it —
can be genuinely difficult to bend into place, even though the curve does
not look severe on the plans. Webbing straps and windlasses alone will not
always draw a stubborn panel down; cutting it into three smaller sections at
bulkheads one and two, or cutting grooves along its length to encourage the
bend, are both workable but destructive last resorts.

Before resorting to cutting the panel up, the recommended fix is to
**re-check the plans' measurements first**, since a genuinely oversized or
misshapen panel is easier to correct on paper than by force; then, if the
shape is right and the panel still won't take the curve, switch to a
**thinner sheet** — typically about **half the specified thickness**, or as
thin as **one third** for an unusually tight curve — laid up dry first to
confirm it will conform, then glued down; once cured and sanded, a second
layer is added on top to bring the panel back up to full thickness. The
second layer goes on markedly easier than the first once the shape is
already established, but needs a full coat of epoxy for adhesion and to
guard against trapped air pockets between the layers.

If even the thinner sheet resists bending dry, **heat and moisture** will
let it take a curve "in ways you never imagined": soaking the panel, warming
it with a heat gun or quartz lamps (or simply resting it near a heat source,
short of scorching it), and keeping it moist throughout. This works
reliably, but only on plywood laminated with waterproof glue — applying
heat and water to ply that isn't will show damage later, sometimes not for
weeks. A more mechanical alternative for fitting the panel down onto the
beams: start at the **center screw** and work down one side and then the
other from the center out, rather than working end to end.

Two further fixes address a curve mismatch between the deck panel and its
beams directly, rather than the panel's own stiffness. Where the deck beams
themselves came out with more crown than the plans intended, leaving a
noticeable hump between bulkheads one and two, fitting a **separate deck
panel from the stem to bulkhead one** (a comparatively flat run) and shaving
down the top of bulkhead two isolates the problem area rather than fighting
the whole panel at once. Alternatively, the panel can be **cut lengthwise
down the centerline**, each half fitted and glued separately, and the
resulting gap between them filled — a deliberately unconventional fix, but
a workable one; a contrasting inlay strip of wood along the cut, or a thin
line of visible white caulk, turns the necessity into a deliberate design
detail.

A separate build's front-deck difficulty turned out to have a different
root cause: **the sheerstrake's top bevel cut too steep**, so the deck
panel was only ever meeting the sheerstrake's inside edge rather than
bearing on it fully, which read as the panel refusing to take the
designed curve. Cutting **1½ in-spaced grooves into the panel's
underside** with a 60-degree router bit added some flexibility, but the
real fix was correcting the bevel — planing it back, then filleting and
epoxying the joint.

[33] Archive message page_2836 (Chris / "wedgesail" / Chris Ostlind, `paradoxbuilders`, msgnum 2867, 2005-06-17) — "Re: Front Deck": recommends re-checking the plans for precise measurements before cutting further; suggests a sheet of thinner plywood (typically about half the specified thickness, as thin as a third for a nasty curve), dry-fit first, then glued down and, once cured and sanded, a second layer added to bring the panel back to full thickness; if the thin sheet still won't bend dry, soaking it and applying heat (a heat gun, quartz lamps, or simply a nearby heat source) while keeping it moist will let it bend further than expected, though this risks damage — not evident for weeks — to plywood not laminated with waterproof glue; the second layer goes on more easily than the first, but needs a full coat of epoxy for adhesion and to avoid trapped air. Signed "Chris."
