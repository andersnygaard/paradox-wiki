# Deck and cabin beam lamination

The Paradox's **deck and cabin beams** are made from thin timber, **laminated up to the required thickness** on a purpose-built mold rather than cut from solid stock — the beams' strength comes from their curved shape and the epoxy bond, and thin strips are far easier to bend than solid timber. The standard method:

- The beams are made of **1/4 in timber**, thin enough to bend easily, and laminated to thickness on a mold.
- **Two separate molds are needed**, because the **deck** and the **cabin top** have different amounts of crown (curvature).
- Beams are laminated **long** and only **cut to their final length afterward**, once fitted.
- A practical tip: make **one beam a day** early in the build, so a full set is ready by the time they're actually needed — avoiding a bottleneck later in construction.

This is a distinct lamination technique from the chine runners (see [chine runner construction](/chine-runners/construction.md)), which are built up from small solid-timber blocks or a single kerfed timber rather than thin laminated strips — reflecting the different loads and shapes of the two parts.

## Deck beam dimensions and the deck curve's true shape

The plans do not specify deck beam dimensions on every drawing; **25 x 45 mm** is the size used wherever a deck beam is called for (see also the related [bulkhead #3 side cleats question](/construction/hull-assembly-sequence.md) on the same drawings). The deck curve itself is **not a radius**, but more like a **parabola**, so rather than pulling a radius and drawing an arc, the practical method is to use the dimensions given on **sheet 7 (bulkhead 2)** to build a mold, then laminate **all** the deck beams to the same shape on that one mold, cutting each laminated beam to length afterward for its particular bulkhead. The **roof beams** (cabin top) are made the same way, but their curve is defined on **sheet 10** instead — consistent with the deck and cabin top needing separate molds for their different crowns.

Deck beams can also be laminated from **plywood** rather than solid timber: a **25 x 45 mm** curved beam built up from **six layers of 1/4 in plywood** and epoxy, using a **3-ply BC Southern Pine exterior-grade panel** ("basic cheap exterior ply"), works well. A full set for one boat runs to **seven** deck beams — six positions plus one deliberate spare, kept both as a hedge against defects and as a possible extra support for the cabin roof and a planned **hinged hatch** (see [Motorsailer Paradox](/design/variants/motorsailer-idea.md#pauls-own-planned-modifications) for that modification). A beam that comes out looking "a bit dodgy" need not be discarded — it can still serve as one of the supports between the carlins and the vent trunk. Approximate chord lengths, laminated to the curve on drawing #7: bulkhead #1, 720 mm; bulkhead #2, 1,102 mm; bulkhead #4, 1,090 mm; the transom, 820 mm; plus three intermediate deck beams (forward, 930 mm; aft, 955 mm; cabin frame, ~950 mm).

A simpler shortcut than the full chord-length table above, for a builder who wants a working length rather than a precise one: measure straight off the **lines drawing (sheet 2)** and add **3-4 inches**.[39b] See [JoHanna — cabin beam length and tiller offsets](/boats/johanna.md#cabin-beam-length-and-tiller-offsets) and [Faith — deck-beam length, a tiller-curve mistake, and the helm seat](/boats/faith.md#deck-beam-length-a-tiller-curve-mistake-and-the-helm-seat) for the fuller exchange this answered.

### Why 4 ft strips are long enough for every beam

A builder cutting lamination strips from a standard sheet of plywood need not worry that a 4 ft strip length falls short for the boat's longest deck beams: strips are typically cut across a 4x8 sheet (giving 4 ft lengths), then trimmed to each beam's actual finished length once fitted, rather than cut to a beam's full chord length in advance. The boat's widest point, at bulkhead #3, does not carry a deck beam there at all, so no beam actually needs a strip longer than 4 ft in practice.[39a]

### Plywood versus solid-timber lamination

A laminated-plywood beam holds up as well as one laminated from solid wood, despite roughly a third of the plywood's grain running in the "wrong" direction for best support — a concern that in principle risks a "soft," flexing deck. In practice the beam's main job is to **take and hold nails**, which plywood veneers do well; most deck beams glue directly to the bulkheads, spreading load and stress between deck and bulkhead in a way that limits flexing where it matters most (a few **intermediate** deck beams stand unsupported on their own, by contrast). A plywood-laminated beam flexes only a little, not much, under a builder's full body weight, and a solid beam would not be expected to perform noticeably better.

These and other techniques were written up in a **"Build Book"** — informal building notes describing only techniques known to work from an actual build rather than presenting a definitive method, in the tradition of the build books used at production boatbuilders like **S2 Yachts** to speed up the next hull and serve as a personal shop reference. See [notching the stem](/construction/stem-notching.md) for the Build Book's earlier mention in the context of stem and chine-log joinery.

## Adhesive choice: epoxy and polyurethane

Deck beams are typically laminated with **epoxy**, but **PL polyurethane adhesive** also works: a beam glued up with polyurethane held the shape of the deck beam and was very stiff once cured. This is the only recorded use of polyurethane, rather than epoxy, adhesive for Paradox deck-beam lamination.

## Practicing the lamination process, and the case for patience during cure

A dry run of the lamination technique — clamping up scrap strips on a practice mold before committing to the actual deck beams — is a low-cost way to work out clamp count, glue-up sequence, and timing before the real build depends on it. Once a beam is glued and clamped, the practical temptation is to strip the clamps early; the more reliable practice is to leave each beam clamped for a full **24 hours** before pulling it, on the reasoning that rushing the cure to save time rarely pays off against the cost of a beam that springs out of shape or delaminates later.[39]

## Shimming deck beam #1 (and its neighbor) to fair the centerline

A recurring dry-fit finding across at least three independent builds: after laminating and template-cutting deck beams as above, **beam #1** (at the forward bulkhead) can come out **below fair at the centerline** once mated to its bulkhead — along with the intermediate deck beam immediately aft of it. The shortfall is not a lamination or springback error (recutting a beam's springback against an accurate template does not remove it); it is documented on a **New Zealand Paradox**, which was shimmed at both beam #1 and the next intermediate beam aft, and recurs independently on other builds.

[Aussie Paradox](/boats/aussie-paradox.md) hit the identical problem at **frame 1** and was shimmed about **5 mm at the centerline**; getting a straight carlin running from frame 2 to frame 4 also required shaving a few millimetres off **frame 3**. The issue can be avoided on the *intermediate* deck beams altogether by running them **longitudinally** rather than athwartships (see [Aussie Paradox](/boats/aussie-paradox.md) for its fuller set of deviations) — removing the need to fit a curved beam precisely to each bulkhead's height at that station. On "Little Jim," the same shortfall likely went unnoticed simply because the build required filling so many other gaps that one more escaped particular notice. A fourth independent case: on [JoHanna](/boats/johanna.md#a-deck-beam-shim-a-corner-post-plans-question-and-an-interior-paint-question), the beam #1 shortfall was corrected with a shim strip of **9 mm plywood**, alongside a few millimetres trimmed off the stem's own top to match.[41] Fairing at the bulkhead is not unique to beam #1 either: on at least one further build, every deck beam needed some fairing to seat correctly, not only the one at the forward bulkhead.[42]

The underlying cause traces to the plans themselves: bulkhead #3's deck camber is meant to be plotted directly, without reference to the deck-camber template used for the other beams, but plotted as designed it comes out too *high* relative to the fair line — the same discrepancy as the beam #1 shortfall, met from the opposite direction, and correctable by using the camber template at bulkhead #3 as well and shimming to the deck if a gap results. Because the "instant boat"/stitch-and-glue nature of Paradox's construction gives a builder no full-scale lofting to check against, minor beam-height mismatches of this kind are corrected empirically at trial assembly rather than anticipated on paper — and are common enough, recurring independently on at least three builds, that they should be expected rather than read as a building error.

**Mounting deck beams to the sheer clamp.** One method for fitting a beam's height at the clamp: cut an **angled trough** into the sheer clamp and a matching angle on the end of the beam, which allows the beam to be raised or lowered to reach its correct height (start a beam slightly high, then trim down, rather than the reverse). Where a beam comes out a little low, holding it in position with a screw nail until epoxy thickened to a peanut-butter consistency cures in the gap fixes it — an application of the old boatbuilder's jingle, "putty and paint will make her what she ain't." A beam-height shortfall at the bulkhead is independent of the clamp fit; intermediate beams mount normally once clamps go in, and an occasional shim does not compromise the result. A simpler general precaution than shimming after the fact: leave deck-beam cleats proud of their final height and shape them down once the deck itself goes on for final assembly, rather than fitting them to a fixed height in advance.[43]

## Chine runners laminated to the sides before the bottom goes on

Beyond the beam-height question, one further sequencing deviation is worth noting alongside the standard build order (see [chine runner construction](/chine-runners/construction.md)): [Aussie Paradox](/boats/aussie-paradox.md)'s chine runners were **laminated to the hull sides before attaching the bottom**, rather than the more usual order, with no outcome report on record for this sequencing choice.

## The Build Manual's "Deck Beam Template" and "Laminating Jigs" installment

The group [Build Manual](/construction/build-effort.md#the-build-manuals-preparations-installment-build-site-and-a-full-tool-inventory)'s installment on deck-beam templates and laminating jigs — the next installment after "Preparations" — is the fullest single procedural account on record for this step. Jigging and laminating the deck, cabin, and hatch beams is a good place for a builder to get started on construction generally.

**Building the deck beam template.** Sheet 7, bulkhead #2 gives measurements to the **top** of the deck beam, with the "deck beam crown typical throughout ship" — the basis for the template and laminating jig; the cabin and hatch beam camber is found separately on the upper-left corner of sheet 10. To build the deck beam template: take a **200 x 1220 mm** piece of suitable sheet material (doorskin, or cheap 6 mm light-colored plywood), draw a base line 10–20 mm below the top edge and a centerline (CL) at the sheet's center, then mark 100 mm divisions off the centerline with a straight-edge and square. Measuring up from the base line, mark each division at the plotted top-of-deckbeam height; the resulting tick marks, viewed together, should look symmetrical with no obvious humps or hollows. A flexible batten, sprung around brads driven at each tick mark (with the brad left proud so the batten can spring around it, and a brad or weight anchoring each end), draws the fair line through the marks; fine-tune brad or batten position until the line is fair and symmetrical, then mark and cut the template to the line, finishing the edge with a sanding board so the drawn line survives only as a thin sliver at the edge. Because the two sides of the centerline will not be a perfect mirror image, mark one side of the finished template "forward" and the other "aft" to avoid using it backwards later. The same procedure, using a **120 x 850 mm** blank, produces the cabin and hatch beam template.

**Building the laminating jig.** Because both templates mark the **top** of the beam, and the laminations wrap **around** the jig, the jig itself must be built to the **bottom** edge of the beam — found by tracing the top-edge template's line onto a second piece of material, then running a combination square set to the beam's height (**45 mm** for the deck beam) along that traced edge to plot the bottom-edge line. Picture the top-edge template as 12 mm plywood and the bottom-edge template as 25 mm stock, fastened together with a constant 45 mm gap between them — that lamination, wrapped around the resulting two-tier jig, comes out square on one side down to the bottom edge, needing little further milling. Available clamp size sets the jig's practical thickness: with 6 in C-clamps as the assumed standard, and a 5–6 mm clamp pad added to each side of a 45–55 mm beam stack, a jig of roughly 100 mm (±) thickness fits the clamps well. A bandsaw can cut jig stock from ordinary dimensioned lumber; a jigsaw, which will not cut 2-inch stock square, instead wants the top-edge piece cut from a single sheet of 12 mm plywood and the bottom-edge piece built up from two or three stacked, equal 12 mm plywood pieces. The same method, at the cabin/hatch beams' own (different) dimensions, produces their jig.

**Choosing a lamination thickness.** Thicker strips are harder to bend, need less glue and fewer clamps, waste less material, and show less springback out of the jig, but are more susceptible to environmental instability over time; thinner strips are easy to bend, need more glue, more clamps, and waste more material as sawdust (though they tolerate lower-quality stock), and show more springback, but are more dimensionally stable long-term. There is "a happy medium" between the two, and it varies by species and climate — a strip of Cypress in Florida bends differently than the same-sized strip of Douglas Fir in Alaska — so a builder should test-cut a few strips between 4 mm and 7 mm, in **25–27 mm** width, and find what works locally before settling on a thickness and working out the resulting material list (gross length of each of the seven beams, plus 50 mm each, multiplied by however many layers reach the required 45 mm minimum thickness, plus a 5–10% waste allowance). A tablesaw is the ideal tool for ripping the strips, though a good Skilsaw with a solid fence will also do the job.

**Laminating procedure.** Bundle and tape the cut strips by beam, with a centerline drawn across each strip's edge to keep the stack aligned to the jig's own centerline as work proceeds (refreshing the line as sanding and scraping wear it away). Cut leftover short strips into **clamp pads**, marked "wax" and coated with paste car wax, to protect both clamps and laminate from picking up stray epoxy. Wax the jig itself wherever a beam will contact it, then secure the jig to a sawhorse or in a Workmate. Tape the first strip of a stack (to keep it from bonding fully to the waxed jig, and so it can be peeled free if it does stick), then glue up the stack one strip at a time with epoxy lightly thickened with Cab-o-sil for a visible white tint thin enough to wick between the layers, applied with an acid brush. Lay the glued stack onto the jig taped-side down, centerline aligned, and clamp first at the centerline (perpendicular to the bend), then alternate additional clamps side to side, horizontally and vertically, working out to both ends — snug rather than fully tight until every clamp is in position. Leave the part in the jig a full 24 hours; removing it sooner raises the risk of springback. Once a batch of beams is out of their jigs, a planer cleans them up quickly, or a cabinet scraper followed by a sanding board for a hand-finished result. The cabin and hatch beams follow the same process once the deck beams are familiar, with some of their laminations stacked around "spacer" strips per the plans.

**A note on accuracy.** Careful template work keeps parts consistent with the design; a few millimeters' difference may look inconsequential on a single part, but boatbuilding errors compound — a mistake made early in construction tends to become a bigger problem as work progresses. How much fit-and-finish to chase is each builder's own call, and that judgment, including where "tricks of the trade" and shortcuts are safe to take, develops with experience. A couple of millimeters either way will, almost certainly, make no difference at all to the finished boat.

**Precision versus a workboat finish.** A deliberately "slapdash", workboat-finish build can turn out exactly the intended sailing boat — millimeter-level accuracy at the mold stage "just doesn't matter" to the outcome in practice. At the same time, the design's plywood self-fairs enough that bulkheads, beams, and floor timbers need not be individually fair to go together correctly, though early sloppiness still tends to snowball through the rest of a build. Both positions agree: the Build Manual's aim is a basis for building this design to its plans, not a general boatbuilding treatise.

**Lamination thickness and springback in practice.** Thinner laminates hold their shape better once cured; thicker ones spring back *more* — the opposite of what intuition suggests when prebending strips against expected springback. Beams laminated in 5 mm Honduras mahogany needed 30–40 clamps per beam for a uniform glue-up; in hindsight 7–8 mm strips would have worked better, and thinner stock's chief penalty is simply needing more clamps, manageable with a full-length 6–8 mm clamp pad (waxed and masking-taped) laid along the stack instead of many individual pads. Prebending is unnecessary — springback becomes negligible once the epoxy has at least partially cured — though a beam's top edge may still need sawing to match the template after some springback. *The Gougeon Brothers on Boat Construction* and the WEST System website's laminating article are useful general references. Using **7 mm strips, steamed before laminating**, cuts springback low enough to need only **3 clamps** per beam — and since deck plywood fitting will require shaving the tops of some beams regardless, chasing exact beam-mold accuracy at this stage is not worth over-investing in.

## Cabin-top and hatch-cover beam bevels: open questions from sheet 10

Building the laminating jig for the **cabin-top beams and hatch-cover beams** raises questions the deck-beam jig above does not: sheet 10 shows a full set of **six cabin-top beams and three hatch-cover beams**, and unlike the deck beams, only three of the nine share the same dimensions — most vary in size from one to the next. The **foremost cabin beam** is roughly **22 x 44 mm**, with a bevel on both its fore and aft faces; the forward-facing bevel would need to be picked up after the cabin framework is built, while the aft-facing bevel is not critical. Of the **two aft cabin beams**, the lower is roughly **22 x 72 mm** with a critical aft-facing bevel, while the upper is notched to lock the hatch shut. At the **forward hatch opening**, the forward face is similarly notched to take the weather seal. One builder's own pencilled plans give the foremost cabin beam at **52 mm** and the lower aft cabin beam at **75 mm**, both bevels shaped freehand with an angle grinder and sanding disc — the forward bevel and the deck beam's own bevel sighted by eye while standing in the roofless cabin against the beam's side-to-side curve. Whether each of the hatch-lock and weather-seal notches is better routed out of a full-sized beam after lamination, or laminated up with the notch already in the right place, comes down to whatever tooling is easiest for the builder: a router gives an easier route for either notch, but a beam without one on hand can simply be laminated with a narrower bead of material on top instead.[39c][39d] See [JoHanna — cabin beam jig questions from sheet 10](/boats/johanna.md#cabin-beam-jig-questions-from-sheet-10) for the fuller context this question arose from.

## Cabin-top beam count: three versus four, and whether the roof takes a standing load

Sheet 10's cabin-top beam layout has appeared with **four** beams (forward, an intermediate between forward and central, central, and aft), but at least one built Paradox — [Little Jim](/boats/little-jim.md) — has only **three** cabin-top beams and has never needed a fourth. The reasoning for omitting the intermediate beam: the cabin hatch is normally left open when the boat is in harbour, so the exposed cabin-top area behind it is not somewhere a person would sit or stand, and the fibreglass sheathing over the plywood adds further stiffness beyond the beams alone. A builder weighing the same question on his own boat — having laminated his beams roughly two years before actually fitting them, and working from a memory of only three beams being called for — raised the possibility that the intermediate beam still matters if someone does stand on the forward part of the cabin top; no resolution beyond the open-hatch reasoning is recorded.

Pressed further on whether a closed hatch changes the answer — someone might still stand or sit on the forward cabin-top section with the hatch shut, and the plans' own front and back beams read as **absolutely straight** when a rule is laid across them in plan view — the open-hatch reasoning was restated without a structural test to settle the standing-load question either way.[40c]

## Bending polycarbonate glazing around the forward and aft window curves

The plans draw the forward and aft cabin-top beams with a straight edge in plan view, which can read as though the polycarbonate window below has little curve to take up — but the **side view (sheet 1)** shows the same beams are not flat, curving vertically even though they appear straight from above; the forward window's curve is more pronounced than the aft window's. In practice, polycarbonate glazing is flexible enough that this is not a difficult bend: a test offcut roughly 20 x 300 mm could be bent into half a reef knot without breaking. The recommended technique for matching the top and bottom beam profiles before fitting the glazing: stand inside the cabin with an angle grinder fitted with a coarse sanding disc, sight down the two beams, and freehand-grind the profile until it looks fair, checking periodically with a batten and straight edge; small gouges from overgrinding can be filled with epoxy afterward. Hardboard templates made from the faired profile, tacked in place before cutting the actual glazing, give a further check that everything is fair. Because a good weatherproof seal at the aft end of the hatch depends on it, the **aft beam's edge needs to stay straight**, even though a curved forward beam might in principle make shaping that window easier.

A further reading of the plans' forward window raised an apparent contradiction: the window's very top edge, where it meets the forward beam, is drawn absolutely straight, yet the window's own plane is shown curved elsewhere, more so toward the bottom than the top — suggesting a small curvature at the top, where the window meets the beam, would make more sense than a dead-straight edge, though the glazing material's own flexibility may simply absorb the mismatch.[40c] The resolution: a window pane held vertical would read straight, and one held horizontal would take on the roof's own curvature exactly; because the forward window sits at an angle partway between the two, its curvature is itself partway between a straight line and the roof's full curve — accounting for the apparent contradiction without any error in the plans.[40d]

## Corner post angles on sheet 10: the 30-degree and 14-degree window-seating detail

Sheet 10 also carries an exploded-view detail of the cabin's **corner post** that puzzles first-time readers trying to tell whether the drawing is a plan view or an elevation. A photograph of the finished frame resolves the confusion at a glance where the drawing alone does not.[42] The detail is in fact a **section through the uprights**, cross-referenced to the sheet's own "side frame" layout by a linking arrow — not a true plan view, since the uprights themselves slope inward. The two dimensioned angles, **30 degrees and 14 degrees**, specify the angle at which the **end windows** need to seat once wrapped around the front and rear of the cabin: the forward window curves more than the aft one, so it needs the larger of the two angles to sit flat against the post. The fit is not critical to get exactly right off the drawing — it can be tweaked at assembly with a sanding disc on an angle grinder.[43] Working from a photograph of the frame rather than the drawing's own angles, one build ended up with the support posts' forward edges faired to run parallel rather than tapering their tops to the drawn angle, and turned out stronger at the upper end for it, if "only aesthetically...less pleasing."[42]

## Cabin side frame corners confirmed: just butted and faired

A first-time reader of sheet 10, unable yet to picture how the cabin side frame's top and bottom rails meet the laminated front and rear deck-framing pieces at the corners, got the same answer already recorded above about the corner posts: the pieces simply **butt together and are faired in with epoxy**, and how they fit becomes obvious once the cabin side frames are actually offered up to the ends of the front and rear laminated pieces rather than worked out from the drawing alone. The corner-post tops are treated the same way, simply trimmed to fit together — "easier to do than to explain."[51][52][53]

## Drawing bulkhead curvature from the finished beams, rather than the plans

Rather than drawing each bulkhead's top curvature from the plans and then trimming a beam to match it afterward, a finished, already-laminated beam can simply be traced directly onto the bulkhead stock, using it as its own template. The difference between the two approaches is slight, since a laminated beam shows only about **2 mm of springback** at each end coming out of the jig. This works because beams are normally laminated before the bulkheads go into the boat, so if the beams are already made up, using them as a bulkhead template is a perfectly good "other way to skin a cat." The exact shape does not matter much either way, since most beams and bulkheads end up fine-tuned again once the deck itself is fitted.

## Fitting the forward deck skin to a tight curve

The forward deck panel itself — as distinct from the beams underneath it — can be genuinely difficult to bend into place, even though the curve does not look severe on the plans. Webbing straps and windlasses alone will not always draw a stubborn panel down; cutting it into three smaller sections at bulkheads one and two, or cutting grooves along its length to encourage the bend, are both workable but destructive last resorts.

Before resorting to cutting the panel up, the recommended fix is to **re-check the plans' measurements first**, since a genuinely oversized or misshapen panel is easier to correct on paper than by force; then, if the shape is right and the panel still won't take the curve, switch to a **thinner sheet** — typically about **half the specified thickness**, or as thin as **one third** for an unusually tight curve — laid up dry first to confirm it will conform, then glued down; once cured and sanded, a second layer is added on top to bring the panel back up to full thickness. The second layer goes on markedly easier than the first once the shape is already established, but needs a full coat of epoxy for adhesion and to guard against trapped air pockets between the layers.

If even the thinner sheet resists bending dry, **heat and moisture** will let it take a curve "in ways you never imagined": soaking the panel, warming it with a heat gun or quartz lamps (or simply resting it near a heat source, short of scorching it), and keeping it moist throughout. This works reliably, but only on plywood laminated with waterproof glue — applying heat and water to ply that isn't will show damage later, sometimes not for weeks. A more mechanical alternative for fitting the panel down onto the beams: start at the **center screw** and work down one side and then the other from the center out, rather than working end to end.

Two further fixes address a curve mismatch between the deck panel and its beams directly, rather than the panel's own stiffness. Where the deck beams themselves came out with more crown than the plans intended, leaving a noticeable hump between bulkheads one and two, fitting a **separate deck panel from the stem to bulkhead one** (a comparatively flat run) and shaving down the top of bulkhead two isolates the problem area rather than fighting the whole panel at once. Alternatively, the panel can be **cut lengthwise down the centerline**, each half fitted and glued separately, and the resulting gap between them filled — a deliberately unconventional fix, but a workable one; a contrasting inlay strip of wood along the cut, or a thin line of visible white caulk, turns the necessity into a deliberate design detail.

A separate build's front-deck difficulty turned out to have a different root cause: **the sheerstrake's top bevel cut too steep**, so the deck panel was only ever meeting the sheerstrake's inside edge rather than bearing on it fully, which read as the panel refusing to take the designed curve. Cutting **1½ in-spaced grooves into the panel's underside** with a 60-degree router bit added some flexibility, but the real fix was correcting the bevel — planing it back, then filleting and epoxying the joint.

A temporary fit of the fore and aft decks can reveal the foredeck as genuinely hard to shape near the stem even once the plans check out, prompting the idea of cutting a shallow groove — about **3 mm deep and 400 mm long** up the centerline with a circular saw — to let the panel bend more easily, on the reasoning that any mark on the outer surface will be filled, faired, and glassed over regardless.[44] This cut carries two costs worth weighing first. Leverage from the panel's own shaped "wings," left standing proud when each side is trimmed, can bend the forward part of the foredeck without any cut at all, though even that method can open a small crack, roughly 9 in long, up the centerline. A saw-groove cut deliberately in the same area is more likely to crack there than to avoid the problem, and once the panel takes its final shape the two sides of the cut straighten themselves out, leaving a ridge that stays clearly visible — structurally harmless once glassed over, but a less pleasing result than the continuous curve the design intends.[45]

A similar differential-shrinkage trick works on solid timber, not only plywood panels: left on a damp lawn in the sun, a board's top side shrinks while its underside stays moist against the ground and expands, curving it — the same method straightened out a batch of studs that had dried crooked, flipped to treat the opposite face once the first side had done its work.[46]

One build that had left none of the panel's own "wings" for such leverage found the fix harder going: with the stem sitting a few millimetres too high, sighting along the finished deck showed a curling "Turkish slipper" profile rather than a fair line, calling for further grinding.[47] Steaming the panel — as already used successfully on the chine logs — was one option weighed for the roughly 400 mm problem area right at the stem, alongside simply wetting it on a lawn as in the differential-shrinkage method above, though sunshine to complete that method could not be counted on.[48] The exchange closed on a lighter note, with a joke about dressing the stubborn curve up with bells or tassels instead.[49]

A different approach sidesteps the tight-curve problem rather than fighting it: building a lighter, thinner-ply foredeck in two halves around a **kingplank**, using a fair batten clamped to each bulkhead and a pre-beveled sheer clamp to mark and plane the deck-edge angle — see [NOLI's foredeck built in two halves, avoiding the tight-curve problem](/boats/noli.md#nolis-foredeck-built-in-two-halves-avoiding-the-tight-curve-problem).[50]
