# Pressure-treated wood for the bottom and chine members (rejected)

Building the [Paradox](/paradox.md)'s **bottom**, **chine logs**, and **chine
runners** out of **pressure-treated (PT) lumber** was considered as an
alternative to the plywood and solid fir normally used (see
[hull scantlings](/design/hull-scantlings.md) and
[chine runner construction](/construction/chine-runner-construction.md)), and
ultimately rejected.

## The case for pressure-treated wood: extra weight and rot resistance where it counts

Pressure-treated wood is roughly **twice as heavy** as untreated wood of the
same size. That extra weight could be put to good use on Paradox's
**bottom** and **chine logs** — parts that sit low in the hull, where added
weight helps [ballast](/design/weight-and-ballast.md) rather than hurting
stability — with the
[chine runners](/construction/chine-runner-construction.md) as further good
candidates. The treatment would also **limit future rot**, a recurring
concern across the archive (see
[checking a used Paradox for rot](/construction/rot-inspection-ice-pick.md)).

## The open question: epoxy adhesion

The key unknown was how well pressure-treated wood would **accept epoxy**,
which called for destructive testing on samples before committing any hull
material to the idea.

## Destructive test: epoxy held, screws didn't — but doubts remained

A **tee joint** built from pressure-treated 2x6 fir was tested two ways: one
sample bonded with **epoxy and thickener** alone, a second fastened with
**3.5 in wood screws**. Both were attacked with a **3 lb hammer** to
"dis-assemble" them:

- The **epoxy joint never broke** — the surrounding wood failed first.
- The **screwed joint's screws eventually pulled through** the wood under
  repeated hammering.

This result — after only **two days'** cure time — looked favorable for
epoxy on pressure-treated wood, but left an unresolved concern: freshly-cut
pressure-treated wood consistently seemed **damp inside**, raising the
question of whether the **copper-based preservative solution** ever fully
dries, or whether only freshly-treated stock behaves this way. This residual
moisture may explain why PT lumber weighs more than untreated wood in the
first place, rather than the wood itself being denser — an open question for
anyone with experience using well-**seasoned** pressure-treated wood.

## An imminent EPA arsenic ban undercut the proposal

Pressure-treated wood's preservative contains not just copper but
**arsenic**, and was believed to be about to be **taken off the market**,
with an EPA ban starting that year or the next — a fact that undercut the
whole proposal's future viability regardless of how the adhesion question
resolved.

## Glue and paint don't bond well to PT lumber either

The finding that settled the matter: **glue and paint do not stick well to
pressure-treated lumber**. Common construction-trade advice is to let PT
lumber **weather for a full season** before attempting to finish it —
standard practice for decks and fences, but impractical for a boat hull that
needs to be sealed and finished as part of the build sequence.

## Outcome

Between the confirmed glue/paint-adhesion problem, the unresolved
residual-moisture question, and the announced/imminent arsenic-based-preservative
ban, no builder is recorded adopting pressure-treated wood for a Paradox —
the idea is best read as **explored and effectively abandoned**, not as an
open question.
