# Puttying and fairing

Puttying an epoxy/plywood hull is inherently repetitive work, and the whole discipline around it is aimed at one goal: minimum sanding. A hole rarely fills perfectly on the first pass, and the right attitude going in is to expect a spot to need puttying two or three times before it is right, rather than to fight it — "I know I'll probably have to putty this two or three times before I get it right" is the working assumption, not a sign something has gone wrong.[2] Every choice downstream of that attitude, from the filler mix to the sanding sequence, exists to avoid sanding off epoxy that has already been paid for.

## The no-sanding doctrine

The governing rule is to sand as little as possible, in every part of the process.[1] Practical consequences follow directly from it: never sand off epoxy that has already been paid for, since there are ways to avoid it; buy coarse (40–60 grit) good-quality aluminum-oxide paper rather than fine paper, since coarse paper cuts fast, does not require a smooth finish, and cleans easily with a brush; and save used pieces of paper in a bag for later small jobs rather than discarding them.[1] Where a tool other than sandpaper can do the same job, it should — a Surform, a cabinet scraper, or a block plane all remove wood or cured epoxy without sanding, and a big disc grinder loaded with 36 grit does the work of many small sheets of paper in one pass.[1] Surfaces that will be painted need only light sanding if the epoxy underneath was applied correctly in the first place. When puttying, the putty itself should be mixed with plenty of easy-sanding filler so that whatever sanding is left is quick. Fillets built correctly should need no sanding at all.[1] Surfaces that will be painted need only light sanding if the epoxy underneath was applied correctly in the first place, and applying epoxy with a putty knife rather than a squeegee for the coat after the first avoids the hollows a squeegee leaves no matter how many passes are made over them — "think minimum puttying."[3]

## The palette

Puttying is always done off a shop-made palette, a tool borrowed from masons and plasterers who have relied on it since early civilizations.[1] Make the palette from thin plywood, with a handle that lets it be tipped and rotated, sized to hold about 3 oz of mixed putty. Give the working surface two coats of epoxy: putty lifts off a smooth surface more easily, and the palette itself cleans up more easily once puttying is done. Spreading the putty flat across the palette also extends its working (gel) time, since spreading it over more surface area lets it cool.[1]

## Fillers and mixing

The best choices of filler for a sandable putty are Microlight (WEST SYSTEM 410), low-density filler (WEST SYSTEM 407), and microballoons, always followed by Cab-O-Sil to keep the mix from running or sagging.[1] A workable mix: stir the epoxy completely first, then pour in Microlight by eye — no need to measure — stirring and adding more until the mix looks right, then add Cab-O-Sil. The mix is ready when the putty clings to a stir stick lifted out of it. Transfer the putty from the mixing cup onto a clean palette and spread it flat before starting to putty.[1]

Filler colour is chosen deliberately and switched partway through a build. Brown low-density filler (WEST SYSTEM 407) is used until the fiberglass cloth goes on; after that, white filler (microspheres) takes over.[4] The reasoning is that the white colour is distracting while wetting out cloth, but once the cloth is down, the same white colour makes it easy to see exactly where the putty has gone and where the low spots are.[4]

## Preparing and applying a dab

Before any putty goes on, the hole itself must be free of dust and grit, and the surrounding area smooth and free of chips or uneven surfaces. Nail heads need to be set at least a fingernail's thickness below the surface, not merely flush or barely below it. The putty knife used should be four to five times wider than the hole, and no bigger than that.[1] Only two tools are needed to apply putty: a metal putty knife with a good bevel edge, and an acid brush — the acid brush works best for dabbing small gobs of putty onto small areas and for smoothing out small pools of epoxy.[1][3]

The amount of putty applied should be just enough to fill the hole and no more — a large gob over a small hole makes a mess. Pick up a small gob on the outside edge of the putty knife and dab it on top of the hole rather than wiping it on, then turn the knife flat and smooth the putty down. Finish by lifting the thin epoxy residue left around the hole, first with the putty knife and then with a wiping tool, so the result looks as if nothing had been done there except that the hole is now filled.[1] A good putty job, seen from a distance, already looks smooth — no gobs, no ridges left by the knife, no pits waiting on another pass.[1]

## The two failure modes

Two things commonly go wrong. First, a pit can form in the putty: the fix is to reapply a small dab and smooth it from a different direction, or from several directions, without spending much time on it — mark the spot as one to return to later rather than fighting it in place.[1] Second, putty can flow off the outside edges of the knife and leave ridges: this is pure clean-up, with the bevel edge held up, scooping the excess, wiping the blade clean, then scooping the rest.[1]

A dirty knife should never go back to the surface. Any trace of putty left on the blade will either scrape what was just finished or leave streaks, so the knife is sprayed with vinegar and wiped clean with a cloth every single time before it picks up more putty.[1] A damp wiping-cloth board, kept soaked in vinegar, serves the same purpose as a rag and takes only seconds to use.[1]

## Long cracks, large flats, and curved surfaces

Long cracks or splits in plywood are filled lengthwise first. The knife is then turned flat to straddle the split, and a modified putty knife — ground with a slight concavity in its edge — is run down the length of the split to mound the putty over the crack, while a standard bevelled knife picks up the edge residue.[1] Large flat surfaces are worked the same way, just with a larger knife.[1] Curved surfaces call for a different touch entirely: no pressure on the knife edge at all, just lightly moulding the putty on top of the surface. Curved-surface putty jobs reliably take more sanding and more return visits than flat ones.[1]

The same light-pressure lesson applies at larger scale when pressing thickened epoxy over a laid-up bottom: a wide metal putty knife under hard pressure, working fore and aft, thins the coat properly, leaving only small ridges at the knife's edge to be flattened afterward; without that hard pressure the putty goes on "like frosting on a cake," leaving a hilly rather than a fair surface.[5]

## Fairing large hull surfaces

Fairing a large surface starts before any putty goes on. Walk around the hull and survey it by eye and with the palms of the hands, carrying a small Surform, and shave every bump and protrusion flush — the fairing tool needs a smooth surface to glide over without bumping or catching on something.[4] For a good bond, any fairing should be done within a couple of days of applying fiberglass to the hull or deck.[4]

Putty is always applied with the grain of the plywood — fore and aft on flat surfaces, or at 30–45° to the centreline on a curved deck — so the knife rides the plywood's hard resin ridges. Working across the grain instead produces endless hills and valleys and a never-ending puttying job.[4] Apply with firm pressure using at least a six-inch metal putty knife, never a squeegee, since a squeegee follows the surface too closely and leaves hollows no matter how many passes are made over it.[3][4] A small ridge is left at the edge of the knife regardless; smoothing it out immediately, while puttying, is the point — otherwise it just has to be sanded away later, and the whole aim is to sand as little as possible.[4]

## The sanding sequence

Once a puttied coat has cured hard, sanding follows a fixed order: first a small Surform to knock down the large bumps, then an orbiting sander or a foam pad focused on the ridges left by the putty knife's edge, and finally a long sander over the entire surface, worked at a slight angle to the fore-and-aft direction.[4] A hull side sanded this way should take no more than 10 to 15 minutes.[4]

Five rules govern the whole sequence: sand with the grain; never use power sanders on deck edges or on fillets; always use long, sweeping strokes; add putty only to the lows, not all over; and do not over-sand — minimum sanding is the goal throughout.[4] A sander should never be held in one spot, or it will grind a low into the surface, and a power tool should never cut into the deck's radiused edge — the edge is sanded with a long sander used gently, and corners are handled with a hand block sander instead.[4]

## Surveying instead of sanding more

After the fixed sanding pass, the next step is analysis rather than more sanding. Walk the length of the hull, running a palm over the surface in the direction water would flow, feeling for lows and hollows — sometimes with a short swing of the arm, sometimes a long sweep. These lows run in one of two directions, fore-and-aft or up-and-down, and each one found gets a strip of masking tape marking that direction, so putty is added only where it is actually needed rather than spread everywhere out of habit.[4]

## Where to stop

Small pits and minor imperfections are left for the paint to fill, and where non-skid finish is going onto the deck, an ultra-smooth surface is not needed at all — "it's a homebuilt boat, not the side of a Cadillac."[4] This is a deliberate contrast with a professional method of laying on a thick layer of putty and sanding it down with a long power sander, which works but only at the cost of extra weight on the boat and wasted epoxy and filler.[4]
