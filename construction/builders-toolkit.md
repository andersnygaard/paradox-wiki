# The builder's toolkit

Modern epoxy-and-plywood construction retires most of a traditional wooden-boatbuilder's kit before it's even unpacked.[1] Epoxy's gap-filling strength means gluing surfaces no longer have to match perfectly, and power tools that cut and remove material fast eliminate the slow hand-fitting older methods required — a finishing plane, a spokeshave, or a draw-knife has essentially no job left to do on a [Paradox](/paradox.md).[1] What replaces that kit is a short list of power tools built around cutting, grinding, and sanding, plus a handful of small, cheap, unglamorous items that a builder ends up reaching for constantly. Department-store tools are largely unsuited to the job — most are built for light work — so the right source is a well-stocked hardware store, a large lumber yard, or, for a few specific items, an auto parts store.[2]

## The three tools that matter most

Of the whole kit, three tools carry the bulk of the work on a boat of Paradox's complexity; everything else ranks well below them in importance.[7]

- **A table saw.** Straight and bevelled parts have to be cut accurately, and it would be difficult to build the boat without one — a small saw is adequate for a Paradox, and a used or borrowed one will do.[3][7] Plans-accuracy for the main hull parts runs to 1.5–2 mm (1/16 in), so cuts should be run against a fence rather than guided by eye off the saw's blade-position slot.[3]
- **A disc grinder with heavy 16–36 grit discs**, run on a hard rubber pad for stock removal and a soft foam pad for fairing.[7] The economics matter here: a single #16 or #36 grit disc lasts the entire boat, where adhesive-backed stick-on discs are a recurring expense that adds up fast.[4] Used flat and kept off edges and corners, the grinder turns what would be a long hand-fairing job into a few seconds of work, and it is aggressive enough to warrant a little practice before turning it loose on a real part.[4]
- **A low-angle block plane, Stanley #118.** It is small next to the other two, but it belongs on the same list.[7] A single block plane, used going with the grain, does double duty radiusing beam edges and roughing out curves before a palm sander finishes the job.[4]

## Measuring and marking

- **Tape measure**: get the widest blade available — a full-inch blade will stand out unsupported roughly 5 ft without bending, where a thinner blade sags, and the wider scale is easier to read.[5]
- **Wooden rulers**: cheap (25¢) rulers, inches on one side and metric on the other, are handier for short measurements than a tape measure, which can be awkward with its bent tip and tendency to snap shut at the wrong moment. Buy two — cut one down at the 16 cm mark to carry in a pocket, and keep the full-length one in a tub on the bench. Because they're made flush with zero at the end, they can be butted straight against a face.[6]
- **Ultra-fine laundry marker** (Sanford, series 37000): keep a black one and a red one. The marker is never used to lay out a line — pencil remains the right tool for that — but it makes a line highly visible once the layout is settled, drawing dark and fast even with the tip held right against an edge (draw slower than with a pencil and rotate the pen as it moves, to let the ink flow). The red marker in particular is never used to draw a line at all; it exists purely to flag something — drawn as a circle, cross-hatching, or a written caution — to catch the eye later.[6]
- **Crayola "Cool Rule"**, $1.25: a bevel gauge with accurate degree marks, a fast way to draw circles up to 12 in across for cutting ports, a metric scale, and — trimmed to its zero mark — a quick, handy short ruler in its own right, all for the price of a candy bar.[4]

## Cutting and shaping

- **Stanley Surform #21-115** and the narrower **Stanley Surform file #21-125** (an older-hardware-store item worth tracking down): the #21-115 knocks epoxy lumps and drips off beam sides using the tool's curve and heel (the heel cuts more aggressively), and the narrow #21-125 gets used more than any other single hand tool on the boat — it can cut a 1/2 in slot in 2 in material in a few seconds.[4][3]
- **6-1/2 in electric handsaw**: chosen over a bigger saw specifically for being lighter, which matters over the many hours of a build — a smaller, cheaper saw still would have been preferred if one had existed.[4]
- **Small electric drill**: pick the shortest-bodied, variable-speed, reversible drill available, since it has to fit into tight spots where an extra half inch of body length matters; hundreds of screws get installed, backed out, and reinstalled over a build.[4]
- **Dremel tool**: reaches epoxy and cuts stainless threads that nothing else can get at.[4]
- **Orange break-off-blade cutter**: cheaper and better than comparable utility knives, with uses across nearly every stage of the build.[4]
- **Staple gun**: avoid staples beveled on the ends, which tend to go in crooked; when the goal is simply driving a staple flush, bear down on the gun with the palm of the hand.[4]
- **Panel saw**: not used as a saw at all — repurposed as a flexible file.[4]

## Sanding and fairing

- **Orbiting sander** (Black & Decker 5 in): an aggressive sander good on wood, cured resin, and cured paint, best kept off fir plywood's soft grain, where it digs valleys; its hook-and-loop disc system made changing grits far faster than a belt sander's.[4][3]
- **Palm sander**: small, high-rpm (12,000 rpm minimum), loaded with resin-based sandpaper, and the safest, best tool for sanding edges.[4]
- **"Flapper"**: reaches inside holes and curves and smooths their edges.[4]
- **Sanding disc for the table saw**: swaps in for the blade and gives quick, smooth finishes.[4]
- **3M Paint & Sanding Stripper / Paint and Rust Stripper**: not what its name suggests — for boatbuilding it functions as a non-clogging sander, grinder, and general surface-scuffing tool that chucks into a hand drill, used on either its flat face or its outer edge, and it can all but replace sandpaper on the boat.[4][8] Its listed uses: scuffing cured epoxy for a better gripping surface, grinding away cured epoxy lumps (and even scuffing uncured glue), scoring plywood or wood surfaces to key them for gluing, and scuffing bumpy epoxy surfaces — such as a woven-roving bottom — that need cloth or putty applied over them, a job no other tool does as easily.[8] It cleans up with vinegar or another cleaner when needed.[8]
- **Vinegar**: doubles as a cleaner for epoxy tools, including the 3M stripper above.[8]

## Clamping and holding

- **Mini-Quick Grips**: an extra pair of hands wherever something needs holding; a two-pack under $20 covers most jobs, and the large size is generally too bulky to be useful.[4]
- **Spring clamps**: a baseline item, useful everywhere.[4]
- **Rubber bands**, cut from car or bicycle inner tubes and kept in a range of sizes, hold parts together while they wait to be glued or while glued parts cure; string serves the same purpose.[4]

## Epoxy handling

- **"Goop" hand cleaner**: hard to imagine building an epoxy boat without it — used to clean hands (in a separate tub from the one used for brushes) with a hand lotion follow-up in the evening, but kept well away from the boat itself, its parts, and tools, since epoxy does not get along with it.[4]
- **Metal putty knives**, in every size, cleaned with "Goop" and sandpaper.[4]

## Shop-made tools

A long shop-made sander — built because production long-sanding-bars come with inadequate small knobby handles that cramp the hand — is used to sand plywood edges and the flat hull sides and bottom.[9] It's built around a cut length of sanding belt held by end caps, with the sandpaper itself pinned in place by nails driven through dowels; it produces enough force, and works from the top or the side, to round and smooth hull edges quickly, though the disc grinder is still needed first to radius the thicker bottom and woven-roving edges.[9] A separate shop-made "Spherical Filleting Tool" is used to lay epoxy fillets at bulkhead joints; its main advantage over a plain radiused stick is that it can be tilted in any direction without changing the shape of the fillet it leaves behind, and two working methods exist — a prepared "Superfast" method for fillets planned in advance, and a quicker improvised "Right-Now" method for impromptu or leftover-putty fillets, which gives a less finished result but is faster and needs no preparation.[10]

An independently built copy of the same shop-made sander confirms both its dimensions and its purpose. Made from half-inch plywood, 16.5 inches long and 3 inches wide, faced with strips cut from a belt sander's own abrasive belt, with a stout handle at each end to let the user settle into a sanding rhythm, it was used on flat surfaces such as GRP sheathing and epoxy filler on the hull and rudder, and for rounding edges ahead of sheathing them — a plain copy of the original design that "works well" in practice.[11] The same tool, described years later while shaping an unrelated boat's boom, is given slightly different dimensions — 15 inches long with a 3-inch (75 mm) grit pad — against the 16.5-inch figure recorded at the time of building; the contemporaneous figure is the more reliable one.[12]

## Organizing the kit, and consumables

Tools are best kept sorted by task, in crates that move as a unit from one area of the boat to another — large clamps in one crate, small clamps in another, planes and files in a third, electrical tools in a fourth, and so on.[4] A tool pouch worn while working — front pockets, a hammer loop at the side, generally holding a tape, a pencil, a scrap of sandpaper, and the orange cutter — stops the constant back-and-forth to a bench or a crate to fetch the next thing needed.[4] A portable clip-on lamp on an electric cord should travel wherever the work does, since seeing what's actually being cut, sanded, or glued matters more than it might seem.[4] Some items on this list will look out of place next to a traditional boatbuilder's kit — the governing principle is simple: if it works, it gets used regardless of how anyone else feels about it.[4]

## Hindsights

Looking back over the build, two changes stand out. Hook-and-loop sanding discs eventually stopped holding to their pad even while the grit surface itself was still good, which was a source of ongoing frustration.[7] And not knowing about the Starrett digital-readout tape measure (DIGI TAPE) at the time cost real speed and accuracy that a simple tool swap would have bought back.[7]
