# Epoxy brands, chemistry, and cure behavior

Which **epoxy brands** have actually proved out on Paradox and other [Matt Layden](/matt-layden/biography.md)-design boats, their handling and cure properties, and the underlying epoxy chemistry — as distinct from the separate **cost** comparisons of West System versus Raka (see [lower rudder fitting — bottom roving and cheap epoxy sourcing](/construction/rudder-bearing-materials.md) and [epoxy and fiberglass materials sourcing](/references/materials-sourcing-links.md)).

## Brands used in practice

**West System**, bought by the gallon, is a common choice but is comparatively expensive. Cheaper alternatives in use include:

- **RAKA**: a sample reported as "a little too flexible and hard to sand" — gumming up sandpaper — traces to a small test batch or cool cure conditions rather than the epoxy itself (see temperature sensitivity below).
- **FASCO**: used exclusively (for gluing and filling, mixed with glass/microballoons) to repair [Swamp Thing](/matt-layden/swamp-thing.md) in Key Largo for worm damage and dock-impact damage to her chine runners. It was slow to cure — overnight, using the "tropical" (slow) formula — but sanded a lot easier than West once cured. FASCO has also been used building boats in the Florida Keys. Source cited: `tikiwatersports.com`.
- **System Three**: Matt Layden and other builders used System Three building their own Paradoxes, buying in quantity together for a price break.
- **"Defender Industries" generic epoxy**: [Little Cruiser](/matt-layden/little-cruiser.md) was built with this generic epoxy, with **no delaminations** in the boat or spars after almost **ten years** of hard use. Matt Layden is believed to have used this same generic epoxy on all his earlier boats too.
- **SP Systems**: used on the Isle of Wight, chosen simply as the cheapest option available at the time — no properties beyond "sticky" and effective are on record. **UK Epoxy** recurs favorably by reputation among UK builders, corroborating UK Epoxy Resins' own track record noted below in the skin-sensitivity discussion.[29]

Many different epoxy brands appear usable for general boatbuilding: some are thicker and better for gluing, others thinner and better for laminating. Builders are best served by testing a small amount of whatever brand interests them; no genuine side-by-side comparison of major brands and generics is known to exist, likely because advertising spend favors the established brands over such a comparison. Testing across System Three, RAKA (127 resin), and West System yields a concrete recommendation: before committing to a bulk order from any retailer, buy a small amount first and actually work with it — laminate some fiberglass, glue some wood, mix up a batch of fairing compound — checking gel time, stiffness, and how easily it sands once cured, since so much of a build is spent handling the stuff that comfort with the product matters as much as its specifications. West System proved the choice after running exactly this kind of test, with cloth, fillers, and most other supplies bought from raka.com and johnrsweet.com.

## System Three's flexibility and a regional-supplier preference

Choosing a brand is also "a matter of regional pride and respect," favoring local suppliers where practical. West System has "always performed perfectly," though at a price premium; System Three's regular epoxy, used only occasionally (mainly its "Quick Cure" for interior trim), performed well, with the cured resin seeming **a bit more flexible** than West's — likely a hardener/resin-ratio difference rather than a defect. System Three's broad product range and reasonable prices make it a reasonable choice for the remainder of a build.

## A changed product line, and generic sourcing advice for newcomers

System Three revised its product lineup at some point after the brand reports above, leaving at least one newcomer unsure which combination of general-purpose epoxy and hardener to buy from the reorganized range even after downloading the company's own "The Epoxy Book" guide; Don Elliott's construction-book epoxy chapter proved the clearer resource for resolving the same confusion.[31][33]

Independent generic sourcing advice reinforces the two-manufacturer picture above: brand loyalty matters less than composition, since nearly all epoxy on the US market traces back to only a handful of major chemical distributors reselling closely similar formulations; a California-based general polymer supplier, bought via eBay, delivered epoxy for well under $50/gallon. Pre-thickened "thixotropic" epoxy lines, though convenient-sounding, are more of a hassle to work with in practice than buying plain, unthickened, paintable epoxy and mixing each batch to the specific job at hand, adding thickening or other additives afterward rather than buying them pre-blended in. Buying the full run of consumables — disposable gloves, masks, tongue depressors, and mixing cups and sticks — before starting a job, rather than partway through, avoids a common newcomer stumble.[32]

## The chemistry behind the brands

Only **two companies actually manufacture epoxy** — **DuPont** and **Ciba-Geigy** — and every branded product on the market resells one or the other's base resin/hardener under many different formulations. **WEST** holds a **patent on the hardener** it developed, which explains why West System costs more than generic or off-brand competitors selling similarly-sourced base chemistry.

## RAKA's temperature-dependent cure behavior

RAKA's cure speed is unusually sensitive to ambient temperature, which bears on the "too flexible" sanding complaint above:

- Using RAKA all winter in Florida establishes **60°F as the important threshold**: below 60°F the epoxy can take days to harden at all (the slow hardener takes a couple of days to harden regardless of temperature), while above 60°F the fast hardener sets "like a rock" within 24 hours.
- RAKA's **100% fast hardener** also works down to about **50°F and even a bit below** — with heavy amine blush and up to two days to set up well in cold night conditions. Waiting about a **week** before sanding, even in warm weather, gives better results and reduces (but does not eliminate) the sandpaper-clogging problem. An April issue of *WoodenBoat* magazine (cited informally) carried an article on **scraping cured epoxy as an alternative to sanding**, using a tool similar to a cabinet scraper.

This temperature-sensitivity finding refines, rather than contradicts, the initial impression that RAKA is hard to sand: a sample cured under cool conditions, or sanded too soon, would show exactly that symptom.

## Balcotan polyurethane adhesive as a possible interior alternative to epoxy

**Balcotan**, a waterproof polyurethane adhesive, was raised as a possible substitute for epoxy specifically for gluing interior fittings — cleats and similar small parts to the bulkheads — rather than for the hull's structural joints or sheathing.[21]

## Cold-weather cure and heating a workspace

Beyond RAKA's own temperature threshold, cure-in-the-cold is a recurring concern for any builder working through a winter in an unheated garage or shed. A Canadian-made epoxy called **COLD-CURE** has a track record of working well in cold conditions specifically, though it has not been widely reported on since. Absent a cold-tolerant epoxy or a heated workspace, builders working through the winter can expect their main gluing and coating tasks — decks, interior painting — to wait on improving the space's heating arrangement rather than pushing an epoxy cure at marginal temperatures.[20]

## West System hardener discoloration with age

**West System fast hardener** can turn a **reddish color** after about a year on the shelf while still hardening normally. This is normal — epoxy simply **darkens with age**, and its shelf life is **"almost indefinite."**

## Pricing and supplier watch: sales and a lower-cost source

**uscomposites.com** is a lower-cost source for epoxy and fiberglass supplies: cloth priced comparably to other suppliers such as NOAH, a slow-hardener formula that suits general boatbuilding work with a comfortably long working time before a mixed cup kicks off, quick order turnaround, and fiberglass tape that works fine — though for drapability around corners, **[Dynel](/construction/epoxy-coating-technique.md#why-fiberglass-the-boat-at-all-cost-technique-and-a-damage-history-case-for-full-encapsulation)** fabric (sourced from Jamestown) outperforms both: ordinary fiberglass cloth will not crease sharply and pulls away from any corner that is not well rounded, while Dynel drapes around corners far more satisfactorily.

## Converting a US epoxy quantity to litres or kilograms

Epoxy is commonly quoted in **US gallons** in build-materials lists (see the plans' own **5-7 gallon** estimate), which needs converting for builders working in metric units. One US gallon is **3.785 litres** — not the **4.5 litres** of a UK/Imperial gallon, a distinction worth checking explicitly for any builder (such as in Canada) who might see either gallon size in local use. A litre of epoxy weighs **slightly more than a litre of water** (1 kg), so a plans-quoted 5-7 US gallons works out to roughly **19-26 litres**, or a little over that in kilograms.

## How much epoxy a build actually uses

Beyond the plans' own **5-7 US gallon** estimate (see the litre/kilogram conversion above), real-world reports vary with technique and rework. One Paradox build, working on a deliberate "better to use too much than too little" principle, used **two lots of 25 kg** (about 5 kg of which was left over at the end), favoring epoxy's gap-filling properties over the material savings a more polished woodworking technique might have allowed. At the other extreme, a build that had to strip and completely redo the hull bottom used substantially more epoxy than planned across the whole project as a result, with the total spend left untracked.

## A skin sensitivity case, and switching epoxy brands or adding a mask

Laminating curved deck beams with **West System** epoxy produced a painful, itchy skin rash across the arms, hands, and face for one builder, despite taking the recommended precautions — gloves and protective clothing — and working with the shed door wide open for ventilation; the reaction came before any epoxy sanding had been done, ruling that dust out as the cause and pointing instead to skin contact with, or fumes from, the liquid resin/hardener itself. The builder considered the reaction may require switching to a different epoxy brand altogether, or adding a proper respirator mask even for mixing and stirring, rather than gloves and clothing alone. This is a genuinely different case from the caution elsewhere in the archive against using solvents like vinegar to clean uncured epoxy off skin (which dilutes and helps it migrate into the body, see [epoxy coating technique](/construction/epoxy-coating-technique.md#citations)) — here the reaction is a straightforward contact/inhalation sensitivity to the epoxy chemistry itself, of the kind that affects some individuals more than others regardless of technique.[22]

## Responses to a skin sensitivity case: alternative brands, resin types, and glove/mask technique

The skin-sensitization case above drew a range of practical responses. UK Epoxy Resins was suggested as an alternative brand, having caused no adverse reaction across an entire build.[23] MAS epoxy was separately reported to have caused no problems across a full build, with a mask worn only occasionally, depending on ventilation.[24] Epoxy sensitization is a recognized affliction: it can appear the very first time an individual uses epoxy, or only after years of exposure, and once sensitized a person tends to react to any epoxy brand, not just the one that triggered it. Buying only a small amount of any alternative brand before committing further avoids wasting money if the reaction recurs; a barrier cream of the kind used by auto mechanics to keep grease and oil off the skin, and wearing two or three pairs of surgical gloves at once, both help — the extra glove layers also let a gooped-up outer pair be stripped off quickly to answer a phone or use a tool without contaminating it.[25] **Gorilla Glue**, a polyurethane adhesive with a different base chemistry from epoxy entirely, is a much faster-curing alternative; it stains skin black/dark, so rubber gloves are advised, and cured glue can be removed from skin with sandpaper if it has built up thickly enough.[26] Following up, a skin reaction was eventually narrowed down to the **latex gloves** themselves rather than the epoxy: reading the box warning that natural rubber latex can cause allergic reactions prompted a switch to vinyl gloves to test the theory, isolating variables one at a time since gloves are cheaper to experiment with than epoxy.[27] **Vinylester resin** is a further alternative to epoxy, much less prone to causing sensitization, though it carries a strong, unpleasant odour that some find worse than epoxy's; the same precautions (gloves, ventilation) are still advised regardless of resin choice.[28] Vinylester and other vacuum-infused composite techniques are covered further at [glass-bottom idea](/design/ideas/glass-bottom-idea.md) and the foam/glass construction discussion below.

## A composite/vacuum-infusion workshop, and a speculative foam/glass Paradox

A workshop covering composite construction over foam using vinylester resin and vacuum infusion produces completely fair, flat panels that can be bent into shape once cured — a technique praised as producing very quick, neat results for anyone used to ordinary hand-laid fiberglass. This raised the speculative idea of building a Paradox in foam/glass rather than plywood, purely for the challenge and the practice, though no such build is recorded as having gone ahead.[28]

## Ethanol's effect on epoxy and polyester fuel tanks

Most modern gasoline blends contain some percentage of ethanol, which degrades both epoxy and polyester resin over time; **vinylester** is the only resin reported to withstand it. This bears directly on any **wet** fuel tank built into a hull, as distinct from a locker holding a removable fuel container — built in anything but vinylester, such a tank is a matter of when, not if, it leaks. See [auxiliary outboard power — further stern-quarter fuel-storage proposals](/operations/auxiliary-outboard-power.md#further-stern-quarter-fuel-storage-proposals-vented-bins-preferred-over-built-in-tanks-and-a-copper-tank-design) for the fuel-storage debate this answered.[30]

## MAS epoxy validated for general use, and a California supplier

**MAS** low-viscosity 2:1 epoxy resin, sold at marine stores, has drawn no reports of trouble in the boatbuilding community and is judged likely to perform as well elsewhere in a build as it already has for laminating deck beams, provided a builder is already comfortable and familiar with it and it remains conveniently available — consistent with the general no-single-best-brand conclusion above. **System Three** is commonly reserved specifically for a boat's water tanks even where MAS or another brand covers the rest of the build. Southern California builders can source structural epoxies, System Three included, from **Aircraft Spruce** in Corona, alongside its usual aircraft-supply stock — a useful alternative to mail order for anyone in the region. A further, independent instance of the eBay-sourced generic-epoxy route already noted above (see [a changed product line, and generic sourcing advice for newcomers](#a-changed-product-line-and-generic-sourcing-advice-for-newcomers)) comes from a builder in San Francisco who buys all of his epoxy that way and swears by its price, building many boats on it.

A further MAS data point comes from a builder who ran his own build on the brand throughout, starting on their **Flag** resin before switching to **Low Viscosity** on a fellow builder's recommendation; the Low Viscosity grade proved excellent, and with MAS's full international sales and backup was expected to perform every bit as well as West System or System Three, though the final proof would only come with time.[39] Closing out the same MAS-versus-System-Three thread above, the newcomer weighing the two settled on **West System** for the bulk of his own build — more readily available and cheaper near him than System Three, which would have meant an hour's drive or a mail order — while keeping the System Three he had already ordered specifically for the water tanks, to be done strictly per the plans and manual; the small amount of MAS he had already used on his deck beams had proved very strong, reinforcing the general view that brand matters less than following the mix and use instructions correctly.[40]

## Disposable brushes, a sawdust-based filler tip, and a further no-complaints MAS report

A separate West System loyalist, planning at least five gallons for a full build, reports having heard nothing bad about MAS either, despite using West System himself throughout — a further no-complaints data point alongside the MAS validation above. Beyond brand choice, two practical consumable tips: keep a wide range of disposable paint brushes on hand, from 1 in up to 3-4 in wide, plus rollers for fiberglass-coating work; and save accumulated sawdust to mix into a cheap epoxy filler/putty rather than buying a dedicated thickening agent.[38]

## Epoxy over polyester: wax, impregnation, and a professional's caution

Impregnating bare wood with a diluted coat of polyester resin before laminating over it with epoxy is not a reliable shortcut. Epoxy bonds well to bare, clean wood — the basis of its reputation as a strong glue — but bonds only weakly to most cured polyester resin, since polyester typically leaves a **wax** on its surface that must be fully removed before epoxy will adhere to it at all. Diluting polyester resin does not solve the underlying problem either: however much a polyester resin is thinned, it is the **solvent alone** that penetrates the wood, not the resin itself, so the wood is not actually impregnated by it. Where cost rules out epoxy altogether, polyester resin is workable to glass the bottom up to the waterline, with the rest simply painted; investing in a good paint job serves a hull better than using polyester resin on the wood generally.[41][42] A professional formulator's perspective — from someone who had worked at a marine-resin manufacturer — draws the same line even more sharply: **never put epoxy over polyester**. The compatible sequence runs the other way: seal the wood first with an epoxy sealer, then laminate over that seal with epoxy resin.[43] Weighing the advice against a tight budget, one builder settled on epoxy resin just above the waterline, painting the rest.[44] A further reply arriving after that exchange had already run its course offered a different combination entirely: **epoxy thinned with acetone** in place of a polyester pre-coat, avoiding any adhesion mismatch with later epoxy work altogether.[45]

## Reviving old, crystallized epoxy: a heat trick and a microwave mishap

Old epoxy remains usable well past three years, even stored at a constant low temperature — epoxy "much older" than that has worked fine, with the **hardener**, not the resin, the part most affected: some WEST System hardener thickened over time to "a strong resemblance to molasses," workable but not ideal.

Epoxy resin can also **crystallize** near the bottom of an old can. The fix is to **boil water and stand the can in the pot**, off the stove, and stir — West System's own testing on ten-year-old epoxy found **no loss of cured strength**, only a thickened, harder-to-mix hardener, matching the molasses comparison above. Heating the hardener itself to see whether that would thin it remains untried. The technique carries a real hazard: mixing a small amount of epoxy for a small joint on a very cold day and giving it 15 seconds in a microwave to warm it caused the mixture to **exotherm so fast it nearly overflowed the microwave** before it could be pulled out — a warning against microwaving epoxy even briefly.

[42] Archive message page_5425 (arvent / "Nels", `paradoxbuilders`, msgnum 5472, 2008-09-06) — "Re: POLYESTER/EPOXY": full text and citation also at [people/nels-arvidson.md](/people/nels-arvidson.md#design-and-construction-contributions); epoxy works best on bare, clean wood, which is why it makes such a good glue; it will stick to some polyester resins, but not nearly as well as to bare wood; most polyester resins leave a wax on the surface that must be removed first or the epoxy won't adhere at all; no matter how much polyester resin is diluted, it does not impregnate the wood — only the solvent does; if epoxy is unaffordable, use polyester resin to glass the bottom up to the waterline and paint the rest; better to invest in good paint than to use polyester resin on wood generally. Signed "Nels."
