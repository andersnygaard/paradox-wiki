# Epoxy safety and personal protection

Building a [Paradox](/paradox.md) in plywood and epoxy means daily exposure to sanding dust, uncured resin, and solvents, and the published construction record treats personal protection as inseparable from the build technique itself, covering respirators, gloves, and the correct cleaning liquids in the same breath as the gluing and coating methods described at [epoxy coating technique](/construction/epoxy-coating-technique.md) and [epoxy brands and chemistry](/construction/epoxy-brands-and-chemistry.md).[1] The one genuine safety dispute in that record concerns whether vinegar is safe to use on skin, and it is set out here even-handedly, with the manufacturer's published caution given as the operative guidance.

## Respiratory protection

A filter is worn on the neck at all times during a build, kept there specifically so it is never worth the trouble of skipping it.[1] Working with hand tools — a plane, a chisel — does not itself call for putting the mask on, so long as the filter stays on the neck within reach, but picking up any power tool, turning on the table saw, or starting to mix epoxy calls for putting the mask on before beginning.[1] Two grades of filter cover the work: a light paper type used for most tasks, and a heavy-duty filter reserved for grinding, where dust production is heaviest.[1] The mask stays on even after a tool is switched off, giving raised dust time to settle, and it stays on while sweeping and vacuuming, since a vacuum exhausts some of what it picks up rather than containing all of it.[1] Off the face, the mask is stored in an airtight container rather than left open to shop air.[1] The reasoning behind all of this is stated bluntly in the published record: epoxy additives that reach the lungs do not leave.[1]

## Hand protection

Gloves are worn every time epoxy is handled, and the disposable thin kind is specified over latex.[1] They stay on for the whole task, not just for applying the epoxy — through cleaning the brush and squeegee, wiping up excess glue, and picking up used cups — since contact with epoxy continues well past the point where the resin itself has been applied.[1] A small tear that lets a dab of epoxy reach the skin is not considered harmful provided it is cleaned off right away.[1] Clean-up itself uses a waterless mechanic's hand cleaner, with separate containers kept for hands and for brushes so the two do not cross-contaminate each other.[1]

Sticky gloves resist simple removal — talc does not stop them clinging to sweaty skin — and pulling straight off tears the fingertips away, leaving bare, epoxy-covered hands and a ruined glove.[2] A technique avoids this: ease each fingertip free in turn, starting at the pinky, and stop as soon as real resistance is felt; then grip the cuff at the wrist and peel it back over the knuckles at the back of the hand and palm, stopping just short of the first finger joint; then pull at the fingertips again, which frees the glove intact.[2]

## Solvents

Among the liquids used in building, denatured alcohol and rubbing alcohol can still deal with uncured epoxy resin before it reaches its gel stage, with denatured alcohol doing the job better than rubbing alcohol.[3] Acetone is the most powerful solvent of the group and, on that same account, the most dangerous: it is absorbed through the skin, hazardous to breathe, and a fire hazard, and the guidance is to minimize its use, keep it off skin, and avoid using it at all wherever possible.[3]

## The vinegar dispute

Vinegar was promoted in the original construction record as the mildest and safest of the cleaning liquids used in building — a food-safe acid with millennia of use, set against rubbing alcohol as a weaker version of denatured alcohol and against acetone as the most dangerous liquid of the group.[3] A published correction from an epoxy manufacturer's technical advisor took direct issue with recommending vinegar for cleaning epoxy off skin.[4] Household vinegars, whether distilled white or cider, run 4 to 10 percent dilute acetic acid along with low percentages of alcohols and mineral salts; applied to skin to remove epoxy, vinegar slightly dissolves it and then carries it through the skin's protective layers into subdermal tissue, increasing both the likelihood of an allergic reaction and its intensity if one occurs.[4] On that guidance, vinegar remains safe for cleaning tools but not skin, and the way to avoid needing any skin cleaner at all is to work clean in gloves and long sleeves so bare skin never contacts epoxy in the first place.[4]

A published reply denied that cleaning skin with vinegar had ever been advised, holding that the safety precautions given throughout the series specified keeping any blend of epoxy and solvent — vinegar included — off bare skin, and maintaining that vinegar is still the safest cleaning agent available when set against acetone, lacquer thinner, and other solvents.[5] The final installment of the series restated the point on the epoxy manufacturer's own authority: vinegar is an approved cleaning agent according to representatives of System Three, provided a blend of epoxy and vinegar never reaches the body and vinegar is never used to wash hands.[6] Given the direct conflict between promoting vinegar as a general skin-safe cleaner and warning that it can drive epoxy into the skin, the manufacturer's caution is treated here as the conservative and operative guidance: vinegar for tools, never for skin, with gloves and long sleeves as the real defense against ever needing a skin cleaner at all.[4] The fuller record of this exchange, including the trademark and terminology corrections that accompanied it, is at [Building Paradox series](/references/building-paradox-series.md).[4][5][6]

## The revised clean-up protocol

The final installment of the series settled on a layered glove protocol for wiping up uncured epoxy that keeps any cleaning solvent away from skin entirely: the thin working gloves come off first; hands are then cleaned with a mechanic's hand cleaner and washed with soap and water; only after that do heavy solvent-resistant gloves go on to do the actual epoxy clean-up — gloves too bulky to use for gluing itself, but appropriate once the gluing is done.[6]

## Lead ballast handling

Casting lead ballast pigs is its own hazard, separate from epoxy handling, and has to be done outdoors, never in an enclosed space.[7] Pouring molten lead calls for heavily padded gloves, eye goggles, and a respirator.[7] Once poured, the lead is left to cool on its own; it must never be cooled with water, which risks a violent reaction when water meets molten metal.[7] Some cities regulate lead smelting by law, worth checking before setting up a pour.[7]
